CHAPTER 8: The Museum of Borrowed Faces

The basement lights flickered once, twice, and then held steady.

Fang stood at the bottom of the stairs, his breath visible in the sudden cold. The refrigerator hummed behind him, a sound he had once found comforting—the reliable thrum of preservation, of science, of control. Now it sounded like a hive waking up.

The Taoist followed him down, his wooden beads clicking with each step. Mrs. Chen remained in the operating room upstairs, sedated and strapped to the table. Her new face had stopped talking, but it hadn't stopped watching. Fang had felt its gaze follow him all the way to the basement door.

"Open it," the Taoist said.

Fang's hand hesitated on the refrigerator handle. "Some of these are old. Twenty years old. I don't know what they've become."

"You should have thought about that before you started collecting." The Taoist's voice was flat. No anger. No judgment. Just the tired authority of a man who had seen this before. "Open the door, Doctor."

Fang pulled.

The cold hit him first. Then the smell. Not rot—he was too careful for rot. It was the smell of preserved things. Formaldehyde. Alcohol. The sterile chemical scent of death held at bay.

The refrigerator was a walk-in. Floor-to-ceiling metal racks, each one lined with glass containers. Jars of various sizes, all labeled with dates and names. Fang had been meticulous. Every piece of skin was catalogued. Every patient's file cross-referenced. He had told himself it was for quality control. For repeat customers. For the business.

He had never admitted, even to himself, that it was a trophy room.

The Taoist walked past him, his robes brushing against the jars. He stopped at the center of the room, turning slowly, reading the labels.

"Lin Mei. 2019. Harvested from a university student. Twenty-two years old. Drowning victim." He touched a jar containing a pale, translucent sheet. "You used her for the mayor's wife. The one who wanted to look like she was twenty-five again."

Fang said nothing.

"Zhang Wei. 2021. Factory worker. Twenty-nine. Fell from a scaffolding." The Taoist moved to the next jar. "Her skin went to the television anchor. The one with the late-night talk show."

"You know my clients."

"I know your victims." The Taoist turned to face him. "You think I don't see the pattern? You don't harvest from random spirits. You hunt specific ones. Young ones. Pretty ones. Ones who died before their time, when their skin was still perfect."

Fang's jaw tightened. "They were going to cross over anyway. I just... repurposed them."

"Repurposed." The Taoist laughed. It was a dry, hollow sound. "You peeled their faces off and sold them to rich women who wanted to cheat time. That's not repurposing. That's desecration."

"Mrs. Chen was happy."

"She was happy until her face started talking to her in the mirror." The Taoist stepped closer. "And she's not the only one. I've been tracking you for six months. Do you know how many calls I've gotten? How many women have woken up screaming because the face they put on at night is trying to tell them who it used to be?"

Fang's phone buzzed. He ignored it.

"The skin remembers, Doctor. That's the part you never understood." The Taoist picked up a jar, holding it to the light. Inside, a piece of flesh floated, pale and featureless. "Every spirit leaves a residue. Memories. Emotions. Pain. You think a chemical bath can wash that away? You think refrigeration stops the dead from dreaming?"

A low vibration ran through the room. Not from the refrigerator's motor. From the jars themselves.

Fang stepped back.

"They're waking up," he said.

"They never went to sleep." The Taoist set the jar down carefully. "They've been waiting. Biding their time. Growing stronger in the cold." He looked at Fang with something close to pity. "You kept them in the dark for so long, they learned to see in it."

The vibration grew. The jars began to rattle against the metal racks. A low, rhythmic tapping, like fingers on glass from the inside.

Fang pressed himself against the wall. "You said you wanted to help."

"I want to stop a massacre." The Taoist pulled a string of beads from his wrist, holding them up like a weapon. "These spirits have been trapped in your refrigerator for years. Some of them for decades. They've had nothing to do but remember how they died. Nothing to do but hate the man who cut them open."

"I didn't kill them."

"No. You just took what was left." The Taoist began to walk toward the door. "I'm going upstairs to deal with Mrs. Chen. You're going to stay here and face what you've created."

"You can't leave me with them!"

"I'm not leaving you. I'm giving you a chance." The Taoist paused at the bottom of the stairs. "Apologize to them. Every single one. Mean it. And maybe—maybe—they'll let you live long enough for me to figure out how to unbind them."

"Apologize? To jars of skin?"

The Taoist's face hardened. "They're not just jars of skin. They're people. People you stole from. People whose voices you silenced so rich women could have prettier faces." He turned and started up the stairs. "You have ten minutes. Use them well."

The door at the top of the stairs closed. The lock clicked.

Fang was alone in the basement.

The rattling grew louder. The jars began to shift on their racks, sliding toward the edges, tilting, threatening to fall. Fang pressed himself into the corner, his hand finding the scalpel in his pocket—a pathetic weapon against spirits.

A jar fell. It shattered on the concrete floor.

The skin inside did not stay still.

It crawled. A patch of translucent flesh, no bigger than a dinner plate, dragging itself across the floor by some impossible mechanism, leaving a trail of preservative fluid behind it. It moved like a slug, like a living thing that had been waiting for this moment.

It stopped at Fang's feet.

Then it spoke.

*"You took my face, Doctor."*

The voice came from the skin itself, vibrating through the floor, through the air, through Fang's bones. It was a woman's voice. Young. Sad. Hungry.

"Lin Mei," Fang whispered.

*"You took my face and gave it to that woman. The one with the pearls. The one who laughed at my funeral."*

Fang remembered. The mayor's wife had been at a charity event. Someone had mentioned the drowning of a university student. She had laughed. Said something about natural selection.

He had told her that story. He had told her where the skin came from.

*"I watched her wear me. I watched her smile in my skin. I watched her kiss her husband with my lips."* The skin twitched, curling at the edges. *"And I could do nothing. Because you trapped me in a jar."*

Another jar fell. And another.

The floor became a sea of crawling flesh. Patches of skin from a dozen different bodies, all moving toward him, all whispering, all remembering.

Fang pressed his back against the wall. The scalpel was useless. The door was locked. The Taoist was gone.

"I'm sorry," he said.

The skin stopped moving.

*"What?"*

"I'm sorry." Fang's voice cracked. "I thought... I thought I was helping people. I thought the living mattered more than the dead. I thought—"

*"You thought wrong."*

The skin lunged.

It hit him in the chest, wet and cold and suddenly heavy. It wrapped around his torso, clinging, pulling. Fang screamed and grabbed at it, but his fingers slipped through it like water. It was not solid. It was not liquid. It was something in between, something that had learned to move like a living thing.

*"Now you'll wear us,"* the voice said. *"Now you'll know what it feels like to be covered in someone else's death."*

More skin joined the first. They crawled up his legs, over his arms, across his face. Fang felt them pressing against his eyes, his mouth, his nostrils. He couldn't breathe. He couldn't see. He was being buried alive in the faces of the dead.

Then the door at the top of the stairs opened.

"Enough."

The voice was not the Taoist's.

It was a woman's voice. Familiar. Impossible.

The skin stopped moving.

Fang pulled it away from his face, gasping for air. He looked up the stairs.

A woman stood in the doorway. She was young. Early twenties. Dressed in jeans and a simple blouse, her hair pulled back in a ponytail. She looked like a college student, like someone who had stumbled into the wrong building.

But her face was perfect.

Too perfect.

It was the face of someone who had never had a scar. Never had a blemish. Never had a single day of stress or sadness or pain.

It was a face that had never been lived in.

"Who are you?" Fang asked.

The woman smiled. It was a practiced smile, the kind you learn from watching other people.

"My name is Li Na," she said. "And I'm one of your patients."

Fang stared at her. He didn't recognize her. He had performed thousands of procedures. But something about her face was wrong. The proportions were slightly off. The skin was too tight around the eyes.

"Don't you remember?" She stepped down into the basement. The skin on the floor parted to let her pass, pressing itself against the walls like a crowd making way for royalty. "You did my face three years ago. I was the first one. The prototype."

Fang's blood went cold.

The first one.

The woman who had come to him before he had perfected the procedure. The woman whose face he had pieced together from four different spirits because he hadn't known how to keep a single skin alive.

"Li Na died," he said. "The procedure failed. Her body rejected the skin. She—"

"She didn't die." Li Na stopped in front of him, her perfect face inches from his. "She just stopped being Li Na. The skin took over. The spirits inside her started talking. And eventually, they learned to share."

She reached up and touched her own cheek.

"Three spirits live in this face, Doctor. Three women who died before their time. And they've been very patient. They've been waiting for me to find you." She smiled again. It was not her smile. It was a collaboration. "We have so much to discuss."

The skin on the floor began to crawl up the walls, covering the fluorescent lights, plunging the basement into darkness.

And somewhere in the darkness, the refrigerator door began to open.
