CHAPTER SIXTEEN: The Refrigerator Opens

The vibration started as a low hum, barely perceptible beneath the fluorescence of the operating room lights. Then it grew. A rhythmic thumping, like a heartbeat amplified through concrete. The floor trembled. The instruments on the tray rattled against each other.

Fang knew that sound.

He'd heard it every night for the past six months, alone in his office, when the clinic went dark and the cleaners had gone home. The refrigerator in the basement had a pulse. He'd told himself it was the compressor. The coolant cycling through the pipes. The natural expansion and contraction of metal under temperature stress.

But he'd never believed it.

The Taoist's beads stopped clicking. His hand dropped from Mrs. Chen's face. He turned toward the floor, toward the source of the vibration, and his expression shifted. Not fear. Something older. Something that recognized the sound the way a soldier recognizes artillery.

"How many?" the Taoist asked.

Fang's throat had gone dry. "Thirty-seven."

"Thirty-seven containers. Thirty-seven spirits. And you kept them all in one place?"

"They were organized by date of harvest. Alphabetical by patient name. I have a cataloging system."

"A cataloging system." The Taoist laughed. It was not a happy laugh. "You built a library of stolen faces and you're proud of the Dewey decimal system." He grabbed Fang's wrist. "We have to move. Now."

"Move where? The refrigerator is in the basement. We're on the third floor."

"We need to get to the roof. High ground. Running water. Preferably a place with a clear exit route."

Mrs. Chen sat up on the operating table. Her new face—the face of the girl from the karaoke bar—had stopped weeping. It was listening. The eyes, still closed, tracked toward the door like a dog catching a scent.

"They're coming," she said. The voice was different now. Less human. More layered, like multiple people speaking through the same throat. "They're coming, and they're hungry."

"Shut up," the Taoist snapped. "You're not helping."

"I'm not trying to help. I'm trying to warn you. They don't just want their skins back. They want to wear yours."

The vibration intensified. Somewhere below, glass shattered. Then another. Then a cascade of breaking containers, one after another, like dominoes falling in a gallery of porcelain.

Fang's phone buzzed. Then again. Then a third time, the messages coming faster than he could read them. He glanced at the screen. Fifteen new notifications. All from patients. All with the same subject line: *It's moving.*

He opened one. A video. His patient—Mrs. Zhang, sixty-eight years old, post-procedure day four—staring into her bathroom mirror. Her new face was perfect. Smooth. Youthful. But her reflection was not.

The reflection was screaming.

Not her. The reflection. The skin on her mirror-double's face was peeling back in strips, revealing muscle and bone beneath. And beneath that, something else. Something that did not belong in a human body.

"Doctor Fang, please." Mrs. Zhang's voice was barely a whisper. "Please tell me what's happening. Please tell me this is normal."

The video ended.

Fang's hand trembled. He dropped the phone.

"Those women," he said. "My patients. The spirits are reaching them through the skin grafts. They're—"

"Possessing them," the Taoist finished. "Yes. That's what happens when you glue a ghost to a living person's face. The ghost learns to drive."

Mrs. Chen stood up. Her movements were wrong. Too fluid. Like a puppet whose strings had been oiled. She walked to the door, her bare feet leaving wet prints on the tile.

"I'm going downstairs," she said. "I want to see my sisters."

"Sisters?" Fang's voice cracked.

"The other faces. The ones you took. We were together in the dark for so long. We shared stories. We shared memories. We shared the pain of being cut away from our bodies." She paused at the threshold. "We're going to be together again. And then we're going to find our bodies. And then we're going to make you watch as we put ourselves back together."

She left.

The vibration from below stopped.

Silence. Thick and heavy and wrong.

"What happens now?" Fang asked.

The Taoist was already moving, grabbing Fang's arm, pulling him toward the stairwell. "Now we run. We get to the roof. We call for backup."

"Backup? You have backup?"

"I have colleagues. Other Taoists. Spirit hunters. People who deal with this kind of thing." He pushed open the stairwell door. "But they're an hour away. And we don't have an hour."

They descended. Two floors. The stairwell was dim, the emergency lights casting long shadows. Fang's footsteps echoed. The Taoist's beads clicked. The air grew colder with each step.

On the landing between the second and first floors, they stopped.

The door to the basement was open.

Light spilled out. Pale. Blue. The color of a dead television screen.

And from within, a sound. Wet. Slopping. Like someone walking through a puddle of mud.

"No," Fang whispered.

"Yes," the Taoist said. "They're out. All of them."

They moved past the basement door. Carefully. Quietly. Fang caught a glimpse of the interior. The refrigerator door was open. The titanium racks were empty. The containers—glass jars, plastic tubs, vacuum-sealed bags—lay scattered across the floor, their contents spilled.

The skins were gone.

But the skins had never been just skins. They'd been anchors. Vessels. The physical remnants of spirits that had been trapped between worlds, waiting for someone to guide them across.

Fang hadn't guided them. He'd harvested them. He'd bottled them. He'd sold them to wealthy women who wanted to look young again.

And now they were free.

They reached the first floor. The reception area was dark. The front doors were locked. Through the glass, Fang could see the street outside. Empty. Silent. The streetlights flickered.

"The power grid," the Taoist said. "They're affecting the electrical systems. They're drawing energy from the city."

"Can they do that?"

"They can do whatever they want. They're spirits who've been trapped in cold storage for months. They're angry. They're hungry. And they've had nothing to do but think about revenge."

The front doors rattled.

Not from outside. From inside.

Something was pushing against them from the other side. Something that wanted to get out.

"We can't go that way," the Taoist said. "Roof. Now."

They turned. The stairwell door was still open. But the light from the basement had grown. It was spreading up the stairs, crawling across the walls like a slow tide.

And from within the light, shapes began to form.

Faces.

Dozens of faces, pressed against an invisible membrane, pushing outward, desperate to break through.

"You can see them?" Fang asked.

"I can see them," the Taoist said. "You should be able to see them too. They're connected to you. You handled their skins. You cut them. You preserved them. You're part of them now."

Fang looked at his hands. The hands that had performed hundreds of procedures. The hands that had sliced through spirit flesh and sewn it onto living bone.

For the first time, he noticed the marks.

Thin lines. Pale. Raised. They ran across his palms, his fingers, his wrists. Like scars. But he'd never been cut there.

"They're inside you," the Taoist said. "Every time you touched a skin, a piece of the spirit entered your bloodstream. You've been carrying them with you for months."

"That's impossible."

"Is it? Look at your reflection."

Fang turned. The window beside the reception desk reflected the room. It reflected him.

But the reflection was wrong.

His face was moving. Not his real face—the reflection. It was smiling. But he was not.

"I'm going to ask you a question," the Taoist said. "And I need you to answer honestly. Did you ever wear one of the skins? Did you ever put one on?"

Fang's memory flickered. A night. Six months ago. Alone in his office. A jar on his desk. The skin of a young woman who'd died in a car accident. Perfect. Unblemished. He'd held it up to the light. He'd wondered what it would feel like to wear it. To be someone else for just a moment.

He'd pressed it against his face.

Just for a second.

Just to see.

"That's a yes," the Taoist said. "That's a definite yes."

The reflection in the window stopped smiling. It leaned forward. Its face pressed against the glass from the inside, distorting, stretching.

"You wore me," it said. "You wore me, and you liked it."

The lights went out.

Complete darkness. The kind that had weight. The kind that pressed against your eyes and filled your ears with the sound of your own heartbeat.

"Don't move," the Taoist whispered. "Don't speak. Don't breathe if you can help it."

Something touched Fang's shoulder.

Cold. Fingertips. Five of them.

He felt them press into his skin. He felt them grip his collarbone. He felt them pull.

The lights flickered back on.

The Taoist was gone.

The stairwell door was closed.

And standing in front of Fang, wearing a face he recognized, was a woman.

She was young. Early twenties. Long black hair. A mole above her left eyebrow.

She was the first one. The very first one. The girl from the karaoke bar. The one whose skin had started this whole nightmare.

"Hello, Doctor Fang," she said. Her mouth moved. But the voice came from everywhere. From the walls. From the ceiling. From inside his own skull. "I told you I'd come back."

She reached up and touched her face. Her fingers found the edge of her jaw. She began to peel.

The skin came away in one piece. Clean. Perfect. She held it in her hands like a mask she'd just removed.

Underneath, there was nothing.

No muscle. No bone. No blood.

Just a void. A darkness so complete it seemed to swallow the light around it.

"This is what you left me," she said. "This is what I've been wearing in the dark. Nothing."

She dropped the skin. It landed on the floor with a wet slap.

"Now I want yours."

The lights went out again.

And from somewhere in the darkness, Fang heard the Taoist's voice. Distant. Muffled. Like he was speaking through water.

"Dr. Fang. Don't let her touch your face. Whatever you do—"

The voice cut off.

And the darkness began to move.
