CHAPTER 17: The Collection Speaks

The vibration started as a low hum, barely perceptible beneath the fluorescent hum of the clinic's lights. But within seconds, it grew into something else. Something rhythmic. Like thirty-seven hearts beating in unison.

Fang felt it in his bones first. The floor beneath his feet began to tremble. The surgical instruments on the tray clattered against each other. The glass cabinet behind him rattled, and he watched as a small vial of anesthetic walked itself to the edge of the shelf and fell, shattering on the linoleum.

The Taoist's beads clicked faster. His eyes never left the floor. "They know we're here."

"Impossible." Fang's voice cracked. "They're just skins. Preserved tissue. Nothing living."

"Nothing living?" The Taoist laughed without humor. "You've been harvesting the dead, Doctor. But you don't understand what you've been harvesting from. A spirit doesn't stop existing just because you peel away its vessel. You've been keeping their faces in jars like trophies. And trophies remember."

Mrs. Chen's face—the borrowed face—began to sing. It was a lullaby. High and thin and ancient. The melody crawled up the walls, seeped into the corners, found every crack in the room's silence.

"Stop that," Fang said.

"I can't." The face's lips moved independently of Mrs. Chen's jaw. "She's calling them. The one you killed first. The girl from the karaoke bar. She was young. She was strong. She's been waiting the longest."

The basement door was at the end of the hallway. Fang had reinforced it with a steel plate, installed a biometric lock, lined the interior with lead sheeting to block any kind of signal. It was his vault. His sanctuary. His grave.

Now it sounded like something was trying to claw its way out.

The Taoist grabbed Fang's arm. His grip was iron. "How many containers?"

"Thirty-seven."

"Total?"

"Thirty-seven patients. But some of them had multiple procedures. Touch-ups. Corrections. I kept extras."

"Extras." The Taoist's voice dropped to a whisper. "You kept extra skins. Like spares. Like—" He stopped. His eyes widened. "Oh, you fool. You absolute fool."

"What?"

"The binding doesn't just work one way. When a patient wears a spirit's skin, the spirit can feel through it. See through it. And if you kept extra pieces—" He turned toward the hallway. The banging had started now. Steady. Methodical. A fist against steel. "They've been building a map. Every time you cut a piece off and stored it separately, you gave them another anchor. Another point of connection. They don't just know where your basement is, Doctor. They know *everything*."

The banging stopped.

Then a voice came through the door. Clear. Calm. Familiar.

"Doctor Fang? It's Mrs. Patterson. From the Claymore procedure. Remember? You said I'd be beautiful again."

Fang remembered. Mrs. Patterson was sixty-three. She'd wanted to look forty. He'd given her the face of a twenty-eight-year-old accountant who'd died in a car accident. The skin had taken perfectly. She'd cried with joy.

"The Claymore skin was a gift," the voice continued. "But I think I'd like it back now."

The Taoist moved. Fast. Faster than a man his age should be able to move. He crossed the room, pulled a length of yellow silk from his sleeve, and pressed it against the doorframe. The silk glowed. The banging resumed, but it was muffled now. Distant.

"That won't hold them long," he said. "They're too close to the physical world. Too many anchors. You've been feeding them for three years, Doctor. Three years of stolen faces. They're not just spirits anymore. They're something else. Something hungry."

Fang's phone buzzed. Then again. Then again. Messages piled up. The screen filled with notifications.

He looked down.

*Doctor Fang, I think my face is trying to leave.*

*Doctor Fang, my reflection just waved at me. I didn't wave back.*

*Doctor Fang, she's talking to me. The girl in the skin. She says she knows you.*

*She says you told her it wouldn't hurt.*

The last message was a video. Fang opened it.

The video showed a woman—Mrs. Lin, he recognized her from the Tuesday procedure—sitting in her bathroom. Her face was calm. Her hands were in her lap. But her skin was moving. Rippling. Like something was trying to get out from underneath.

Then the skin on her cheek split open.

A mouth formed in the gap. A second mouth, smaller, younger, with teeth that didn't match Mrs. Lin's dental records. The mouth opened and spoke.

"Doctor Fang. I can see you through her eyes. She's looking at her phone. And you're looking back."

The video ended.

Fang dropped the phone. It clattered to the floor. The screen cracked, but the display still glowed, still showed the last frame of Mrs. Lin's face splitting open like a piece of rotten fruit.

"You see?" The Taoist's voice was quiet now. Almost gentle. "You've been wearing the dead. And the dead have been watching you back. Every time you looked at your work in the mirror, every time you admired a perfect graft, every time you touched their skin—they felt it. They've been patient. But patience ends."

"We can fix this." Fang's voice was desperate. "We can remove the skins. Reverse the procedures. I have—there are techniques—"

"There are no techniques." The Taoist shook his head. "You didn't graft skin, Doctor. You performed a possession. Surgical possession. The cleanest I've ever seen. The spirits didn't just attach to the flesh. They attached to the blood. The nerves. The soul. Your patients aren't wearing masks. They're sharing bodies with the dead."

"Then what do we do?"

The Taoist was silent for a long moment. The banging on the basement door had stopped. But the silence was worse. It meant they were planning.

"I can sever the bonds," he said finally. "But it will kill the spirits. Completely. Permanently. They won't cross over. They won't reincarnate. They'll just stop existing."

"And the patients?"

"They'll live. But they'll know. They'll remember everything. The moments they shared a body. The thoughts that weren't theirs. The memories of people they never met." He looked at Fang. "Some of them won't survive the knowledge."

Fang thought of Mrs. Chen, still unconscious on the table. He thought of Mrs. Lin, sitting in her bathroom with a second mouth in her cheek. He thought of the other thirty-four. The ones who hadn't called yet. The ones who were still unaware that they carried the dead inside them.

"Do it," he said.

The Taoist studied him. "You understand what you're asking? You're asking me to destroy thirty-seven souls."

"I'm asking you to save thirty-seven living people."

"Semantics." But the Taoist nodded. He reached into his robe and pulled out a small brass bell. It was tarnished, old, covered in symbols Fang didn't recognize. "I'll need access to your collection. Every piece. Every jar. Every scrap of skin you've hoarded."

"The basement."

"I know." The Taoist walked to the door. He paused with his hand on the frame. "When this is over, Doctor Fang, you will answer for what you've done. Not to me. Not to the law. To them. The ones you killed. The ones whose faces you stole. They will have their say."

Fang nodded. He didn't have words.

The Taoist opened the door.

The hallway was empty. But the air was thick. Heavy. Like walking through water. The lights flickered. The floor was wet, but Fang couldn't tell with what. Water? Blood? Something else?

The basement door was at the end of the hall. It looked normal. Steel. Biometric lock. Lead-lined. But the symbols on the Taoist's silk had burned themselves into the frame, glowing faintly, like embers.

"They've been trying to get through," the Taoist said. "But they're disorganized. Too many voices. Too many agendas. The girl from the karaoke bar—she's the strongest. She's been leading them. But the others are fighting for control."

"How do you know?"

"Because I can hear them." The Taoist tapped his ear. "Can't you?"

Fang listened. At first, nothing. Then, beneath the hum of the lights, beneath the drip of whatever was wetting the floor, he heard it. Whispers. Dozens of them. Overlapping. Arguing. Pleading. Demanding.

*He took my eyes.*

*He took my smile.*

*He took my youth.*

*He took my death.*

The voices grew louder as they approached the basement door. The silk on the frame was smoking now, curling at the edges. The symbols flickered.

"Hurry," Fang said.

The Taoist placed his palm against the door. The biometric lock beeped twice, then clicked open. The door swung inward.

The basement was cold. Colder than it should have been. The refrigerator hummed in the corner, its glass door fogged with ice. Inside, the jars sat in neat rows. Thirty-seven of them. Each one labeled. Each one containing a face.

But the faces were not still.

They pressed against the glass. They moved. They twisted. They opened their eyes.

One jar—the one marked "Karaoke Bar, 2019"—vibrated so hard that the glass cracked. A thin line spiderwebbed across the surface, and the face inside smiled.

"Hello, Doctor."

Fang took a step back.

"You've been busy," the face said. Its voice came from everywhere. From the jar. From the walls. From the space behind Fang's eyes. "But we've been busy too. Did you know that your patients can hear us now? All of them. We've been talking. We've been telling them what you did. How you found us. How you cut us. How you wore us."

"I didn't wear you." Fang's voice was barely a whisper. "I gave you to others."

"Same thing. We were still being worn. Still being used. Still being seen in mirrors that didn't belong to us." The crack in the jar widened. A thin trickle of liquid escaped, ran down the shelf, dripped onto the floor. "But you know what the worst part is, Doctor? The worst part is that we liked it. At first. Being seen again. Being beautiful again. Being alive again."

"Then why—"

"Because we remembered. Eventually. We remembered that we were dead. And being dead isn't so bad when you're at peace. But you took our peace. You took our faces. And now we want them back."

The jar shattered.

Liquid exploded across the basement, and the face—the face of a young woman, no older than twenty-two, with dark hair and dark eyes and a smile that was all wrong—flew through the air. It landed on the floor, wet and glistening, and began to crawl.

The Taoist rang the bell.

The sound was sharp. Clean. It cut through the whispers like a blade. The face stopped moving. The other jars went still.

"Enough," the Taoist said. "You will be silent."
