📖 The Merchant Prince's Second Life by Wei Lin

Chapter 4: The First Card Falls

~1.6k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The morning sun painted Huangling Town in shades of amber and dust. Zhao Feng stood at the mouth of an alley, watching the Xuan family's medicine shop open for business. Old man Xuan shuffled to the door, his apprentice trailing behind with buckets of water for the morning cleansing.

Forty years ago, Zhao Feng had walked past this shop a hundred times without a second glance. Now he saw everything.

The faded paint on the signboard. The crack in the wooden counter. The way Xuan's hands trembled when he counted coins.

*Desperation*, Zhao Feng thought. *The smell of a business drowning slowly.*

He stepped out of the alley.

"You're open early, Master Xuan."

The old man looked up, squinting. Recognition flickered, then confusion. The Zhao family's disgraced son, dressed in patched robes, standing before his shop like he owned it.

"Zhao Feng." Xuan's voice carried the careful neutrality of a merchant who didn't want trouble. "I heard about your father's decision. Unfortunate."

"Fortunate, actually." Zhao Feng smiled. "It gave me freedom."

Xuan's apprentice snorted. "Freedom to starve in the streets."

Zhao Feng ignored him. He reached into his sleeve and pulled out a sheet of paper, yellowed and covered in dense characters. He placed it on the counter.

"I have a proposal."

Xuan glanced at the paper. His eyebrows rose slightly. "This is... a supply contract."

"For the next twelve months. I'll provide your shop with three times your current stock of common healing herbs, at seventy percent of your current wholesale price. You sell them at your standard rate. Keep the difference."

Silence.

The apprentice stopped scrubbing. Xuan's hands had stopped trembling.

"Three times the stock," Xuan repeated slowly. "At seventy percent cost. How?"

"I have connections you don't."

"Connections." Xuan's eyes narrowed. "You're eighteen years old, Zhao Feng. Your family just threw you out. What connections could you possibly have?"

Zhao Feng leaned forward. "The same connections that tell me your shop has been losing money for eight months. That your primary supplier, the Liu family, raised their prices after your daughter refused their son's marriage proposal. That you've been bleeding coin trying to keep this place open for her sake."

Xuan's face went pale.

The apprentice dropped his bucket.

"How..." Xuan's voice cracked. "How could you know that?"

*Because I watched your daughter die in my past life*, Zhao Feng thought. *Because the Liu family crushed you, and you hanged yourself in the back room three months from now. Because I remember everything.*

"I know many things," Zhao Feng said instead. "I know you have seventeen silver taels remaining in your emergency fund. I know you've been skipping meals to afford your daughter's medicine. I know you think you have no options left."

He tapped the contract.

"But you do. Sign this, and I'll have the first delivery at your door by sunset tomorrow. One month of payment upfront. If I fail to deliver, you owe me nothing."

Xuan stared at the paper. His fingers twitched toward it, then pulled back.

"Why?" he whispered. "Why help a stranger?"

"Because you're not a stranger anymore." Zhao Feng extended his hand. "You're my first business partner."

---

The afternoon passed in a blur of negotiations.

Zhao Feng walked through every street of Huangling Town, his feet carrying him to shops he'd memorized in another life. The ironmonger whose son died in a mining accident. The herbalist who'd been cheated by his brother. The innkeeper whose wife had run away with a traveling merchant.

Each had a wound. Each had a need.

And Zhao Feng offered solutions.

By evening, he had signed contracts with six businesses. Not for cash—he had barely three silver taels to his name—but for future shares. Promises of profit. A percentage of revenue in exchange for stock that would flow through his hands.

His past life had taught him one immutable truth: *money moved through trust, not through gold.*

Trust was a currency he could mint from nothing.

---

Dusk fell. Zhao Feng sat in his rented room above the noodle shop, counting the contracts spread across his thin mattress.

*Six shops. Six supply lines. A web that could grow.*

He reached for his brush to calculate the numbers, but stopped.

A knock at the door.

Three beats. Pause. Two more.

Zhao Feng's blood ran cold.

That rhythm. He knew that rhythm.

He rose slowly, sliding the contracts beneath his pillow. His hand found the knife he'd hidden under the loose floorboard.

"Who is it?"

"Open the door, brother."

The voice was smooth. Young. Familiar in a way that made Zhao Feng's stomach turn.

He opened the door.

Zhao Liang stood in the hallway, oil lamp in hand, his shadow stretching long and thin across the cracked wooden floor. He was seventeen, one year younger than Zhao Feng. Handsome in that soft way their mother had favored, with eyes that always seemed to be calculating.

In the past life, Zhao Liang had been the first to betray him.

"Father sent me," Zhao Liang said, smiling. "He wanted me to check on his disappointing son. Make sure you haven't disgraced the family name any further."

Zhao Feng didn't move. "Tell Father I'm thriving."

"Thriving?" Zhao Liang laughed. "You're renting a room above a noodle shop. I can smell the grease from here."

"Smell doesn't bother me. Neither does poverty."

"Bold words for a man with nothing."

Zhao Feng met his brother's eyes. In his past life, he'd missed the cruelty hiding behind that smile. Missed the hunger. The jealousy. The quiet satisfaction when Zhao Liang had finally driven a knife into his back.

*Not this time.*

"I have something better than money," Zhao Feng said. "I have time."

Zhao Liang's smile flickered. "Time for what?"

"To rebuild. To remember. To make sure that when I rise again, everyone who laughed at me will be the ones begging."

Silence.

The oil lamp flickered.

"You've changed," Zhao Liang said slowly, studying him with new eyes. "Exile suits you."

"It's not exile. It's opportunity."

Zhao Liang's lips curled. "Father was right. You've lost your mind."

"Perhaps." Zhao Feng stepped back, preparing to close the door. "But madmen are the only ones who change the world."

He shut the door. Locked it. Leaned against the wood, heart pounding.

*The first card has fallen*, he thought. *Now the game begins.*

---

He worked through the night.

By lamplight, Zhao Feng mapped out his plan on a scrap of parchment. The names of every merchant in Huangling Town. Their connections. Their debts. Their weaknesses.

Six contracts meant six supply lines. Six supply lines meant leverage. Leverage meant power.

But power required capital.

He had three silver taels. Enough to buy a cart and a mule. Enough to travel to the outer villages where farmers sold their herbs for pennies, then turn around and sell them in town for silver.

A simple plan. A merchant's plan.

But in this world, merchants were kings.

He dipped his brush, wrote a single line at the bottom of the page:

*The Qi Stone Exchange opens in two months. I will be there.*

Outside, a dog barked. Wind rattled the windows.

Zhao Feng looked up.

Shadows moved in the street below. Two of them. Maybe three.

*Watching.*

He extinguished the lamp. Moved to the window, pressing himself against the wall, peering through a crack in the shutters.

The figures had stopped. One pointed at his window.

*Zhao Liang*, Zhao Feng thought. *You always did move fast.*

But he'd expected this. The Zhao family wouldn't let him escape so easily. They'd want to ensure he stayed broken. They'd send watchers to track his movements, report his failures, wait for him to fall.

*Let them watch.*

He smiled in the darkness.

*Let them report how their disgraced son rises from the gutter.*

*Let them see the empire I build.*

*And when the time comes, let them realize too late that they helped me build it.*

He settled onto his mattress, the contracts tucked beneath his pillow, the knife within reach.

Tomorrow, he would buy a cart.

Tomorrow, he would visit the villages.

Tomorrow, the game continued.

But tonight, he slept with the smile of a man who had already won.

---

Dawn came gray and cold.

Zhao Feng woke to a pounding on his door.

He sat up, knife already in hand.

"Zhao Feng!" A woman's voice. Desperate. "Zhao Feng, please!"

He recognized it. The innkeeper's wife from two streets over. The one whose husband had beaten her.

He opened the door.

She stood there, face tear-streaked, clutching a crumpled piece of paper.

"They took him," she sobbed. "They took my husband."

Zhao Feng's stomach dropped.

"Who?"

"The Liu family." She thrust the paper into his hands. "They said he owes them. Said if you want him back, you need to come alone. Before noon. To the abandoned warehouse by the river."

Zhao Feng read the message. His hands didn't tremble.

*The Liu family*, he thought. *Already moving against me.*

He looked at the woman. At her tear-streaked face. At the fear in her eyes.

This was a trap. A clear, obvious trap.

But if he didn't go, he'd lose every contract he'd signed. Every merchant who'd trusted him would see him as a coward. His empire would die before it was born.

He folded the paper, tucked it into his sleeve.

"Wait here," he said.

He turned, grabbed his knife, and walked out the door.

*The first card has fallen*, he thought as he stepped into the morning light. *Now I see what the Liu family holds.*
