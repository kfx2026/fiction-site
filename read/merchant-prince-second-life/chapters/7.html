📖 The Merchant Prince's Second Life by Wei Lin

Chapter 7: The Shadow in the Market

~1.8k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The morning sun cast long shadows through the merchant district as Zhao Feng made his way toward the central market. Three days had passed since Xiao Chen had taken the pouch. Three days of watching, waiting, and preparing.

The boy had proven useful. More than useful.

Each night, Xiao Chen would slip through the crack in the warehouse wall—a gap so narrow that Zhao Feng himself couldn't fit through—and report what he'd learned. Wu Chen's schedule. His contacts. The way he lingered too long at the teahouse across from the auction house, watching the flow of qi stones with hungry eyes.

But last night's report had been different.

"There's another man," Xiao Chen had said, his voice barely a whisper in the darkness. "Dressed in silk. Comes to see Wu Chen after midnight. They argue."

"What about?"

"Couldn't get close enough. But I heard one name. 'Lin.'"

Zhao Feng's blood had frozen. Lin. The same family that had backed Wu Chen in his past life. The same family that had bought Zhao Feng's first warehouse out from under him, leaving him scrambling for scraps.

They were moving early.

Now, as he walked through the market, Zhao Feng kept his eyes open and his expression neutral. The stalls were busy with morning trade—vegetables, meats, cheap talismans for warding off evil spirits. Nothing of real value. Nothing worth his attention.

He was looking for something else.

The Immortal Ascension Tournament was six months away, but the preparations had already begun. In his past life, he'd learned about the hidden markets that operated beneath the city's surface. Places where forbidden techniques were traded, where cultivators desperate for power sold their services, where information could be bought for the right price.

The entrance was supposed to be in the back of a noodle shop.

He found it easily enough. A faded wooden sign, a stooped old man stirring a pot of broth, the smell of cheap noodles wafting through the air. Nothing remarkable.

"Bowl of noodles," Zhao Feng said, sliding onto a worn stool.

The old man didn't look up. "Five copper coins."

Zhao Feng placed the coins on the counter, but when the old man reached for them, Zhao Feng's hand moved faster. He pressed a second coin—a qi stone, low-grade but real—on top of the coppers.

"I'm looking for the back room," he said quietly.

The old man's eyes flickered up. His gaze was sharp, assessing. "Who sent you?"

"No one. I found my own way."

A long silence. The old man's hand closed around the coins, including the qi stone. "Eat your noodles first. Then walk to the back. Don't look at anyone. Don't stop."

Zhao Feng nodded and ate the bland noodles in silence. When he finished, he stood and walked toward the back of the shop, pushing through a curtain of beads into a narrow hallway.

The hallway led to a door. Behind the door, stairs that descended into darkness.

He counted twenty-seven steps before the stairs ended and a new world opened before him.

The underground market was massive. Torches lined the walls, casting flickering shadows across stalls piled high with spirit herbs, beast cores, and cultivation manuals. The air was thick with the smell of incense and something metallic—blood, maybe, or qi-rich minerals. People moved through the aisles with their heads down, their faces hidden behind hoods and masks.

This was where the real business happened.

Zhao Feng moved slowly, cataloging everything he saw. A stall selling maps of spirit beast territories. Another offering "guaranteed breakthrough" pills that were probably poison. A third displaying spirit swords that gleamed with barely contained power.

He stopped at the fourth stall.

It was unremarkable—just a wooden table covered in a black cloth, with a few scattered manuals on display. But the manuals caught his attention. Not because of their quality, but because of their titles.

*Foundations of Lightning Cultivation.*
*The Thunder Heart Method.*
*Conducting the Heavenly Current.*

In his past life, these exact manuals had been sold to a young woman six months before the tournament. She'd used them to develop techniques that had stunned the entire cultivation world. The major families had fought over her, offering fortunes, land, marriage alliances.

But she'd refused them all. She'd disappeared after the tournament, taking her secrets with her.

Zhao Feng had never learned her name. He'd only heard rumors—a girl from the outer disciples, nobody special, nobody important. Until she'd stepped onto that stage and changed everything.

He reached for the first manual.

"Those aren't for sale."

The voice came from behind the stall. A woman emerged from the shadows, her face partially hidden by a wide-brimmed hat. She was young—maybe twenty-five—with sharp features and eyes that seemed to glow faintly in the torchlight.

"I'm just looking," Zhao Feng said.

"Looking costs nothing. Touching costs your hand."

He pulled his hand back slowly. "Then let's talk. I'm interested in buying."

The woman's lips curved into a thin smile. "You can't afford them."

"Name your price."

"Fifty thousand spirit coins. Each."

Zhao Feng kept his expression neutral, but his mind was racing. Fifty thousand was more than he'd made in the past month combined. More than he had in total assets. But he knew what those manuals were worth. In the right hands, with the right student, they could change the balance of power in the entire region.

"I don't have that kind of money," he said. "But I can offer you something better."

"And what's that?"

"Information."

The woman laughed. It was a cold sound, like ice cracking. "Everyone has information. Few have fifty thousand spirit coins."

"Everyone has information," Zhao Feng agreed. "But not everyone knows that the Lin family is already moving on the Immortal Ascension Tournament. Not everyone knows that they've been buying up the lightning-type techniques for the past three months, trying to corner the market."

The woman's smile faded.

"You're lying."

"Am I? Check with your contacts. See who's been asking questions about lightning cultivation. See who's been visiting the auction houses with Lin family tokens."

He turned to leave.

"Wait."

He stopped.

"Who are you?" the woman asked.

"Someone who wants to make a deal. I can't buy your manuals today. But I can give you something else. Protection."

"Protection from what?"

"From the people who will come looking for you once they know what you have."

The woman studied him for a long moment. Then she reached under the table and pulled out a small jade slip—a storage device, used for transferring information.

"Take this," she said. "It contains the first chapter of the Thunder Heart Method. Proof that I have what I claim."

Zhao Feng took the slip. It was warm in his hand, pulsing with qi.

"Come back when you have something real to offer," the woman said. "Not just words."

He nodded and turned away, slipping the jade into his sleeve. Behind him, he heard the woman's voice again.

"And boy? If you're lying about the Lin family, I'll find you. And I'll make you wish you'd never been born."

Zhao Feng didn't look back.

---

He emerged from the noodle shop into the bright morning light, blinking against the sudden glare. The jade slip was a heavy weight in his sleeve, a promise of power that was still out of reach.

But he had a lead. A connection. A path forward.

He was halfway back to the warehouse when Xiao Chen appeared at his side, materializing from the crowd like a ghost.

"Wu Chen is moving," the boy said, his voice urgent. "He's got men. They're heading for your warehouse."

Zhao Feng's stride didn't falter. "How many?"

"Six. Maybe seven. They're carrying weapons."

"Where is the warehouse guard?"

"Dead. Found him this morning. Throat cut."

Zhao Feng's jaw tightened. He'd hired the guard three days ago—a retired cultivator with decent skills. Not a friend. Not someone he cared about. But dead meant someone was sending a message.

"Take me there. But stay hidden."

They moved through the alleys, keeping to the shadows. When they reached the edge of the merchant district, Zhao Feng stopped and surveyed the warehouse from a distance.

Xiao Chen was right. Seven men surrounded the building, their postures tense, their hands resting on weapons. They weren't trying to be subtle. They wanted to be seen.

And standing in the middle of them, arms crossed, was Wu Chen.

Zhao Feng's old enemy looked younger in this life—fresher, less worn down by years of scheming. But the eyes were the same. Cold. Calculating. Hungry.

"Zhao Feng!" Wu Chen called out, his voice carrying across the empty street. "I know you're watching. Come out. Let's talk."

Zhao Feng considered his options. He could run. Let Wu Chen take the warehouse, the qi stones, everything. Start over somewhere else.

But that wasn't who he was anymore.

He stepped out of the shadows.

"Wu Chen. I wasn't expecting a visit."

Wu Chen smiled. It didn't reach his eyes. "I heard you've been doing well for yourself. Making deals. Buying property. Very impressive for a nobody from the outer districts."

"I've had good teachers."

"I'm sure you have." Wu Chen's smile widened. "Which is why I'm here with an offer. A generous one."

"Let's hear it."

"Join me. Work for the Lin family. You've got talent, boy. I can see it. With our resources behind you, you could go far."

"And if I refuse?"

Wu Chen's hand moved, and one of the men pulled out a small leather pouch. He tossed it at Zhao Feng's feet. It landed with a wet thud, and a red stain spread across the dirt.

Inside was a severed finger. A woman's finger, judging by the size. A jade ring still circled the middle knuckle.

Zhao Feng recognized the ring.

It belonged to Mei Ling—the herbalist who had supplied him with his first batch of spirit grass. The only merchant in the district who had treated him fairly.

His hands began to shake.

"That's just a warning," Wu Chen said. "The next one won't be a finger."

Zhao Feng looked up, and in that moment, all pretense of calm fell away. His eyes went cold, his voice dropping to a whisper that carried more threat than any scream.

"Where is she?"

Wu Chen's grin never faltered. "Safe. For now. You have until sunset to make your decision."

He turned and walked away, his men falling into formation around him. They disappeared into the maze of alleys, leaving Zhao Feng alone with a pouch full of blood and a dead guard's body.

Xiao Chen crept up beside him. "What do we do?"

Zhao Feng looked at the severed finger. At the warehouse. At the space where Wu Chen had stood.

"We burn everything," he said quietly. "And then we make him pay."
