Chapter 2: The Warden of the Forgotten Pit

~1.8k words · 2026-06-22

The pit stank of rot and despair.

Lin Xiao pressed his back against the damp stone wall, his lungs burning with each breath. The crippled wind-snake coiled around his wrist, its scales cold and lifeless. Above him, the circle of torchlight seemed impossibly far—fifty feet, maybe more. The Warden had thrown him here without a word, without a trial. Just a boot to the chest and the grinding sound of the iron grate slamming shut.

"You're dead, boy," a voice rasped from the darkness.

Lin Xiao's head snapped up. He couldn't see anything beyond the faint green glow of his own beast core—that pathetic, flickering mote of light that marked him as the weakest beast master in Thousand Beast Valley's history. The core pulsed weakly against his sternum, barely illuminating the grime-caked floor.

"Who's there?"

A wet laugh echoed through the pit. "Someone who made the same mistake you did. Trusted the wrong person. Challenged the wrong master."

The wind-snake stirred, its forked tongue brushing against Lin Xiao's thumb. *Danger,* it whispered into his mind—not with words, but with sensations. A beast master's bond worked that way when it was deep enough. Feelings, images, instincts. The snake had been broken for years, its core shattered by a hunting accident before Lin Xiao had even been born. But it still remembered how to fear.

"I'm not dead yet," Lin Xiao said, forcing steel into his voice.

"Yet." The voice chuckled. "That's the key word. The Warden doesn't throw people into the Forgotten Pit to have tea with them. This is where beast masters come to die. Slowly. Quietly. Until their cores go dark and their beasts abandon them."

Lin Xiao's hand drifted to his belt pouch. The lightning hawk feather was still there, tucked against his hip. He'd found it yesterday, moments before everything went wrong—a single iridescent feather, crackling with residual lightning energy, lying in the mud of the lower training grounds. He'd picked it up on instinct, feeling the faint pulse of the beast that had shed it.

In the distance, a bell tolled. Once. Twice. Three times.

"That's the evening watch," the voice said. "You've got until dawn. After that, the Warden sends in the scavengers."

"Scavengers?"

"Beasts that feed on broken cores. They'll smell yours like blood in water. And when they come—" The voice paused, and Lin Xiao heard the rattle of chains. "When they come, boy, you'd better hope you've made peace with whatever gods you worship."

Lin Xiao closed his eyes. His mother's face floated in his memory—that final look of disappointment before she'd turned her back on him. "A crippled snake for a crippled son," she'd said. "At least you match."

He'd believed her. For sixteen years, he'd believed her.

Then yesterday happened.

The lightning hawk feather pulsed against his fingers. Lin Xiao pulled it out, watching the faint blue sparks dance along its edges. The wind-snake raised its head, its broken eyes fixed on the feather.

*Hungry,* it sent.

"No," Lin Xiao whispered. "Not hungry. Resonant."

The word felt strange on his tongue. He'd never heard anyone else use it. The [Beast Resonance] talent had awakened in him during the attack—that moment when the Shadow Panther had lunged at him, and something had *clicked* inside his chest. For half a heartbeat, he'd felt the panther's thoughts, its instincts, its *power* flowing through him like a river of midnight silk.

But he didn't have the panther anymore. He had a crippled snake and a feather.

And a pit full of darkness and death.

"I need to get out," Lin Xiao muttered.

"Everyone needs to get out," the voice said. "Nobody does."

Lin Xiao ignored him. He pressed the feather against his beast core—that pitiful, flickering light—and concentrated. He didn't know what he was doing. No one had taught him. The valley's masters taught cultivation through meditation, through breathing techniques, through the slow accumulation of qi. They taught nothing about *resonance*.

But Lin Xiao had felt it. That connection. That merging.

He closed his eyes and *pushed*.

The feather grew hot. Then cold. Then it *dissolved* into his skin, and Lin Xiao gasped as lightning flooded his veins.

"WHAT WAS THAT?" the voice shouted.

Lin Xiao couldn't answer. His body was no longer his own.

The lightning hawk's memories crashed into him like a tidal wave—the feeling of riding thermals above the valley, the scent of rain on distant mountains, the *ecstasy* of a dive at terminal velocity. He felt its hunger, its loneliness, its rage at being trapped in a cage too small for its wings.

And he felt its power.

The beast core in his chest flared. Not the weak, flickering light of before—but a brilliant, blinding *blue*. Lightning arced across his skin, dancing between his fingers, crackling in his hair. The wind-snake *screamed*—a high-pitched, keening sound—and wrapped itself around his arm so tightly he felt its scales cutting into his flesh.

*YES,* the snake sent. *YES YES YES.*

Lin Xiao opened his eyes.

The pit was no longer dark. He could see everything—the slick stone walls, the bones scattered across the floor, the figure in chains huddled against the far corner. The man was gaunt, bearded, his beast core barely a dim ember in his chest. He stared at Lin Xiao with wide, disbelieving eyes.

"How—" the man started.

Lin Xiao didn't answer. He was too busy understanding.

The lightning hawk's power was *his* now. Not borrowed. Not channeled. *His*. The feather had been shed weeks ago, but the beast's essence had lingered in it, waiting for someone who could receive it. Someone with [Beast Resonance].

Someone like him.

He looked up at the grate fifty feet above. The bars were iron, thick as his wrist, spaced just close enough to keep a man from squeezing through. But a man with lightning in his blood?

"Stay back," Lin Xiao said.

"What?"

"I said stay back."

He raised his hand, and lightning *answered*.

The bolt that erupted from his palm was not gentle. It was not controlled. It was raw, primal, *angry*—the fury of a caged hawk finally given wings. It struck the grate with a thunderclap that shook the pit, sending chunks of molten iron raining down around them.

The man in chains screamed and covered his head.

Lin Xiao fired again. And again. And again.

Each bolt was weaker than the last. The lightning hawk's power was finite—a single feather's worth of essence, not the full beast itself. He could feel it draining, the brilliant blue light in his core dimming with every discharge.

But it was enough.

On the fourth bolt, the grate shattered. Iron fragments clattered against the walls, and a circle of moonlight appeared above—the first light Lin Xiao had seen since they'd thrown him in.

"Impossible," the chained man whispered.

Lin Xiao turned to him. The man's eyes were wet. Not with tears—with *hope*. The desperate, terrible hope of someone who had given up decades ago and was suddenly forced to believe again.

"Who are you?" the man asked.

"Lin Xiao. Son of Lin Feng. Beast master of Thousand Beast Valley."

"No." The man shook his head. "That's not what I meant. *What* are you?"

Lin Xiao didn't have an answer. He could feel the lightning hawk's power fading, the essence almost spent. In a few minutes, he'd be back to his crippled snake and his flickering core.

But for now, he was something else.

"I don't know," he said honestly. "But I'm going to find out."

The man's chains rattled as he tried to stand. He couldn't—his legs were too weak, his muscles too atrophied from years in the dark. "Take me with you."

"I don't even know who you are."

"My name is Shen Wei. I was the Warden's apprentice. I know his secrets. His weaknesses. I know why he threw you in here." The man's voice cracked. "I know what he's *building*."

Lin Xiao hesitated. The wind-snake tightened around his arm, sending a pulse of warning. *Danger,* it sent again. *Lies.*

But the man's core was broken. He had no beasts left—Lin Xiao could sense their absence like missing teeth in a smile. He was no threat.

"Talk fast," Lin Xiao said.

Shen Wei licked his cracked lips. "The Warden isn't just a beast master. He's a *collector*. He's been hunting the valley's rarest beasts for years, caging them, breeding them, trying to create—"

A footstep scraped above them.

Lin Xiao looked up. A silhouette stood at the edge of the broken grate, backlit by the moon. Tall. Broad-shouldered. Wreathed in a cloak that seemed to drink the light.

"Lin Xiao," the Warden said, his voice a low rumble. "I was wondering when you'd stop pretending to be weak."

The lightning hawk's essence was almost gone. Lin Xiao could feel it flickering, dying, leaving him empty.

"I'm not weak," he said.

"No." The Warden's teeth gleamed in the darkness. "You're not. You're something far more interesting." He reached into his cloak and pulled out a cage—small, iron, humming with power. Inside, a creature stirred. Lin Xiao couldn't see it clearly, but he felt its presence. It was vast. Hungry. *Ancient*.

"You see," the Warden said, "I've been waiting for someone like you. Someone with the Resonance. The ability to merge with beasts." He smiled. "Do you know what happens when you merge a god with a mortal?"

The cage door swung open.

And the world went black.

When Lin Xiao's vision returned, he was on his knees. The pit was silent except for the ragged sound of his own breathing. Shen Wei was gone—the chains lay empty on the floor, the man vanished like smoke.

Above him, the Warden stood alone.

"Where—"

"Gone," the Warden said. "He served his purpose." He crouched at the edge of the broken grate, studying Lin Xiao like a merchant appraising livestock. "You have potential, boy. Raw. Unrefined. But potential nonetheless."

"What did you do to me?"

"I gave you a gift." The Warden's smile widened. "A parasite. It will live in your core, feeding on your cultivation, growing stronger with every beast you bond. And when it's ready..." He stood, turning away. "When it's ready, I'll come back for it."

Lin Xiao tried to stand. His legs wouldn't obey. He tried to call lightning—nothing. The wind-snake was limp around his arm, barely breathing.

"You can't fight it," the Warden said without looking back. "Resistance only makes it grow faster. So go ahead. Struggle. Bond with beasts. Grow stronger." He laughed, a cold, hollow sound. "Every step you take toward power is a step closer to me."

His boots echoed against the stone as he walked away.

Lin Xiao collapsed face-first into the mud, the taste of blood and failure filling his mouth. Something stirred in his core—a presence that wasn't his, a hunger that wasn't his own
