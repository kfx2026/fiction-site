📖 Doctor Reborn in the Apocalypse by Chen Wei

Chapter 1: The Day Before Hell

~1.5k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The needle went in smooth. Twenty years of practice made it that way.

Lin Mei watched the morphine drip into the IV line, her hands steady despite the blood matting her hair, the gash across her ribs, the way her left arm hung wrong at the shoulder. The warlord's men had done their work well enough. She'd still managed to drag herself to the infirmary.

"Colonel Zhang will recover," she said to no one. The tent flap hung open. Rain pattered against canvas. "His fever will break by dawn. The infection in his leg—"

The knife slid between her ribs from behind.

She knew the angle before she felt the steel. T5-T6 intercostal space. Direct path to the heart. Someone had studied anatomy.

"You've outlived your usefulness, Doctor."

Li Wei's voice. The man whose guts she'd stitched back into his belly three winters ago. The man she'd taught to suture. The man who'd called her *shifu* for six years.

Lin Mei fell forward. The IV stand clattered. Morphine pooled on the dirt floor.

"Colonel Zhang's orders," Li Wei said, and there was nothing in his voice. No regret. No anger. Just emptiness. "A new world needs new leadership. And you know too much about the old one."

She hit the ground face-first. The smell of mud and antiseptic filled her nose. Her blood spread in a dark fan beneath her chest.

*Twenty years.*

Twenty years of cutting into the living. Twenty years of pulling bullets from children's bellies, of amputating limbs with a hacksaw, of watching people die and die and die while the world burned around her. Twenty years of keeping the warlord alive.

And this was her reward.

Darkness took her. But in that darkness, she felt something strange.

Not peace. Not the void.

*Anger.*

A cold, crystalline fury that locked around her spine and pulled.

---

Her eyes snapped open.

The ceiling was white. Not canvas white. Paint white. With a water stain in the corner shaped vaguely like Hainan Island.

Lin Mei lay motionless. Her body felt strange. Wrong. Too light. The constant ache in her knees was gone. The scar tissue across her abdomen didn't pull when she breathed. Her left arm—

She lifted it. Rotated the shoulder. No grinding. No grating. No bone fragments shifting where they shouldn't.

*Impossible.*

She sat up. The room spun. A bedroom. Small. A desk with textbooks stacked neatly. A laptop she didn't recognize. Posters on the wall—medical diagrams, acupuncture points, a calendar from the China Medical University.

She looked at her hands.

Young. Smooth. No calluses. No burn scars from cauterizing wounds. No yellowed nicotine stains from the years she'd traded her surgical skills for cigarette rations.

Her heart hammered. She grabbed her phone from the nightstand.

*September 12, 2024.*

The numbers didn't change no matter how many times she blinked.

*September 12, 2024.*

The day before.

The day before the sky split open. The day before the qi storms rolled in from the Gobi Desert, turning the air to acid, driving every living thing mad. The day before the world ended.

She had one day.

Lin Mei moved on instinct. Twenty years of battlefield triage had burned away hesitation. She was on her feet before her conscious mind caught up, pulling on clothes, shoving her feet into sneakers.

The apartment was small. A one-bedroom in a Beijing suburb. Photographs on the wall showed her with parents she hadn't seen since the storms took Beijing. A diploma from Peking University, framed and hung with pride.

She grabbed a backpack from the closet. Empty. Good.

Her phone buzzed. A message from someone named "Mama": *Have you eaten? Don't forget your auntie's birthday dinner tomorrow.*

Tomorrow.

When the sky would burn.

Lin Mei typed back: *I'll be there.* A lie. But the last thing she needed was her mother calling the police for a wellness check while she was out preparing for the end of the world.

She left the apartment without looking back.

---

The pharmacy was a twenty-minute walk from her building. She knew exactly which one. She'd passed it a thousand times in her old life, back when she was just a resident, before the storms, before everything.

The bell above the door chimed as she entered.

A young woman at the counter looked up. "Can I help you?"

"Antibiotics," Lin Mei said. "The full shelf."

The woman blinked. "I'm sorry?"

Lin Mei pulled out her medical license. Still valid. Still current. In her old life, she'd learned that paperwork meant nothing after the storms. But before them? Paperwork was everything.

"I'm a doctor," she said. "There's been a contamination at the hospital. We need to stockpile. Emergency protocol."

The woman's eyes went wide. "I haven't heard anything—"

"You will." Lin Mei's voice carried the weight of twenty years of giving orders. "How fast can you fill the list I'm about to give you?"

The woman grabbed a notepad.

Lin Mei recited from memory. Amoxicillin. Ciprofloxacin. Metronidazole. Surgical gloves. Scalpels. Sutures, both dissolvable and non. Betadine. Alcohol wipes. Gauze. Bandages. The list went on.

In her old life, she'd learned the hard way that the first week after the storms was chaos. Hospitals would be overwhelmed within hours. Pharmacies would be looted by nightfall. The people who survived were the ones who prepared. And the people who thrived were the ones who knew how to treat the wounds the storms left behind.

"What about traditional medicine?" Lin Mei asked.

"The back wall. Herbal supplements."

Lin Mei walked past shelves of vitamins and protein powders to the section labeled *Chinese Herbal Medicine*. Dried ginseng. Astragalus. Goji berries. Reishi mushrooms. Things that would become useless in the new world.

But the herbs she needed weren't here.

She turned back to the counter. "Where's the nearest herb shop? The kind that sells raw ingredients."

"Dongcheng District. About forty minutes by subway."

"Good. Have the antibiotics ready by the time I get back. I'll pay cash."

The woman nodded, still looking dazed. "Doctor, what's really happening?"

Lin Mei paused at the door.

She wanted to tell her. Wanted to grab her by the shoulders and shake her and say *run, hide, stockpile, the world ends tomorrow and there's nothing you can do but prepare.*

But she knew how that would end. The woman would call the police. A psych hold. Twenty-four hours in a hospital bed while the sky burned.

"Just a precaution," Lin Mei said. "Better safe than sorry."

The bell chimed as she left.

---

The subway was crowded. Rush hour. Bodies pressed together, faces buried in phones, earbuds in. No one looked at each other.

Lin Mei watched them.

The man in the business suit, sweating through his collar, probably heading home to a wife and kids. The teenager with a schoolbag, texting furiously, probably complaining about homework. The old woman clutching a bag of groceries, gray-haired and tired, probably cooking dinner for a family that wouldn't survive the week.

In her old life, she'd watched ninety percent of Beijing die in the first month. The qi storms didn't discriminate. They killed the rich and the poor, the young and the old, the good and the evil. They killed everyone.

But not her.

Not the first time.

And not this time either.

She got off at Dongcheng and found the herb shop tucked between a noodle house and a repair shop. The sign was faded, the windows dusty, but the smell hit her before she pushed through the door. Dried roots. Bark. Mushrooms. The smell of old China.

An ancient man sat behind the counter, reading a newspaper. He looked up when she entered.

"Can I help you, little sister?"

"I need," Lin Mei said, "raw astragalus root. Fresh, not dried. Dang shen. Huang qi. And lingzhi, the wild kind, not cultivated."

The old man's eyebrows rose. "That's a specific list."

"I'm a doctor. I know what I need."

"You're a child."

Lin Mei met his eyes. "Tomorrow, you'll know I wasn't."

Something flickered in his gaze. A recognition. A shiver, maybe. He set down his newspaper and shuffled into the back room.

She waited, hands steady, heart cold.

Twenty years in hell had taught her one thing above all else: preparation was survival. And she had twenty years of preparation compressed into twenty-four hours.

The old man returned with three paper-wrapped bundles. "Two thousand yuan."

She paid without haggling. "Do you have a mortar and pestle? Good quality stone?"

He sold her that too.

She left with her purchases, the weight of them solid and real in her bag. Not enough. Not nearly enough. But a start.

The first step on a long road to surviving the end of the world.

And maybe, just maybe, to finding Li Wei before he found her.

The sun was setting as she stepped back onto the street. Golden light painted the buildings, the trees, the people hurrying home. A beautiful evening. The kind people would remember as the last good night before everything changed.

Lin Mei checked her phone.

*September 12, 2024. 6:47 PM.*

Twenty-nine hours until the first storm hit.

She had a lot of work to do.
