📖 Doctor Reborn in the Apocalypse by Chen Wei

Chapter 5: The Dead Man's Prescription

~1.9k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The first tremor hit at 3:47 AM.

Lin Mei felt it through the bathroom floor tiles, a low vibration that traveled up through the concrete and into her bones. The military hospital's emergency generators kicked on, flooding the hallway with harsh fluorescent light. She didn't flinch. She'd felt worse. Much worse.

Twenty-seven hours until the qi storms.

The surgery had gone well. Clean. The gang leader's son would keep his hand, would keep his function, would wake up wondering why a woman he'd never met had saved his life for a debt he didn't know existed. Lin Mei stripped off her gloves, watching the water run pink down the drain.

She'd been scrubbing for three minutes when the second tremor came.

Stronger.

Glass rattled in its frame. Somewhere above, a patient screamed. Lin Mei grabbed her bag—already packed, already waiting—and stepped into the hallway just as Dr. Zhao came running, his face the color of old paper.

"We need to evacuate. The seismic sensors—"

"It's not an earthquake," Lin Mei said.

Zhao stopped. Blinked. "What?"

She could have told him the truth. Could have explained about the qi currents building beneath the city's fault lines, about the way the world's energy was about to tear itself apart. But she'd watched Zhao die in the first wave, three years from now, torn apart by a corrupted beast while trying to save a patient who was already dead.

She didn't have time to save everyone.

"The basement," she said. "Storage Room 7B. There's a reinforced shelter."

"What? How do you—"

"Go. Now. Tell no one else."

He opened his mouth to argue. Closed it. Something in her voice must have reached him—the flat certainty of someone who'd already seen the end. He turned and ran.

Lin Mei walked.

The hospital was chaos. Nurses running. Patients crying. A man in a wheelchair trying to drag himself toward the exit while his wife screamed at him to move faster. Lin Mei stepped around them, her feet finding the path through the confusion like a knife finding the gap between ribs.

She'd learned to navigate chaos. Twenty years of battlefield surgery in a world that had lost all pretense of civilization. She knew exactly where she needed to go.

The surgical wing. Fourth floor. Office of Dr. Sun Yuxuan.

The door was locked. Lin Mei didn't bother knocking. She kicked it once, twice, and the cheap wood splintered around the latch. Inside, the office smelled like old tea and newer lies.

Dr. Sun sat behind his desk, a phone pressed to his ear. He looked up when the door crashed open. His eyes went wide.

"Dr. Lin. I was just—"

"Put down the phone."

He didn't. "The hospital director is on the line. There's been a seismic event. We need to—"

Lin Mei crossed the room in four steps. She took the phone from his hand—gently, precisely, the way she might remove a scalpel from a wound—and pressed the disconnect button.

"I'm leaving the city," she said. "I need the master key to the herbal pharmacy."

Sun's face cycled through expressions like a failing light. Confusion. Fear. Something that looked almost like recognition.

"The pharmacy is restricted. You know that. Only department heads—"

"I know." Lin Mei leaned forward, her hands flat on his desk. "I also know you've been selling hospital supplies to the Red Faction for the past six months. I know you've been skimming from the surgical fund. I know about the patient who died under your knife last March, the one you blamed on the anesthesiologist."

Sun's throat clicked as he swallowed.

"How do you—"

"I'm not interested in your crimes, Dr. Sun. I'm interested in the pharmacy. Give me the key, and I'll forget everything I know."

He stared at her. A vein pulsed in his temple.

"You can't just—"

The third tremor hit.

This one was different. Deeper. Lin Mei felt it in her teeth, in the fillings from a dental surgery she'd had at age twelve, in a life she'd already lived once. The building groaned. Cracks spiderwebbed across the ceiling.

Sun grabbed his desk to steady himself. "What's happening?"

"Something worse than you can imagine." Lin Mei held out her hand. "The key. Now."

He opened his drawer. Pulled out a steel ring with two electronic cards and a physical key. His hand shook as he passed it over.

"There's a backup generator on the pharmacy's circuit. The locks will hold even if the power fails."

Lin Mei took the key. She almost said thank you. Almost.

Instead, she turned and walked out.

---

The herbal pharmacy occupied the entire west wing of the basement. Lin Mei had memorized its layout on day one of her first life, back when she'd thought knowledge would save her. She'd spent years learning the properties of every plant, every root, every rare fungus that might extend a patient's life by even an hour.

In the apocalypse, knowledge was currency. And she intended to be rich.

The card reader beeped green. The locks clicked open. Lin Mei stepped inside and breathed in the smell of a thousand dried herbs: ginseng, astragalus, reishi mushroom, goji berry, cordyceps. The air was cool and dry, circulated by climate control systems that would keep these medicines viable for years.

She pulled a duffel bag from her pack and started loading.

She worked fast. Methodical. Her hands knew what to grab without her eyes needing to confirm. Lingzhi for boosting qi circulation. Dang gui for blood purification. Huang qi for immune system fortification. She took everything that would be useful in the first months, when the qi storms would corrupt the land and make natural herbs toxic.

Behind her, someone cleared their throat.

Lin Mei didn't stop. "I don't have time for visitors, Director Chen."

The hospital director stepped into the light. He was a small man, bald, with the kind of face that had never needed to show emotion. He held a tablet in one hand and a cup of tea in the other, as if the world wasn't shaking apart around them.

"I received an interesting call," Chen said. "A man claiming to be from the Public Security Bureau. He wanted to know about Dr. Lin Mei. Whether she'd been acting strangely. Whether she'd made any unusual purchases."

Lin Mei moved to the next shelf. Red peony root. White atractylodes. She'd need both for the anti-corruption pills that would keep the qi sickness at bay.

"And what did you tell him?"

"I told him that you were one of my best surgeons. That you'd never given me any reason for concern." Chen took a sip of his tea. "But that was before I found you in a restricted pharmacy at 4 AM, filling a duffel bag with controlled substances."

Lin Mei turned to face him.

"Director Chen. I respect you. In another life, I worked under you for fifteen years. You taught me how to close a chest wound in under three minutes. You taught me that the patient always comes first."

Chen's eyebrow rose. "I don't recall having taught you that."

"Not in this life." Lin Mei set down her bag. "How much do you know about the seismic activity?"

"Enough to know it's not normal. The sensors are picking up energy signatures that don't match any known geological pattern. It's as if the earth itself is... vibrating. On a frequency we can't measure."

"In twenty-seven hours, those vibrations will intensify. They'll tear open the sky. Creatures will pour through—things that shouldn't exist, things that our science has no name for. The world as you know it will end."

Chen stared at her. His tea had gone cold in his hands.

"That's impossible."

"I spent twenty years living in the aftermath of that impossibility. I learned to heal people with nothing but herbs and a scalpel. I learned which plants could purify corrupted blood and which would kill you faster." She picked up her bag. "I'm not crazy, Director. I'm prepared."

"Prepared for what?"

"To survive. To save who I can." She slung the bag over her shoulder. "I'm leaving the city before dawn. I know a place in the mountains—an old research station that was abandoned after the last pandemic. It has its own water source. Its own power grid. It's defensible, and it's far enough from the population centers to avoid the worst of the initial chaos."

"You're asking me to believe that the world is ending."

"I'm not asking you to believe anything. I'm asking you to give me the hospital's supply of antibiotics and surgical equipment. Everything that will expire in the next six months anyway."

Chen was silent for a long moment. The lights flickered. Somewhere above, another tremor shook the building.

"Why should I trust you?"

Lin Mei reached into her pocket. Pulled out a small notebook—dog-eared, stained, filled with handwriting that had taken twenty years to perfect. She'd started writing it the moment she woke up in her old body, pouring every piece of knowledge she could remember onto the pages before she forgot.

She held it out to Chen.

"Read page seven. The entry about the lotus seed formula."

Chen took the notebook. Opened it. His eyes moved across the page, and Lin Mei watched his face change—from skepticism to confusion to something that looked almost like recognition.

"This formula doesn't exist in any medical text I've ever seen."

"It will. In about three years, a doctor in Shanghai will discover it by accident. He'll use it to save seventeen people from qi corruption. He'll become famous for it."

"You wrote this before it was discovered."

"I wrote it because I saw him use it."

Chen closed the notebook. His hand was steady, but Lin Mei saw the vein pulsing in his temple. The same vein that had pulsed in Sun's temple, in Zhao's temple, in every person she'd ever told the truth to.

"I can't give you the hospital's supplies," he said.

Lin Mei's heart dropped.

"I can't give them to you because I can't explain the paperwork. But I can give you the key to the central supply room. I can give you the code for the security cameras. And I can be in my office, reviewing patient files, for the next hour."

He pulled a key from his pocket. Pressed it into her hand.

"If you're wrong about this, Dr. Lin, I'll have no choice but to report you to the authorities."

"I'm not wrong."

"If you're right..." He paused. "If you're right, then I hope you find what you're looking for in those mountains."

He turned and walked away. His footsteps echoed down the hallway, steady and measured, the footsteps of a man who had just made a decision that would define the rest of his very short life.

Lin Mei watched him go.

Then she picked up her bag and headed for the central supply room.

---

She had six hours until dawn.

Six hours to gather supplies. Six hours to load her car. Six hours to drive to the research station before the first qi storm hit and turned the roads into killing fields.

She worked without rest. Without pause. Her body screamed at her—the surgery had taken four hours, and she hadn't slept in thirty-six—but she'd learned to ignore pain. In the wasteland, pain was a luxury. Pain meant you were still alive to feel it.
