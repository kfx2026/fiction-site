📖 Dungeon of the Jade Emperor by Kuang Shan

Chapter 2: The First Floor of Heaven

~1.8k words · Updated 2026-06-22

Wei Chen’s eyes snapped open.

Cold stone pressed against his back. The air smelled of dust and something metallic—old blood, perhaps, or rust. Above him, a ceiling of carved jade stretched into darkness, its surface etched with constellations that pulsed with a faint, dying light.

He sat up too fast. His head spun, and his hand went instinctively to his chest. The jade shard was still there, lodged beneath his sternum like a second heart. He could feel it now—not just physically, but *conceptually*. It hummed in his awareness, a constant low vibration that seemed to synchronize with his pulse.

*The Heavenly Dungeon Core*, it had called itself. *The throne of the Jade Emperor.*

“Right,” Wei Chen muttered, his voice echoing in the empty chamber. “Because my life wasn’t complicated enough.”

He stood, brushing dust from his robes—the same coarse fabric he’d worn into the tomb, now torn and stained. He’d been a tomb raider for fifteen years. He’d broken into cursed burial mounds, dodged ghost-traps, and outrun corpse kings. But never—*never*—had he woken up with a piece of celestial jade fused to his ribcage.

The chamber around him was vast. Pillars of white marble rose toward the unseen ceiling, each one carved with dragons mid-flight. Their eyes were hollow sockets now, but Wei Chen could feel the *intent* behind them—the lingering echo of a power that had once made the heavens tremble.

*This is the first floor of the Heavenly Palace,* the core’s voice whispered in his mind. *The Hall of Azure Clouds. Once, it welcomed visiting immortals. Now, it welcomes you.*

“I’m not an immortal,” Wei Chen said flatly. “I’m a grave robber who made a very bad decision.”

*You are the Dungeon Lord. The decision was not bad—it was inevitable.*

Wei Chen grimaced. “I hate prophecies.”

He walked forward, his footsteps echoing in the silence. The hall stretched on, impossibly long, as if space itself had been folded. At the far end, a throne sat upon a raised dais—simple, unadorned, but *heavy* with presence. It was carved from the same jade as the shard in his chest, and as he approached, he felt the core pulse in response.

*Sit,* the core urged. *Claim your seat.*

“I’m not sitting in a dead god’s chair.”

*The Jade Emperor is not dead. He is… absent. And his throne awaits a new occupant.*

Wei Chen stopped at the base of the dais. The throne was close enough to touch, but he held back. Something about it felt *wrong*—or perhaps *right* in a way that terrified him. He’d spent his life avoiding attention, slipping through cracks in the world. The last thing he wanted was to sit on a throne.

“What happened to the old emperor?” he asked instead.

Silence. The core’s presence flickered, as if it were accessing memories buried deep.

*The Heavenly Court fell ten thousand years ago. The dark forces that destroyed it are not gone—they are merely sleeping. Waiting. And now that the core has awakened, they will sense it.*

Wei Chen’s blood ran cold. “Let me guess. They’ll come for me.”

*They will come for the throne. You are merely the one sitting on it.*

“I’m not sitting on it yet.”

*You are bound to it. The distinction is academic.*

Wei Chen closed his eyes and took a long breath. He’d survived worse odds. Probably. He chose not to think too hard about it.

“Fine,” he said. “If I’m stuck with this thing, I need to know what I’m working with. Give me the tour.”

The core’s presence pulsed, and the hall shifted. The pillars of jade shimmered, and suddenly Wei Chen could *see*—not with his eyes, but with his mind. The dungeon unfolded before him like a map painted on his consciousness.

**Heavenly Dungeon – Floor 1: Hall of Azure Clouds**
- **Status:** Damaged (67% structural integrity)
- **Defenses:** None (guardian seals broken)
- **Resource Generation:** Negligible (celestial qi leak detected)
- **Threat Level:** Low (no active enemies detected)

“Sixty-seven percent?” Wei Chen said. “That’s generous.”

*The hall has suffered greatly. The celestial qi that once sustained it has bled into the mortal realm. You must repair the seals and restore the flow.*

“And how do I do that?”

*Cultivators. They carry qi within them. When they enter the dungeon, their energy feeds the core. The stronger they are, the more they provide.*

Wei Chen’s eyes narrowed. “You want me to lure cultivators here. Like bait.”

*Not bait. Pilgrims. The Heavenly Palace was once a place of enlightenment. Cultivators came to test themselves, to gain insights, to advance. In return, their qi sustained the palace. It was a symbiotic relationship.*

“And now?”

*Now, they will come to conquer. The dungeon has become a trial—a place of danger and reward. Those who survive will grow stronger. Those who fall will feed the core.*

Wei Chen rubbed his temples. “So I’m building a death trap for power-hungry cultivators.”

*You are rebuilding Heaven. One floor at a time.*

There was a pause. The core’s presence flickered again, and Wei Chen felt a sudden spike of alarm.

*Something approaches.*

“What? Where?”

*The outer gate. A cultivator. He is weak—barely at the Qi Condensation stage. But he carries a talisman of detection. He will find the entrance soon.*

Wei Chen’s heart hammered. He was a tomb raider, not a fighter. His skills lay in stealth and cunning, not direct combat. If a cultivator found him now…

*You are the Dungeon Lord. You do not fight—you command. The hall has no guardians, but it has traps. Ancient ones. I can activate them, but they require qi to function.*

“How much qi?”

*The amount you possess. Which is none.*

“Great.”

*But you have the shard. It holds a reserve—enough for one activation. Choose wisely.*

Wei Chen looked around the hall. The pillars, the throne, the empty sockets of the dragon carvings. This was his domain now, broken and bleeding as it was. If he wanted to survive, he needed to start acting like a lord.

“Activate the traps,” he said. “All of them.”

*The reserves will be depleted. You will have no energy for anything else.*

“If that cultivator gets in here and finds me helpless, I won’t need energy. I’ll need a coffin.”

The core pulsed—was that amusement?—and the hall groaned. The jade constellations on the ceiling flickered, and Wei Chen felt a surge of power flow from the shard into the stone beneath his feet. The dragon carvings’ eyes glowed red.

**Trap Matrix: Azure Clouds Formation – Activated**
- **Trap 1:** Mist of Confusion (disorients intruders)
- **Trap 2:** Jade Serpent Strike (projectile attack)
- **Trap 3:** Gravity Well (slows movement)
- **Duration:** 1 hour (qi reserves critical)

Wei Chen felt the weight of the activation settle on him like a physical burden. His limbs grew heavy, and his vision swam for a moment. The shard in his chest throbbed, but it was warm now—alive.

“He’s coming,” the core said aloud, its voice echoing through the hall. “The outer gate has opened.”

Wei Chen moved. He couldn’t fight, but he could observe. He climbed the dais and positioned himself behind the throne, peering through a gap in the jade. The hall stretched before him, empty and silent, waiting.

Minutes passed. Then—

The doors at the far end of the hall creaked open.

A figure stepped through. He was young, maybe early twenties, with the lean build of a martial artist. He wore blue robes embroidered with silver clouds—the insignia of the Azure Cloud Sect, a minor cultivation sect from the eastern provinces. In his hand, he held a jade compass, its needle spinning wildly.

“By the ancestors,” the young cultivator breathed, his eyes wide as he took in the hall. “It’s real. The legends were *real*.”

He stepped forward cautiously, his free hand resting on the hilt of his sword. His qi was faint, barely a flicker—Qi Condensation stage, as the core had said. A mortal who had just begun to cultivate.

Wei Chen felt a pang of sympathy. The boy had no idea what he was walking into.

The cultivator took another step. Then another. The Mist of Confusion began to seep from the pillars—thin, silvery wisps that curled around his ankles. He didn’t notice at first. He was too entranced by the carvings, the throne, the sheer *presence* of the hall.

“The Heavenly Palace,” he whispered. “If I can claim a treasure here, I’ll be promoted to Inner Disciple. I’ll finally be someone.”

*Poor fool,* the core whispered in Wei Chen’s mind. *He seeks glory in a grave.*

The mist thickened. The cultivator stopped, blinking, his hand going to his head. “What… what is this?”

The Mist of Confusion took hold. The boy’s eyes glazed over, and he staggered, his compass clattering to the floor. He turned in a slow circle, lost, his sword drawn but useless.

“Where… where am I?”

Wei Chen watched from behind the throne. The traps were working. The boy was helpless. All he had to do was wait for the mist to drain his qi, and he would collapse. Then Wei Chen could decide what to do with him.

But as he watched the young cultivator stumble, lost and terrified, something twisted in Wei Chen’s chest. He’d been that boy once—desperate, hungry for power, willing to risk everything for a chance to rise above his station.

*No,* he told himself. *This is survival. If I let him go, he’ll bring more. The sects will come. The dark forces will come. I can’t afford mercy.*

The cultivator fell to his knees, gasping. His sword slipped from his grasp.

“Please,” he whispered, his voice breaking. “Anyone… help me…”

Wei Chen closed his eyes.

And then the hall exploded.

Stone shattered. Light blazed. Wei Chen was thrown from his hiding spot, slamming into the base of the dais. The Mist of Confusion scattered, torn apart by a wave of golden energy that radiated from the entrance.

A figure stood in the doorway. Tall. Armored in bronze and jade. His face was hidden behind a mask of a snarling lion, and in his hand, he held a spear that crackled with lightning.

“So,” the figure said, his voice deep and resonant, “the Heavenly Dungeon awakens. And a tomb raider sits on the throne.”

Wei Chen’s blood ran cold. The newcomer’s qi was immense—a pressure that made the air itself feel heavy. This was no Qi Condensation novice. This was a Core Formation elder, or worse.
