📖 Dungeon of the Jade Emperor by Kuang Shan

Chapter 14: The Weight of Ten Thousand Souls

~1.7k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The morning of the fourth day arrived like a blade pressed against Wei Chen's throat.

He stood at the entrance of the Celestial Audience Hall, watching the sun creep over the broken peaks of what had once been the Heavenly Court. The light did nothing to warm him. His hands were shaking. His qi reserves were dangerously low, and the illusion formations he'd spent the last thirty-six hours perfecting felt flimsy, like paper stretched over a pit.

*They're coming,* the core pulsed. *Three of them.*

"You've been saying that for an hour."

*Now they are close. Very close.*

Wei Chen cursed under his breath. He'd hoped for more time. Another day, at least. Maybe a week. But the Heavenly Dungeon didn't get to choose its first guests, and neither did he.

He turned back to the hall. Guardian One stood motionless by the eastern pillar, its blank face staring into nothing. The meditation room was hidden behind a wall of illusion—a technique designed to make cultivators think the room didn't exist until they proved worthy of finding it. The main chamber looked impressive enough. Golden light, swirling mist, the faint hum of qi. But it was all surface. A mask over emptiness.

*Trust the formations,* the core said. *They are strong.*

"Strong enough to fool cultivators who've spent their whole lives learning to see through tricks?"

*Strong enough to buy us time.*

Wei Chen took a deep breath. Then he felt them.

It was like a ripple in still water—three presences, sharp and distinct, approaching from the east. One was strong, steady, like a slow-burning fire. The second was sharp, cutting, like a blade honed to perfection. The third was... wrong. Twisted. It made Wei Chen's skin crawl.

"You didn't mention there was something nasty in the group," he muttered.

*I did not sense it until now. It hides itself well.*

"Great. Just great."

He retreated behind the throne, into the shadows where the illusions were thickest. If this went wrong, he needed a way out. He'd already mapped three escape routes from the hall—a hidden passage behind the throne, a gap in the eastern wall that led to the ruins, and a fall through the floor into the unfinished second floor below. None of them were good options, but they were options.

The three cultivators entered without ceremony.

The first was an old man in white robes, his beard long and silver, his eyes calm and knowing. He moved with the easy grace of someone who had long ago mastered his body and his qi. Wei Chen pegged him at Foundation Establishment, maybe higher. The second was a woman in her thirties, dressed in practical leather armor, a short sword at her hip. Her eyes scanned the room with professional efficiency—tomb raider, or something close to it. She was Qi Condensation, but experienced. Dangerous.

The third made Wei Chen's blood run cold.

He was young, barely twenty, with pale skin and black eyes that seemed to swallow light. He wore dark robes embroidered with silver thread, and he smiled as he entered the hall—a smile that didn't reach his eyes. His qi was a writhing mass of shadows, coiled tight and waiting.

A demonic cultivator.

Wei Chen had heard stories. Demonic cultivators were those who had abandoned the righteous path, who fed on the life force of others to accelerate their own growth. They were hunted by every sect in the mortal realm. But here, in the ruins of the Heavenly Court, there was no one to enforce the old laws.

"This is it?" the demonic cultivator said, his voice smooth and mocking. "The great Heavenly Dungeon? I expected more."

The old man raised a hand. "Patience, Xiao. The dungeon has only just awakened. Give it time."

"If you say so, Master Liang." Xiao's smile widened. "But I hope you didn't drag me all the way up here for a few glowing walls and a bad imitation of celestial architecture."

The woman—Wei Chen didn't catch her name—circled the room slowly, her fingers brushing the pillars. "The formations are recent. Crude, but functional. Someone's been working here."

"Then someone is here," Xiao said. "Let's find them."

Wei Chen's heart hammered against his ribs. He pressed deeper into the shadows, willing the illusions to hold.

*Do not move,* the core whispered. *Let them pass.*

But Xiao was already walking toward the throne.

He moved like a predator, his dark robes trailing behind him like wings. His eyes swept the hall, missing nothing. He paused at Guardian One, tilting his head.

"Interesting. A construct without a soul. Low quality." He tapped the guardian's armor with a finger. "The one who made this is either very weak or very lazy."

Wei Chen felt a flash of anger, hot and sharp. He'd spent hours on Guardian One. It wasn't much, but it was his. His creation. And this smug bastard was mocking it.

*Ignore him,* the core said. *He is baiting you.*

"I know," Wei Chen muttered, barely audible.

Xiao's head snapped toward the throne.

His black eyes locked onto the shadows where Wei Chen was hiding.

"Well, well," Xiao said, his smile turning predatory. "What do we have here?"

Wei Chen froze. The illusions should have held. He was hidden. Invisible. There was no way Xiao could see him.

But Xiao walked straight toward the throne, his hand reaching for the shadows.

*He cannot see you,* the core said, and there was something strange in its voice. Alarm. Confusion. *He senses your anger. Your qi leaked.*

Wei Chen moved.

He rolled out from behind the throne, coming up with a jade dagger in his hand—a relic from one of his tombs, sharp enough to cut through steel. He wasn't a fighter. He was a tomb raider. But tomb raiders knew how to survive.

"Stop," he said, his voice steadier than he felt.

Xiao stopped. His smile widened.

"The dungeon master reveals himself. And he's just a mortal." Xiao laughed, a cold, hollow sound. "This is rich. The Heavenly Court chose a grave robber as its heir?"

Master Liang stepped forward, his expression unreadable. "Young man, we mean you no harm. We came to explore the dungeon, not to fight its master."

"Then leave," Wei Chen said. "The dungeon isn't ready. Come back in a month."

"A month?" Xiao's laughter died. "I'm afraid we don't have a month. The demon sects are moving. The Heavenly Court's fall has created a power vacuum, and there are those who would fill it with darkness." His eyes gleamed. "I intend to be one of them."

"You're a demonic cultivator," Wei Chen said. "You're already darkness."

Xiao's smile didn't waver. "Sharp. I like that." He took a step closer. "Here's the deal, grave robber. You give me access to the dungeon's core. I let you live. Maybe even let you serve me. The Heavenly Court was always meant to be ruled by those strong enough to take it."

"Xiao," Master Liang said sharply. "We agreed. No violence."

"Agreements change, Master Liang." Xiao didn't look away from Wei Chen. "The dungeon core is the key to ascending beyond mortal limits. You think I came all this way to play by your rules?"

The woman drew her sword. "Xiao, stand down. We came as a team—"

"Silence," Xiao said, and the word carried power.

The woman staggered back, blood trickling from her nose. Master Liang's hand went to his own weapon, but he didn't draw. His eyes were fixed on Xiao, calculating.

Wei Chen saw his chance.

He ran.

Not toward the exit—that would be too obvious. He ran toward the eastern wall, where the gap in the stone led to the ruins. If he could lose them in the maze of broken temples and shattered courtyards, he might survive long enough to—

Xiao's hand closed around his throat.

The demonic cultivator moved like smoke, appearing in front of Wei Chen before he'd taken three steps. His grip was iron, his touch cold and unnatural.

"Running?" Xiao said, his face inches from Wei Chen's. "That's disappointing. I expected more from the Jade Emperor's chosen."

Wei Chen choked, his hands clawing at Xiao's wrist. The jade dagger fell from his grip, clattering against the floor.

*Wei Chen,* the core pulsed, its voice urgent. *I can help you. But you must let me in.*

"W-what?" Wei Chen managed.

*Let me into your body. I can give you power. Enough to fight him.*

"Last time I let you in, I almost—"

*You have no choice.*

Xiao's grip tightened. Dark qi began to seep from his hand, crawling up Wei Chen's neck, into his veins. It was cold. Wrong. It felt like death.

"Don't fight it," Xiao whispered. "It will be easier if you don't fight."

Wei Chen's vision blurred. His body was growing numb. The dark qi was spreading, freezing him from the inside out.

*Wei Chen,* the core said again. *Trust me.*

He had no other option.

*Yes,* he thought. *Take it. Take everything.*

The dungeon core exploded inside him.

Golden light erupted from Wei Chen's chest, throwing Xiao across the hall. The demonic cultivator crashed into a pillar, cracking the stone. Master Liang and the woman dove for cover.

Wei Chen fell to his knees, gasping. The power was overwhelming—raw, boundless, ancient. It filled every corner of his body, pushing against his limits, threatening to tear him apart.

*I am sorry,* the core whispered. *I did not want to do this. But there was no other way.*

"What did you do to me?" Wei Chen gasped.

*I gave you a piece of myself. A piece of the Heavenly Court.* The core's voice was tired, strained. *You now carry the weight of ten thousand celestial souls.*

Wei Chen looked down at his hands. They were glowing—golden light pulsing beneath his skin, veins of celestial energy tracing patterns across his arms.

When he looked up, Xiao was standing again, his smile gone, replaced by something like hunger.

"Finally," Xiao said, his voice trembling with excitement. "A worthy opponent."

He lunged.

And Wei Chen, for the first time in his life, didn't run.

He raised his hand.

And the light answered.
