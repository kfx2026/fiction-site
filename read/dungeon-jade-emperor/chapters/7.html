Chapter 7: The First Trial Begins

~1.9k words · 2026-06-22

The morning sun cast pale fingers of light through the broken windows of the Celestial Audience Hall. Wei Chen stood at the entrance, his newly crafted robes—simple gray cloth with golden thread at the cuffs—billowing in a breeze that shouldn't exist inside a dungeon. He'd spent the last hour pacing, checking every formation, every illusion, every corner of his pathetic first floor.

Guardian One stood motionless in the center of the hall, spear held at attention, featureless face turned toward the entrance. The illusion formations hummed beneath the floor, ready to activate at his command. The meditation room sat empty, its cushion pristine, its inscriptions glowing with soft amber light.

It wasn't much. But it was all he had.

"Any sign of them?" he asked the core.

*Three cultivators. Approaching from the east. One Nascent Soul. Two Core Formation.*

Wei Chen's blood ran cold. "Nascent Soul? I thought we'd get Qi Condensation at best!"

*The Heavenly Dungeon's presence is... noticeable. Strong cultivators sense opportunity.*

"Opportunity or death sentence?" Wei Chen muttered. He'd never fought a Nascent Soul cultivator. He'd never fought anyone, really—his tomb raiding career had relied on stealth, traps he'd set weeks in advance, and running away. Direct confrontation was not in his skillset.

The core pulsed with what might have been amusement. *You have me.*

"That's supposed to be comforting?"

*It is not.*

Wei Chen cursed under his breath and moved to the shadows beside the entrance. He'd prepared a spot—a small alcove hidden by an illusion formation, where he could observe without being seen. The core had taught him the technique, and he'd practiced until his head throbbed. Now he pressed his back against the cold jade wall and waited.

They came through the entrance like gods descending to judge the unworthy.

The leader was a woman in her forties, dressed in robes of deep purple silk that shimmered with embedded formation arrays. Her hair was pulled back in a severe bun, held in place by a jade hairpin that radiated killing intent. Her eyes swept the hall with cold assessment, taking in every detail in the space of a breath.

Behind her came two men. One was young, barely twenty, with arrogant eyes and a sword at his hip that practically hummed with spiritual energy. The other was older, weathered, his face a mask of professional disinterest. Both wore the uniforms of a sect Wei Chen didn't recognize—black robes with silver trim, a stylized mountain peak embroidered over their hearts.

"So this is it," the young man said, his voice echoing in the empty hall. "The legendary Heavenly Dungeon. Looks like a ruin."

"Looks are deceiving, Chen Wei," the woman said. Her voice was calm, measured, and completely devoid of emotion. "The Heavenly Court did not fall because it was weak. It fell because it was betrayed."

Wei Chen's breath caught. Betrayed? The core had never mentioned betrayal. It had only shown him the final battle, the darkness consuming the court, the Jade Emperor's last stand. But not why.

"In any case," the woman continued, "our mission is clear. Assess the dungeon's current state. Determine if it has chosen a new master. And if it has..."

She let the sentence hang.

"Kill them?" the young man asked eagerly.

"Observe," she corrected. "The Heavenly Court's secrets are worth more than a single life. The right to bind oneself to this dungeon is worth more than a thousand."

Wei Chen's hands trembled. He forced them still. These weren't cultivators coming to test themselves against the dungeon's trials. They were spies. Scouts. Sent by some sect to claim the Heavenly Court's power for themselves.

The core pulsed. *They will not find what they seek.*

"How can you be sure?" Wei Chen whispered.

*Because they seek a master. They do not seek a servant. The Heavenly Dungeon does not bind to those who wish to dominate it.*

"That's... poetic. And terrifying."

*I have had ten thousand years to think on such things.*

The woman walked slowly through the hall, her fingers trailing along the golden pillars. She stopped at Guardian One, studying its blank face with narrowed eyes. "A construct. Crude but functional. The new master is not skilled."

"Should we find them?" the weathered man asked.

"No. They will come to us. Or they will reveal themselves through their dungeon's defenses." She turned in a slow circle, taking in the entire hall. "This floor is incomplete. Only the barest foundations of a trial. The dungeon is still growing, which means the master is still learning."

She smiled. It was not a pleasant expression.

"We have time."

Wei Chen's mind raced. He had to do something. Let them explore, and they'd find the meditation room, the incomplete formations, the core itself. And if they found the core...

He didn't want to think about what would happen.

"Core," he whispered, "can I activate the illusions now?"

*They are not yet in position. The formations are designed to trigger in sequence. If you activate them prematurely, they will be less effective.*

"I don't need effective. I need distracting."

A pause. Then: *Understood. Activating first layer.*

The hall changed.

Golden light that had pulsed along the walls flickered once, twice, then died completely. Shadows rushed in to fill the void, thick and absolute. The woman stopped mid-stride, her hand going to the hilt of her sword.

"What is this?" the young man asked, his arrogance cracking.

"Stay calm," the woman said. "It's an illusion. The dungeon is testing us."

But her voice had lost some of its certainty.

The shadows deepened. Shapes moved in the darkness—vague, formless things that might have been people, might have been beasts, might have been nothing at all. The air grew cold, then cold, then cold enough that the young man's breath began to fog.

"Senior Sister," he said, "I think—"

"Silence."

The woman closed her eyes. When she opened them, they glowed with purple light. "I see through your tricks, dungeon. Show yourself."

The shadows writhed. And then, from the center of the hall, a voice spoke.

It was not Wei Chen's voice. It was something older, deeper, resonant with the weight of ten thousand years. The core was speaking through him, using his qi as a conduit.

"Who comes before the Heavenly Court?"

The woman's eyes widened. For a moment—just a moment—she looked genuinely afraid. Then she composed herself, bowing deeply.

"I am Elder Ling Yue of the Violet Cloud Sect. I come seeking knowledge of the Heavenly Court's fall. My sect wishes to pay our respects to whatever remains of the Jade Emperor's legacy."

The core's voice rumbled through the hall. "The Jade Emperor's legacy is not for those who come with hidden blades."

Elder Ling Yue's face froze. Her hand, Wei Chen noticed, had drifted to the jade hairpin in her hair—a weapon disguised as ornament.

"I do not know what you mean," she said carefully.

"Your hairpin contains a killing formation. Your robes are lined with defensive arrays. Your companions carry weapons designed to sever spiritual bonds." The core's voice grew colder. "Did you think the Heavenly Court would not recognize the tools of assassins?"

The hall exploded into motion.

Elder Ling Yue's hairpin flew from her hair, transforming mid-air into a sword of crystallized lightning. The young man drew his blade, spiritual energy flaring around him like a wildfire. The weathered man simply vanished, reappearing at the hall's eastern wall, hands glowing with sealing techniques.

"We take the core," Elder Ling Yue commanded. "Kill anything that moves."

Wei Chen's heart hammered against his ribs. This was it. This was the moment he died—killed by cultivators before he'd even finished building his first real floor.

But the core was not done.

The shadows surged.

From every corner of the hall, darkness erupted like water from a broken dam. It wasn't just illusion anymore—the core was spending qi, burning through its reserves to create physical constructs. Shadows took the shape of armored warriors, their eyes burning with golden light.

"Fall back!" Elder Ling Yue shouted, but the young man was already engaged, his blade cutting through the first shadow warrior like it was made of smoke.

It was.

The blade passed through without resistance, and the warrior reformed, its golden eyes mocking.

"These are just illusions!" the young man cried. "They can't hurt us!"

"Don't be a fool!" the weathered man yelled. "Illusions that can't hurt you are meant to distract you!"

He was right. While the young man had been swinging at shadows, Wei Chen had been moving. He slipped through the darkness, using the formations the core had taught him, navigating the hall like a fish through water.

He reached the core's chamber just as Elder Ling Yue's lightning sword carved through the door.

She stood in the doorway, her purple robes billowing with unleashed power. Her eyes found him instantly, and her smile returned—sharp, predatory.

"Ah. There you are."

Wei Chen's back hit the wall. The core pulsed at his feet, its golden light flickering weakly. He'd used too much of its energy on the illusions. There was nothing left for defense.

"I don't want to fight you," he said, his voice steadier than he felt.

"Good. Then don't." Elder Ling Yue raised her lightning sword. "Just tell me how to bind the core. I might let you live."

Wei Chen looked down at the core. It pulsed once, weakly, and he felt its presence brush against his mind—not a command, not a plea, but something softer.

*Thank you.*

For what? For trying? For failing?

"No," he said, and he meant it with every fiber of his being. "I won't."

Elder Ling Yue's sword began to descend.

And then the world went white.

The light was absolute, blinding, pure. It came from everywhere and nowhere, burning through the darkness, through the shadows, through the illusions. Elder Ling Yue screamed, dropping her sword, clutching at her eyes.

Wei Chen felt something grab his shoulder—not a hand, but a presence. Warm. Ancient. Familiar.

A voice spoke in his mind, and it was not the core's.

*Well done, little tomb raider. You have passed the first test.*

The light faded.

Elder Ling Yue was gone. The weathered man was gone. The young man was gone. The hall was empty, silent, untouched by the battle that had raged moments before.

And standing in the center of the audience hall, where Guardian One had stood, was a figure of solid gold.

It was a warrior, tall and proud, its armor etched with characters so ancient Wei Chen couldn't read them. Its face was no longer blank. It had features now—a stern jaw, a straight nose, eyes that burned with the light of a thousand suns.

It turned to face Wei Chen, and when it spoke, its voice was the voice of the core, but stronger, clearer, more alive.

"I am Zhenwu. The Perfected Warrior. The Dark Emperor of the North." It bowed, one knee touching the jade floor. "I have been summoned to serve the new Jade Emperor."

Wei Chen stared.

"What."

Zhenwu rose, its golden eyes meeting his. "The first trial is complete. You have defended the core against invaders. You have refused to surrender. You have proven worthy of the Heavenly Court's protection." A pause. "I am that protection."
