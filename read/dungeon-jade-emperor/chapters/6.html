Chapter 6: The First Adventurers Arrive

~1.9k words · 2026-06-22

The morning sun had not yet breached the mountains when Wei Chen felt them coming.

He was in the meditation room, cross-legged on the cushion, his consciousness spread through the dungeon like spider silk. The core had taught him this trick on the second day—a way to sense disturbances in the qi field that surrounded the Heavenly Dungeon. At first, the sensation had been vague, like trying to hear a whisper through a hurricane. But now, after three days of constant practice, he could feel the individual vibrations of approaching life.

There were five of them. Moving fast. Purposefully.

Wei Chen's eyes snapped open. "They're early."

He scrambled to his feet, his robes rustling in the silence. The dungeon's first floor gleamed around him—the golden pillars now etched with formation arrays that hummed with barely contained power, the jade floor polished to a mirror sheen, the misty ceiling hiding secrets he hadn't yet unlocked. Guardian One stood at attention near the entrance, its featureless face turned toward the door.

Wei Chen had made improvements. The construct's armor now had actual definition—shoulder plates, a breastplate etched with cloud patterns, greaves that caught the light. Its spear had gained a blade of condensed spiritual energy, sharp enough to cut through mortal steel. But its face remained blank. That would have to wait.

"You," Wei Chen said, pointing at the guardian. "Stand here. Look imposing. If they attack, don't kill them. Just... discourage."

The guardian didn't respond. It couldn't. But it shifted its stance slightly, planting its feet wider, angling its spear forward.

Wei Chen took a deep breath. Then he walked to the entrance of the audience hall and waited.

The five cultivators arrived ten minutes later.

They came through the main doors—the massive bronze portals that had once welcomed celestial officials to the Jade Emperor's court. Now they hung crooked on their hinges, covered in centuries of grime and neglect. But the cultivators didn't seem to care. They pushed through without hesitation, their weapons drawn, their eyes scanning for threats.

Wei Chen studied them as they entered. The leader was a man in his forties, built like a bear, with a scar running from his temple to his jaw. He wore the robes of a minor sect—blue and white, with a crane embroidered on the chest. His cultivation was solid. Qi Condensation, sixth level. Strong enough to cause problems, weak enough to be manageable.

Behind him came a woman with a bow, her eyes constantly moving, her fingers never far from the string. Third level Qi Condensation. A scout. Then two younger men, brothers by the look of them, both carrying swords and wearing matching scuffed leather armor. Second level. Greenhorns.

And bringing up the rear...

Wei Chen's breath caught.

A girl. No more than sixteen. She wore simple gray robes, the kind given to outer disciples in the smallest sects. Her cultivation was barely perceptible—Qi Condensation, first level, and shaky at that. She carried no weapon. Her eyes were downcast, her shoulders hunched, as if she expected to be struck at any moment.

*What is she doing here?* Wei Chen thought. *This isn't a training ground. This is a tomb.*

The scarred leader spotted him first. "You," he said, his voice rough. "Are you the caretaker?"

Wei Chen raised an eyebrow. "Caretaker?"

"The old man who sent the message. Said this place had opened up. Said there were treasures inside."

Wei Chen's mind raced. Someone had spread word about the dungeon. That was both good and bad. Good, because it meant cultivators would come. Bad, because he had no control over who knew or what they expected.

"I'm the... guide," Wei Chen said carefully. "This dungeon is new. Untested. I wouldn't recommend bringing—"

"Save your breath," the leader cut him off. "We know the risks. We've been doing this for years. Tombs, ruins, ancient temples. This is just another one."

"It's not."

"What?"

"This isn't just another ruin," Wei Chen said, stepping forward. He let his voice drop, let a hint of the dungeon's power seep into his words. "This is the Heavenly Court. The seat of the Jade Emperor. The gods themselves once walked these halls."

The leader snorted. "Empty gods. Dead gods. Their treasures are ours for the taking."

"Or their traps are yours for the springing."

The leader's hand went to his sword. "Are you threatening us, guide?"

"I'm warning you." Wei Chen met his eyes. "The first floor is safe. For now. But if you push deeper, if you take what isn't offered, the dungeon will respond. And it won't be gentle."

The woman with the bow shifted, her fingers brushing her string. "Brother, maybe we should—"

"No." The leader's voice was granite. "We came here for treasure. We're leaving with treasure. Guide, step aside."

Wei Chen didn't move.

For a long moment, the two of them stared at each other. The dungeon hummed around them, the golden light pulsing in time with Wei Chen's heartbeat. Guardian One stood motionless, its blank face somehow menacing.

Then the leader laughed. "Fine. Have it your way. We'll play by the dungeon's rules. For now." He gestured at his group. "Fan out. Check every corner. Touch nothing until I say so."

The two brothers moved immediately, spreading to the sides of the hall. The woman with the bow climbed a collapsed pillar, gaining height. The girl in gray stayed where she was, her eyes still downcast.

Wei Chen watched them work. They were efficient, he had to give them that. They checked the walls for hidden compartments, tested the floor for pressure plates, examined the pillars for formation arrays. They knew what they were doing.

But they didn't know about the illusions.

Wei Chen had placed them carefully. The first was at the entrance—a subtle distortion that made the hall seem larger than it was, that hid the true dimensions of the space. The second was in the center of the room, a shimmer in the air that made the jade floor look solid when it was actually a thin layer over a pit. The third was the most dangerous: a false treasure chest, gleaming with gold and jewels, that would disappear the moment someone tried to open it.

The illusions weren't perfect. They were the best he could manage in three days, but they had gaps, weaknesses. A keen-eyed cultivator might spot them.

The brothers didn't.

They walked straight into the center of the room, their boots thudding on the jade floor. The shimmer in the air flickered, and for a moment, the pit beneath them was visible—a dark hole that dropped into nothing.

Then the illusion reasserted itself, and they kept walking, oblivious.

Wei Chen let out a breath he hadn't known he was holding.

"Nothing here," one of the brothers called out. "Just empty space."

"Keep looking," the leader growled.

The woman with the bow jumped down from her pillar. "There's a door at the back. Leads deeper in."

"We're not going deeper," the leader said. "Not yet. We secure the first floor, then we establish a base camp."

The girl in gray finally looked up. Her eyes met Wei Chen's, and he saw something there that made his chest tighten. Fear. Not the fear of the dungeon, or the fear of the unknown. The fear of someone who had been hurt before. Who expected to be hurt again.

*Who are you?* Wei Chen wondered. *What are you doing with these people?*

But before he could say anything, the leader turned to him. "Guide. You said the first floor was safe. Show us where the real treasures are."

Wei Chen's jaw tightened. He had prepared for this. Sort of. He had placed a few genuine treasures on the first floor—small things, baubles that would satisfy greedy cultivators without giving away the dungeon's true power. A jade pendant that stored a small amount of qi. A bronze bell that chimed with spiritual energy. A scroll containing a basic cultivation technique.

"Follow me," he said.

He led them to a side chamber, one of the smaller rooms off the main hall. In the center, on a stone pedestal, sat the jade pendant. It glowed with a soft, green light, pulsing gently.

The two brothers rushed forward.

"Stop," Wei Chen said.

They froze.

"That pendant is protected by a formation. If you grab it without knowing the sequence, it will burn your hand off."

The leader narrowed his eyes. "You know the sequence?"

"I do."

"Show me."

Wei Chen walked to the pedestal. He had, in fact, placed a simple formation around the pendant—one that would shock anyone who touched it without first channeling qi in a specific pattern. He demonstrated the pattern now, his fingers tracing the air, his qi flowing through the dungeon's veins.

The pendant lifted from the pedestal and floated into his hand.

"There," he said, holding it out. "One genuine treasure. Qi storage, about a week's worth of cultivation. Worth a few spirit stones, maybe more to the right buyer."

The leader took it, turning it over in his hands. "Not bad. But we're going to need more than this."

"This is all the first floor has to offer."

"I don't believe you."

Wei Chen met his gaze. "Believe what you want. But I'm telling you the truth. This dungeon is new. It hasn't had time to generate deeper treasures. The second floor might have more, but I wouldn't recommend going there without proper preparation."

"I'll decide what's proper." The leader pocketed the pendant. "We're staying the night. We'll explore the second floor tomorrow."

"That's a mistake."

"No one asked you."

The leader turned and walked away, his group following. The girl in gray lingered for a moment, her eyes meeting Wei Chen's again. She opened her mouth, as if to say something, then closed it and hurried after the others.

Wei Chen watched them go. His hands were shaking.

*I can't let them go to the second floor,* he thought. *It's not ready. It's not safe.*

But he had no way to stop them. Not without revealing his true nature. Not without using the dungeon's power in a way that would attract attention he couldn't afford.

The core pulsed in his mind. *They will die.*

"I know."

*The second floor is incomplete. The formations are unstable. The guardians have not been placed.*

"I know."

*We must do something.*

Wei Chen closed his eyes. He felt the dungeon around him, felt its presence settling in his bones. He was not alone. The dungeon was with him. The old Heavenly Court, broken and battered, had placed its trust in him.

And he had to protect it.

"Alright," he said, opening his eyes. "I'll go with them. Keep them out of trouble."

*That is not wise. You are not strong enough to—*

"I know that too." Wei Chen started walking toward the back door, toward the second floor. "But I'm all they've got."

He reached the door and paused. Behind him, in the main hall, Guardian One stood motionless, its blank face watching him go.

Wei Chen took a deep breath.

Then he pushed open the door and stepped into the darkness.

The second floor was not ready. It was a chaotic mess of half-formed structures, unstable formations, and lurking dangers that even Wei Chen didn't fully understand. The cultivators would find no treasure here. Only death.
