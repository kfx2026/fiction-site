📖 Dungeon of the Jade Emperor by Kuang Shan

Chapter 20: The Ninth Floor's Secret

~1.8k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The morning light filtered through the Jade Palace's fractured dome as Wei Chen stood at the entrance of the eighth floor, his palms slick with sweat. Three weeks had passed since the first cultivators arrived. Three weeks of desperate construction, of near-death encounters, of watching the dungeon grow from a single pathetic hall into something resembling a true trial ground.

And now, something was wrong.

"The qi flow is off," he muttered, pressing his hand against the wall. The stone felt cold—colder than it should. The dungeon's energy channels, normally warm and pulsing like a living thing, had gone sluggish. Dead.

*Something has entered the lower floors,* the core whispered, its voice thin and strained. *Something that does not belong.*

Wei Chen's blood ran cold. "From where? The entrance?"

*No. From below.*

That was impossible. The dungeon had no lower floors. Below the first floor was solid bedrock, reinforced by formation arrays that had held for ten thousand years. He had checked. Multiple times.

"I'm going down there."

*That is unwise.*

"Since when have I done the wise thing?" Wei Chen grabbed the iron bell he'd taken from a Ming dynasty tomb—his latest offering to the core, now imbued with detection properties—and began the descent.

The stairwell between the eighth and first floors had changed. The walls, once pristine jade, were now cracked and blackened, as if something had clawed its way through. The air grew thick, heavy with a qi that felt wrong—tainted, like meat left too long in the sun.

By the time he reached the second floor, Wei Chen's hands were shaking.

The Celestial Soldier Hall, once filled with golden constructs standing at attention, was empty. The soldiers were gone. In their place, scattered across the jade floor, were fragments of shattered armor and wisps of dissipating light.

"What happened here?" he whispered.

*They were destroyed.*

"I can see that. By what?"

The core didn't answer.

Wei Chen pushed forward, his heart hammering against his ribs. The first floor's meditation room was untouched, the cultivation formation still humming with gentle energy. But the Audience Hall—

He stopped at the threshold, his breath catching in his throat.

The Hall had been transformed. The golden pillars were wrapped in black vines that pulsed with sickly light. The ceiling, once open to misty heights, was now covered in a web of darkness that dripped like melting shadow. And in the center, where the Jade Emperor's throne should have stood, there was a hole.

A perfect circle, ten feet across, descending into absolute blackness.

"You've got to be kidding me," Wei Chen breathed.

He approached the edge cautiously, the iron bell in his hand ringing a soft warning. The hole went down—further than it should, further than the dungeon's boundaries allowed. At the bottom, far below, he could see movement. Shapes shifting in the dark.

Something was building down there. Something that wasn't him.

"The Heavenly Court was destroyed," he said, more to himself than the core. "But you said the dungeon was sealed. That nothing could get in or out."

*Nothing from outside could breach the seal. But what was already inside—*

"What's inside?"

Silence.

"Core. What's inside?"

*A prison.*

The word hit him like a physical blow. "A prison? You mean there are prisoners? In the dungeon?"

*The Heavenly Court did not only judge the living. It judged the dead, the damned, the divine who had fallen from grace. Below the first floor, there were nine levels of confinement. The ninth floor held those who could not be destroyed. Only contained.*

"But you told me there were only eight floors! You showed me the map!"

*I showed you what I could show. The lower levels were sealed so deeply that even I forgot they existed. Until now.*

"Forgot." Wei Chen laughed, bitter and sharp. "How do you *forget* a prison full of divine monsters?"

*The seal was woven into my core. As I grew stronger, the seal weakened. I am sorry, Wei Chen. I did not know.*

He wanted to scream. He wanted to punch the wall, to curse the core, to run as far and as fast as he could. But running wasn't an option. The dungeon was bound to him. If it fell, he fell with it.

"What's on the ninth floor?"

*The first warden. The one who guarded the prison. When the Heavenly Court fell, the warden was sealed with the prisoners. Now it has awakened.*

"You said the ninth floor held things that couldn't be destroyed. Is the warden one of them?"

*The warden is the reason they could not be destroyed. Without it, the prisoners cannot be held.*

Wei Chen stared into the hole. The movement below was getting closer. Faster. He could hear sounds now—a scraping, like metal on stone, and a low, rhythmic breathing.

"How long do I have?"

*Hours. Maybe less.*

"And the cultivators? The ones on the upper floors?"

*They are not in immediate danger. The warden is focused on the seal. Once it breaks through—*

"It kills everyone."

*Yes.*

Wei Chen turned away from the hole and sat down heavily on the jade floor. His mind raced, searching for solutions, for loopholes, for anything that could buy them time.

The core pulsed, weak and apologetic. *I have failed you.*

"No. You didn't know. But now we do, so we figure it out." He took a deep breath. "Can I go down there? To the ninth floor?"

*You would die.*

"Probably. But can I?"

Another long silence. Then: *The seal is broken enough for passage. But the path is unstable. The warden will sense you.*

"Good. Let it sense me." Wei Chen stood, brushing off his robes. "I didn't sign up to be a dungeon master so I could watch everything fall apart. I signed up to rebuild the Heavenly Court. And you can't rebuild something without getting your hands dirty."

He turned back toward the stairwell, then paused. "Core. If I don't make it back—"

*You will.*

"Let me finish. If I don't make it back, seal the upper floors. Let the cultivators out. They don't deserve to die because of my mistakes."

*They are in the dungeon. They knew the risks.*

"That doesn't make it right."

The core was silent for a long moment. Then, softly: *You are a strange Jade Emperor.*

"I'm not the Jade Emperor. I'm a tomb raider who got in over his head." Wei Chen smiled, thin and wry. "But I'm the only one you've got."

He walked to the edge of the hole. The darkness below pulsed, hungry and waiting.

"Any last advice?"

*Do not trust what you see. The warden's power is illusion. It will show you your deepest fears, your greatest desires. It will try to break you before it kills you.*

"Good to know." Wei Chen stepped forward, into the void.

The fall was shorter than he expected. He landed hard on stone, the impact jarring his knees. Around him, the darkness was absolute—so thick it felt like a physical weight pressing against his skin.

He raised the iron bell. Its chime echoed in the space, revealing a corridor carved from black rock. The walls were covered in characters—seals, he realized, thousands of them, each one a binding spell.

Some of them were broken.

He followed the corridor, the bell's light pushing back the darkness inch by inch. The air grew colder, the breathing louder. And then, the corridor opened into a vast chamber.

Nine pillars of black jade rose from the floor, each one carved with the image of a prisoner—beings of shadow and flame, creatures with too many eyes, figures that hurt to look at. Eight of the pillars were empty, their chains dangling uselessly.

The ninth pillar stood at the center. And bound to it, wrapped in chains of golden light, was the warden.

It was humanoid, roughly the size of a man, but its skin was the color of old bone, stretched tight over a frame that seemed too thin, too angular. Its eyes were closed, its head bowed. But as Wei Chen approached, those eyes opened.

They were gold. Pure, molten gold, without pupil or iris.

"You are not the Jade Emperor," the warden said, its voice like grinding stone. "You are nothing. A speck. A ghost wearing a dead god's skin."

"I get that a lot." Wei Chen kept the bell raised, ready to strike. "I'm here to reseal you."

The warden laughed. The sound shook the chamber, sending cracks spiderwebbing across the pillars.

"You cannot seal me. I am the seal. I am the lock and the key, the prison and the prisoner. The Heavenly Court bound me to this duty, and when it fell, I became the very thing I guarded." Its golden eyes fixed on Wei Chen, cold and amused. "You have no power over me, little ghost. But I have power over you."

"And what power is that?"

The warden smiled. Its teeth were sharp, too many for a human mouth.

"The power of truth."

The chains of golden light snapped.

Wei Chen threw himself backward, the iron bell ringing a desperate alarm. But the warden didn't attack. It stood, stretching its arms as if waking from a long sleep, and looked down at him with ancient, terrible pity.

"I have been bound for ten thousand years," it said. "I have watched the Heavenly Court rot from within. I have seen the Jade Emperor's greatest sin, the one that doomed them all. And now, little ghost, I will show you."

"No thanks." Wei Chen raised his hand, summoning the dungeon's energy. The walls pulsed, responding to his will. "I've seen enough."

"You have seen nothing."

The warden raised its hand, and the world went white.

When Wei Chen's vision cleared, he was no longer in the prison. He stood in a vast hall, grander than anything in his dungeon—the true Heavenly Court, before its fall. And on the throne, robed in celestial light, sat the Jade Emperor.

But the Jade Emperor's face was twisted in grief. And at his feet, bound in chains of starlight, knelt a woman.

"You must understand," the Jade Emperor said, his voice breaking. "This is the only way."

"Brother," the woman replied, tears streaming down her face, "you cannot seal the truth. You can only delay it."

And then Wei Chen understood.

The prison wasn't for monsters.

It was for knowledge.

The warden didn't guard criminals.

It guarded the Jade Emperor's greatest secret—a secret that, if revealed, would shatter everything.

Wei Chen stumbled backward as the vision faded, his mind reeling. The warden stood before him, no longer smiling.

"Now you see," it said. "The Heavenly Court fell because of a lie. And you, little ghost, have inherited that lie."

"What lie?"

The warden leaned close, its golden eyes burning into his.

"That the Jade Emperor was ever worthy of the throne."

And the dungeon began to scream.
