CHAPTER 12: THE CONTRACTOR'S GAMBIT

The morning light crawled across the canyon floor like a wounded animal. I sat with my back against the cliff wall, staring at the twenty-foot-tall mushroom that had grown overnight from the blood-soaked soil where the carrion worm had died.

My vine pulsed against my wrist, hungry and alert.

Three days since the worm attack. Three days since I'd discovered that whatever power lived in my blood could accelerate growth by factors I couldn't even calculate. The mushroom's cap was easily the size of a small house, its gills glowing with that same sickly bioluminescence I'd seen in the deep caves.

But it was the smaller growths that bothered me more.

Around the giant mushroom, clusters of fist-sized fungi had sprouted in neat rings. They weren't random. They formed patterns—symbols I didn't recognize but felt in my bones.

Like a language trying to be born.

"You look like shit," a voice rasped from the shadows.

I didn't flinch. I was getting used to Kaelen appearing without warning.

The abyss-touched merchant emerged from behind a rock formation, his cloak pulled tight despite the growing warmth. His skin had taken on a grayish pallor since we'd left the surface settlement, and his left eye had started developing a vertical pupil.

"The mushroom," he said, pointing. "How?"

"I don't know."

"Bullshit. You're doing something. That thing didn't grow naturally."

I stood, brushing dirt from my clothes. "You want the truth? I'm a delivery driver from a world that doesn't exist anymore. My great-grandfather left me a book full of farming instructions that shouldn't work. And apparently my blood can make plants grow like they're on steroids."

Kaelen stared at me for a long moment. Then he laughed—a dry, cracking sound that echoed off the canyon walls.

"You're either the biggest fool or the most dangerous man I've ever met."

"Can I be both?"

"That's what scares me."

He tossed something at my feet. A leather pouch, bulging with what looked like dried herbs. "Payment for saving my life. Nightshade root. Grind it, mix with water, and it'll keep the smaller abyss creatures away for about six hours."

I picked up the pouch. "Why are you helping me?"

"Because you're going to die if you don't understand what you've stumbled into." Kaelen's vertical pupil dilated. "The Abyss isn't just a place. It's a force. A consciousness. And it's been sleeping for ten thousand years."

"Sleeping?"

He pointed at the mushroom. "That thing? It's a beacon. Every spore it releases carries a piece of the Abyss's will. The creatures down there? They're not monsters. They're symptoms. Immune responses to an infection that started long before any of us were born."

I felt the vine tighten around my wrist. "What infection?"

"The surface world. Us. We're the disease, and the Abyss is the cure."

The words hit me like a physical blow. I'd been thinking of this place as a hostile environment I needed to conquer. But what if I was the invader?

"That's why the book exists," I said slowly. "My great-grandfather wasn't trying to fight the Abyss. He was trying to... negotiate with it."

Kaelen's eyes widened. For the first time since I'd met him, he looked genuinely afraid.

"You have the Codex Arboris."

It wasn't a question.

"The what?"

"The Book of the World Tree. It's a legend among my people. A text that predates the Abyss itself. It was supposed to contain the original contracts—the agreements that kept the different realms balanced."

I pulled the weathered book from my jacket. The cover was leather, but not from any animal I recognized. It seemed to shift colors in the light, bleeding from brown to green to black.

"Where did you get that?" Kaelen whispered.

"My great-grandfather's will. He died sixty years ago."

"He can't be dead. The keepers of the Codex don't die. They... transfer."

A chill ran down my spine. "I never met him. He died before I was born."

"Then how—"

The mushroom exploded.

Not with fire or force, but with growth. In the space of a heartbeat, it doubled in size, then doubled again. The ground beneath it cracked open, and something emerged from the fissure.

A root, as thick as my torso, covered in thorns that glowed with purple light.

The root moved with purpose, slithering across the ground toward me.

"Don't let it touch you!" Kaelen shouted.

But I couldn't move. The vine on my wrist was pulsing so hard I could feel it in my teeth. It wanted me to reach out. To touch the root.

To complete the circuit.

The book fell open in my hands, pages flipping on their own. They stopped at a page I'd never seen before, covered in symbols that burned with inner light.

I could read them.

*Contract of the First Branch: The Blood That Waters Must Also Drink*

The root stopped inches from my feet. At its tip, a flower bloomed—a single black petal with veins of gold that formed a face.

My face.

"What the hell..." I breathed.

The flower spoke. Not with sound, but with words that appeared directly in my mind.

*You carry the seed of the World Tree. You have watered it with abyss blood. The contract is incomplete.*

"What contract?"

*The one your blood signed before you were born. Your ancestor made a deal. He took our power. We took his line.*

Kaelen grabbed my shoulder. "Don't listen to it. The Abyss lies. It's trying to trick you into—"

The flower turned toward him, and Kaelen screamed.

He didn't burn or dissolve. He just... stopped. Frozen mid-sentence, eyes wide, skin turning the color of stone.

"Kaelen!"

*He is not dead. He is simply... paused. We have much to discuss, Contractor.*

"I'm not a contractor. I don't know what you're talking about."

*Your blood sings with the old songs. Your grandfather's grandfather tasted our depths and survived. He planted a seed that would bloom in his bloodline when the time was right.*

"The time for what?"

*The harvest.*

The ground beneath me started to shake. More roots burst from the cracks, forming a wall around me and the mushroom. The canyon walls themselves began to shift, ancient stone groaning as something massive stirred beneath them.

*The Abyss has been patient. It has waited through empires and ages, through ice and fire. But patience has limits.*

"I'm just a delivery driver," I said, my voice cracking. "I don't know how to save the world."

*Who said anything about saving it?*

The roots wrapped around my ankles, pulling me toward the fissure. The vine on my wrist didn't resist. It joined them, twining with the abyss roots like they were old friends.

Maybe they were.

*You have three choices, Contractor. Feed the World Tree with your blood and become the bridge between realms. Refuse, and watch everything you've ever known consumed by the Abyss. Or...*

"Or what?"

The flower's face twisted into a smile that was not human.

*Or find the third path. The one your ancestor hid. The one that might save you all.*

"How? How do I find it?"

*You already carry it.*

The book in my hands was burning. Not with fire, but with light so bright I could see through my own flesh. The pages were rearranging themselves, new symbols appearing as the old ones faded.

And in that light, I saw him.

A man who looked exactly like me, standing in a field of impossible flowers, holding the same book. He turned, and our eyes met across sixty years of time.

He smiled, sad and knowing.

Then he pointed at something behind me.

I turned just in time to see the giant mushroom split open, revealing a staircase that descended into absolute darkness.

*The path is open,* the flower said. *But once you walk it, there is no turning back. What grows in the Abyss must be harvested. What is harvested must be consumed. And what is consumed becomes part of the cycle.*

"Or what?" I asked again.

*Or it consumes you.*

The roots pulled me forward. I stumbled, caught myself, and stood at the edge of the staircase. The darkness below pulsed with a rhythm that matched my heartbeat.

Behind me, Kaelen remained frozen. Above me, the canyon walls continued to shift. And around me, the world I was trying to save continued its slow slide into chaos.

I looked at the book in my hands. It had changed completely. The cover now showed a tree with roots reaching into darkness and branches touching stars. And at the center of the trunk, a single figure.

Human.

Blurred.

But holding a farmer's scythe.

I took a breath. Then I stepped onto the first stair.

The darkness swallowed me whole.
