CHAPTER 7: The Debt Collector's Smile

The mountain path twisted downward through layers of morning mist, each step carrying Shen He further from everything he'd known. He'd walked for three hours, his legs burning, his lungs aching, but the physical pain was nothing compared to the hollow emptiness in his chest.

*Exiled. For a pill that doesn't even work.*

He stopped at a rocky outcropping overlooking the valley below. The Azure Cloud Sect's pagodas rose behind him, their golden spires catching the morning light like spears piercing heaven. He'd spent ten years scrubbing their floors, cleaning their libraries, enduring their contempt. And now he had nothing to show for it except a crumpled page of notes and the lingering taste of failure.

Shen He pulled the salvaged page from his bag. The ink was smudged, the characters barely legible, but he could still make out the core formula: *Seven-leaf Spirit Grass, ground to powder. Phoenix Tail Root, aged twelve years. Meridian Awakening Dew, extracted under a blood moon.*

The last ingredient caught his eye. He'd never heard of Meridian Awakening Dew. The scroll he'd copied had been fragmented, parts of it lost to time and decay. Maybe that was why the pill killed people—they were missing a crucial component.

*Or maybe I'm just fooling myself again.*

He crumpled the page and shoved it back into his bag. It didn't matter now. He had no workshop, no ingredients, no future. He was just a cripple with delusions of grandeur, walking toward a world that had no place for him.

The path leveled out as he reached the base of the mountain, and the forest gave way to a small trading post. A dozen wooden buildings clustered around a dirt road, their roofs sagging under the weight of years. Merchants hawked their wares from carts, their voices carrying across the morning air.

Shen He's stomach growled. He hadn't eaten since yesterday, and his last meal had been a bowl of thin gruel that tasted like regret. He had a few spirit stones—meager currency in the cultivation world, but enough to buy a meal in a mortal village.

He approached a food stall run by an old woman with kind eyes and calloused hands. "Two meat buns, please."

The woman looked him up and down, taking in his tattered robes and hollow cheeks. "You a cultivator, boy?"

"Was," Shen He said. "Not anymore."

She nodded as if she understood something he hadn't said. "Two buns, two copper coins."

He fished out a spirit stone—a small, cloudy crystal that pulsed with faint qi. "I don't have copper. Will this do?"

The woman's eyes widened. "Spirit stone? Boy, that's worth more than this entire stall. I can't—"

"Then consider it payment for the buns and some information." He placed the stone on her counter. "I'm looking for work. Alchemy work. Is there anyone in this area who deals with pills and elixirs?"

The woman pocketed the stone quickly, as if afraid he'd change his mind. "There's a man called Old Chen. Runs a medicine shop three streets east. But he's... particular about who he works with."

"I'm particular about who I work for," Shen He said. "Thank you."

He ate the buns as he walked, savoring their warmth. They were simple fare—pork and vegetables wrapped in dough—but to his starving body, they tasted like a feast. The qi from the spirit stone had already begun to seep into his damaged meridians, healing the small tears and fractures that had accumulated over years of failed cultivation attempts.

*At least my body can still absorb pill qi,* he thought. *Even if I can't cultivate normally.*

The medicine shop sat at the end of a narrow alley, its wooden sign so weathered that the characters were barely legible: *Chen's Curatives and Cures.* The windows were grimy, the door hung crooked on its hinges, and the smell that wafted out was a strange mixture of herbs, rot, and something that might have been burning hair.

Shen He pushed open the door.

The interior was chaos. Jars and bottles lined every surface, their contents ranging from dried herbs to preserved organs to things Shen He couldn't identify and didn't want to. A man sat behind a cluttered desk, his face half-hidden by a wild beard and his eyes sharp as a hawk's.

"You're not a customer," the man said. It wasn't a question.

"I'm looking for work," Shen He said.

Old Chen laughed—a hacking, phlegmy sound that turned into a cough. "You? Boy, you look like you couldn't crush a pill, let alone refine one. What makes you think you can work for me?"

Shen He reached into his bag and pulled out the crumpled page. "I understand alchemy. Advanced alchemy. I've studied the fundamentals, the intermediate techniques, and I've attempted forbidden formulas." He paused. "I nearly blew up a quarter of my sect trying to refine a Meridian Restoration Pill."

Old Chen's laughter stopped. His eyes narrowed. "Meridian Restoration? That's a death sentence, boy. Everyone who's tried it has ended up as fertilizer."

"I know. But I also know that the formula is incomplete. The scroll I copied was missing a key ingredient—Meridian Awakening Dew. Do you know what that is?"

The silence stretched. Old Chen's fingers drummed on the desk, a steady rhythm that matched Shen He's heartbeat. Finally, the old man spoke.

"I know what it is. And I know where to find it." He leaned forward, his eyes gleaming. "But it's not something you can buy. It's something you have to earn. And earning it requires a journey that most people don't survive."

Shen He's heart raced. "Tell me where."

"The Crimson Bog," Old Chen said. "Three days east of here. Deep in the marsh, there's a shrine dedicated to a forgotten god. The dew forms on the shrine's altar once a month, under the light of the blood moon. But the bog is filled with venomous beasts, poisonous plants, and worse—cultivators who've gone mad from the swamp's miasma."

"And you expect me to go there?"

Old Chen smiled—a predator's smile. "I expect you to die. But if you somehow survive and bring me the dew, I'll give you a job. More than that—I'll give you access to my laboratory, my ingredients, and my library. Everything you need to perfect your little death pill."

Shen He stared at the old man, searching for the lie. He found none.

"Deal," he said.

---

Three days later, Shen He wished he'd never made that deal.

The Crimson Bog was worse than Old Chen had described. The air was thick with humidity and the stench of decay. Mosquitoes the size of his thumb buzzed around his face, their bites leaving welts that oozed a foul-smelling liquid. The ground squelched beneath his feet, threatening to swallow him with every step.

He'd prepared as best he could. Old Chen had given him a map, a pouch of repellent herbs, and a small knife for self-defense. But the old man's sneer as Shen He left the shop said everything: *You're going to die out there.*

*Maybe,* Shen He thought, *but not today.*

He'd been walking for hours, following the map's faded lines through a landscape that seemed designed to kill him. Strange creatures slithered through the murky water—serpents with multiple heads, frogs that glowed with toxic light, insects that swarmed in clouds so thick they blocked out the sun.

The shrine appeared suddenly, rising from the mist like a ghost. It was small and crumbling, its stone walls covered in moss and strange symbols that pulsed with faint light. The altar at its center was a slab of black stone, smooth as glass, and on its surface—

Dew. Glowing, silvery dew that seemed to hum with power.

Shen He's breath caught. *Meridian Awakening Dew.*

He approached cautiously, his senses on high alert. The symbols on the walls were old—older than any language he'd studied. They seemed to writhe in the corner of his vision, whispering secrets he couldn't quite hear.

He reached the altar and pulled a small glass vial from his pouch. The dew was thick, almost viscous, and it shimmered with an inner light that made his fingers tingle. He carefully scraped it into the vial, filling it to the brim.

That's when the ground shook.

Shen He spun around, the vial clutched to his chest. The mist had parted, revealing a figure standing at the edge of the clearing. It was a woman—tall, beautiful, and radiating a pressure that made his bones ache.

"Well, well," she said, her voice like honey laced with poison. "A little rat, come to steal from the shrine."

Shen He's mind raced. He'd heard stories of cultivators who guarded ancient sites, bound by oath or madness to protect them from intruders. He'd hoped the shrine would be unguarded.

He'd hoped wrong.

"I'm not stealing," he said, his voice steady despite his fear. "I'm taking what I need to save my life."

"Save your life?" The woman laughed, the sound echoing through the swamp. "You're a cripple. A waste of qi. You couldn't save your life if I handed you the elixir of immortality."

She took a step forward, and the ground trembled. "But I'm feeling merciful. Give me the dew, and I'll let you leave with your limbs intact."

Shen He's hand tightened around the vial. This was his only chance. Without the dew, he'd never perfect the Meridian Restoration Pill. He'd never prove the sect wrong. He'd never—

*I'd rather die trying than live as a failure.*

"No," he said.

The woman's smile vanished. "Then you'll die here, forgotten, your bones feeding the swamp."

She raised her hand, and the mist around her coalesced into blades of shimmering qi. They shot toward Shen He with the speed of falling stars.

He dove to the side, rolling through the mud as the blades tore through the space where he'd been standing. The altar cracked, the ancient stone shattering under the impact.

*Think, Shen He. Think.*

He had no cultivation. No combat skills. No weapons except a small knife and a pouch of repellent herbs.

*Wait. Repellent herbs.*

He ripped open the pouch and threw its contents into the air. The herbs exploded into a cloud of bitter-smelling dust, and the woman recoiled, her qi blades faltering.

"What is this?" she snarled.

"Something that keeps bugs away," Shen He said. "And apparently, it works on snakes too."

He ran.

The woman's screams followed him through the swamp, growing fainter as he plunged deeper into the mist. He didn't stop. He didn't look back. He just ran, the vial clutched to his chest, his lungs burning, his legs screaming.

When he finally collapsed, hours later, he was at the edge of the bog. The sun was setting, painting the sky in shades of blood and gold. Shen He lay on his back, gasping for breath, the vial still pressed against his heart.

He'd done it. He'd survived.

But as he looked up at the darkening sky, a shadow fell over him. A man's voice, cold and familiar, cut through the air.

"Shen He. Did you really think you could escape the sect's judgment so easily?"
