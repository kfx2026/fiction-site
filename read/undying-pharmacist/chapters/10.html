Chapter 10: The Poison's True Master

~1.8k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The night air tasted of rot and regret as Shen He stumbled through the outskirts of Greenport Town. Three days since his exile. Three days of sleeping in ditches and eating wild herbs that weren't quite poisonous enough to kill him but weren't quite nutritious enough to keep his strength up.

His stomach growled. Loudly.

"Shut up," he muttered to himself, pressing a hand to his abdomen. "You're not helping."

The town's outer wall loomed ahead, patched with wooden planks where the original stone had crumbled. Even the mortal world couldn't escape decay. Shen He had hoped to find work here—a healer's apprentice, a herb gatherer, anything that would keep him fed while he pieced together what remained of his research.

But the guards at the gate had taken one look at his sect robes—torn, singed, and barely recognizable—and chased him off with spears. "No vagrants," they'd said. "No cultivators down on their luck."

So here he was. Skirting the edge of civilization like a stray dog.

He found shelter in a collapsed barn half a li from the main road. The roof sagged dangerously, but enough of it remained to keep off the morning dew. Shen He settled into a corner, pulling out his crumpled page of notes.

The Meridian Restoration Pill.

The sect master had called it a failure. A death sentence in pill form. But Shen He had read the ancient text differently. The original formula wasn't flawed—it was *incomplete*. The alchemists who created it had lacked a crucial ingredient, something that would stabilize the volatile qi reaction and prevent the meridians from tearing apart.

He'd been so close to discovering it before the explosion.

A sound interrupted his thoughts. Footsteps. Light, deliberate, approaching the barn from the east.

Shen He tensed, his hand moving to the dagger at his belt. A pathetic weapon against a cultivator, but it might buy him a second to run.

"Come out," a voice said. Female. Young. Familiar.

Lin Xue stepped through the broken doorway, her white robes immaculate even in the dim light. She carried no weapon, but she didn't need one. Her cultivation base alone could crush Shen He into paste.

"What do you want?" he asked, rising slowly. "Come to finish the job? Make sure I don't embarrass the sect further?"

Lin Xue's expression was unreadable. "You look terrible."

"Three days of eating weeds will do that to a person."

She reached into her sleeve and pulled out a cloth bundle. Tossed it to him. Shen He caught it instinctively, unwrapping it to find bread, dried meat, and a small flask of water.

"I don't understand," he said, suspicion warring with hunger.

"It's not poisoned. If I wanted you dead, I wouldn't need poison."

He believed her. Lin Xue was the Azure Cloud Sect's prized disciple—a genius who had reached the Qi Condensation realm at sixteen. She could kill him with a thought.

So why was she feeding him?

"Eat," she said. "Then we talk."

Shen He ate. Pride was a luxury he couldn't afford.

When he finished, Lin Xue sat down across from him, her back straight, her hands resting on her knees. "I lied to you," she said.

"You lied about a lot of things."

"About the Meridian Restoration Pill."

Shen He froze. "What?"

"The sect master told you it was a failure. That it destroys meridians instead of healing them." Lin Xue's eyes met his. "He was wrong. Or rather, he was half-right. The pill does destroy meridians—*if* it's made correctly. But the version you found in the restricted section? That was a fake. A deliberate misdirection placed there centuries ago to protect the real formula."

"The real formula?"

"The one that *works*."

Shen He's mind raced. If what she said was true, then everything he'd been through—the explosion, the trial, the exile—had been based on a lie. The sect hadn't punished him for attempting a forbidden refinement. They'd punished him for stumbling too close to their most closely guarded secret.

"Why are you telling me this?"

Lin Xue was silent for a long moment. When she spoke, her voice was barely a whisper. "Because I need your help."

"You? Need *me*?" Shen He laughed, the sound hollow and bitter. "You're the sect's prodigy. I'm a cripple who can't cultivate."

"You're a cripple who can refine pills that shouldn't exist."

She pulled a scroll from her sleeve, unrolling it to reveal a complex array of characters and diagrams. Even in the dim light, Shen He recognized the structure—it was an alchemical formula, but one far more advanced than anything he'd ever seen.

"This is the true Meridian Restoration Pill," Lin Xue said. "I stole it from the sect master's private vault three years ago."

"Three years—" Shen He's eyes widened. "You've had this the entire time?"

"I've had it, but I haven't been able to use it. The formula requires a type of qi control that I don't possess. It's not about cultivation level—it's about a specific affinity for pill refinement. A talent that the sect hasn't produced in generations."

Shen He looked at the scroll. At the intricate sequence of ingredients and techniques. At the final line, written in a script so ancient it predated the current cultivation era.

*The one who refines this pill must possess the body of a mortal and the soul of an alchemist.*

He looked up at Lin Xue. "You think I'm the one."

"I know you are. The explosion in the library wasn't a failure, Shen He. It was proof. You stabilized a reaction that should have been impossible. You channeled qi through a body that has no meridians." She leaned forward, intensity burning in her eyes. "You didn't fail that night. You succeeded beyond what anyone thought possible. You just didn't realize it."

Shen He's hands trembled. He looked down at the scroll, then at his own palms. The same hands that had been called useless. The same body that had been declared worthless.

"What do you want in return?" he asked.

"I want the pill."

"For yourself?"

"For my mother."

Lin Xue's composure cracked, just slightly. For the first time, Shen He saw something other than cold perfection in her eyes. He saw desperation.

"She was a cultivator. A powerful one. But she was poisoned by a demonic cultivator during a raid twenty years ago. The poison has been eating away at her meridians ever since. The sect's healers said there's no cure." Lin Xue's voice hardened. "They lied. There is a cure. The Meridian Restoration Pill is the only thing that can save her. But I can't refine it alone."

"And the sect master? Elder Wu?"

"They would rather see her die than admit that an ancient technique they deemed 'impossible' is actually real. It would make them look foolish. Weak."

Shen He stared at the scroll. At the promise it held. A pill that could fix his crippled meridians. A pill that could make him a real cultivator. A pill that could save Lin Xue's mother.

It was too good to be true.

"What's the catch?"

Lin Xue's expression darkened. "The ingredients. Three of them are incredibly rare. One is nearly extinct. And the last..."

"The last what?"

"The last requires a sacrifice. The alchemist who refines the pill must channel their own life force into the final stage of refinement. If they survive, they lose a decade of their lifespan. If they don't..."

"They die."

"Yes."

Shen He sat back, the weight of her words pressing down on him. A decade of his life. Or death. For a pill that might not even work.

But if it did...

If it fixed his meridians...

"Let's say I agree," he said slowly. "Where do we find these ingredients?"

Lin Xue smiled—a genuine smile that transformed her face from cold beauty to something almost warm. "I know where the first one is. But getting it won't be easy."

"Nothing ever is."

"The first ingredient is the Blood Lotus. It grows in the heart of the Black Marsh, a swamp overrun by venomous beasts and miasma. It's guarded by a demonic python that's been alive for three hundred years."

Shen He swallowed. "And the other two?"

"The second is the Starfall Crystal. Only found in the ruins of a fallen celestial palace, hidden somewhere in the Storm Ridge Mountains. The storms there can tear a cultivator's soul from their body."

"Lovely."

"The third... the third is the most dangerous. It's the core of a living demon. Not a beast, not an elemental—a true demon that has attained human form."

Shen He was silent for a long moment. Then he laughed—a genuine laugh, full of irony and dark humor.

"You're asking a cripple to hunt down a demon's core."

"I'm asking an alchemist to save my mother."

The words hit harder than he expected. Save someone. Not for power, not for status, but because it was the right thing to do.

Shen He thought of his own mother. Dead for ten years now. He'd never been able to save her.

"I'll do it," he said.

Lin Xue's eyes widened. "You will?"

"On one condition."

"Name it."

"When this is over, you help me find a way to refine the pill without sacrificing my lifespan. I'm not dying for anyone. Not yet."

Lin Xue nodded slowly. "Agreed."

They shook hands, and Shen He felt a spark of something he hadn't felt in days.

Hope.

"One more thing," Lin Xue said, rising to her feet. "The sect master knows I've been investigating the restricted section. He suspects I have the scroll. Which means..."

"He's watching you."

"Yes. Which is why I can't be seen helping you openly." She pressed a jade token into his hand. "This will lead you to a contact in the next town. She'll provide supplies and information. But trust no one else."

"What about you?"

"I'll meet you at the Black Marsh in three weeks. If I'm not there by then..."

"You'll be dead."

Lin Xue's silence was answer enough.

She turned and disappeared into the night, leaving Shen He alone with a stolen scroll, a jade token, and a promise that might cost him everything.

He looked up at the stars, scattered across the sky like scattered spirit stones.

"Alright," he said to himself. "Let's go hunt a demon."

He packed his meager belongings and stepped out of the barn, heading east toward the Black Marsh.

Behind him, a shadow detached itself from a nearby tree. It watched him go, then melted back into the darkness.

Far away, in the Azure Cloud Sect's inner sanctum, Elder Wu smiled.

"Perfect," he whispered. "He's taking the bait."
