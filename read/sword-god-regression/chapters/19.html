CHAPTER 19: The Truth Wears a Familiar Face

Jin Tian stood in the center of the ring, his wooden sword resting lightly in his grip. The afternoon sun beat down on the tournament grounds, casting long shadows across the packed earth. Around him, the crowd had grown threefold since his last match—word of the outer disciple who had challenged Chen Wei's position had spread like wildfire through the sect.

But Jin Tian's attention was fixed entirely on the man approaching from the opposite end of the ring.

Inner Disciple Liu Feng.

He looked younger than Jin Tian remembered. Of course he did—this was three hundred years in the past. Liu Feng was only twenty-two now, already ranked seventh among the inner disciples, his reputation built on a foundation of brutal efficiency and overwhelming fire qi. He moved with the easy confidence of someone who had never known defeat.

In Jin Tian's first life, that confidence had been well-earned. Liu Feng had gone on to become one of the sect's elders, a master of flame techniques that could reduce mountains to ash. But at the moment of Jin Tian's death, it had been Liu Feng's sword that found his heart, not his fire.

Why?

"Outer Disciple Jin Tian," Liu Feng said, his voice carrying easily across the ring. He didn't bother with a bow. "I've heard interesting things about you. The boy who knows his numbers better than his sword."

"I know both," Jin Tian replied evenly. "I find numbers more reliable than people."

Liu Feng's eyes narrowed slightly. "Clever tongue. Let's see if your blade matches it."

The tournament overseer raised his hand. "The match begins when the flag falls. Standard rules—first to yield or three points wins. No lethal strikes." He paused, looking at Jin Tian. "Outer disciple, you may withdraw now and return to your regular training. There is no shame in recognizing your limits."

"I recognize my limits," Jin Tian said. "They're farther than most people think."

The overseer shrugged and dropped his hand. The flag fell.

Liu Feng exploded forward.

Fire qi erupted around him, coating his wooden sword in a sheen of orange flame. The technique was basic—Flame Wrapping—but the speed and control behind it were anything but. Liu Feng closed the distance in three heartbeats, his sword arcing toward Jin Tian's neck in a diagonal slash that would have ended most outer disciples instantly.

Jin Tian sidestepped.

Not a dodge—a sidestep. Precise. Minimal. He moved exactly three inches to the left, letting the flaming blade pass within a hair's breadth of his shoulder. His own sword came up in a counter-thrust aimed at Liu Feng's exposed ribs.

Liu Feng twisted, deflecting the strike with his forearm. The impact sent a shock through Jin Tian's hands. The inner disciple was stronger. Faster. His qi reserves dwarfed Jin Tian's by a factor of ten.

None of that mattered.

In his first life, Jin Tian had faced Liu Feng seven times. He had won twice, lost three times, and drawn twice. He knew every technique in Liu Feng's arsenal, every pattern, every tell. The slight twitch in his left eye before he used his signature technique. The way his weight shifted to his back foot when he was about to retreat. The micro-expression of satisfaction that crossed his face when he thought he had you cornered.

All of it was written in Jin Tian's memory like a manual he had memorized long ago.

Liu Feng pressed forward, his attacks coming faster and faster. Flame Wrapping gave way to Blazing Comet, a two-strike combination that left trails of fire in the air. Jin Tian deflected the first, ducked under the second, and answered with a thrust to Liu Feng's knee.

The inner disciple hissed and jumped back, his eyes widening slightly. "You're faster than the reports said."

"Reports are only as good as the people writing them," Jin Tian said. He didn't give Liu Feng time to recover. He surged forward, his wooden sword becoming a blur of motion as he unleashed a technique that didn't exist yet in this era.

The Falling Leaf Strike.

It was a foundational technique in the Verdant Sword Sect's advanced curriculum, developed by an elder who wouldn't be born for another hundred years. The strike was deceptively simple—a horizontal slash that seemed to hang in the air before accelerating suddenly, catching opponents off guard.

Liu Feng's eyes went wide as the blade slipped past his guard and cracked against his ribs.

"First point to the outer disciple!" the overseer called out, surprise evident in his voice.

The crowd erupted. No one had expected an outer disciple to score first against a ranked inner disciple. Jin Tian could hear the whispers spreading through the stands, the reassessments of his potential, the sudden interest from disciples who had never noticed him before.

Liu Feng straightened slowly, his hand pressed against his ribs. When he pulled it away, there was no blood—the wooden sword couldn't cut—but the bruise would be impressive. He looked at Jin Tian with something that might have been respect.

Might have been.

"You've been holding back," Liu Feng said.

"I've been waiting for the right opponent."

Liu Feng smiled. It was not a pleasant smile. "Then let me show you what waiting costs."

Fire exploded from his body.

The technique was Dragon's Wrath, a mid-level fire technique that consumed enormous qi to create a temporary aura of flame around the user. It was expensive, dangerous, and nearly impossible to maintain for more than a few seconds. In Jin Tian's first life, Liu Feng hadn't developed this technique until he was an inner elder.

He was using it now. At twenty-two. Against an outer disciple.

The temperature in the ring soared. Sweat evaporated from Jin Tian's skin before it could form. The wooden sword in his hand began to smoke, the protective seal on its surface straining against the heat.

"You're too confident for someone who's about to lose," Liu Feng said, his voice distorted by the flames.

"You're too aggressive for someone who doesn't know what he's fighting."

Liu Feng's eyes flashed. He lunged.

The speed was incredible—nearly double what it had been before. Jin Tian barely got his sword up in time to deflect the first strike. The second caught him on the shoulder, sending a jolt of fire qi through his body. The third he avoided by throwing himself to the side, rolling across the scorched ground.

He came up with his sword in a guard position, breathing hard. The flame aura was too strong. He couldn't get close without being burned, and he couldn't maintain distance forever—Liu Feng's reach was longer, his attacks faster.

But Jin Tian had a secret weapon.

He had seen this technique before. He knew its weakness.

Dragon's Wrath consumed qi at a rate that couldn't be sustained. Liu Feng had maybe thirty seconds left before the technique collapsed from its own weight. And when it did, he would be vulnerable—his qi reserves drained, his techniques slowed, his defenses weakened.

Jin Tian just had to survive thirty seconds.

He stopped trying to attack. He focused entirely on defense, on movement, on staying alive. He dodged and weaved and parried, letting Liu Feng's attacks flow around him like water around a stone. Each strike that came close was a lesson—a reminder of how Liu Feng moved, how he thought, how he fought.

Twenty seconds.

Liu Feng's attacks grew more frantic. He could feel the technique slipping, the qi draining faster than expected. His movements became wilder, less controlled.

Ten seconds.

"I'll end this!" Liu Feng roared, gathering the last of his flame aura into a single devastating strike. The fire condensed around his sword, turning the wooden blade white-hot. He swung with everything he had.

Jin Tian stepped forward.

Not back. Forward.

He moved into the attack, letting the tip of Liu Feng's sword pass inches from his face. His own sword came up in a straight thrust—no technique, no flourish, just pure intent and perfect form.

The tip of his wooden sword pressed against Liu Feng's throat.

The flame aura died.

Silence.

"Match to Jin Tian," the overseer said, his voice flat with shock. "Two points. Jin Tian advances."

Liu Feng stood frozen, the white-hot sword still raised, the heat of his failed attack dissipating into the air. He looked at Jin Tian with an expression that shifted from rage to confusion to something else entirely.

Something cold.

"I yield," Liu Feng said, lowering his sword. "You've earned your victory."

Jin Tian didn't lower his sword. "Why did you do it?"

Liu Feng blinked. "Do what?"

"Kill him."

The words hung in the air. The crowd went quiet. Even the overseer paused, his hand halfway to the flag that would end the match.

Liu Feng's face went pale. "What are you talking about?"

"I'm talking about the murder of Inner Disciple Zhao Wei," Jin Tian said, speaking loudly enough for everyone to hear. "Six months ago. Found dead in his quarters, his heart pierced by a blade that matched your sword's dimensions."

He had no proof of this. In his first life, Zhao Wei's murder had been blamed on a rogue cultivator from outside the sect, a convenient scapegoat who had been found dead three days later. But Jin Tian had always suspected. Zhao Wei had been investigating something—something connected to the seven disciples who would later betray him.

And Liu Feng had been the one who delivered Jin Tian's killing blow.

The connection was too convenient to ignore.

Liu Feng's face cycled through emotions too fast to track. "That's—that was ruled an accident. A rogue cultivator—"

"Who was found dead before he could be questioned," Jin Tian finished. "Convenient, don't you think?"

"You have no proof."

"I have my memories."

Liu Feng stared at him for a long moment. Then he did something unexpected.

He laughed.

"Memories," he repeated, shaking his head. "You think your memories can prove anything?" He stepped closer, his voice dropping to a whisper that only Jin Tian could hear. "You don't know what you're involved in, boy. You think you've uncovered something? You've barely scratched the surface."

"Then tell me."

Liu Feng smiled. It was the same smile Jin Tian had seen in his final moments—the smile of someone who knew a secret that would destroy everything.

"There's someone you need to meet," Liu Feng said. "Someone who's been watching you since the day you joined the sect."

"Who?"

"Meet me at the abandoned meditation pavilion behind the main hall. Midnight. Come alone." Liu Feng turned and walked away, leaving Jin Tian standing in the ring with the crowd's eyes on him and a storm of questions raging in his mind.

As Liu Feng reached the edge of the ring, he paused and looked back over his shoulder.

"Your memories are a curse, Jin Tian. But they might also be the only thing that saves you."

He disappeared into the crowd.

Jin Tian stood alone in the ring, the wooden sword still in his hand, the weight of three hundred years pressing down on his shoulders. He had won the match. He had uncovered a connection. He had taken another step toward the truth.

But as he looked at the spot where Liu Feng had vanished, he couldn't shake the feeling that he was walking into a trap.

A trap that someone had been setting for a very long time.
