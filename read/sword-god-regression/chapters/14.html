CHAPTER 14: The Shadow in the Flame

The arena was smaller than Jin Tian remembered. In his first life, the inner division tournament had been held in the Grand Coliseum, a massive structure capable of holding ten thousand spectators. But this was merely a training ring, cordoned off by formation flags and surrounded by a handful of elder disciples who had gathered to watch.

It made sense. The tournament wouldn't begin for another three days. This was just an exhibition match, a chance for the outer sect's prodigy to test himself against an established inner disciple.

Jin Tian stood at the edge of the ring, his wooden practice sword resting across his shoulders. The weapon felt wrong in his hands—too light, too brittle. In his prime, he had wielded the Heaven Sunder, a blade forged from a fallen star and quenched in the blood of a thousand demons. Now he held a stick.

But a stick in the hands of a master was still a weapon.

Across from him, Liu Feng entered the ring with the casual confidence of a predator surveying prey. He was taller than Jin Tian remembered, broader in the shoulders, his face carrying that same arrogant smirk that had haunted Jin Tian's dying moments. He carried a real sword—standard inner disciple issue, steel with a copper core, but sharp enough to kill.

"Outer disciple Jin Tian," Liu Feng said, the words dripping with condescension. "I heard you caused quite a stir in the selection hall. Chen Wei must be humiliated."

"Chen Wei will recover," Jin Tian replied evenly. "His pride is durable."

Liu Feng laughed, a harsh sound that echoed off the surrounding buildings. "You've got teeth. I'll give you that. Let's see if you've got anything else."

Elder Zhou raised his hand from the observation platform. "This is a sparring match to first blood or submission. No killing. No crippling techniques. Begin."

Liu Feng moved before the word finished echoing.

Fire exploded from his palm, a lance of crimson qi that streaked toward Jin Tian's chest. In his first life, this opening strike had caught him off guard, searing through his defensive stance and leaving him vulnerable for the follow-up.

Now he simply wasn't there.

He had shifted three inches to the left, letting the fire lance pass so close it singed the fabric of his robe. The heat was intense, but pain was an old friend. He had been burned before. He had been burned alive, once, in a cave-in during his fifth century of cultivation. This was nothing.

Liu Feng's eyes widened slightly, but he recovered quickly, pivoting into a low sweep aimed at Jin Tian's knees. The sword cut through the air with a whistle, but Jin Tian had already read the movement in the shift of his opponent's shoulders, the subtle adjustment of his hips.

He jumped, planted his foot on Liu Feng's blade, and launched himself into a spinning kick aimed at the inner disciple's temple.

Liu Feng blocked with his forearm, the impact sending a shockwave through the arena. He stumbled back, his smirk replaced by genuine surprise.

"You're fast," he admitted, shaking out his arm. "But speed isn't everything."

He planted his feet and raised his sword above his head. Qi gathered around him like a storm, heat shimmering off his skin until the air itself seemed to warp. The technique was familiar—too familiar.

*Flame Dragon Ascension.*

In Jin Tian's first life, Liu Feng had used this technique to kill a rogue cultivator who had been terrorizing a village. It was one of his signature moves, a powerful area-of-effect attack that consumed everything within thirty feet.

But here, in this training ring, with spectators barely ten feet away?

*He's not trying to win,* Jin Tian realized. *He's trying to kill me.*

The fire exploded outward.

Jin Tian moved, not away from the blast, but into it. His wooden sword extended before him, qi flowing through the grain in a pattern he had perfected over three hundred years. The *Void Piercer*—a technique that used the opponent's own energy to create a path through their attack.

The flames parted around him like water around a stone. He emerged on the other side, his wooden sword pressed against Liu Feng's throat.

Silence.

The elder disciples stared, their mouths hanging open. Elder Zhou had risen from his seat, his eyes wide with incredulity.

Liu Feng's sword clattered to the ground. His hands were shaking, not from fear, but from something else. Something Jin Tian couldn't read.

"First blood goes to the outer disciple," Elder Zhou announced, his voice carrying a hint of wonder. "The match is over."

Jin Tian lowered his sword and stepped back. But as he did, he noticed something. A mark on Liu Feng's wrist, barely visible beneath the sleeve of his robe. A tattoo, small and intricate, depicting a serpent coiled around a crescent moon.

The Mark of the Moon Serpent.

Jin Tian's blood ran cold.

He knew that mark. He had seen it before, in the aftermath of his death. Not on Liu Feng's body—he had never seen Liu Feng's body after the betrayal. No, he had seen it on the leader of the assassins who had hunted him through the Shadow Realm during his final days.

The Moon Serpent Sect. A shadow organization that operated in the spaces between the great sects, selling their services to the highest bidder. They had been the ones who provided the poison that weakened his qi. They had been the ones who tracked his movements, reported his weaknesses, engineered his downfall.

And Liu Feng, the man who had delivered the killing blow, bore their mark.

"You don't remember me, do you?" Liu Feng whispered, so quietly that only Jin Tian could hear.

Jin Tian froze.

"I thought not. You've changed too much. But I remember you, Sword God. I remember what you did to my village."

The words hit Jin Tian like a physical blow. He staggered back, his mind racing through centuries of memories, searching for a village, for a face, for anything that could explain the hatred burning in Liu Feng's eyes.

"I don't know what you're talking about," he said, his voice steady despite the chaos in his mind.

Liu Feng smiled, a cold, broken thing. "You will. Before this tournament is over, you will remember. And when you do, I'll be there to finish what I started."

He turned and walked away, leaving Jin Tian standing alone in the center of the ring, the wooden sword hanging limp in his hand.

The crowd was cheering, but Jin Tian didn't hear them. He was lost in a labyrinth of memories, searching for a truth he had never known.

*What did I do? What village? What crime could justify three hundred years of hatred?*

He looked down at his hands. They were still the hands of a twelve-year-old boy, soft and unmarked by the decades of battle that awaited him. But they were also the hands of the Sword God, soaked in the blood of thousands.

*How many villages had he destroyed? How many families had he torn apart?*

In his pursuit of power, in his rise to the peak of cultivation, he had left a trail of bodies behind him. He had always justified it as necessary, as the cost of progress in a world where the strong devoured the weak.

But now, staring at the mark of the Moon Serpent, he wondered if the cost had been too high.

Elder Zhou approached him, his expression unreadable. "Impressive display, Jin Tian. You've proven yourself capable beyond your years. The inner division will be honored to have you."

Jin Tian bowed mechanically. "Thank you, Elder."

"I have to ask, though. That technique you used—the one that parted the flames. I've never seen anything like it. Where did you learn it?"

The question was innocent, but Jin Tian felt the trap beneath it. If he answered honestly, he would reveal his secret. If he lied, he would invite suspicion.

*Think. You've been here before. You know how to navigate these waters.*

"I read about it in a scroll," he said, choosing his words carefully. "In the library. The *Void Piercer* technique was developed by an ancient cultivator who specialized in countering fire-aspected attacks."

Elder Zhou's eyes narrowed. "I've spent forty years in the sect's library. I've never seen such a scroll."

"It was in the restricted section," Jin Tian said, letting a hint of embarrassment creep into his voice. "I may have... borrowed it without permission."

For a long moment, Elder Zhou stared at him. Then he laughed, a booming sound that echoed across the arena. "A rule-breaker with talent. You'll fit right in with the inner disciples. Just try not to get caught next time."

He clapped Jin Tian on the shoulder and walked away, still chuckling to himself.

Jin Tian let out a breath he hadn't realized he was holding. The lie had been accepted, but it would only hold for so long. Sooner or later, someone would start asking questions he couldn't answer.

He looked at the mark on his wrist, the same mark he had seen on Liu Feng. He had been so focused on his seven disciples, on the betrayal that had ended his first life, that he had never considered the possibility that his enemies extended beyond them.

The Moon Serpent Sect had been involved. And if they had been involved in his death, they had likely been involved in the events that led to it.

*Who hired them? Who wanted me dead badly enough to pay the Moon Serpent's price?*

The answer, he suspected, lay in the memories he had buried. The faces of the dead. The villages he had burned. The families he had destroyed.

For the first time in three hundred years, Jin Tian was afraid of what he might find when he looked into his own past.

He turned and walked away from the arena, the weight of a forgotten crime pressing down on his shoulders. The tournament would begin in three days, and with it, the first steps toward uncovering the truth.

But as he walked, he couldn't shake the feeling that he was being watched. That somewhere, in the shadows between the buildings, eyes were tracking his every move.

The Moon Serpent had found him once.

They would find him again.

And this time, he would be ready.

Or so he told himself.

But as he disappeared into the maze of the sect's corridors, a single thought echoed in his mind, growing louder with every step:

*What did I do?*
