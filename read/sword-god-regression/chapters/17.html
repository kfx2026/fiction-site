Chapter 17: The Eighth Disciple's Shadow

~1.9k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The training grounds were empty when Jin Tian arrived, the afternoon sun casting long shadows across the worn stone flags. He had three hours before his match against Liu Feng, and every second counted.

He closed his eyes, letting his mind drift back through three hundred years of accumulated knowledge. Liu Feng. Fire-aspected techniques. Aggressive combat style. The man had been a terror in the inner division, his flames consuming everything in their path. But Jin Tian remembered something else—something he had only noticed in retrospect, after decades of hindsight and bitter reflection.

Liu Feng had always fought with a slight hesitation in his left foot. A half-step delay when pivoting for his signature technique, the Crimson Phoenix Descends. It was a flaw so minor that most would never see it, but Jin Tian had been a Sword God. He had learned to read bodies like others read books.

*That hesitation will be his death,* Jin Tian thought, drawing his practice sword. *Or mine, if I'm not careful.*

He moved through the forms slowly, deliberately, letting muscle memory reawaken. The Sword Heart Sutra hummed beneath his skin, his qi flowing through channels that had been scarred and broken in his past life. Now they were clean. Unused. Full of potential.

Three hours passed like water through fingers.

When the gong sounded, Jin Tian opened his eyes. His robes were soaked with sweat, his muscles trembling with exhaustion, but his mind was clear. Sharp. Ready.

The inner division arena was larger than the outer division's, ringed with formation arrays that would absorb stray attacks and protect the spectators. Hundreds of disciples had gathered, their whispers creating a constant hum of anticipation.

"This should be interesting," a voice said from behind him.

Jin Tian turned to find a young woman watching him with open curiosity. She was plain-featured, with short-cropped hair and a scar running from her temple to her jaw—a mark of violence that most inner disciples would have healed away. She wore the robes of an inner disciple, but there was a roughness to her that spoke of battles hard-won.

"And you are?" Jin Tian asked.

"Nobody important. Just someone who likes watching ambitious children get crushed." She smiled, revealing a missing tooth. "You're the outer disciple who challenged Chen Wei's spot, aren't you? Bold move. Stupid, but bold."

"Thank you."

"It wasn't a compliment." She leaned closer, her voice dropping to a whisper. "Liu Feng has been training specifically for this tournament. He's been using techniques I've never seen before. Something's changed in him these past few months."

Jin Tian's blood went cold. "What do you mean?"

"He used to be predictable. Brute force, overwhelming power, no finesse. But lately..." She shook her head. "He moves differently. Calculates differently. It's like someone's been teaching him."

"Who?"

"If I knew that, I wouldn't be warning you." She stepped back, her smile returning. "Good luck, outer disciple. You're going to need it."

Before Jin Tian could ask more, the arena doors opened, and Liu Feng strode onto the platform.

He was older than Jin Tian remembered, perhaps twenty, with a lean, predatory build and eyes that burned with barely contained fire. His hair was the color of dying embers, and his robes seemed to shimmer with heat haze. In his hand, he carried a blade that looked like it had been forged from a single flame—crimson, pulsing, alive.

*The Crimson Sun Sword,* Jin Tian recognized. *He shouldn't have that for another five years.*

Something was wrong. Terribly wrong.

"Outer Disciple Jin Tian," Elder Zhou announced, his voice carrying across the arena. "You have been granted the honor of testing your skills against Inner Disciple Liu Feng. This match will determine whether you are worthy of promotion. Do you accept the terms?"

Jin Tian stepped onto the platform, facing his killer. "I accept."

Liu Feng's smile was cold, predatory. "So you're the one who humiliated Chen Wei. I expected someone... bigger."

"Size doesn't determine victory," Jin Tian replied. "Skill does."

"Then let's see how much skill you have."

The gong sounded.

Liu Feng exploded forward, his blade trailing crimson fire. The heat was immediate, suffocating, pressing against Jin Tian's skin like a physical force. He sidestepped, letting the flaming blade pass inches from his face, and countered with a thrust to Liu Feng's exposed ribs.

But Liu Feng was already moving, his body twisting in a way that was completely unfamiliar. He caught Jin Tian's blade on his own and shoved, sending Jin Tian stumbling backward.

*He's faster than he should be,* Jin Tian realized. *And he's reading my movements.*

He pressed the attack, using his superior technique to drive Liu Feng back. For a moment, it worked—Liu Feng retreated, parrying desperately, his confidence flickering. But then something changed. Liu Feng's eyes glazed over, and his movements became mechanical. Perfect.

*This isn't right.*

Jin Tian launched a feint to the left, followed by a true strike to the right—a combination that should have taken any inner disciple off guard. But Liu Feng blocked it as if he had seen it coming a thousand times. His counter-strike caught Jin Tian across the ribs, sending blood spraying across the arena floor.

The crowd gasped.

Jin Tian stumbled, clutching his side. The wound was shallow, but the fire-aspected qi was already spreading, trying to burn its way deeper into his flesh. He suppressed it with his own qi, but the effort cost him precious seconds.

"You're fighting well," Liu Feng said, his voice oddly flat. "But you can't win. I've been trained for this moment. *Prepared* for it."

"Trained by whom?" Jin Tian asked, buying time.

Liu Feng's smile widened, and for just a moment, Jin Tian saw something in his eyes that made his blood run cold. Recognition. Not of a rival. Not of an enemy.

Of a target.

"You'll find out soon enough," Liu Feng said. "When you're dead."

He attacked again, his blade becoming a storm of fire and steel. Jin Tian deflected, dodged, countered—but every move he made was anticipated. Liu Feng was reading him like a book, and the only thing keeping Jin Tian alive was the three hundred years of experience that let him adapt faster than his body should have been able to.

But experience wasn't enough. Not against someone who knew his every move before he made it.

*Someone taught him my fighting style,* Jin Tian realized with dawning horror. *Someone from my past life.*

The thought was impossible. He had been reborn three hundred years in the past. No one should remember the Sword God. No one should know the patterns he had developed over centuries of combat.

And yet.

Liu Feng's sword lashed out, and Jin Tian barely managed to deflect it. The impact sent a shockwave through his arms, and he felt something crack in his wrist. He stumbled, his vision swimming.

"I have to admit," Liu Feng said, circling him like a predator, "I was disappointed when I heard I'd be fighting an outer disciple. I thought it'd be boring." His blade traced lazy patterns in the air. "But you're different. You fight like someone who's been doing this for decades. Centuries, even."

"Maybe I'm just talented."

Liu Feng laughed, and there was nothing human in the sound. "Talented. Yes. That's one word for it." He paused, tilting his head. "Do you know what they call you in the inner division? The Ghost. Because you appear from nowhere, win impossible fights, and then vanish back into obscurity." His eyes narrowed. "I don't believe in ghosts."

"Then what do you believe in?"

"I believe in patterns," Liu Feng said. "And your pattern is wrong. You fight like someone who knows what's coming. Like someone who's seen it all before." His smile turned cruel. "Like someone who's died before."

The words hit Jin Tian like a physical blow. *He knows. How does he know?*

Liu Feng saw the reaction and laughed again. "That's right. I see it in your eyes. You're not just talented. You're *broken*. Something happened to you, something that gave you knowledge you shouldn't have." He raised his sword, flames dancing along its edge. "But it doesn't matter. Knowledge without power is useless. And you, Outer Disciple Jin Tian, don't have enough power to save yourself."

He attacked.

The flames consumed the arena, and Jin Tian was thrown backward, his body screaming in protest. He hit the ground hard, his sword skittering away from his grasp. Liu Feng stood over him, blade raised for the killing blow.

"Any last words?" Liu Feng asked.

Jin Tian looked up at his killer—the same face that had been the last thing he saw before darkness claimed him in his past life. But this time, he noticed something he hadn't before. Something he'd been too blind to see.

Liu Feng's left hand was trembling. Not from exertion. Not from fear.

From the strain of resisting something.

*He's being controlled,* Jin Tian realized. *Someone's puppeting him.*

"Your master," Jin Tian said, his voice hoarse. "Whoever taught you my patterns. They're controlling you, aren't they?"

Liu Feng's face twitched, and for just a moment, the mechanical precision in his eyes flickered. Something human surfaced—fear, desperation, a plea for help.

Then it was gone, replaced by cold emptiness.

"That's not your concern," Liu Feng said, his voice hollow. "Goodbye, Jin Tian."

The blade fell.

And Jin Tian moved.

He rolled to the side, ignoring the pain in his wrist, and kicked Liu Feng's knee with all his strength. The joint buckled, and Liu Feng stumbled, his perfect form breaking for just an instant. Jin Tian scrambled to his feet, grabbed his sword, and drove it upward—

Into Liu Feng's shoulder.

Not a killing blow. Not even a serious wound. But enough to break the rhythm.

Liu Feng screamed, and for a moment, his eyes cleared. He looked at Jin Tian with something like gratitude, something like fear. "Run," he whispered. "She's coming. She knows about you. She knows *everything*."

"Who?" Jin Tian demanded. "Who's coming?"

But Liu Feng's eyes were already glazing over again, the mechanical precision returning. He pulled Jin Tian's sword from his shoulder and tossed it aside, his wounds healing with unnatural speed.

"You've delayed the inevitable," Liu Feng said, his voice flat. "But it doesn't matter. The Eighth Disciple sends her regards."

Jin Tian's blood turned to ice.

The Eighth Disciple. In his past life, he had taken seven disciples. But there had been an eighth—a girl he had rejected, deemed unworthy of his teachings, cast out from the sect. She had vanished, and he had forgotten her.

Until now.

*She's back,* he realized. *And she's been waiting for this moment.*

Liu Feng raised his sword for the final blow, but before he could strike, a bell tolled across the arena. The match was over. Time had run out.

Elder Zhou's voice cut through the silence. "The match is declared a draw. Both disciples will advance to the next round."

The crowd erupted in chaos.

Liu Feng's face twisted with rage, but he couldn't move. The arena formations held him in place, the match's end binding his actions. He could only glare at Jin Tian with hatred burning in his eyes.

"This isn't over," he hissed.

"No," Jin Tian agreed, his heart pounding
