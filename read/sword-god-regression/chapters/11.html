Chapter 11: The Flames of Truth

~1.8k words · Updated 2026-06-22

Jin Tian did not return to his quarters.

Instead, he walked the empty training grounds of the outer sect, his bare feet pressing into the cold stone as the afternoon sun cast long shadows across the courtyard. The air smelled of dust and dried blood—remnants of the qualifiers that had ended only hours ago.

Three hours until he faced Liu Feng.

Three hours until he stood across from the man who had ended his first life.

The name echoed in his skull like a wound that refused to heal. Liu Feng. Ranked seventh among the inner disciples. A practitioner of the Blazing Sun Arts, a fire-aspected cultivation method that allowed him to weapons channel searing qi through his blade. In his first life, Jin Tian had considered Liu Feng a worthy rival—someone to push against, to grow from.

He had never considered him an enemy.

But enemies didn't stab you in the heart while you were distracted. Enemies didn't watch your blood pool beneath you with empty, calculating eyes. And enemies certainly didn't whisper your name as if it were a prayer of gratitude.

*"Jin Tian... thank you."*

Those had been Liu Feng's last words to him in his first life. The memory surfaced now with crystalline clarity—the way Liu Feng's voice had cracked with something that sounded almost like relief. Like a burden being lifted.

Jin Tian stopped walking.

A breeze stirred the dust around him, carrying the distant sound of clashing weapons from the inner sect training grounds. He closed his eyes and reached into his dantian, feeling the familiar pulse of his cultivation base. Meridian Opening, Peak Stage. Not enough. Not nearly enough.

Liu Feng was at the Qi Condensation stage—a full realm above him. The gap between Meridian Opening and Qi Condensation was not merely a difference in power; it was a difference in kind. Qi Condensation cultivators could project their qi externally, forming shields, launching attacks, enhancing their weapons with elemental properties. A Meridian Opening cultivator could only circulate qi internally.

The gap was supposed to be unbridgeable.

But Jin Tian had bridged it before. In his first life, he had defeated Liu Feng in the inner division tournament's semi-finals, using a technique he had developed over decades of cultivation—the Shadowless Sword Art. It was a style that relied on speed and deception, on striking where the opponent least expected it.

The problem was that technique required a foundation of Qi Condensation to execute properly.

Jin Tian opened his eyes. His hand drifted to the hilt of his practice sword—a cheap, unremarkable blade that had been issued to him on his first day as an outer disciple. The metal was poorly refined, the edge already chipped from months of use.

He drew the blade and assumed a stance.

The Shadowless Sword Art had seven forms. The first three could be performed at Meridian Opening, though they would lack the full power of the later techniques. But Jin Tian didn't need power. He needed precision. He needed to understand Liu Feng—not the Liu Feng of three hundred years ago, but the Liu Feng who existed now.

In his first life, they had met in the tournament's final stage. Now, they would meet in the first round. The timeline had shifted.

*Why?*

The question gnawed at him. He had changed nothing of significance—only his performance in the qualifiers. But that single change had ripple effects, altering the bracket, forcing new matchups, creating a path that led directly to Liu Feng.

Was this fate? Or was something else at work?

Jin Tian dismissed the thought. He didn't believe in fate. Fate was an excuse for the weak and the complacent. He had been given a second chance, and he would carve his own destiny with the edge of his sword.

He began to move.

The first form of the Shadowless Sword Art was called "Falling Leaf." It was a feinting strike designed to draw the opponent's guard high, leaving their lower body exposed for a follow-up attack. Jin Tian executed it slowly, feeling the way his qi flowed through his meridians, the way the blade responded to his intent.

Too slow. Too weak.

He tried again, increasing his speed. The blade whistled through the air, tracing an arc of silver light. Better, but still not enough. The technique required an explosive burst of qi at the moment of contact—a burst he couldn't generate at his current cultivation level.

He would have to adapt.

Jin Tian lowered his sword and began to pace. His mind raced through possibilities, discarding each one as quickly as it formed. He couldn't match Liu Feng's power. He couldn't match his speed. He couldn't match his qi reserves.

But he could match his experience.

In his first life, Jin Tian had studied Liu Feng's techniques for years. He knew the Blazing Sun Arts inside and out—their strengths, their weaknesses, their tells. He knew that Liu Feng favored a right-handed overhead strike, that he always led with fire before following with steel, that his defensive posture left a gap under his left arm when he was preparing to unleash his signature technique.

The question was whether that knowledge still held true. Three hundred years had passed in Jin Tian's mind, but only days had passed in this timeline. Liu Feng was still young, still developing, still learning the techniques that would define him as a cultivator.

Which meant he was also still making mistakes.

A smile touched Jin Tian's lips. Not a warm smile, but the smile of a hunter who had found the crack in his prey's armor.

"You seem pleased."

The voice came from behind him, soft and familiar. Jin Tian turned, his blade rising instinctively before he recognized the speaker.

Liang Xue stood at the edge of the training ground, her arms crossed, her expression unreadable. She was dressed in the blue robes of an inner disciple, her hair bound in a simple ponytail that accentuated the sharp lines of her face.

"I didn't expect to find you here," she said, stepping onto the stone courtyard. "I thought you'd be resting before your match."

"I don't need rest."

"Everyone needs rest." She stopped a few feet away, her eyes dropping to his blade. "You're practicing. Before the biggest match of your life."

"I'm preparing."

"For what? You're facing an inner disciple. A Qi Condensation cultivator." Liang Xue's voice carried no mockery, only a strange note of concern. "Do you even understand what you're walking into?"

Jin Tian considered his answer carefully. Liang Xue had been an ally in his first life—a trusted friend, a fierce warrior, one of the few people he had genuinely respected. But she had also been absent during his final moments. He had never learned where she was, what she was doing, why she hadn't been there to help him.

He still didn't trust her completely.

"I understand," he said simply. "I understand that Liu Feng has a tell when he's about to use the Blazing Sun's Wrath. I understand that his left side is weaker than his right. I understand that he always spends the first three exchanges testing his opponent's strength before committing to an attack."

Liang Xue's eyes widened. "How do you—"

"I've been watching him. Studying him." The lie came easily. "Every match he's fought in the inner sect has been recorded. I've watched them all."

"That's..." She paused, her expression shifting to something like respect. "That's thorough. Most outer disciples don't bother learning about their opponents. They just charge in and hope for the best."

"Most outer disciples die young."

A silence stretched between them. Liang Xue studied him with renewed intensity, as if seeing him for the first time. Her hand drifted to the hilt of her own sword—a beautiful blade wrapped in leather, the guard shaped like a crescent moon.

"You're not what I expected," she said finally.

"And what did you expect?"

"I don't know. Someone smaller. Someone more afraid." She shook her head. "You're neither. You're standing here, practicing your forms, planning your strategy, as if facing an inner disciple is just another match."

"It is just another match."

"No, it's not." Liang Xue stepped closer, her voice dropping to a whisper. "Liu Feng is not like the other inner disciples. He's connected. His family has ties to the sect elders, to the council, to people who can make problems disappear. If you beat him, you'll make enemies. Powerful ones."

Jin Tian met her gaze. "And if I lose?"

"Then you'll be forgotten. Just another outer disciple who reached too high and fell."

"That's not much of a choice."

"No," Liang Xue agreed. "It's not." She turned to leave, pausing at the edge of the courtyard. "One more thing. I heard something interesting in the inner sect this morning. A rumor."

"What kind of rumor?"

"There's been talk of a traitor in the outer sect. Someone feeding information to a rival sect. The elders have been investigating for weeks, and they've narrowed it down to three suspects." She glanced back at him, her eyes unreadable. "One of them is you."

Jin Tian's blood ran cold.

"The evidence is circumstantial," she continued. "But it's enough to warrant an inquiry. If you lose your match tonight, the inquiry will proceed. If you win, it might buy you enough time to clear your name."

"How do you know this?"

"Because I'm the one who found the evidence." Liang Xue's voice hardened. "And I know it's a fabrication. Someone is setting you up, Jin Tian. Someone wants you gone. Not just defeated—gone."

She left without another word, her footsteps fading into the evening shadows.

Jin Tian stood alone in the courtyard, his sword hanging loosely at his side. The weight of her words pressed down on him, heavier than any blade.

Someone was setting him up.

Someone wanted him dead.

And in three hours, he would step into the arena against a man who had killed him once before—while a knife waited for him in the dark, ready to strike if he failed.

He looked up at the sky, where the first stars were beginning to emerge.

*So this is how it begins,* he thought. *Not with a blade, but with a lie.*

Jin Tian sheathed his sword and began walking toward the arena. He had three hours to prepare for a battle that would decide more than just his rank.

He had three hours to become the man who would survive the night.
