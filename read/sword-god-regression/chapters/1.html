Chapter 1: The Seventh Betrayal

~2.0k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The pain came from seven directions at once.

Jin Tian, the Sword God of the Northern Wastes, felt each blade enter his body with surgical precision. His own disciples—the seven he had raised from children, taught to cultivate, gifted with techniques that would make emperors weep—had chosen their moment perfectly.

The first strike came from his left. Little Phoenix, his youngest, drove a short sword through his kidney. Her eyes, the same eyes that had once looked at him with pure adoration, now held only cold calculation.

"Why?" he managed, blood bubbling from his lips.

She didn't answer. But the second strike did.

"Because your era is over, Master."

Third Disciple Wei Chen twisted the blade in Jin Tian's back. The poison on its edge—Night Lotus Venom, rare enough to cost a kingdom—was already spreading through his meridians, freezing his qi pathways one by one.

The third strike severed his right hamstring. Fourth Disciple Li Ming, the one who had always been too eager to please, too desperate for validation. "The Heavenly Sword Sect has offered us protection," Li Ming said, his voice trembling. "They promised us resources. Position. A future."

"A future built on betrayal?" Jin Tian laughed, though it cost him blood. "I taught you better than this."

"You taught us to survive," said Fifth Disciple Shen Yue, stepping into his field of vision. Her beauty was wasted on cruelty. "And survival means knowing when to abandon a sinking ship."

Sixth and Seventh disciples—the twins, Hao and Jun—delivered the fourth and fifth strikes simultaneously. Twin sabers, their signature technique, practiced to perfection over twenty years. Jin Tian had designed that technique himself. He never imagined it would be used against him.

"Father," Hao said, still calling him by the title Jin Tian had never encouraged but never forbade, "please understand. This is not personal."

"Then what is it?" Jin Tian's vision was dimming. His cultivation base, three hundred years of accumulated power, was being drained by the Night Lotus Venom. He could feel his dantian cracking, his core shattering like a frozen lake under a hammer.

"It's opportunity," said the first disciple. The strongest. The one Jin Tian had trusted most.

Xiao Feng had not struck yet. He stood before his master, his long sword still sheathed, his face unreadable. Of all the seven, he had been with Jin Tian the longest. Forty years. A lifetime.

"Opportunity," Jin Tian repeated. The word tasted like ash.

"The Heavenly Sword Sect wants the Nine Heavens Sword Manual," Xiao Feng said. "They've wanted it for centuries. You possess the only complete copy in existence."

"I possess it," Jin Tian agreed. "And you want it."

"We want what it can give us." Xiao Feng finally drew his sword. The blade sang as it left the scabbard—Jin Tian had forged that sword himself, using Starfall Iron from a meteor that had crashed in the Eastern Wastes. He had given it to Xiao Feng on the day he accepted him as his first disciple.

"Give us the manual," Xiao Feng said, his sword point hovering over Jin Tian's throat, "and we will make your death painless."

"And if I refuse?"

"Then we will make it last for days."

Jin Tian looked at his seven disciples. His legacy. His greatest achievement and now his greatest failure. He had taken them from poverty, from obscurity, from families that had abandoned them. He had fed them, clothed them, taught them the Way of the Sword. He had loved them as a father loves his children.

And they had repaid him with steel and poison.

"Very well," Jin Tian said, his voice barely a whisper. "Come closer. I will give you what you want."

Xiao Feng leaned in, his eyes gleaming with anticipation. The other six disciples pressed closer, hungry for the secret that would elevate them to the peak of the martial world.

Jin Tian smiled.

In the instant before death, a cultivator's soul experiences something unique. A final burst of insight, if they are lucky. A moment of perfect clarity before the darkness claims them.

Jin Tian's insight came in the form of a question: If you could do it all again, knowing what you know now, what would you change?

The answer came immediately.

Everything.

And then the blade descended.

---

Jin Tian's eyes snapped open.

He was staring at a wooden ceiling. Rough-hewn beams, stained with age and smoke. The smell of cheap oil lamps and unwashed bodies filled his nostrils. Somewhere nearby, someone was snoring.

He sat up so fast his head spun.

His body—no, this was wrong. His hands were too small. His arms were too thin. His cultivation base, that vast ocean of power that had taken three centuries to build, was gone. Completely empty. Not even a single strand of qi remained in his dantian.

"What—"

The voice that came out of his mouth was not the voice of a seventy-year-old Sword God. It was the voice of a child. High-pitched. Untested.

Jin Tian looked down at himself. He was wearing rough gray robes, the kind issued to outer disciples of a minor sect. His body was small and underdeveloped, barely five feet tall. He had no calluses on his hands, no scars on his arms.

A memory surfaced. Not his memory—not the memory of the Sword God—but the memory of the boy whose body he now occupied.

The boy's name was also Jin Tian. He was twelve years old. An outer disciple of the Falling Cloud Sect, a minor cultivation sect in the Eastern Province. He had been born to a concubine of a minor noble house, sent to the sect at age eight when his potential for cultivation was deemed "mediocre at best."

Today was the day before the annual sect tournament.

Tomorrow, in the tournament, this boy would lose his first match. He would be humiliated. He would be assigned to the sect's herb garden, where he would spend the next six years doing manual labor before being expelled for failing to reach the second stage of qi condensation.

That was the boy's fate.

But the boy was no longer in control.

Jin Tian—the real Jin Tian, the Sword God who had lived three hundred years and died at the hands of his disciples—sat on the thin sleeping mat and took stock of his situation.

He was alive. Somehow. Impossible as it seemed, he had been given a second chance. He had been sent back in time, into the body of his twelve-year-old self, three hundred years before his death.

No. That wasn't quite right.

He examined the memories more carefully. This body's memories were different from his own. In his original life, he had been born a prodigy, the son of a sect elder, given every advantage from birth. But this boy—this alternate version of himself—had been born to nothing. A concubine's son. A nobody.

The timeline had diverged. This was not his past. This was a different world, a different fate.

But the knowledge remained.

Jin Tian closed his eyes and reached into his mind. The Nine Heavens Sword Manual was there, every character, every diagram, every secret technique he had spent a lifetime collecting and creating. The Falling Rain Sword Technique. The Heaven Sundering Strike. The Void Step. All of it, preserved in perfect detail.

He also remembered his disciples. Every face. Every word. Every betrayal.

And he remembered something else. Something he had never known in his previous life.

The Heavenly Sword Sect. The name Xiao Feng had mentioned before killing him. In his original life, Jin Tian had never encountered them. He had died before he could learn their secrets. But now... now he had time. Time to grow stronger. Time to discover who had orchestrated his death. Time to prepare.

Time to make them pay.

A bell rang somewhere in the compound. Morning assembly. The boy's memories told him that outer disciples were required to attend, to perform basic qi circulation exercises, and to receive their daily tasks.

Jin Tian rose. His body protested—it was weak, untrained, barely capable of the most basic movements. But he ignored the pain. Pain was a teacher. Pain was a friend.

He dressed in the gray robes, noting the coarse fabric against his skin. In his previous life, he had worn silk and spirit-threaded armor. Now he wore rags. It didn't matter. The sword did not care about the clothes of the one who wielded it.

The dormitory was a long building with rows of sleeping mats. Other boys his age were stirring, rubbing sleep from their eyes. They paid him no attention. In their world, Jin Tian was invisible. A background character in the story of their own lives.

He left the building and followed the stream of disciples toward the training ground. The Falling Cloud Sect was small, maybe five hundred disciples total, with only a handful of elders and a single Grand Elder who had reached the Nascent Soul stage. In Jin Tian's previous life, he would have considered such a sect beneath his notice. Now, it was his starting point.

The training ground was a flat expanse of packed earth, surrounded by wooden practice dummies and weapon racks. The outer disciples arranged themselves in rows, facing the platform where the sect elders would address them.

Jin Tian took his place among the other twelve-year-olds. He stood still, his hands at his sides, his breathing even. He observed everything. The way the elders moved. The qi signatures of the senior disciples. The layout of the compound. The defensive formations hidden in the walls.

Three hundred years of experience filtered through a twelve-year-old's eyes.

The head elder, a man named Feng Hao, stepped onto the platform. He was at the Core Formation stage, impressive for a sect of this size, but laughable compared to what Jin Tian had been. Feng Hao's qi was unstable, his foundation flawed. He had probably used pills to advance, sacrificing long-term potential for short-term gain.

"Disciples," Feng Hao announced, his voice carrying across the training ground, "tomorrow is the annual tournament. As you know, your performance will determine your placement for the coming year. Those who excel will be promoted to inner disciples. Those who fail..." He paused, letting the threat hang in the air. "Will be assigned to support duties."

A murmur rippled through the crowd. Jin Tian noticed several boys his age looking nervous. One of them, a chubby kid with a frightened face, was actually trembling.

The tournament. In the boy's memories, this was a source of terror. He knew he would lose. He knew he would be humiliated. He knew his future was already written.

But Jin Tian was not that boy anymore.

"Before we begin today's exercises," Feng Hao continued, "I want to introduce a special guest. Disciple Xiao Feng of the Heavenly Sword Sect has graciously agreed to observe our tournament."

Jin Tian's blood turned to ice.

Xiao Feng stepped onto the platform.

He was young. No more than twenty, with the arrogant bearing of someone who had never known true defeat. His robes were white, embroidered with golden clouds—the symbol of the Heavenly Sword Sect. His sword hung at his hip, a fine blade but unremarkable.

Jin Tian stared at the face of his murderer.

The same face. The same eyes. The same cruel mouth that had smiled as he drove a sword through his master's heart.

He was here. In this time. In this place.

The Heavenly Sword Sect had already sent someone to observe the Falling Cloud Sect tournament. Why? What were they looking for? What were they planning?
