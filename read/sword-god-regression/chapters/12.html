Chapter 12: The Price of Victory

~2.0k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The arena bells had not yet rung, but Jin Tian could already hear the blood pounding in his ears.

Three hours. He had three hours to prepare for a confrontation with a man who had ended his life in a past existence. Three hours to bridge the gap between a twelve-year-old outer disciple's body and the techniques of a Sword God reborn.

He slipped away from the gathering crowd, weaving through the stone corridors of the sect's inner compound. His robes still bore the scent of incense from the judgment hall, and his hands trembled—not from fear, but from the raw, searing anger that threatened to consume him.

*Focus.* He forced his breathing to slow. *Anger is a blade without a handle. It cuts the wielder first.*

He found an empty training pavilion hidden behind the main hall, its wooden floor worn smooth by generations of disciples. The afternoon sun cast long shadows through the lattice windows, painting stripes of gold and darkness across the practice room.

Jin Tian closed his eyes and reached inward.

His cultivation base was still pitifully small. In his first life, he had been at the sixth level of Qi Condensation by this age, a prodigy among prodigies. Now he barely scraped the third level, his meridians still clogged with impurities that would take months to clear.

But cultivation base was not everything. Not when you had mastered sixteen sword forms across three hundred years of bitter training.

He drew the practice sword from the rack—a simple wooden blade, unremarkable in every way. To the outside observer, it was identical to the hundreds of other practice swords in the sect. But Jin Tian felt its weight, its balance, its subtle imperfections, and adjusted his grip accordingly.

"Liu Feng," he murmured to the empty room. "Ranked seventh among inner disciples. Known for the Crimson Lotus Flame technique. Aggressive, relentless, but predictable in his patterns."

He had watched Liu Feng fight dozens of times in his first life. Had studied his movements, his tells, the way he shifted his weight before launching a fire-aspected strike. They had been rivals once, equals who pushed each other to greater heights.

Until the day Liu Feng's blade found his heart.

*Why?* The question burned in Jin Tian's chest like a brand. *What did they promise you? What could possibly justify—*

He stopped himself. Answers would come. But first, he had to survive.

Jin Tian began to move through the forms, starting slow, building speed. The wooden sword sang through the air, tracing arcs that seemed to bend the light around them. He was not practicing the sect's standard techniques—those were too rigid, too predictable. Instead, he wove together fragments of advanced sword styles from his past life, adapting them to his current body's limitations.

The first form: Falling Petal. A defensive technique that created the illusion of multiple blade trajectories, forcing the opponent to hesitate.

The second form: Hidden Current. A strike that flowed beneath the opponent's guard, targeting the gaps in their qi circulation.

The third form: *Thousand Reflections*—but his body screamed in protest. His meridians were too weak, his cultivation too shallow. The technique fizzled, sending a spike of pain through his arm.

Jin Tian stumbled, catching himself against a pillar. Blood beaded on his lip where he had bitten through the skin.

*Fool,* he cursed himself. *You're trying to run before you can walk.*

He needed a different approach. Liu Feng was stronger, faster, and more experienced in this life. Direct confrontation would end in disaster. But Liu Feng also had a weakness—a flaw that Jin Tian had discovered in their many spars during his first lifetime.

Liu Feng's Crimson Lotus technique required precise control of fire qi through his right meridian. If that meridian was disrupted, even briefly, his offensive would collapse.

Jin Tian's mind raced through possibilities. He had no cultivation techniques capable of disrupting meridians at his current level. But he didn't need qi techniques. Not when he knew exactly where to strike.

The human body had pressure points—junctures where qi flowed close to the surface. A precise strike, delivered with enough force, could momentarily stun a meridian. Against a weaker opponent, it could paralyze an entire limb.

Against Liu Feng, it might buy Jin Tian the opening he needed.

He practiced the strike a hundred times. A hundred more. His arm ached, his fingers blistered, but he did not stop. The sun crawled across the sky, and the bells of the arena began to toll.

Jin Tian lowered his sword, breathing hard. Sweat dripped from his brow, and his muscles screamed for rest. But something else stirred within him—a cold certainty that had nothing to do with confidence and everything to do with purpose.

He would face Liu Feng. He would survive. And he would learn the truth.

---

The inner division arena was a different world from the outer disciple training grounds.

Carved from white jade and reinforced with formation arrays that hummed with barely contained power, the arena could withstand attacks that would level mountains. Seating rose in tiered rings around the combat space, packed with inner disciples, elders, and visiting dignitaries.

Jin Tian stood at the entrance, feeling the weight of hundreds of eyes upon him. Whispers rippled through the crowd.

"Is that the outer disciple who challenged Chen Wei?"

"He's just a child. What is Elder Zhou thinking?"

"I heard the boy's efficiency score was higher than Chen Wei's. Must be a fluke."

Jin Tian ignored them. His attention was fixed on the figure standing at the far end of the arena.

Liu Feng was tall for his age, with broad shoulders and a shock of red hair that matched his fiery temperament. He wore the crimson robes of an inner disciple, and a blade of remarkable quality hung at his waist—a spirit weapon, if Jin Tian's eyes did not deceive him. The sword's edge shimmered with a faint orange glow, heat distortion rising from the metal.

Their eyes met across the arena floor. Liu Feng's expression was unreadable, but Jin Tian saw something flicker in his gaze. Recognition? No, that was impossible. They had never met in this life.

But there was something else. A tension in Liu Feng's shoulders, a tightness around his eyes that spoke of discomfort. He did not want to be here.

*Interesting.*

Elder Zhou rose from his seat, his voice carrying across the arena without effort. "The first match of the inner division tournament: Outer Disciple Jin Tian versus Inner Disciple Liu Feng. The match will continue until one party yields, is unable to continue, or is thrown from the arena. No lethal strikes are permitted."

A murmur of disappointment rippled through the crowd. No lethal strikes meant no bloodshed—a restriction that made the match less entertaining for the spectators.

Jin Tian stepped onto the jade floor. The formation arrays hummed beneath his feet, and he felt his qi respond, drawn upward by the ambient energy of the arena. It was a small advantage, but he would take it.

"Begin!" Elder Zhou's voice rang out.

Liu Feng moved first, as Jin Tian had expected. He exploded forward, his crimson robes trailing fire as he drew his blade in a single, fluid motion. The spirit weapon screamed through the air, leaving a trail of heat that distorted the space around it.

Jin Tian did not block. He could not block. Instead, he sidestepped, letting the strike pass within inches of his chest. The heat washed over him, searing his robes, but he was already moving, circling around Liu Feng's extended guard.

The inner disciple recovered quickly, spinning to face him. "Fast," he said, his voice carrying a hint of surprise. "But speed won't save you."

He pressed the attack, his blade becoming a blur of fire and steel. Each strike was precise, powerful, and backed by the full weight of his cultivation base. Jin Tian dodged, weaved, and parried only when absolutely necessary, each deflection sending shocks through his arms.

*He's holding back,* Jin Tian realized. *He's not using his full power.*

Why? The question gnawed at him as he continued to evade. Liu Feng had every reason to crush him quickly, to demonstrate the gap between inner and outer disciples. Instead, he was fighting cautiously, almost reluctantly.

Then Jin Tian saw it.

A flicker of movement in the stands. A figure in white robes, partially obscured by shadow. The figure was watching the match with an intensity that bordered on obsession, their gaze fixed not on the combat, but on Liu Feng.

And Liu Feng knew it. He kept glancing toward the figure, his movements becoming more erratic, more aggressive. He was trying to prove something—or someone was forcing him to.

Jin Tian's blood ran cold.

He recognized the robes. The bearing. The way the figure sat, perfectly still, like a predator waiting to strike.

*Chen Wei's master,* Jin Tian realized. *Elder Bai.*

The elder was a master of the sect's internal affairs division, a man of considerable power and influence. He was also, in Jin Tian's first life, one of the architects of his downfall. Elder Bai had been the one who orchestrated the betrayal, who had promised the seven disciples rewards beyond their wildest dreams in exchange for Jin Tian's death.

And Elder Bai was here, watching Liu Feng fight a twelve-year-old outer disciple.

The pieces clicked into place with terrible clarity.

Liu Feng had not killed Jin Tian out of ambition or hatred. He had been coerced. Blackmailed. Perhaps his family was threatened, or he owed a debt he could not repay. Elder Bai had been pulling the strings all along.

But why? What had Jin Tian done in his first life to warrant such elaborate betrayal?

He didn't have time to think. Liu Feng's blade came screaming toward his neck, and Jin Tian threw himself backward, the edge grazing his skin. Blood welled from a shallow cut, staining his collar red.

"You're distracted," Liu Feng said, his voice tight. "Focus on the fight, boy. Or I'll end it right now."

There was a warning in his words—not a threat, but a plea. *Don't make me do this. Don't force my hand.*

Jin Tian understood.

He changed his stance, dropping into a low guard that exposed his right side. It was a deliberate weakness, an invitation that only someone who knew his fighting style would recognize.

Liu Feng's eyes widened for a fraction of a second. Then he attacked, lunging forward with a thrust aimed at Jin Tian's exposed flank.

It was exactly what Jin Tian had hoped for.

He twisted, not away from the blade, but toward it. The tip of Liu Feng's sword sliced through his robes, skimming his ribs, but Jin Tian was already inside the inner disciple's guard. His wooden sword came up, striking the pressure point on Liu Feng's right arm with surgical precision.

The effect was immediate. Liu Feng's arm went numb, his grip on the spirit weapon faltering. Fire qi erupted from the disrupted meridian, scorching his own robes and sending him stumbling backward.

The crowd gasped.

Jin Tian did not press the advantage. He stepped back, lowering his sword, his eyes locked on Liu Feng's.

"Yield," he said quietly. "You don't have to do this."

For a long moment, Liu Feng stared at him, his expression cycling through shock, confusion, and something that looked almost like hope. Then his gaze flickered toward the stands, toward Elder Bai's shadowed figure.

The hope died.

"I can't," Liu Feng whispered. He raised his sword again, his hand still trembling from the meridian strike. "I'm sorry."

He charged, fire blazing along his blade, his speed doubled by desperation. The attack was wild, unrefined, but backed by the full force of his cultivation base
