Chapter 13: The Weight of a Name

~1.8k words · Updated 2026-06-22

Jin Tian stood at the edge of the training grounds, the afternoon sun casting long shadows across the packed earth. His hands remained steady at his sides, but inside, the storm raged.

Liu Feng.

The name echoed through his memories like a funeral bell. He had spent the past three hours in meditation, cycling his qi through pathways that most cultivators his age couldn't even sense. He had reviewed every technique, every counter, every weakness Liu Feng had ever displayed in their countless sparring matches across Jin Tian's first life.

But something was wrong.

In his memories, Liu Feng had been a straightforward fighter. Fire-aspected techniques, aggressive pursuit, little patience for defense. A brute with talent. But the more Jin Tian thought about it, the more gaps appeared in his recollection.

Why had Liu Feng been at the assassination? He wasn't one of the seven disciples. He had no reason to hate Jin Tian. They had been rivals, yes, but rivals respected each other. They had shared drinks after tournaments. They had saved each other's lives in the Shadow Realm Incursion.

And then Liu Feng's blade had found his heart.

"You're early."

The voice came from behind him, smooth and measured. Jin Tian turned slowly, his hand instinctively drifting toward the wooden sword at his hip.

Liu Feng stood twenty paces away, arms crossed, his crimson robes billowing gently in the breeze. He was older than Jin Tian by four years, already fifteen, with a lean build that spoke of speed rather than raw power. His face was handsome in a way that seemed almost forgettable—the kind of face that could disappear into a crowd.

But his eyes were anything but forgettable.

They were black. Deep, endless black, like wells that had no bottom. In Jin Tian's memories, those eyes had been brown. Warm brown, the color of earth and hearth fires.

"So you're the one who stole Chen Wei's slot," Liu Feng said, his tone carrying no hostility. If anything, he sounded amused. "I expected someone taller."

"Size doesn't determine victory," Jin Tian replied, keeping his voice neutral.

"No, it doesn't." Liu Feng uncrossed his arms and began walking toward him, each step measured and deliberate. "But experience does. And talent. And preparation." He stopped five paces away, close enough that Jin Tian could smell the faint scent of smoke clinging to his robes. "I've been training for this tournament for three years. I've memorized the techniques of every potential opponent. I've studied the terrain of the arena, the placement of the arrays, the timing of the qi fluctuations."

He smiled, and it didn't reach his eyes.

"And you? You've been an outer disciple for what, two months?"

Jin Tian met his gaze without flinching. "Long enough."

Liu Feng laughed, a sound that was supposed to be light but carried an undercurrent of something darker. "I like you, Jin Tian. You have guts. But guts won't save you in the arena." He leaned in, lowering his voice. "I'm going to give you some advice, free of charge. Withdraw from the match. Claim illness. Tell them you're not ready."

"And why would I do that?"

"Because I've been ordered to hurt you." Liu Feng's smile vanished, replaced by something cold and businesslike. "Not defeat you. Not embarrass you. Hurt you. Permanently, if possible. The kind of injury that takes months to heal, if it heals at all."

Jin Tian's blood ran cold, but he kept his expression neutral. "Ordered by whom?"

Liu Feng's eyes flickered to the side, toward the administrative pavilion where Elder Zhou and the other overseers were gathered. Then he looked back at Jin Tian, and for just a moment, his mask slipped.

There was fear in those black eyes.

"You already know the answer to that," Liu Feng said quietly. "You're smarter than you look. That's why they want you crippled." He stepped back, his voice returning to its normal volume. "Consider my advice, little brother. Some battles aren't worth fighting."

He turned and walked away, his crimson robes trailing behind him like a bloodstain.

Jin Tian watched him go, his mind racing. Liu Feng had been ordered to hurt him. By whom? Chen Wei's family? The Heavenly Cloud Sect's elders? Someone higher?

And why had Liu Feng warned him?

In his first life, Liu Feng had been his enemy. The man who killed him. But this Liu Feng was different. Younger. More uncertain. And clearly operating under duress.

Was the assassination ordered from the very beginning? Had someone been pulling the strings all along, using Jin Tian's disciples as pawns and Liu Feng as the executioner?

The implications were staggering.

He was still processing when a hand landed on his shoulder. He spun, his hand going for his sword, but stopped when he saw Wei Zhen's concerned face.

"You look like you've seen a ghost," Wei Zhen said. "What did Liu Feng say to you?"

Jin Tian hesitated. Wei Zhen was his friend, but trust was a luxury he couldn't afford. Not yet. "He told me to withdraw."

"Withdraw?" Wei Zhen's eyes widened. "Why would he—never mind, I know why. He's ranked seventh. He probably thinks you're beneath him." He squeezed Jin Tian's shoulder. "Don't listen to him. You earned that slot. You beat Chen Wei's score fair and square."

"Did I?"

Wei Zhen blinked. "What do you mean?"

Jin Tian looked past him, toward the training grounds where disciples were gathering for the afternoon announcements. Chen Wei stood among them, surrounded by his usual entourage, but his eyes were fixed on Jin Tian with an intensity that bordered on obsessive.

"I mean," Jin Tian said slowly, "that I'm starting to wonder if the tournament was rigged from the start. If my score wasn't supposed to be higher. If Chen Wei wasn't supposed to lose."

Wei Zhen's face paled. "That's... that's a serious accusation, Jin Tian."

"I know."

"If you're wrong, they'll expel you. Or worse."

"I know."

"And if you're right..." Wei Zhen swallowed hard. "If you're right, then the people who rigged it are the same people who run the sect. The same people who decide who lives and dies."

Jin Tian turned to face his friend fully. "Wei Zhen, I need you to listen carefully. In three hours, I'm going to fight Liu Feng. If I win, I'll have to fight again tomorrow, and the day after that, and the day after that. I'll be climbing a ladder that someone else built, and every step will bring me closer to whoever wants me dead."

He paused, letting the words sink in.

"But if I lose... if I withdraw... I'll be exactly where they want me. Weak. Powerless. Forgettable."

Wei Zhen's hands were shaking. "What are you going to do?"

Jin Tian looked up at the sky, where clouds were gathering on the horizon. A storm was coming. He could feel it in the air, in the qi, in the way the world seemed to hold its breath.

"I'm going to fight," he said. "And I'm going to win. But first, I need to know something." He met Wei Zhen's eyes. "Can I trust you?"

Wei Zhen's face went through a dozen emotions in the span of a heartbeat. Fear. Uncertainty. Loyalty. And finally, something that looked almost like resolve.

"Yes," he said. "You can trust me."

Jin Tian nodded. "Then I need you to do something for me. Something dangerous."

"Name it."

"I need you to find out who Chen Wei's family has been meeting with. Not the public meetings—the private ones. The ones that don't appear on any official records."

Wei Zhen's eyes widened. "That's impossible. Those records are sealed. Only the inner elders have access."

"Which is why you're not going to look for records." Jin Tian reached into his sleeve and pulled out a small jade token, the same one that had allowed him access to the library archives. "You're going to find a disciple named Mei Ling. She works in the administrative pavilion. She's quiet, forgettable, and she notices everything."

"How do you know that?"

Jin Tian allowed himself a thin smile. "Because in another life, she saved my life three times. She was the best spy the sect never knew it had."

Wei Zhen stared at the token, then at Jin Tian. "Who are you, Jin Tian?"

The question hung in the air between them, heavy with meaning.

"I'm someone who's running out of time," Jin Tian said. "Take the token. Find Mei Ling. Tell her that the man who returned the stolen jade book needs her help."

"What jade book?"

"She'll know." Jin Tian pressed the token into Wei Zhen's hand. "Go now. Before anyone notices you're gone."

Wei Zhen hesitated for only a moment, then nodded and disappeared into the crowd, moving with a purpose that Jin Tian hadn't seen in him before.

Jin Tian watched him go, then turned back toward the arena where his match against Liu Feng would take place. The sun had begun to set, painting the sky in shades of orange and red.

Soon, it would be time.

He had three hours to prepare for a battle against a man who had been ordered to kill him. Three hours to figure out why Liu Feng had warned him. Three hours to decide whether he could trust a man who, in another life, had been his murderer.

And somewhere in the shadows, the person who had given that order was watching, waiting, calculating.

Jin Tian closed his eyes and reached for his qi, feeling it flow through his meridians like a river of light. He was only twelve years old, trapped in a body that hadn't yet grown into its potential. But inside, he carried the memories of three hundred years of cultivation. The techniques. The strategies. The understanding of sword intent that most cultivators spent a lifetime pursuing.

It would have to be enough.

Because if he lost this match, he wouldn't just lose the tournament.

He would lose everything.

The bells chimed in the distance, signaling the start of the evening assembly. Jin Tian opened his eyes and began walking toward the arena, his wooden sword at his side, his heart steady as a drum.

Behind him, in the shadow of the administrative pavilion, a figure watched him go.

The figure smiled, and their eyes glowed with the faintest hint of crimson.

"So," the figure whispered, their voice carrying on the wind like a forgotten melody, "the Sword God awakens at last. How delightful."

They turned and vanished into the darkness, leaving no trace of their presence.

And somewhere in the depths of the sect's archives, a jade book that had been hidden for three hundred years began to glow with a faint, pulsing light.
