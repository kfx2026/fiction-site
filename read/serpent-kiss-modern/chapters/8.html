📖 Serpent's Kiss by Xu Xian

Chapter 8: The Chain of a Thousand Years

~1.9k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The golden eyes fixed on Xu Ming with the weight of centuries.

Fahai stepped fully into the shop, his wet robes leaving dark trails across the floor. His prayer beads clicked with each step—bone, Xu Ming realized. Carved from something that had once been alive.

"You have been deceived," Fahai said. His voice was gentle, almost kind. "The snake is skilled at wearing pretty masks. She has had a thousand years to practice."

Bai Suyin placed herself between them. Her body trembled, but her voice was steel. "He knows what I am."

"Does he?" Fahai tilted his head. "Does he know about the village in Jiangxi? The sixty-three souls you consumed in a single night?"

"That was self-defense."

"The magistrate's daughter? She was six years old."

Bai Suyin's breath caught. "I did not—"

"You did." Fahai's voice remained calm, almost pitying. "You were young then. Barely three hundred years of cultivation. Your hunger ruled you. I understand. But understanding does not bring back the dead."

Xu Ming's hands had stopped shaking. They were still now, frozen at his sides.

"Xu Ming." Bai Suyin's voice cracked. "Don't listen to him. He twists everything. He—"

"How did your teacher die, Bai Suyin?"

She went silent.

"Tell him." Fahai stepped closer. The prayer beads clicked. "Tell the pharmacist how your master was burned alive. How you watched. How you did nothing."

"I was chained," she whispered. "He chained me in a cave with suppression talismans. I could hear her screaming. I could smell her flesh burning. But I could not move."

"Because you were weak." Fahai's smile did not waver. "And you have been weak ever since. Running from life to life. Playing at humanity. But a snake is a snake, no matter how many skins it sheds."

Xu Ming found his voice. "Why are you here?"

Fahai's gaze shifted to him. The golden eyes were ancient, carrying a weight that made Xu Ming's knees want to buckle. "Because she has finally done something worth my attention. She has found a mortal willing to love her. This is the final step. If she completes it, she becomes human—truly human, beyond my reach, beyond the laws that bind her kind."

"And you want to stop her."

"I want to save you." Fahai reached into his robe and pulled out a scroll, yellowed with age. "This is a record of every mortal who has been caught in her web. Seven men. Four women. All dead within a year of meeting her. The last one threw himself from a bridge. He left a note: 'The snake's kiss is sweet, but the venom never leaves.'"

Bai Suyin made a sound like an animal wounded.

"That is not—" Her voice broke. "They were not—"

"Were they not?" Fahai unrolled the scroll. Names. Dates. Manner of death. "Zhang Wei, 1127. Drowned. Liu Mei, 1143. Fell from a height. Chen Bao, 1206. Heart failure. Shall I continue?"

"Stop." Xu Ming's voice was barely a whisper.

"Or you could ask her yourself." Fahai rolled the scroll, tucked it away. "Ask her why she failed the heart trial seven times before. Ask her what happened to those who tried to love her."

Xu Ming turned to Bai Suyin.

Her face was wet. Not from rain.

"Every time," she said, her voice raw. "Every time I failed. Because I could not love them enough to save them. Because Fahai found me before the trial was complete. Because they saw what I was and ran."

"Is that what happened?"

"No." Fahai's voice cut through. "They saw what she was and stayed. And she consumed them anyway. Not out of malice. Out of instinct. A snake does not choose to eat the mouse. It simply does what it is."

"Get out." Xu Ming's voice surprised him. It was steady. Firm.

Fahai raised an eyebrow. "Excuse me?"

"Get out of my shop." Xu Ming walked past Bai Suyin, stood between her and the abbot. "You have no right to bring your war into my home. Whatever happened a thousand years ago, it has nothing to do with me."

"Everything to do with you." Fahai did not move. "She has marked you. Her scent is on your skin. Her power is in your blood. Even if I leave, I will find you again. And when I do, I will ask you one more time. But the next time I ask, I will not be kind."

"Then I will answer the same way."

For a long moment, Fahai studied him. Then he laughed—a sound like stones grinding together.

"You would make a fine monk, pharmacist. Pity you chose the snake." He turned toward the shattered door. "I will return in three days. By then, you will have seen the truth. And you will beg me to save you."

He stepped into the rain.

And vanished.

Not walked away. Vanished. One moment he was there, the next the rain fell through empty air.

Xu Ming stood in the silence, heart hammering.

"He let us live." Bai Suyin's voice was hollow. "That means he has already won."

"What are you talking about?"

"He would never leave if he thought he could win tonight. He left because he knows something I don't. Because he has already set his trap." She grabbed his arm, her fingers cold. "Xu Ming, you need to leave. Tonight. Go somewhere far away. Forget me."

"Don't."

"I am serious." Her eyes were wild. "Every word he said was true. I have failed seven times. Seven mortals died because they loved me. I watched them die. I held their bodies. I buried them with my own hands."

"And yet you came here."

"Because I am selfish. Because I have been alone for a thousand years and I cannot bear another century of silence." Her voice broke. "But I will not add you to my list of graves."

Xu Ming looked at her. The rain dripped from her hair. Her dress was torn. She looked nothing like the elegant woman who had walked into his shop days ago. She looked like someone who had been running for centuries and had finally run out of road.

"Why me?" he asked.

"What?"

"Seven mortals. Seven failures. Why try again? Why me?"

Bai Suyin's lips parted. For a moment, she looked like she might lie. Then she closed her eyes.

"Because you remind me of her."

"Your teacher?"

"My teacher." Her voice was barely audible. "She was mortal. A pharmacist in a small village. She found me when I was wounded, nursed me back to health, never asked what I was. She taught me to read. To write. To mix herbs. She taught me that humans could be kind without expecting anything in return."

"What happened to her?"

"Fahai burned her alive." Bai Suyin opened her eyes. "He chained me in a cave so I would have to listen to her scream. And when it was over, he told me that this was the price of loving mortals. That I would always bring death to those I cared for."

"He was wrong."

"Was he?" She gestured at the scroll on the floor, where Fahai had dropped it. "Seven names. Seven deaths. Seven times I tried to build a life and seven times he tore it down. And he is right about one thing. The last one—Chen Bao—he jumped. Because he saw my true form and could not live with what he had loved."

Xu Ming picked up the scroll.

It was heavy. The names were written in elegant calligraphy, each entry detailing a life cut short. He read them all. When he finished, he looked up.

"These people died because of Fahai. Not because of you."

"What?"

"Read it again." He held out the scroll. "Zhang Wei was drowned. The entry says he was pulled into a river by an 'unseen force.' Liu Mei fell, but the entry says she was 'pushed.' Chen Bao jumped, but the entry before it says he was 'shown the truth' by a monk three days prior."

Bai Suyin's face went pale.

"He made them die." Xu Ming's voice was shaking, but not from fear. "He killed them. And he made you think it was your fault."

The shop was silent except for the rain.

"He has been doing this for a thousand years," Xu Ming continued. "Not hunting demons. Creating them. He needs you to be a monster so he can be a hero. If you ever became human, his purpose would be gone."

Bai Suyin stared at him.

"You read that in three minutes."

"I'm a pharmacist. I'm trained to notice details." He stepped closer. "Bai Suyin. You are not a monster. You are a woman who has been tortured by a madman for centuries. And I am not going to run."

"You should."

"I know." He reached out, touched her face. Her skin was cold. "But I won't."

Her hand came up, covered his. Her eyes were wet again, but this time she was smiling.

"Xu Ming. You are either the bravest man I have ever met or the most foolish."

"Probably both."

She laughed—a real laugh, surprised out of her. It transformed her face. Made her look young, human, alive.

"I have an idea," she said. "A way to break his hold. But it will be dangerous."

"More dangerous than a thousand-year-old snake spirit and a monk with golden eyes?"

"Much more."

"Tell me."

She took a deep breath.

"I need to show you my true form. Not a fraction. All of it. And you need to see it and not run."

"That's it?"

"That's it." Her eyes were serious. "Fahai's power comes from fear. He makes mortals see the monster so they reject the spirit. If you see me—truly see me—and stay, his hold on this trial is broken. I become human."

"And if I run?"

"Then I fail. Again." She looked down. "And this time, I will not try again. I will let him kill me."

"Don't say that."

"It is the truth." She met his eyes. "I am tired, Xu Ming. A thousand years is too long to be alone. If you run, I will not chase you. I will let him end it."

Xu Ming thought of his small shop. His quiet life. His predictable days.

Then he thought of her standing in the rain, shaking, asking him to see her.

"Do it," he said.

"Here?"

"Here. Now. Before I lose my nerve."

Bai Suyin stepped back. Her eyes began to glow—not the faint shimmer from before, but a deep, burning white.

"Remember," she said. "You asked for this."

The lights went out.

Not the electricity. The light itself. The room plunged into darkness so complete that Xu Ming could not see his own hands.

Then the pressure began.

It pressed against his skull. His chest. His lungs. The air grew thick, heavy, impossible to breathe. Somewhere in the dark, something moved. Something vast. Something that had no business existing in a world of walls and ceilings and small pharmacies.
