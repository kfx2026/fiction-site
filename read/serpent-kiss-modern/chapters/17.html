Chapter 17: The Price of Scales

~1.9k words · 2026-06-22

The golden light faded from Fahai's eyes, leaving them ordinary brown again. Ordinary, except for the centuries of hatred swimming in their depths.

"I expected more resistance," he said, stepping over the shattered remains of Xu Ming's back door. Rain streamed through the opening, pooling on the floor. "The last pharmacist who sheltered her lasted three days before begging for death."

Xu Ming placed himself between Fahai and Bai Suyin. His legs shook. His voice cracked when he spoke. "Get out of my shop."

"Brave words for a man who mixes cough syrup for a living." Fahai's prayer beads clicked as he rotated them — black beads, carved from something that looked like bone. "Do you know what she is? What she's done?"

"I know what you are," Xu Ming said. "A hunter who calls himself holy."

Fahai laughed. The sound had no warmth. "She told you about her teacher? The one I killed?" He tilted his head, studying Bai Suyin like a collector examining a specimen. "Did she tell you why I killed him?"

"Don't," Bai Suyin whispered.

"Because he was teaching her to consume human souls." Fahai's voice dropped to something almost gentle. "Three hundred and forty-seven souls. That's how many she absorbed before I stopped her teacher. That's how much power she needed to climb from serpent to woman."

Xu Ming felt the floor shift beneath him. He turned to look at Bai Suyin.

Her face was stone. "It's not—"

"Not true?" Fahai produced a scroll from his robes, yellowed with age, sealed with red wax. He tossed it at Xu Ming's feet. "Names. Dates. Villages. The mountain pass where she and her master feasted on travelers for seven years before anyone noticed."

Xu Ming didn't pick up the scroll. He didn't need to. The look on Bai Suyin's face told him everything.

"I was different then," she said, her voice barely audible. "I was young. I followed my teacher. I didn't know—"

"You knew." Fahai's beads clicked faster. "You knew every time you tasted a soul. You knew the screams. You knew the faces of the people you consumed. And you continued."

"Why are you telling him this?" Bai Suyin's composure cracked. "To turn him against me? You could have killed me a dozen times tonight. You could have killed me in the street. Instead you come here, to his shop, to speak of old sins—"

"Because I want him to choose." Fahai smiled. "I want him to know exactly what he's protecting. Then I want him to hand you over himself."

"Never," Xu Ming said.

But Bai Suyin saw the hesitation. The way his hands trembled. The way he couldn't quite meet her eyes.

Fahai saw it too. "You have until dawn to decide, pharmacist. Bring her to Leifeng Pagoda, and I will spare your life. Hide her, and I will burn this city to find her. And you will watch." He turned and walked back into the rain, his robes dry despite the downpour, his footsteps silent on the wet concrete.

The door hung open. The rain kept falling.

Xu Ming stood frozen, staring at the scroll at his feet.

"Pick it up," Bai Suyin said.

"I don't want to."

"You need to." Her voice was hollow. "You need to know who I was. So you can decide who I am now."

He bent down. His fingers touched the ancient paper, and it crumbled slightly at the edges. He didn't open it. He held it in his hands like it might bite him.

"How many?" he asked.

"Three hundred and forty-seven. That's what he said."

"I'm asking you."

She closed her eyes. "Four hundred and twelve."

The number hit him like a physical blow. Four hundred and twelve souls. Four hundred and twelve lives, consumed so she could grow stronger. So she could become human.

"When did you stop?"

"The last one was eight hundred years ago." She opened her eyes. "I was trapped in a bottle for three hundred years after that. A monk sealed me in a jar and threw me into the West Lake. I floated at the bottom, unable to move, unable to die, listening to the boats pass overhead. That's where I learned to change."

"That's where you learned to pretend," Xu Ming said.

"Yes." She didn't deny it. "I learned to be human by watching humans. Their kindness. Their cruelty. Their capacity for love. I studied them like a scholar studies texts. And when I emerged, I swore I would never consume another soul. I would earn my humanity honestly."

"Can you?" Xu Ming's voice broke. "Can you earn back four hundred lives?"

"I don't know." Tears streamed down her face. "But I can try. I have been trying. For eight centuries, I have helped the sick. I have protected the weak. I have loved—" she choked on the word. "I have tried to love, truly, even when it meant watching those I love grow old and die while I remained."

"You've done this before."

"Yes. Other heart trials. Other mortals." She wiped her face with the back of her hand. "I failed every time. Because I couldn't let go of my nature. Because when they discovered what I was, they ran. Or they tried to kill me. Or Fahai found them first."

"And you expect me to be different?"

"I expect nothing." She stepped closer to him, her feet soundless on the wet floor. "I came to Hangzhou because I heard about a pharmacist who treated beggars for free. Who stayed open during festivals. Who cried when an elderly patient died of a cough he couldn't cure." She touched his face. "I came because I thought maybe, this time, I could find someone whose heart was pure enough to see past what I was."

Xu Ming's eyes burned. "You lied to me."

"I withheld the truth. There's a difference."

"Is there? You came here to use me. To complete your trial. To hide from Fahai."

"Yes." Her hand dropped from his face. "And now I am telling you everything. Because if you're going to betray me to Fahai, I want you to do it with full knowledge of who you're betraying."

The rain hammered against the roof. The shop's lights flickered.

Xu Ming looked at the scroll in his hands. Four hundred and twelve names. Four hundred and twelve lives sacrificed to power.

He thought about his grandmother, dying of cancer in a hospital bed across the city. He thought about the young couple who came to his shop last week, desperate for medicine for their sick child. He thought about the beggar who slept in the doorway, the one he gave free tea to every morning.

All of them could have been targets, eight hundred years ago. All of them could have been consumed by a serpent who wanted to be human.

And yet.

He looked at Bai Suyin. At the woman who had pressed his palm to her chest and let him feel her heart beat. At the woman who had shown him her true form, terrifying and beautiful. At the woman who was crying, not for herself, but for the lives she couldn't bring back.

"You said Fahai killed your teacher," Xu Ming said slowly. "Why?"

"Because my teacher refused to stop. After I left, after I swore off soul consumption, my teacher continued. He found new students. He taught them the old ways. Fahai tracked him to a mountain temple and burned it to the ground with everyone inside."

"Everyone?"

"My teacher. His students. Twenty-seven monks who had been taken hostage." Bai Suyin's voice hardened. "Fahai didn't check. He didn't care. He burned them all because he believed the ends justified the means."

"Like you believed consuming souls justified becoming human."

"Yes." She met his eyes. "We are both monsters, Xu Ming. The difference is that I know I am one. Fahai believes he is holy."

Xu Ming set the scroll down on his counter. He looked at his hands — hands that had mixed herbs, that had held the dying, that had never killed anything larger than a mosquito.

"Four hundred and twelve," he said.

"Yes."

"That's not nothing."

"I know."

"Can you become human? Truly human? With all the guilt, all the regret, all the weight of what you've done?"

Bai Suyin's expression shifted. For the first time since Fahai's arrival, something like hope flickered in her eyes. "That's what the heart trial is. To love someone so completely that their humanity becomes yours. To share their mortality. To die when they die."

"To suffer for eternity."

"To feel." She touched her chest. "To truly feel, for the first time in a thousand years."

The rain began to slow. The sky outside was still dark, but somewhere, beyond the clouds, dawn was approaching.

"I have conditions," Xu Ming said.

"Name them."

"First, you never consume another soul. Not even to save yourself. Not even if Fahai is about to kill you."

"Agreed."

"Second, you tell me everything. No more secrets. No more half-truths. If I'm going to die for you, I want to know what I'm dying for."

Her lips curved into something that was almost a smile. "And third?"

Xu Ming picked up the scroll. He held it over a candle flame. The ancient paper caught fire, curling inward, the names turning to ash.

"Third," he said, watching the fire consume the evidence, "you help me destroy Fahai. Not because I want revenge. Because people like him, people who think they're holy, they don't stop. They never stop."

Bai Suyin stared at him. "You would kill a monk for me?"

"I would kill a monster for the woman I love." The words came out before he could stop them. He felt his face flush. "Or. The woman I'm beginning to—"

She kissed him. Her lips were cold, like winter rain, but her heart hammered against his chest, faster than any human heart should beat.

"You are a fool," she whispered against his mouth.

"I know."

"A beautiful fool."

"That too."

She pulled back, her eyes glowing faintly in the dark. "Fahai will come at dawn. He will bring his relics. His blessed chains. He will try to trap me in a bottle again."

"Then we won't be here." Xu Ming grabbed his coat. "I know a place. My grandmother's house, in the countryside. No one knows about it. Not even my cousins."

"He will track my essence."

"Then we'll mask it." Xu Ming grabbed bottles from his shelves — herbs, powders, things he'd never used because they were too dangerous. "I've been a pharmacist for ten years. I know how to make a person disappear."

"Not a person," Bai Suyin said. "A snake spirit with a thousand years of cultivation."

"Then I'll learn."

The rain stopped. The silence that followed was absolute.

And in that silence, they heard it — the sound of chanting, distant but growing closer. Monks. Many monks. Their voices rising in a hymn that made the air vibrate.

Fahai had not come alone.

Bai Suyin's face went pale. "He brought the monastery. The entire monastery."

"How many?"

"If he brought the full order, at least two hundred monks. Each one trained in binding rituals. Each one armed with blessed weapons."

Xu Ming's heart dropped. Two hundred monks. His tiny shop. His grandmother's house in the countryside.

They would never make it.
