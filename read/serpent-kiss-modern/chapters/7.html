CHAPTER 7: The Weight of Scales

The rain had stopped, but the water still dripped from the broken doorframe, each drop striking the floor like a countdown. Fahai stood in the gap, his golden eyes fixed on Bai Suyin with the patience of a hunter who had tracked his prey across centuries.

"You always choose the interesting ones," Fahai said, stepping over the splintered wood. His robes brushed the floor without making a sound. "The last time, it was a scholar. Before that, a soldier. Now a pharmacist. You have a pattern, Bai Suyin. You always seek the gentle ones."

Bai Suyin moved between Xu Ming and the monk, her body tensed like a spring. "He has nothing to do with this."

"He has everything to do with this." Fahai's prayer beads clicked as he rotated them. "He is your anchor. Your mortal shield. Kill him, and you lose your grounding. Your cultivation falters. Your heart trial fails." He smiled. "I have read the texts."

Xu Ming's hand found the counter behind him, searching for something—anything—that could serve as a weapon. His fingers brushed against a mortar and pestle. Worthless. A jar of dried ginseng. Useless.

"You won't touch him," Bai Suyin said. Her voice was flat, but Xu Ming caught the tremor beneath it. Not fear. Something else. Rage, held in check by a thin chain.

"I don't need to touch him." Fahai reached into his robe and withdrew a small bronze mirror, its surface clouded with age. "I only need him to see."

He held the mirror up, angled it toward Xu Ming.

The world lurched.

Xu Ming was no longer in the shop. He stood on a mountainside, smoke curling from burning trees, ash falling like snow. Below him, a village burned. Above him, something massive moved through the clouds—a shape of scales and fury, its body winding through the sky like a river of moonlight.

The white serpent.

*She* was beautiful. Terrifying. Her eyes were the color of jade, and they swept across the burning village without emotion, without pity. She was a force of nature, and the people below were ants.

"Watch," Fahai's voice whispered. "Watch what she truly is."

The vision shifted. Xu Ming stood in a temple courtyard, swords clashing, robes flying. A younger Fahai—his eyes still human, his face unlined—fought a woman who moved like water, her body flowing around his strikes. Bai Suyin. But different. Harder. Her face was carved from ice, and when she spoke, her voice was not the gentle tone he knew.

"You cannot cage me, monk," she hissed. "I have lived a thousand years. I have loved and lost and killed. What is your lifetime to me?"

"You killed my master," Fahai said, blood running from a cut on his forehead.

"Your master was a fool who thought he could bind the heavens."

The vision shattered.

Xu Ming gasped, stumbling backward, his hand clutching his chest. His heart hammered. His vision swam. When he looked at Bai Suyin, she was watching him with something close to fear.

"Those were half-truths," she said. "Twisted by his mirror."

"The Mirror of Reflection shows only truth," Fahai said. "Your pharmacist saw you for what you are. A killer. A monster wearing a woman's skin."

"He saw me in war," Bai Suyin snapped. "Show him the rest. Show him why I fought. Show him the monastery that was using children for blood rituals. Show him the abbot who was eating human hearts to extend his life." She turned to Xu Ming, desperation cracking her voice. "I did not kill innocent people. I killed monsters who wore holy robes."

Fahai's smile did not waver. "Every killer believes they killed monsters."

"Put down the mirror," Xu Ming said.

Both of them turned to look at him. Fahai's expression flickered with surprise—he had clearly expected more arguing, more revelation, more drama. Bai Suyin looked at him with something like hope.

"I said put it down." Xu Ming's voice was steady. His hands were still shaking, but his voice was not. "I don't know what I saw. I don't know what's true. But I know she came to me for herbs, not violence. I know she held my grandmother's qin like it was sacred. And I know you broke my door."

Fahai's smile vanished. "You are a fool."

"Maybe. But I'm a fool who knows that someone who breaks down doors usually has something to hide." Xu Ming stepped forward, past Bai Suyin, until he stood between her and the monk. "If she's a demon, let her be judged by heaven. Not by a man with a magic mirror and a grudge."

The shop went silent.

Bai Suyin's hand found his, squeezed once. Her fingers were cold.

Fahai looked at them—at the pharmacist standing in front of a snake spirit, at their joined hands—and something shifted in his expression. Not anger. Not triumph. Something closer to... pity?

"You have already fallen," he said softly. "You just don't know it yet."

He raised his hand.

The prayer beads flew.

They wrapped around Xu Ming's throat before he could blink, tightening, burning, searing into his skin like hot iron. He choked, clawed at them, but they were impossibly tight—each bead branded him with symbols he couldn't read, couldn't understand.

"Xu Ming!" Bai Suyin's voice was distant, muffled by the roaring in his ears.

She lunged at Fahai, but the monk was already moving, his hand pressed against her forehead, pinning her in place with a single touch. Her body went rigid. Her eyes widened.

"You forget," Fahai said, "that I have been hunting your kind for eight hundred years. I know your weaknesses. I know the meridians you use to hide your true form." He pressed harder, and Bai Suyin's face twisted in pain. "And I know that if I seal your cultivation, you will be nothing more than a woman. A mortal woman. Barely alive."

"No," she whispered.

"You chose this life. Now you will live it without power." Fahai's fingers traced symbols on her forehead, each one leaving a faint golden glow. "Until you learn what it means to be human. Truly human. Not a spirit playing dress-up."

Xu Ming's vision was darkening. The beads were tightening, compressing, grinding against his windpipe. He couldn't breathe. He couldn't think. He could only hear Bai Suyin's ragged breathing, Fahai's calm chanting, and the distant sound of rain starting again.

Then—nothing.

---

He woke on the floor of his shop, gasping.

The beads were gone. Fahai was gone. Bai Suyin sat beside him, her face pale, her eyes empty. When she saw him awake, she smiled, but it didn't reach her eyes.

"He's gone," she said. "For now."

"What happened?" Xu Ming's voice was hoarse. He touched his throat—the skin was smooth, unmarked, but he could still feel the phantom pressure of the beads.

"He sealed me." She held up her hands, flexed her fingers. "I can feel it. The energy is still there, but I can't reach it. Like trying to grab smoke through glass." She laughed, a hollow sound. "A thousand years of cultivation, and a monk with a charm can undo it in three minutes."

"He said you would be mortal."

"He said I would be nothing." She looked at him, and for the first time, Xu Ming saw her truly afraid. "Without my cultivation, I am vulnerable. Fahai can find me. Anyone can find me. A snake spirit without power is just... meat."

Xu Ming sat up, his head spinning. "Then we leave."

"Where would we go?"

"Anywhere. My grandmother's village. The mountains. Somewhere he can't find us."

"He will always find me." She touched his face, her fingers tracing the line of his jaw. "Unless I am not a snake spirit anymore."

"What does that mean?"

Her eyes met his. "The heart trial. It is not just about love. It is about transformation. If I complete it—if I love you truly, and you love me truly—I will become human. Fully human. No scales, no cultivation, no power. Just a woman." She paused. "And Fahai will have no reason to hunt me."

"Because you won't be a spirit anymore."

"Because I will be beneath his notice. A mortal. A ghost of no consequence." She smiled again, and this time, there was something fragile in it. "I will lose everything I have worked for. My power. My memories. My connection to the heavens. I will become small and weak and mortal."

"But alive."

"But alive."

Xu Ming took her hand, felt her cold fingers, her too-slow pulse. "Then we complete the trial."

"You don't understand." She pulled away, stood up, walked to the shattered door. The rain was falling again, cold and steady. "The trial is not a ceremony. It is not a confession. It is a burning. I will have to give up everything I am. Every power, every memory, every piece of my spirit that made me *her*. And in exchange..." She turned to face him. "I will become someone new. Someone who loves you because she was born to love you, not because she was forced to by a trial."

"I don't understand the difference."

"Neither do I." Her voice cracked. "And that is what terrifies me."

The shop's bell rang.

Both of them froze.

A figure stepped through the broken door, umbrella dripping, footsteps light on the wet floor. She was young—maybe twenty—with dark hair pulled back in a practical bun and glasses that reflected the dim light. She wore a police uniform.

"Xu Ming?" She looked at the broken door, the scattered herbs, the woman in the wet dress. "What happened here?"

"Officer Lin." Xu Ming's heart was racing. "There was—an accident. A break-in. The man who did it ran."

The officer's eyes narrowed. She looked at Bai Suyin, and something flickered in her gaze—recognition? Suspicion? "And you are?"

"Bai Suyin. A customer."

"A customer." Officer Lin stepped closer, her hand resting on her belt. "At this hour? In this rain?"

"I needed medicine," Bai Suyin said smoothly. "I have a cough."

The officer's smile was thin. "I see." She turned to Xu Ming. "I'll need a statement. And you'll need to secure this door before morning. Hangzhou has a lot of rats."

She handed him a card, her fingers brushing his. When she pulled away, Xu Ming noticed something on the back of the card—a symbol burned into the paper, small and intricate.

A lotus.

"That's my number," Officer Lin said. "Call if you remember anything else."

She walked out, umbrella snapping open, and disappeared into the rain.

Xu Ming stared at the card, the lotus symbol burning in his palm.

"That was no police officer," Bai Suyin said, her voice tight.

"Then who was she?"

Bai Suyin took the card, examined the symbol, and her face went white.

"She is Fahai's student. His first disciple." She looked up, eyes wide. "She was not here to investigate. She was here to mark us."
