CHAPTER 13: The Blood Price

The golden light from Fahai's eyes faded as quickly as it had appeared. He stood in the shattered doorway, water pooling around his sandaled feet, his robes untouched by the rain that still hammered the alley outside.

Xu Ming positioned himself between the monk and Bai Suyin. A futile gesture. He knew it. But his body moved before his mind could stop it.

"Step aside, pharmacist." Fahai's voice carried no anger. No threat. Just the absolute certainty of a man who had never been refused. "You have been deceived. The creature behind you is not what she appears."

"She's a patient," Xu Ming said. His voice cracked. He tried again. "This is a place of healing. Whatever dispute you have, take it outside."

"A healer." Fahai's lips curved. Not quite a smile. "You mix herbs for fevers. You wrap bandages around scraped knees. Do you truly believe your little bottles can protect you from what follows in her wake?"

Behind him, Xu Ming heard Bai Suyin's breathing. Shallow. Rapid. The sound of someone calculating odds that grew worse by the second.

"I have no quarrel with you," Fahai continued. He stepped forward. One step. Two. The splintered wood of the doorframe crunched beneath his feet. "Give her to me, and I will forget this night ever happened."

"And if I refuse?"

The golden light flickered in Fahai's eyes. "Then I will take her through you."

Xu Ming's hand found the jar of powdered ginseng on the counter. Not a weapon. But it was something to hold. Something solid in a world that had suddenly become liquid.

"Xu Ming." Bai Suyin's voice was barely a whisper. "Don't."

He turned. She had pressed herself against the back wall, her wet dress clinging to her frame. But it was her eyes that stopped him. They were no longer brown. They had shifted to something else—a deep, ancient gold that mirrored Fahai's own.

"If you fight him," she said, "he will kill you. Not because he wants to. Because you are in his way, and he has never let anything stand between him and his purpose."

"Then what do you suggest?" Xu Ming heard the edge in his own voice. "Surrender? Let him drag you back to whatever hell he's been keeping you from?"

"He cannot drag me anywhere." Her voice hardened. "I have survived eight hundred years of his hunting. I will survive this night too."

She stepped forward, moving past Xu Ming until she stood face to face with the abbot. They were the same height. Same posture. Two predators circling each other in a space too small for either of them.

"Fahai," she said. "We have done this dance before. In Suzhou. In Nanjing. On the slopes of Emei Shan where you burned my teacher alive." Her voice didn't waver. "Each time, you have failed. Each time, I have escaped. What makes you think tonight will be different?"

"Because tonight," Fahai said, "I have something I did not have before."

He reached into his robe and withdrew an object that caught the dim light of the shop's single bulb. A bell. Small, brass, ancient. Worn smooth by centuries of handling.

Xu Ming saw Bai Suyin's face change. For the first time since she'd walked into his shop, he saw fear in her eyes. Real fear.

"Where did you get that?"

"From your teacher." Fahai held the bell up. It did not ring. It did not move. But the air around it seemed to tremble. "Before she died, she tried to hide it. She failed."

"That bell belonged to my mother."

"No." Fahai shook his head slowly. "It belonged to the one who created your mother. The original white snake who first learned to walk as a human. She gave it to your teacher for safekeeping, and your teacher passed it to you before I caught up with her in Nanjing."

Xu Ming watched Bai Suyin's hands clench into fists. Her nails bit into her palms. Blood dripped onto the floor.

"You killed her."

"I purified her." Fahai's voice was gentle. "As I will purify you."

He raised the bell. And for the first time, he rang it.

The sound was not loud. It was not sharp. It was a single, clear note that seemed to hang in the air like smoke. But Xu Ming felt it in his bones. In his teeth. In the spaces between his thoughts.

Bai Suyin screamed.

She fell to her knees, hands pressed to her ears. Her body convulsed. Scales rippled across her skin—not the controlled transformation she had shown Xu Ming before, but something violent. Uncontrollable. Her form flickered between human and serpent, human and serpent, faster and faster until Xu Ming couldn't tell which was real.

"Stop!" Xu Ming lunged forward. He didn't have a plan. He didn't have a weapon. He just moved.

Fahai caught him with one hand. The abbot's grip was iron. His fingers closed around Xu Ming's throat and lifted him off the ground.

"You are a distraction," Fahai said, his voice still calm. "Nothing more. She chose you because you were weak. Because she thought she could control you. Because she thought your love would save her."

Xu Ming clawed at the monk's hand. His vision darkened at the edges.

"But love is not salvation." Fahai's eyes glowed brighter. "Love is the trap she set for herself. Every mortal she has ever loved, I have taken from her. Every life she has tried to build, I have burned to ash. Do you know why?"

Xu Ming couldn't breathe. Couldn't speak.

"Because I am the only one who understands what she truly is." Fahai leaned closer. His breath smelled of incense and old copper. "She is not a woman. She is not a spirit seeking enlightenment. She is a wound in the fabric of the world, and I am the surgeon who will cut her out."

He released Xu Ming's throat.

Xu Ming fell, gasping, his knees hitting the shattered wood. He sucked in air that tasted like blood and rain.

Bai Suyin had stopped screaming. She lay curled on the floor, her body still flickering between forms. Human one moment. Serpent the next. Her eyes were open, but they were not her eyes. They were the eyes of something ancient. Something that had been hunted for so long it had forgotten what it meant to be anything else.

"Now," Fahai said, turning back to her. "Shall I ring it again? Or will you come willingly?"

"Wait." Xu Ming's voice was hoarse. Broken. But he forced himself to stand. "Wait."

Fahai paused. He looked at Xu Ming with something that might have been curiosity.

"You love her."

It was not a question.

"Yes." The word came out before Xu Ming could think. Before he could weigh the consequences. "I love her."

He did not know when it had happened. Perhaps it was the moment she had walked into his shop, rain-soaked and desperate. Perhaps it was the night by the lake, when she had shown him her true face and he had not run. Perhaps it was now, watching her broken on the floor of his ruined pharmacy, knowing he would burn the world down to save her.

"Then you are a fool," Fahai said.

"No." Xu Ming reached into his pocket. His fingers closed around the small vial he had been carrying since the night she had first transformed. He had prepared it without knowing why. A mixture of herbs and minerals, ground fine and suspended in alcohol. He had told himself it was for research. For understanding.

He knew now what it was for.

"I am a pharmacist," Xu Ming said. "And I have been studying you."

Fahai's eyes narrowed. "Studying me?"

"You left a piece of yourself behind. Every temple you've visited. Every spirit you've purified." Xu Ming held up the vial. "Incense ash. Old blood. The residue of your mantras. I collected them. I analyzed them. I found the pattern."

"There is no pattern. I am pure."

"No one is pure." Xu Ming uncorked the vial. "Not even you."

He threw it.

The vial shattered against Fahai's chest. Liquid splashed across his robes. For a moment, nothing happened.

Then Fahai's skin began to smoke.

He looked down at his chest. At the place where the liquid had soaked through his robes. His expression was not pain. It was surprise. Utter, complete surprise.

"What," he said, "have you done?"

"I made a poison," Xu Ming said. "For snakes."

Fahai's head snapped up. His golden eyes blazed.

"I am not a snake."

"No," Xu Ming agreed. "But you have spent eight hundred years hunting them. Killing them. Absorbing their essence into your own cultivation." He stepped forward, his voice growing stronger. "You think I don't know what you are, Fahai? You think I haven't seen the scales beneath your skin? You are not a hunter. You are a cannibal. You have been devouring spirits for centuries, and their poison has become yours."

The smoke rose thicker. Fahai's skin began to crack. Not in lines of blood, but in patterns. Scales. Golden scales, pushing up from beneath his flesh.

"You think this will stop me?" His voice was no longer calm. It was a snarl. "You think a mortal's poison can touch me?"

"It already has."

Fahai looked down. The cracks were spreading. Running up his arms. Across his face. He was transforming, and it was not controlled. It was violent. Painful.

Bai Suyin stirred. Her eyes cleared. She looked at Fahai, then at Xu Ming, then at the vial's fragments on the floor.

"What did you do?" she whispered.

"I told you." Xu Ming's hands were shaking. "I studied him."

"No." She stood, unsteady, her body still flickering. "You couldn't have. He's been hidden for centuries. No one knows his true form."

"I didn't need to know his true form." Xu Ming looked at her. "I just needed to know what he was made of. And you told me."

"Told you?"

"When you said he burned incense. When you said he carried old blood." Xu Ming swallowed. "I traced the compounds. I found the pattern. The poison I made is specific to snake essence that has been corrupted by prolonged exposure to sanctified ash."

Bai Suyin stared at him. "You reversed-engineered a spirit poison from my descriptions?"

"I'm a pharmacist," Xu Ming said. "It's what I do."

Fahai was on his knees now. His robes had burned away, revealing a body that was no longer human. Scales covered every inch of him. His face had elongated. His teeth had sharpened. He was becoming something ancient. Something terrible.

But he was also dying.

"This is not over," he hissed. "I will return. I will always return. And when I do—"

"You won't remember." Xu Ming knelt beside him. "The poison doesn't just break down your body. It breaks down your cultivation. Every spirit you've consumed. Every memory you've stolen. It will all dissolve."

Fahai's eyes widened. For the first time, Xu Ming saw fear in them.

"You cannot."

"I already have."
