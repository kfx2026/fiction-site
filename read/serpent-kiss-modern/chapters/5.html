CHAPTER FIVE: The Taste of Forbidden Fruit

The rain started at midnight.

Xu Ming sat at his desk, the yellowed pages of the medical text spread before him like the wings of a dead bird. Thunder rolled across West Lake, distant and heavy. He'd been reading the same passage for three hours.

*White Snake Strain. Once harvested from the mountains of Emei. Believed extinct since the Tang Dynasty.*

The words blurred. He rubbed his eyes and checked his phone for the tenth time.

No messages.

Three days since Bai Suyin had walked out of his shop. Three days since she'd kissed him on the bridge. Three days since his world had cracked open and something ancient had crawled out.

He'd tried to find her. Asked neighbors, checked the temples around the lake, even called the police station. No one had seen a woman matching her description. It was as if she'd never existed.

But the herbs were real.

He opened the locked drawer beneath his desk, took out the bundle wrapped in silk. The leaves inside were dark green, almost black, with silver veins that caught the lamplight. He'd never seen anything like them. When he'd pressed one to his nose, the scent had been something between jasmine and old copper.

The bell above his shop door chimed.

Xu Ming's heart kicked. He shoved the bundle back in the drawer, slammed it shut, and stood.

"Sorry, we're—"

The words died in his throat.

Bai Suyin stood in the doorway. Her dress was soaked through, white fabric clinging to her skin. Water dripped from her hair, ran down her face like tears she wouldn't shed. Her eyes were different tonight. Not warm. Hungry.

"I shouldn't have come," she said.

"Where have you been?"

"Running."

"From what?"

She stepped inside, closed the door behind her without turning around. The lock clicked. "From the truth. I thought I could leave. Start over somewhere else. Let you forget me."

"Three days isn't enough to forget you."

"I know." She laughed, but there was no humor in it. "I have been walking the mountains around the lake. I climbed to the top of Leifeng Pagoda and sat in the rain. I tried to feel nothing. But you are here, Xu Ming. In my chest like a thorn."

He crossed to her. She didn't move as he took her hand, didn't flinch when his fingers touched her wrist.

Her pulse was wrong.

Too slow. Too steady. Like a drum beating once every ten seconds.

"Your heart," he said.

"Is beating. That is enough."

"No. I'm a pharmacist. I know what a healthy pulse sounds like. Yours is—"

"What it is." She pulled her hand away. "I came to tell you the truth. All of it. And then you can decide if you still want to touch me."

The rain hammered the roof. Water leaked through a crack in the frame, dripping onto the floor in steady rhythm.

"One thousand years ago," she said, "I was a snake."

Xu Ming stood very still.

"I was born in the ice caves of Kunlun. My mother was already dead when I hatched. I learned to hunt in darkness. I learned to kill before I learned to bask in the sun." She spoke without emotion, like she was reading a history of someone else's life. "I was not special. There were thousands like me. But I was lucky. A cultivator found me when I was starving, half-frozen in a blizzard. She took me in. Taught me to breathe the energy of the world. To draw qi into my body and make it my own."

"A snake," Xu Ming repeated.

"Yes. I have been human for barely six months. This body is borrowed from a woman who drowned in the Qiantang River. I found her before the fish did." She met his eyes. "I wore her like a coat."

He should have run. Should have screamed. Should have grabbed the iron pestle from his mortar and held it like a weapon.

Instead, he said: "Why me?"

"Because you are my heart trial."

"Your what?"

"When a spirit cultivates toward humanity, there is a final gate. One obstacle that cannot be passed through meditation or study." She stepped closer. Rainwater pooled at her feet. "To become human, I must love a mortal. Truly love him. And he must love me back. If I succeed, I shed the last of my serpent nature and walk among you forever."

"And if you fail?"

"Then I revert. Snake again. No memory of what I almost became. No hope of trying again." Her voice dropped. "I have waited a thousand years for this. I will not fail."

The words hung between them.

Xu Ming thought of his quiet shop. His carefully organized herbs. His routine that never changed because change had always terrified him.

He thought of her fingers on his skin. Her laugh by the lake. The way she'd looked at him like he was the only thing in the world worth seeing.

"You could have lied to me," he said. "Seduced me. Made me fall in love without ever knowing what you were."

"Yes."

"Why didn't you?"

"Because if you love a lie, it is not love." She touched his face. Her fingers were cold. "I want you to look at me and see the snake. See the monster. See every cold-blooded thing I have done to survive. And still choose me."

Thunder cracked overhead. The lights flickered.

"I don't know if I can," Xu Ming said honestly.

"Then let me show you something."

She stepped back, raised her hands, and began to glow.

The light came from beneath her skin, pale and shimmering like moonlight on water. Her eyes changed first — the irises turning vertical, the pupils dilating into black slits. Scales rippled across her cheekbones, silver-white and gleaming.

Xu Ming stumbled backward. His hip hit the desk, sending papers scattering.

She was beautiful. She was terrifying. She was something that should not exist.

"This is what I am," she said. Her voice had changed too. Something underneath the words, a low vibration that made the bottles on his shelves rattle. "This is the truth beneath the skin."

The transformation spread down her neck. Her fingers elongated, nails turning into claws. Behind her, something moved — a shape in the air, a suggestion of massive coils that couldn't possibly fit in his small shop.

"Stop," Xu Ming whispered.

She froze.

"Please. Stop."

The scales faded. Her eyes returned to human brown. The presence behind her dissolved like smoke. She stood before him again, just a woman in a wet dress, trembling slightly.

"That was only a fraction," she said. "If I chose to show you my true form, I would fill this room. Break the walls. You would see what a thousand years of cultivation looks like when it stops pretending."

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because Fahai knows I am here."

The name hit him like cold water.

"The monk from the temple?"

"He is not a monk. He is a hunter. He has been pursuing me for eight hundred years, across every incarnation, every life I have tried to build." Her jaw tightened. "He killed my teacher. He burned the mountain where I was born. He has made it his holy purpose to destroy any spirit that seeks to become human."

"He's why you came to me."

"Yes. I need protection. A mortal holding. Somewhere he cannot find me." She looked at him with desperate eyes. "But more than that, I need you to love me. Not because of the trial. Because if you love me, truly, I can fight him. If you love me, I can win."

Xu Ming's hands were shaking.

He was a pharmacist. He mixed herbs for coughs and fevers. His life was small and safe and predictable.

And now a thousand-year-old snake spirit was asking him to be her shelter.

"I need time," he said.

"We do not have time." She grabbed his wrist, pressed his palm flat against her chest. Her heart beat once. Twice. The space between each pulse was an eternity. "He has already found my trail. He will be here before the rain stops."

"How do you know?"

"Because I can smell him." Her nostrils flared. "Burning incense. Old blood. He walks the city like a wolf, and he is getting closer."

The shop's back door exploded inward.

Wood splintered. Glass shattered. And a figure stepped through the wreckage, robes dripping wet, prayer beads clicking in the dark.

"白素贞."

The voice was deep. Calm. Absolute.

"你逃不掉了."

Bai Suyin's face went white.

Xu Ming didn't speak Chinese well, but he caught the words she'd taught him by the lake. His name. Her name.

*You can't escape.*

The man in the doorway raised his head, and Xu Ming saw his eyes for the first time.

They were not human eyes. They were gold. Burning gold, like coins heated in a forge.

"I am Abbot Fahai," the man said, switching to Mandarin with a perfect, terrible smile. "And you, pharmacist, are harboring a demon."
