📖 Serpent's Kiss by Xu Xian

Chapter 15: The Price of Scales

~1.7k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The abbot's golden eyes fixed on Xu Ming with the weight of judgement.

"I have watched this city for three days," Fahai said, stepping over the shattered remains of the back door. His robes were dry now. The rain seemed to avoid him, sliding off an invisible barrier. "I smelled her before I saw her. A snake always leaves a trail."

Xu Ming positioned himself between Bai Suyin and the monk. His body moved before his mind caught up. "Get out of my shop."

"Brave. Foolish." Fahai's prayer beads clicked as he rotated them. Each bead was carved bone. Human bone, Xu Ming realized with dawning horror. "She has woven illusions around you. Made you believe she is a woman in need of rescue. But I have seen what she truly is. I have been seeing it for eight hundred years."

"Eight hundred years of chasing a woman who just wants to be human." Xu Ming's voice cracked but held. "That sounds like obsession, not justice."

Something flickered in Fahai's golden eyes. Not anger. Recognition.

"You love her already." The monk's tone shifted, becoming almost pitying. "That is unfortunate. It means you will suffer more when I show you the truth."

He reached into his robes and withdrew a mirror. Bronze. Ancient. The surface rippled like water.

"Do you know what this is, pharmacist? It is a reflection-catcher. Forged during the Tang Dynasty by monks who understood that demons hide behind beautiful faces." He held it up. "Would you like to see what she really looks like?"

"Don't." Bai Suyin's voice was sharp with fear. "Xu Ming, don't look."

But Xu Ming was already turning.

The mirror caught Bai Suyin's reflection, and the image warped. Her face remained, but beneath it, layered like sediment, was something vast. Scales covered her skin in patterns that shifted and crawled. Her eyes split vertically, pupil becoming slit, iris bleeding to gold. And behind her, coiling through the reflected space of his tiny shop, was a serpent so massive it could have wrapped around the entire building three times over.

White scales. Each one the size of a dinner plate. A head that could swallow a man whole. Fangs like curved daggers.

"That is what she hides," Fahai said softly. "That is the beast that wears your lover's face."

Xu Ming's knees buckled. He grabbed the counter to steady himself.

"Xu Ming." Bai Suyin's voice was small. Human. "Please. Look at me. Not the mirror. *Me.*"

He turned. She stood there, wet hair plastered to her face, eyes human and brown and terrified. She looked like a woman who had just been stripped naked in public.

"Every word I told you was true," she said. "I am a snake. I have been a snake for a thousand years. But I am also this." She touched her chest. "This heart. These feelings. They are real."

"The beast learns to imitate humanity," Fahai said. "But it does not feel. She needs your love to complete her trial because she cannot generate it herself. You are a tool, pharmacist. A means to an end."

"Then why didn't she just leave after I kissed her?" Xu Ming's voice rose. "Why is she still here, bleeding in my shop, asking me to protect her?"

Fahai's smile faltered.

"Because the trial isn't complete," Bai Suyin whispered. "It can't be forced. I can't take your love and leave. It has to be given freely, and it has to be sustained. If I abandoned you now, the trial would fail. I would lose everything."

"She explains it so neatly." Fahai stepped closer. "But conveniently omits the cost. Do you know what happens when a snake spirit completes her trial, pharmacist? She becomes human. Truly human. Mortal. Vulnerable. And every ounce of her cultivation — a thousand years of power — burns away in the transformation."

Xu Ming frowned. "So she would lose her power?"

"Yes. She would become weak. Helpless." Fahai's eyes gleamed. "A perfect target for every enemy she has made over a millennium. And she has made many."

"Fahai is one of them," Bai Suyin said. "He fears me as a snake. But he would kill me without hesitation as a human."

"Not kill." Fahai's voice dropped to a whisper. "Capture. Study. Dissect. A snake spirit that has become human is a miracle of transformation. The secrets in her blood could advance spiritual cultivation by centuries. Do you think I am the only one who would want such a prize?"

Xu Ming's mind raced. The pieces clicked together with terrible clarity.

"You're not just a hunter," he said slowly. "You're a collector."

Fahai inclined his head. "I am a preserver of balance. Demons should remain demons. Humans should remain humans. She seeks to break the natural order, and I seek to correct it."

"You seek to profit from it."

The gold in Fahai's eyes flickered. For a moment, Xu Ming saw something ugly beneath the holy facade.

"Enough talk." The monk raised his hand, and the prayer beads glowed. "I will take the serpent now. You can return to your quiet life, pharmacist. Forget this ever happened."

"Or what?"

"Or I will show you what happens to those who harbor demons."

Bai Suyin stepped forward. Her hand found Xu Ming's and squeezed. Her skin was cold. Scale-cold.

"Let me fight him," she said quietly. "I can hold him off long enough for you to escape."

"He'll kill you."

"Better than letting him take me." Her eyes met his. "Xu Ming. If I fall—"

"You're not falling." He turned to face Fahai fully. "You said you need my love. That it gives you strength."

"Yes."

"Then take it."

He pulled her close and kissed her. Not gentle. Not tentative. A kiss that was a declaration, a promise, a weapon.

Bai Suyin gasped against his lips. He felt something shift in her body — a warmth spreading where before there had only been cold. Her fingers dug into his shoulders, and when they broke apart, her eyes were no longer brown.

They were silver. Pure silver, like mercury in moonlight.

"What—" Fahai's voice cracked. "That's impossible. The trial cannot advance without ritual completion."

"Then maybe your rules are wrong." Bai Suyin's voice had changed. Deeper. Resonant. It seemed to come from everywhere at once. "Maybe love isn't a transaction. Maybe it's a choice."

She raised her hand, and the air around her shimmered. Scales appeared on her arms, but they were different now — not the cold white of a beast, but the soft iridescence of pearl. Her hair lifted, dry despite the rain still falling through the broken door.

"You have hunted me for eight centuries," she said, her voice carrying the weight of a thousand years. "You have killed my teacher. Burned my home. Murdered everyone I have ever loved." Her silver eyes locked onto Fahai. "But you made one mistake."

"And what is that?"

"You made me stop running."

The shop exploded with light.

Not fire. Not magic in any way Xu Ming understood. It was presence — the sheer weight of a being that had stopped pretending to be small. Bai Suyin grew taller, her form flickering between human and serpent, and the walls of the shop began to crack.

Fahai raised his mirror, chanting in a language older than Mandarin. The bronze surface flared, trying to capture her, contain her.

The mirror shattered.

Shards of ancient bronze rained down like glass. Fahai stumbled backward, blood streaming from his nose. His golden eyes were wide with shock.

"You broke the reflection-catcher," he whispered. "That was blessed by seven generations of abbots."

"Blessings are just words." Bai Suyin stepped forward, and the floorboards splintered beneath her feet. "And words can be broken."

She reached for him, and Fahai moved faster than Xu Ming could track. He threw something — a pouch of powder that burst into white smoke. When it cleared, the back door was empty, and Fahai's voice echoed from outside.

"This isn't over, serpent. I know what you're becoming. And I know who you love."

The rain swallowed his footsteps.

Bai Suyin collapsed.

Xu Ming caught her before she hit the ground, cradling her against his chest. Her skin was burning hot. Her silver eyes were fading, flickering back to brown.

"What happened?" he asked. "What did you do?"

"I... borrowed your love." She laughed weakly. "Used it like fuel. I've never done that before. I didn't know it would work."

"Are you okay?"

"I'm exhausted." Her eyes fluttered closed. "I need to rest. Just... give me a minute."

"Bai Suyin."

"Mmm?"

"Your arm."

She looked down. The scales on her arms had not faded. They remained, iridescent and beautiful, but unmistakably *not human*.

"I can't change them back," she whispered, and for the first time, Xu Ming heard genuine fear in her voice. "I think... I think I used too much power. I think I'm stuck."

"Stuck how?"

She looked up at him, and her silver eyes held an ancient terror.

"I think I'm becoming the snake permanently."

The rain continued to fall through the broken door. Somewhere in the distance, a temple bell began to toll.

And Xu Ming held her tighter, knowing that Fahai would return, knowing that the hunt was far from over, and knowing that the woman in his arms was slipping away one scale at a time.

"Then we find a way to bring you back," he said.

"You don't understand." Her voice cracked. "There's no going back. Once the transformation starts, it doesn't stop. I'll lose myself. I'll become nothing but a beast."

"Then we change the rules."

"How?"

Xu Ming looked at the shattered mirror, the broken door, the city beyond that held a monk with golden eyes and a thousand years of hate.

"I don't know yet," he admitted. "But I'm not letting you go."

Bai Suyin's hand found his. Her fingers were longer than before. Stronger. Claws beginning to push through the nails.

"I love you," she said. "I don't know if it's real or if the trial made me say it. But I love you."

"It's real," Xu Ming said. "Because I love you too. And nothing — not Fahai, not the transformation, not a thousand years of anything — is going to change that."

Outside, the temple bell stopped ringing.

And in the silence that followed, they both heard a new sound.

Footsteps. Rhythmic. Measured.

Coming closer.
