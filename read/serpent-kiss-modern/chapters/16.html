📖 Serpent's Kiss by Xu Xian

Chapter 16: The Price of Her Heart

~1.7k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The rain had stopped.

Xu Ming stood at the window of his ruined shop, watching dawn creep over the shattered rooftops of Hangzhou. The street below was empty—too empty. No vendors setting up their stalls. No early morning joggers. The kind of silence that meant people had been warned to stay away.

He hadn't slept. Couldn't sleep. Every time he closed his eyes, he saw Fahai's golden gaze burning through the dark.

Three days since the attack. Three days since Bai Suyin had dragged him through the back exit while Fahai's prayer beads turned to chains of fire, whipping through the shop like serpents of their own. They'd fled through alleyways, over walls, across rooftops—her hand clamped around his wrist, her body moving with a fluid grace that was not entirely human.

They'd ended up here. A safe house, she'd called it. An apartment above a closed tea shop in the old quarter, owned by a woman who never asked questions and never came upstairs.

Xu Ming turned from the window. Bai Suyin sat cross-legged on the floor in the center of the room, her eyes closed, her breathing slow and measured. Since the attack, she'd been weaker. The glow beneath her skin had dimmed. Sometimes, when she thought he wasn't looking, he caught her staring at her own hands as if they were strangers.

"It's been three days," he said.

"I am aware."

"He hasn't found us."

"He is waiting."

"For what?"

Her eyes opened. The irises flickered—human one moment, serpentine the next. "For me to make a mistake. For you to leave. For the inevitable."

Xu Ming walked over and sat down across from her. The floor was cold. The room smelled of dust and old tea leaves. "I'm not leaving."

"You should."

"I know."

A ghost of a smile touched her lips. It vanished quickly. "Fahai will not attack directly again. He underestimated me once. He will not make that error twice. The next time he comes, he will bring the full might of his order. Monks who have spent lifetimes training to kill spirits. Artifacts forged in the fires of ancient temples. He will burn this city to find me."

"Then we leave Hangzhou."

"Where would we go? He has hunted me across every province, every dynasty. I have hidden in mountains, in rivers, in the courts of emperors. He always finds me." Her voice dropped. "Because he knows what I am. And what I am leaves a trail that cannot be erased."

Xu Ming reached out and took her hand. Her skin was cold. Always cold. "Then we change what you are."

She looked at him sharply.

"The trial," he said. "You told me you need to love a mortal to complete your transformation. To become fully human. What happens if you succeed?"

For a long moment, she said nothing. Then she pulled her hand away. "You do not understand what you are offering."

"Then explain it to me."

"The trial is not a ritual. It is not a spell I can cast. It is a process that takes root in the soul. It requires—" She stopped. Her jaw tightened. "It requires me to give something in return."

"What?"

"My cultivation."

The words hung in the air like smoke.

"A thousand years of spiritual power," she continued, her voice barely above a whisper. "Every technique I have mastered. Every lifetime of memories. I must surrender them to become human. I would be reborn as a mortal woman—no powers, no immortality, no protection. I would age. I would die. I would be as fragile as you."

"And Fahai?"

"He would sense the moment I begin the transformation. It would draw him like blood in water. He would have one chance to destroy me—while I am caught between forms, neither snake nor human, utterly helpless."

Xu Ming's chest tightened. "How long does the transformation take?"

"Seven days. From the moment the heart trial is complete, I have seven days to shed my old self. During that time, I cannot fight. I cannot flee. I can only endure."

"Then we protect you for seven days."

"You cannot protect me from Fahai. You are a pharmacist."

"I know what I am." His voice came out harder than he intended. "I know I'm not a warrior. I know I can't fight monks with golden eyes and fire chains. But I can hide you. I can keep you safe. I can—" He stopped. Swallowed. "I can love you."

The words fell between them like a stone into still water.

Bai Suyin stared at him. Her expression was unreadable—a mask carved from centuries of survival. But something flickered behind her eyes. Something raw.

"You barely know me," she said.

"I know you came into my shop and asked for herbs that haven't been used in a thousand years. I know you saved my life when Fahai attacked. I know you could have killed me a dozen times over, but instead you held my hand and told me the truth." He leaned forward. "I know that when you smile, it changes your whole face. I know that you hum old songs when you think no one is listening. I know that you're terrified of becoming human because you've spent so long pretending to be one that you've forgotten what it actually means."

A single tear slid down her cheek. She wiped it away quickly, almost angrily. "You are a fool."

"Probably."

"A dangerous fool."

"The best kind."

She laughed. It was a broken sound, like glass cracking. "I have not laughed in three hundred years."

"Then I'm already doing something right."

She reached out and touched his face. Her fingers traced the line of his jaw, the curve of his cheek. Her touch was light, almost reverent. "If I do this—if I begin the transformation—there is no turning back. I will gain mortality. I will lose everything else. And if Fahai finds us before the seven days are complete, he will kill us both."

"I understand."

"No. You do not." Her hand dropped. "There is one more thing. A price I did not tell you about."

Xu Ming's stomach tightened. "What price?"

"The heart trial requires balance. I must love you—truly, completely—but you must love me in return. Not as a spirit. Not as a curiosity. As a woman." She paused. "If your love is not genuine, the trial fails. I am trapped between forms forever. Neither snake nor human. A ghost in my own skin."

"And if my love is genuine?"

"Then I become human." Her eyes searched his. "But you will never be the same. Loving me will change you. It will open your eyes to a world you cannot unsee. You will carry the weight of what I was, what I gave up. Every day, for the rest of your mortal life, you will remember that I was once something more."

"I can live with that."

"Can you?" She stood. Her movements were fluid, graceful, but there was a tension in her shoulders that hadn't been there before. "I have loved before, Xu Ming. In my thousand years, I have loved three times. Each time, it ended in blood. Each time, Fahai found them first."

She turned to face him fully. Her eyes were wet. "He does not just kill them. He makes me watch. He makes me hold their bodies as they grow cold. He tells me that this is the price of loving a demon. And then he lets me go, so I can carry the grief for another century."

Xu Ming stood. He crossed the distance between them in three steps. He took her face in his hands and pressed his forehead against hers.

"I am not them," he said.

"You do not know that."

"I know that I'm still here. That you told me the truth. That you trusted me." He pulled back just enough to meet her eyes. "Start the transformation."

"Xu Ming—"

"I'm not afraid."

"You should be."

"I'm not."

She closed her eyes. Her breath shuddered. And then, slowly, she nodded.

"Seven days," she whispered. "Starting now."

The air changed. Something shifted in the room—a pressure, a warmth, a scent of rain and flowers. Bai Suyin's skin began to glow, soft and white, like moonlight through clouds. Her form wavered, flickered, grew solid again.

When she opened her eyes, they were brown. Human brown.

And behind her, the faint outline of massive coils faded into nothing.

She swayed. Xu Ming caught her.

"It has begun," she breathed.

And then the apartment door exploded off its hinges.

Fahai stood in the doorway, but he was not alone. Behind him, a dozen figures in dark robes stood in perfect silence. Their eyes were not golden. They were empty. Hollow. Like dolls waiting for a puppeteer.

"You made a mistake, serpent," Fahai said. "I felt the transformation begin. I felt your power bleed away." His smile was cold and terrible. "You are weak now. Vulnerable. And I have brought my finest hunters."

He stepped forward. The robed figures moved with him, a single dark wave.

"Seven days," Fahai said. "But I will not give you seven hours."

Bai Suyin pressed herself against Xu Ming's chest. Her heart beat once. Twice. Fragile. Mortal.

And Xu Ming realized, with a clarity that cut through everything, that he had just promised to protect a woman who could no longer protect herself.

He looked at Fahai. Looked at the hunters. Looked at the woman trembling in his arms.

Then he reached into his pocket and pulled out a small vial—one of the herbs she had taught him to mix, back in the shop that now lay in ruins.

"Seven hours," Xu Ming said, uncorking the vial. "That's all I need."

He threw the vial at Fahai's feet.

The glass shattered. White smoke erupted—thick, blinding, carrying the scent of a thousand-year-old forest.

And Xu Ming grabbed Bai Suyin's hand and ran.
