CHAPTER 12: THE BURNING GARDEN

The hospital room smelled of antiseptic and fear.

Xu Ming sat in a plastic chair beside An Xin's bed, watching her chest rise and fall beneath thin white sheets. Her face was pale, her wrists wrapped in gauze where she'd clawed at herself during the seizure. The doctors said she'd be fine. Physically. But they couldn't explain what had caused her brain to spike like a lightning rod, couldn't understand why her pupils had turned vertical for three full minutes before the sedatives kicked in.

"I saw her," An Xin whispered, not opening her eyes.

Xu Ming leaned forward. "Who?"

"The woman in your shop. The one with the white dress." An Xin's fingers twitched. "She was standing in my head. Looking at me through my own eyes. She said... she said I was in her way."

Cold dread pooled in Xu Ming's stomach. He'd left Bai Suyin at the shop, hidden in the storage room behind the herb cabinets, wrapped in layers of salt and iron filings she'd insisted on. The Abbot Fahai had retreated after their confrontation, but his parting words echoed in Xu Ming's memory: *I will return when you are ready to see the truth.*

"You need to stay away from her," Xu Ming said.

"I need to stay away from *you*." An Xin opened her eyes. They were normal now—brown, tired, human—but there was something hard in them he'd never seen before. "Whatever you've gotten yourself into, Xu Ming, it's not safe. She's not safe."

"She's not dangerous."

"She entered my mind without touching me. She made my own brain turn against my body." An Xin's voice cracked. "What do you call that?"

Xu Ming had no answer.

His phone buzzed. A text from an unknown number: *THE WEST LAKE PAGODA. MIDNIGHT. COME ALONE OR SHE DIES.*

He showed it to An Xin. Her face went gray.

"That's Fahai's number," she said. "I saw it in his file. He's been using burner phones since the 90s."

"His file? How do you have his file?"

An Xin closed her eyes. "I've been investigating him for six months, Xu Ming. Ever since my grandmother died. She was the one who told me about the snake spirits, about the war between heaven and earth that's been happening right under everyone's noses." She laughed bitterly. "I thought she was senile. I thought it was dementia talking. But then I found her journals. Photographs. Newspaper clippings going back to 1920. Women with snakes coiled in their hair. Men with golden eyes. A monk who never ages."

"You knew about Fahai before tonight?"

"I knew he existed. I didn't know he was real until he walked into your shop." She grabbed his wrist with bandaged hands. "Don't go to the pagoda. Whatever he wants, it's a trap. He doesn't negotiate. He doesn't show mercy. He just *removes* things."

Xu Ming pulled his hand free. "Bai Suyin is there."

"She's a snake, Xu Ming. A thousand-year-old snake who's been using you for her trial. You said it yourself—she needs you to love her. That's not romance. That's *survival*."

"Even if that's true, I can't let him kill her."

"Then you're a fool."

He stood up. "Maybe. But I'm her fool."

The walk to West Lake took forty minutes. The rain had stopped, leaving the streets slick and gleaming under streetlights. The pagoda rose from the island in the center of the lake like a black finger pointing at the moon. Xu Ming rented a boat from a confused old man who kept muttering about ghosts, and rowed across water that was too still, too dark, too *deep*.

He could feel something watching him from beneath the surface.

The pagoda's ground floor was empty. Dust and prayer scraps. A broken statue of Guanyin with her head replaced by a golden coin. The air smelled of incense, old blood, and something else—something cold and mineral, like a cave where snakes had nested for centuries.

"You came."

Fahai stood at the center of the room, his robes dry despite the humidity. His golden eyes reflected the moonlight like a cat's. Beside him, Bai Suyin knelt on the stone floor, her hands bound in front of her with prayer beads that seemed to pulse with a faint red light. Her face was bruised. One eye was swollen shut.

"Let her go," Xu Ming said.

"Let her go?" Fahai laughed. The sound echoed off the stone walls, multiplied by some acoustics that shouldn't exist. "I have spent eight hundred years hunting this creature. I have lost brothers to her venom. I have watched her devour villages and seduce kings and poison wells. You ask me to let her go as if she were a stray dog."

"She's not a demon."

"She is a serpent who has stolen human form. The difference is academic." Fahai walked around Bai Suyin, his fingers trailing across her shoulders. She flinched but didn't speak. "She told you about her trial, yes? The heart trial. Love a mortal, become human. Kill a mortal, lose your cultivation." He stopped, turned to face Xu Ming. "Did she tell you what happens if the mortal dies *before* she completes the trial?"

Xu Ming's blood went cold.

"I see she didn't." Fahai smiled. "If you die, Xu Ming, her cultivation shatters. All her power, all her centuries of work, gone in an instant. She becomes nothing more than a snake. An ordinary snake, cold and dumb, slithering through the mud until someone steps on her."

"That's why you want to kill me."

"No." Fahai's smile widened. "I want you to kill *her*."

He tossed something at Xu Ming's feet. A knife. Ancient bronze, covered in green patina, its edge still gleaming sharp. The handle was wrapped in dried leather.

"That blade was forged in the Tang Dynasty," Fahai said. "Tempered in the blood of a thousand snakes. One cut, and her spirit is severed from her body. One cut, and she dies permanently. No reincarnation. No rebirth. Just *nothing*."

Bai Suyin raised her head. Her good eye met Xu Ming's, and he saw something in it he'd never seen before: fear. Real, naked, animal fear. The same fear a mouse feels when the cat has it pinned.

"Don't," she whispered. "Xu Ming. Please."

"She's begging you now," Fahai said. "But she won't beg when she kills you. She won't hesitate when she drains your life force to complete her trial. She's been lying to you since the moment you met. Every word, every touch, every moment of tenderness—all calculated. All part of the game."

"She came to me for medicine."

"She came to you because you're weak. Because you're alone. Because no one would notice if you disappeared." Fahai crouched beside Bai Suyin, grabbed her chin, forced her to look at Xu Ming. "Tell him the truth, serpent. Tell him why you really chose him."

Bai Suyin's lips trembled. "I chose him because..."

"Because *what*?"

"Because he looked at me like I was human." Her voice broke. "The first time I walked into his shop, he asked if I was in pain. He offered me tea. He treated me like a *person*." Tears spilled down her cheeks. "Do you know how long it's been since anyone looked at me that way? A hundred years? Two hundred? Everyone else sees a demon, a monster, a thing to be feared or used. But Xu Ming saw *me*."

Fahai dropped her chin. "Lies. All lies."

"Then prove it," Xu Ming said.

Both of them turned to look at him.

He picked up the knife. The weight was strange—heavier than it should be, as if the blade was made of something denser than bronze. The handle fit his palm perfectly, like it had been waiting for him.

"If she's lying," Xu Ming said, "then let me test her. You said she needs me alive to complete her trial. You said she'll lose everything if I die." He raised the knife, pressed the blade against his own throat. The edge was so sharp it drew blood with barely any pressure. "If I kill myself right now, her cultivation shatters. She becomes nothing. You win."

"No!" Bai Suyin lunged forward, but the prayer beads flared red and slammed her back to the floor.

Fahai's golden eyes narrowed. "You would sacrifice yourself for a demon?"

"I would sacrifice myself for someone who made me feel like I mattered." Xu Ming pressed harder. Blood trickled down his neck, staining his collar. "But if you stop her before she can stop me, that proves she's only using me. That proves everything you've said is true."

"Xu Ming, don't." Bai Suyin's voice was raw. "Please. I don't care about the trial. I don't care about becoming human. I care about *you*."

"Then prove it."

He tightened his grip on the knife.

The blade bit deeper.

And Bai Suyin screamed.

It wasn't a human scream. It was raw and ancient and full of anguish that spanned centuries. The sound shattered the windows of the pagoda. The prayer beads around her wrists cracked. Red light exploded outward, and Fahai stumbled back, shielding his face.

Bai Suyin rose.

She was no longer pretending to be human. Her skin had gone pale as moonlight, scales rippling across her cheeks and hands. Her eyes were vertical slits, burning with white fire. The coils of her true form pressed against the walls of the pagoda, cracking the stone.

"Put the knife down," she said, and her voice was not one voice but a chorus, layered and ancient. "Put it down, or I will destroy this entire city to save you."

"See?" Fahai laughed from the corner. "See her true nature? Violence. Always violence. She cannot help herself."

Bai Suyin turned to face him. The air around her shimmered. "You took everything from me. My teacher. My mountain. My first life. My second. My third. You will not take this one."

"Then kill me, serpent." Fahai spread his arms. "Prove to your mortal that I am right. Prove that you are nothing but a beast wearing a human face."

Bai Suyin's hand shot out—

And stopped.

Her claws hovered an inch from Fahai's throat. Her breath came in ragged gasps. The white fire in her eyes flickered.

She was fighting herself.

Xu Ming saw it clearly. She wanted to kill Fahai. Every instinct in her thousand-year-old body screamed for his blood. But she was holding back. For him. To prove that she could be more than a monster.

"Xu Ming," she said, not looking away from Fahai. "Walk away. Go back to your shop. Forget you ever met me."

"And let him kill you?"

"He won't kill me. He can't. Not without the knife." Her voice dropped. "I am the White Snake of West Lake. I have survived dynasties. I will survive him too."

"You'll be alone."

"I have always been alone."

Fahai's hand moved. Faster than Xu Ming's eyes could track. He grabbed Bai Suyin's wrist, twisted, and slammed her to the ground. The pagoda shook. Dust
