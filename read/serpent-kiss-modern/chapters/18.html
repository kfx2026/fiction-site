📖 Serpent's Kiss by Xu Xian

Chapter 18: The Blood on His Hands

~1.8k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The golden light faded from Fahai's eyes, leaving only the cold, patient gaze of a man who had been hunting the same prey for eight centuries. His robes dripped rainwater onto the shattered floorboards. His prayer beads clicked once, twice, three times—each sound like a nail being driven into wood.

"You have thirty seconds to step away from the demon," Fahai said. His voice carried no anger. No threat. Just the flat certainty of a man who had already decided how this night would end.

Xu Ming's hand was still pressed against Bai Suyin's chest. Her heart hammered beneath his palm, a wild drumbeat that told him she was terrified—despite her thousand years of cultivation, despite the power that coiled beneath her human skin, she was afraid of this man.

"No," Xu Ming said.

The word surprised him. It came from somewhere deeper than thought, somewhere primal and stubborn. He had spent his entire life avoiding confrontation. He lowered his head when customers shouted. He apologized when he was right. He had never once stood his ground against anything more threatening than a speeding ticket.

But this. *This* was different.

Fahai's eyebrows rose a fraction of an inch. "You would protect a snake?"

"She hasn't hurt anyone."

"She will." Fahai stepped forward. His bare feet made no sound on the broken glass. "They always do. It is not cruelty that drives them—it is nature. A snake cannot become human any more than fire can become water. The very attempt is an abomination."

"I've heard enough speeches," Bai Suyin said. Her voice had changed. It was deeper now, layered with something that made the air vibrate. She stepped in front of Xu Ming, placing herself between him and Fahai. "Eight hundred years of the same words. *Abomination. Demon. Unnatural.* You murdered my teacher for the crime of wanting to feel sunlight on her skin."

"Your teacher was a serpent who devoured seventy-three mortals before I found her."

"Because you starved her! You trapped her in a cave for forty years with no way to feed except—"

"Enough." Fahai raised his hand. The prayer beads began to glow, each bead lighting up like a miniature sun. "I did not come here to debate history. I came to finish what should have been completed in the Song Dynasty."

He spoke a single syllable. It was not Chinese. It was something older, a sound that seemed to crawl into Xu Ming's skull and set his teeth on edge.

Bai Suyin screamed.

She collapsed to her knees, clutching her head. Her skin rippled—scales appearing and disappearing in waves, her eyes flickering between human brown and serpent gold. The presence behind her, the massive shape that had haunted Xu Ming's nightmares for the past three days, surged forward and was violently yanked back.

"You see?" Fahai said softly. "The sutra binds her. Every lifetime, I have carved these words into her soul. No matter where she runs, no matter what face she wears, I can always find her. I can always *hurt* her."

Xu Ming's body moved before his mind caught up.

He grabbed a mortar from the counter—heavy stone, worn smooth by decades of grinding herbs—and threw it at Fahai's head.

The monk caught it without looking. His fingers closed around the stone and crushed it to dust.

"Foolish," Fahai said. "You are a mortal. Fragile. Temporary. Your entire life is less than a blink to creatures like her. Why would you throw it away for a demon?"

"Because she asked for my help."

"And that is enough?"

"No." Xu Ming stepped forward, his legs shaking. "But she also apologized. She said she was sorry for lying. And she didn't have to say that. She could have taken what she wanted and left. But she stayed."

Fahai studied him for a long moment. Something shifted in his golden eyes—not pity, but a kind of clinical curiosity.

"You love her already."

It was not a question.

"I don't know what I feel," Xu Ming said honestly. "But I know what I *don't* feel. I don't feel afraid of her. Not anymore. And I don't feel like she deserves to die for something she hasn't done yet."

"A beautiful sentiment." Fahai's smile returned, colder than before. "Shall I show you what she has already done?"

He reached into his robe and withdrew a folded piece of paper. It was yellowed, ancient, covered in characters that seemed to writhe when Xu Ming tried to read them.

"This is a record from the year 1247. A village in Fujian. Sixty-three people died in a single night. Their bodies were found with two small puncture wounds on their throats. No blood remained in their veins."

"That wasn't me," Bai Suyin said from the floor. Her voice was weak, strained. "I was in Sichuan that year. I have witnesses."

"Your witnesses were spirits. They would say anything to protect you."

"Then why does the record not name me? If you were so certain, why did you not write my name?"

Fahai's smile flickered.

"Because you did not have a name then," he said quietly. "You were just a white snake with no cultivation, no soul worth recording. I did not hunt you because you were dangerous. I hunted you because you had *potential* to be dangerous. And I was right."

He turned back to Xu Ming.

"Every century, she grows stronger. Every century, she comes closer to completing her transformation. And when she does—when she finally becomes fully human—she will no longer be bound by the laws that restrain spirits. She will be able to kill without leaving a trace. She will be able to walk among mortals as an equal, and no one will ever know what she truly is."

"Unless you stop her."

"Yes." Fahai's voice dropped to a whisper. "Unless I stop her. But I am old, pharmacist. Older than this city. Older than this country. I have killed hundreds of spirits, and each death takes something from me. When I am gone, there will be no one left to hold the line."

He looked at Bai Suyin with something that might have been sorrow.

"I cannot kill her tonight. I came here to warn you. To show you what she is before it is too late."

"Then what do you want?"

Fahai's eyes met his. For just a moment, the golden light faded, and Xu Ming saw something human underneath—tired, broken, desperate.

"I want you to help me complete her trial."

The words hung in the air like poison.

"No," Bai Suyin said. She struggled to her feet, her body trembling. "You cannot. You *cannot*."

"The trial requires true love," Fahai continued, ignoring her. "If the mortal who is her heart trial rejects her—truly, completely, with full understanding of what she is—the trial fails. She loses all her cultivation. She becomes a common snake again, with no memory of what she was."

"You want me to break her heart."

"I want you to save her soul." Fahai stepped closer. "I have pursued her for eight hundred years because I believed death was the only mercy I could offer. But I was wrong. There is another way. A gentler way. If she loses her cultivation, she can live out her natural life as a serpent. No more running. No more hunting. No more fear."

"And she will forget everything," Xu Ming said. "She will forget who she was. She will forget me."

"Yes."

Bai Suyin grabbed his arm. Her fingers were cold, her grip frantic.

"Xu Ming. Listen to me. If I lose my cultivation, I lose everything I am. Every memory. Every lesson. Every life I have lived. I will become a blank slate—a baby snake with no past, no soul, no *self*. That is not mercy. That is annihilation."

"The alternative is death," Fahai said flatly. "I have given you eight hundred years of chances. This is the last one. Choose."

Xu Ming looked at Bai Suyin. Her face was streaked with tears, her eyes wild with fear. She had shown him her true form. She had trusted him with the truth. And now she was asking him to save her—not from death, but from oblivion.

"What if I refuse?" he asked.

Fahai's expression hardened.

"Then in three days, I will return with the full force of my order. We will seal this building. We will burn every herb, every record, every trace of her existence. And we will drag her back to the mountain where she was born and chain her to the earth until her body turns to stone."

"You would do that?"

"I have done it before." Fahai turned toward the shattered door. "Three days, pharmacist. Use them wisely."

He stepped through the wreckage and vanished into the rain.

The shop fell silent.

Bai Suyin collapsed against Xu Ming, her entire body shaking. He caught her, held her, felt her heart racing against his chest.

"I'm sorry," she whispered. "I'm so sorry. I shouldn't have come here. I shouldn't have dragged you into this."

"Stop."

"But—"

"Stop apologizing." He tightened his arms around her. "I'm not letting him take you."

"Then what do we do?"

Xu Ming looked around his ruined shop. The broken door. The shattered bottles. The scattered herbs that had once been his entire world, now reduced to debris. His life had been small and safe and predictable.

And then Bai Suyin had walked in.

"Three days," he said slowly. "He's giving us three days."

"That's not enough time to—"

"It's enough." He pulled back, meeting her eyes. "You said you need me to love you. Truly. For the trial to work."

"Yes."

"Then I need to know everything." His voice was steady now, calm in a way that surprised him. "No more secrets. No more half-truths. Tell me about your teacher. Tell me about the village in Fujian. Tell me about every life you've lived, every person you've loved, every sin you've committed."

"Why?"

He took her hand. Her fingers were warm now, human, fragile.

"Because if I'm going to love you," he said, "I need to know who I'm loving."

Bai Suyin stared at him for a long moment. Then she laughed—a broken, desperate sound that was half sob.

"You are either the bravest man I have ever met," she said, "or the most foolish."

"Probably both."

She leaned forward and pressed her forehead against his.

"Tomorrow," she said. "I will tell you everything tomorrow. But tonight..."

"Tonight?"

"Tonight I just want to be here. With you. Before everything changes."

The rain continued to fall outside. The broken door let in the cold night air. Somewhere in the distance, Xu Ming heard the sound of temple bells, carried on the wind.

Three days.

He held her tighter and tried not to think about what he would have to do if love was not enough.
