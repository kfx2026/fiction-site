📖 Children of the Dragon by Long Wei

Chapter 2: The Weight of Scales

~1.7k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The Celestial Arena stretched across the eastern sky like a wound split open between clouds. Long Jie stood at its edge, his bare feet pressed against stone that hummed with the trapped heat of a thousand suns. Below him, the mortal world spun in lazy blue and green, so far down that the great rivers looked like veins on a sleeping giant's hand.

Six of his brothers had already claimed their positions around the arena's rim. Each one a different shape of power.

Long Yin, the eldest, had taken the form of a coiled jade serpent three times the height of a man, his scales etched with characters of law and judgment. When he breathed, mist curled from his nostrils like the exhalation of mountains. He did not look at Jie. He never looked at Jie.

Long Feng stood opposite his twin, his flames painting shadows across the stone. Fire licked between his teeth, turning his words to smoke. "Still wearing mortal skin, little brother? The Trials begin today, and you come dressed as a man."

Jie touched his own face. Fingers against cheek. Flesh and bone. No scales. No horns. No fire.

"The Emperor's blood runs thin," said Long Shan from somewhere above. He had taken sky form, a dragon of storm clouds and thunder, his voice rumbling through the air like distant war drums. "Perhaps our mother loved a human too well. Perhaps the youngest is not a dragon at all."

The other brothers laughed. Six voices. Six kinds of cruelty.

Jie did not answer. He had learned, in his sixteen years of life, that words were weapons his brothers wielded better than he ever could. They had centuries of practice. He had only the memory of his mother's hands brushing his hair back from his forehead, the way she would whisper, "You have my eyes, little one. Your father said that was a gift."

She had died when he was seven. The Emperor had not come to her funeral.

The Seventh Brother, Long Xu, drifted closer on currents of shadow. He was the quiet one, the one who watched from corners, whose form was never quite solid. "The trials begin at noon," he said, his voice like paper sliding across stone. "Father's steward will announce the first test."

"Father's steward is dead," Long Yin said, unwinding his great body with a sound like grinding jade. "The Emperor's heart stopped beating three days ago. The steward followed him into the void, as is the custom."

The arena fell silent.

Jie felt it then. A shift in the air. A pressure change, like before a typhoon. Something had been broken. Something that could not be fixed with fire or law or the weight of jade coils.

"Then who announces?" Long Feng asked, his flames flickering low.

Long Yin's head turned slowly. His eyes, black and gold, found Jie for the first time in years.

"The youngest holds the Emperor's final decree."

Jie's blood turned to ice.

"I have no such thing."

"You do." Long Yin's voice carried no warmth, no accusation. Simply fact. "Father summoned you on his deathbed. The servants saw you enter. They saw you leave. You carry something beneath your ribs, little brother. The question is whether you will open your hands or keep them closed."

Every eye fixed on him. Six brothers. Six sets of teeth and claws and judgment.

Jie remembered that night. The Emperor's chambers had smelled of sandalwood and something rotting beneath. His father had looked small, smaller than Jie had ever imagined the Celestial Dragon could look. Skin like old parchment stretched across bones that had once held up the sky.

"Come closer, boy."

Jie had approached. Had taken the withered hand when it reached for him. Had felt the cold of death already settling into those fingers.

"Your mother," the Emperor had whispered, "was the only good thing I ever did. And I did not deserve her."

Then the Emperor had pressed something into Jie's palm. Something that burned. Something that crawled up his arm and settled behind his heart like a second pulse.

"Hide it," the Emperor had said. "Hide it until you understand what it means. Then decide."

The Emperor had died before Jie could ask what the thing was. Before he could ask anything at all.

Now, standing in the Celestial Arena with his brothers circling like sharks, Jie understood that whatever his father had given him was not a gift. It was a target.

"I have nothing," Jie said, and the lie tasted like copper on his tongue.

Long Yin's eyes narrowed. "The Trials cannot begin without the decree. If you hold it, you must speak it. If you have destroyed it—"

"I would never."

"Then produce it."

Jie felt the thing pulse behind his heart. Felt it push against his ribs like a caged animal trying to escape. He did not know what it was. He did not know what it wanted. But he knew, with the certainty of a boy who had learned to survive through silence, that showing it would kill him.

"I will speak it," he said, "when the time is right."

"Your judgment is not trusted."

"Father trusted it."

"Father is dead."

The words hung in the air like smoke. Long Yin had spoken them without ceremony, without grief. Just fact. Just the truth of a world that had lost its center.

Jie looked at his brothers. Long Yin, coiled and patient. Long Feng, flames banked but ready. Long Shan, clouds swirling in agitation. Long Xu, watching from shadows. The eighth brother, Long Yi, who had not spoken a word in Jie's presence since they were children. And the ninth, Long Bo, who had taken the form of a great turtle, his shell inscribed with maps of all the realms.

None of them would help him. None of them had ever helped him.

"I will speak the decree," Jie said again, "when I understand what it means."

"You understand nothing," Long Yin said. "You are a child playing at dragon. You wear mortal skin because you cannot hold our form. You speak mortal words because you have not learned our tongue. You are not one of us, little brother. You never were."

The words struck like physical blows. Jie felt them land. Felt them settle into the spaces where his mother's hands used to rest.

"I am the Emperor's son."

"You are the Emperor's mistake."

Long Feng laughed. Long Shan's thunder rumbled in agreement. Even Long Xu, the quiet one, allowed a shadow of a smile to cross his indistinct features.

Jie turned away from them.

He walked to the edge of the arena and looked down at the mortal world. Somewhere down there, in the blue and green, his mother's village still stood. The house where she had raised him. The fields where she had taught him to grow rice. The river where she had shown him how to catch fish with his hands.

She had not been a dragon. She had been human. And she had loved him anyway.

"I will compete in the Trials," Jie said, his voice carrying across the arena despite the wind. "I will learn the decree. And when I understand it, I will speak it."

"You will not survive the first test," Long Yin said.

"Perhaps not."

"And yet you still try?"

Jie turned back. Met his eldest brother's eyes. Saw nothing there but cold calculation and the weight of centuries.

"Yes," he said. "I try. Because that is what my mother taught me. Not to win. To try."

Long Yin's coils tightened. His scales caught the light and threw it back in patterns of judgment. "Then let the Trials begin. But know this, little brother. If you hold the decree and you do not share it, you are not merely disobedient. You are treasonous. And treason carries a single punishment."

The arena grew cold.

"Death," Long Yin said.

Jie felt the thing pulse behind his heart again. Felt it push. Felt it burn.

And for the first time, he understood that his father had not given him a gift.

He had given him a weapon.

"You have three days," Long Yin continued. "Three days before the first trial. Use them to prepare. Or use them to say goodbye to your mortal attachments. It makes no difference to me."

He uncoiled and rose into the sky, his jade body catching the light as he ascended. The other brothers followed. Long Feng with a trail of fire. Long Shan with a rumble of thunder. Long Xu dissolving into shadow. Long Yi and Long Bo drifting after like afterthoughts.

Jie stood alone in the arena.

The mortal world spun below him. The sky stretched above him. And somewhere behind his heart, his father's final secret burned like a coal waiting to catch flame.

He did not know what the decree said.

He did not know why the Emperor had given it to him instead of to Long Yin.

But he knew one thing with absolute certainty.

His brothers would kill him for it.

And they would be right to try.

---

Far below, in the mortal village where Jie had been born, a woman with silver hair and eyes like winter frost looked up at the sky. She saw the dragons departing. Saw the trail of fire and shadow and storm.

She had been waiting for this day for sixteen years.

"Finally," she whispered, and her voice carried the weight of oaths older than the Celestial Empire. "The Emperor is dead. The sons are scattered. And the key walks free."

She reached into her robe and pulled out a scale. Black. Cracked. Thrumming with power that should not exist in mortal hands.

"You owe me a debt, Dragon King," she said to the empty air. "And I have come to collect."

The scale began to glow.

The sky began to darken.

And somewhere in the Celestial Arena, Jie felt a shift in the threads of fate. A pull. A warning.

He did not know what was coming.

But he knew, with the certainty of a boy who had learned to survive through silence, that the Trials were not the only danger he faced.

Someone else was playing the game now.

And they had been playing it far longer than he had.
