Chapter 20: The Witness's Verdict

~1.8k words · 2026-06-22

The courtroom held its breath. Elara felt every eye upon her, measuring, judging, weighing her worth. The hero's smile hadn't wavered, and she understood why—he'd just played the one card she couldn't counter.

*If I refuse the Divine Witness, I look guilty. If I accept, I'm exposed.*

Either way, she lost.

"The accused may respond," the head judge said, his voice carrying no hint of favor.

Elara's mind raced through legal precedent, both from her world and the fragmented memories of the Demon Empress she'd inherited. There had to be a loophole. There was always a loophole.

"I have a counter-request," she said slowly.

The hero's smile faltered.

"The Divine Witness compels truth," Elara continued, buying time to think. "But truth is subjective. What I believe to be true may differ from what another believes. The Witness can only compel *my* truth, not objective reality."

"That's..." the hero started.

"That's correct," the Arbiter's voice interrupted. "The Divine Witness reveals the truth as the speaker knows it. It does not reveal what *is*, only what *was perceived*."

*Thank you, metaphysical legal system.*

"However," Elara pressed on, "if the prosecutor wishes to invoke the Witness, I request equal access. I want to call witnesses of my own, and I want them bound by the same compulsion."

The judges conferred in whispers that sounded like thunder.

"Granted," the head judge said. "But know this—the Witness will remain for the duration of the trial. You cannot escape its gaze."

The hero sat down, his expression carefully neutral. But Elara saw his hands grip the table edge until his knuckles went white.

*Good. Let him worry.*

The courtroom rearranged itself. The obsidian floor rippled, and from its depths rose a pedestal of crystal. Within it swirled something that looked like liquid starlight, pulsing with a rhythm that matched Elara's heartbeat.

"The Divine Witness is present," the Arbiter announced. "Let the trial begin."

The head judge raised a hand. "First charge: The accused did knowingly and maliciously poison the water source of the Azure Lotus Sect, causing the deaths of three hundred and forty-seven cultivators."

*That's... new.*

Elara had read the Demon Empress's memories. She knew about the massacres, the betrayals, the cruelty. But this particular charge wasn't in the record. Either the prosecution had found something she didn't know about, or—

"Presenting Exhibit A," the hero said, producing a jade vial. "This poison was found in the Demon Empress's private chambers. It matches the toxin used in the attack."

A servant brought the vial to the judges. One of them uncorked it, sniffed, and recoiled.

"This is Soul-Breaking Venom," the judge said. "A poison so potent it can kill a cultivator's spirit before their body dies."

"And we have a witness," the hero continued, "who saw the Demon Empress purchasing this exact poison three days before the attack."

The doors at the back of the courtroom swung open. A merchant shuffled in, his face pale with terror. He was a mortal—Elara could tell by the way his energy flickered like a candle in wind.

"State your name and occupation," the hero said.

"Zhang Wei. I... I run an apothecary in the Shadow Market."

"And do you recognize the woman sitting at the defense table?"

Zhang Wei's eyes met Elara's. He trembled. "Y-yes. That's her. The Demon Empress."

"Did she purchase Soul-Breaking Venom from you?"

*Here it comes.*

Under the Divine Witness, Zhang Wei couldn't lie. But Elara had never visited his shop. She knew this from the Demon Empress's memories. The real Demon Empress had been arrogant, cruel, and murderous—but she'd never used poison. She considered it beneath her.

"Zhang Wei," the head judge said, "you are bound by the Witness. Speak only the truth."

The merchant's face contorted. Sweat beaded on his forehead. "I... I..."

The crystal pedestal pulsed. Light seared through the courtroom.

"I don't remember!" Zhang Wei screamed. "I don't remember who bought it! I've been trying to remember for months, but every time I get close, the memory disappears!"

*What?*

The hero shot to his feet. "That's impossible. The Witness compels truth, not memory."

"Unless someone has tampered with his memories," the Arbiter said quietly.

The courtroom went silent.

Elara's mind raced. Memory tampering was a high-level technique. Only cultivators at the peak of their power could manage it, and even then, it was risky. If done wrong, it could destroy the target's mind entirely.

"Who would do such a thing?" the head judge demanded.

No one answered.

But Elara noticed something. The hero's expression had shifted. Not to surprise or anger—to fear.

*He knows something.*

"Your Honor," Elara said, rising, "I request permission to question the witness."

"Denied. You are not a licensed—"

"I'm not questioning him about the poison." Elara's voice cut through the judge's objection. "I'm questioning him about his memory loss. If someone is powerful enough to tamper with a witness's mind in the presence of the Divine Witness, that person might be in this courtroom."

The judges exchanged glances.

"Permission granted. But be careful, accused. The Witness will hold you to your words."

Elara approached Zhang Wei. He flinched as she drew near, but she kept her movements slow, non-threatening.

"Mr. Zhang," she said gently, "I'm not going to hurt you. I just want to understand. When did you first realize your memories were missing?"

"A month ago. Maybe two." He shook his head. "Time is fuzzy. I woke up one morning, and I knew something was wrong. Like I'd forgotten to do something important."

"Did you seek help? A healer, a spiritual doctor?"

"No." He laughed bitterly. "Who would help a mortal? We're disposable. Everyone knows that."

*That's the problem,* Elara thought. *In this world, mortals are worthless.*

"Think carefully," she said. "Before the memory loss, did anything unusual happen? Anyone visit you who seemed out of place?"

Zhang Wei's brow furrowed. "I... there was someone. A man. He came to my shop, but he didn't want to buy anything. He just... looked at me. For a long time."

"Can you describe him?"

"I can't remember his face." Zhang Wei's voice cracked. "I can't remember anything about him except that he was tall. And he wore a cloak."

"A cloak? What color?"

"Black. No. Purple." He clutched his head. "I don't know! Every time I try to remember, it's like trying to hold smoke!"

The crystal pedestal pulsed again, and Elara felt a pressure against her mind. The Divine Witness was testing her, making sure she wasn't lying about her intentions.

*I'm not the one who needs to worry.*

"Thank you, Mr. Zhang." Elara turned to the judges. "Your Honors, I submit that the witness's testimony is compromised. The prosecution's evidence is tainted by a third party's interference. I move to dismiss the first charge."

"Overruled," the head judge said. "The witness's memory issues do not prove the accused's innocence. They merely cast doubt on the evidence."

*That's exactly what I wanted.*

"The prosecution may proceed," the judge continued.

The hero's jaw tightened. He'd lost momentum, and he knew it.

"Second charge," he said, his voice harder now. "The accused did conspire with the Shadow Flame Sect to assassinate the Celestial Sword Sect's Grand Elder."

This time, he didn't bother with witnesses. He produced a letter, sealed with the Demon Empress's personal crest.

"A written confession," the hero said. "Found in the Grand Elder's chambers after the assassination. It details the plan, the payment, and the method of execution."

*That's a forgery.*

Elara knew it instantly. The real Demon Empress had never written such a letter. She'd considered the Shadow Flame Sect beneath her notice.

But proving it was another matter.

"I request permission to examine the letter," Elara said.

The hero hesitated. "Your Honor—"

"Granted."

A servant brought the letter to Elara. She studied it carefully. The paper was old, the ink authentic. The handwriting matched the Demon Empress's—she'd seen enough of it in the palace archives to recognize the style.

But something was wrong.

The date.

The letter was dated three months ago. But the Demon Empress had been imprisoned for four months. She couldn't have written it while locked in a suppression cell.

"Your Honors," Elara said, "this letter is dated three months prior. But the Demon Empress was incarcerated four months ago. She could not have—"

"The letter could have been written before her imprisonment," the hero interrupted.

"Then why does it reference events that happened after?" Elara held up the letter. "It mentions the Grand Elder's death. But the Grand Elder was killed *six weeks* ago. The timeline doesn't match."

The hero's face went pale.

The judges examined the letter again. This time, they noticed what Elara had seen.

"The accused is correct," one judge said slowly. "The letter claims to detail a conspiracy that resulted in a death that had not yet occurred."

"That's impossible," the hero said.

"Is it?" Elara turned to face him. "Or is your evidence fabricated?"

*I have him.*

But the hero recovered quickly. "The letter is a prediction. The Shadow Flame Sect planned the assassination in advance. They wrote the letter before the Grand Elder died, expecting to claim credit afterward."

"That's not how assassinations work," Elara said. "You don't write confession letters before the crime is committed. That would be—"

"Madness?" the hero finished. "Exactly. We're dealing with demons, not rational beings."

The courtroom stirred. Elara could feel the tide turning against her.

"Your Honors," she said, trying a different approach, "I request the Divine Witness be applied to the letter itself."

"A document cannot be compelled to speak truth," the head judge said.

"Not to speak. To reveal. The Witness can show us who touched the letter, who wrote it, who handled it. If the evidence is forged, the Witness will expose the forgery."

The hero's composure cracked. "That's unprecedented."

"So was summoning the Divine Witness to a routine trial," Elara shot back.

The judges conferred again. The crystal pedestal pulsed, and for a moment, Elara felt something cold brush against her mind.

*The Witness is curious about me.*

"Very well," the head judge said. "The request is granted. But know this—if the letter is genuine, the accused will face immediate judgment. There will be no further appeals."

Elara nodded. It was a risk. But she was running out of options.

The servant took the letter and placed it on the crystal pedestal. The liquid starlight swirled, reaching out tendrils that touched the paper.

Then the Witness screamed.

It wasn't a sound. It was a psychic shattering that sent everyone in the courtroom to their knees. Elara felt her mind split open, her secrets laid bare—
