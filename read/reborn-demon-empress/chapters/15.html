📖 Reborn as the Demon Empress by Luna Nightshade

Chapter 15: The Witness of Truth

~1.9k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The courtroom held its breath. Every eye turned to Elara as the weight of the hero's request settled over the assembly like a funeral shroud.

*Think. Think faster than you've ever thought before.*

"The Divine Witness of Truth," Elara repeated, buying time. She'd read about such artifacts in her briefings on cultivation law, but they were supposed to be myths. Legends. Stories told to scare junior cultivators into behaving.

"Only used in the most extreme cases," the head judge said, stroking his long white beard. "When the accused has demonstrated exceptional deception or when the stakes warrant absolute certainty."

"Which is this case," the hero added smoothly. "The Demon Empress has already escaped justice once. She's manipulated the system, twisted words, and used her cunning to avoid accountability. The Divine Witness ensures we reach the truth."

*He's good,* Elara admitted. *He's very good.*

She studied him across the courtroom. In their previous encounters, she'd seen him as a caricature—the righteous hero, the chosen one, the inevitable end to her stolen life. But now, in this arena of legal combat, she saw something else. Intelligence. Strategy. And a personal investment that went beyond simple justice.

"What's your name?" Elara asked. "I can't keep calling you 'the hero' in my head forever."

A ripple of murmurs spread through the gallery. The hero's eyes narrowed.

"Chen Wei," he said. "Though I suspect you already knew that."

"I didn't. The Demon Empress's memories are... fragmented." *Technically true.* "But now I do. Chen Wei. Descendant of the Celestial Sword Sect's founding master. The prophesied one who would end the Demon Empress's reign."

"You know my story."

"I read the files. I always read the files."

The head judge cleared his throat, the sound echoing like thunder. "We are not here for introductions. We are here to determine the fate of the soul known as Elara Chen, currently inhabiting the body of the Demon Empress. The request for the Divine Witness has been made. Do you consent, accused?"

*If I say no, they'll assume I'm guilty. If I say yes, they'll discover I'm a fraud. Either way, I lose.*

Unless.

"I have a counter-proposal," Elara said.

The head judge's eyebrow rose. "You are in no position to negotiate."

"Article 47 of the Celestial Arbitration Code. 'The accused may petition for alternative truth-verification methods if they can demonstrate that the requested method would cause undue harm or violate fundamental rights.'"

Chen Wei laughed. "You're citing mortal law in a celestial court?"

"I'm citing the law that governs this court," Elara shot back. "The same law that allowed me to request a jury. The same law that governs your precious Article 12, section B. Or did you think I only read the parts that benefited me?"

The Arbiter's voice floated down from above, tinged with what might have been amusement. "She's correct. Article 47 is valid precedent."

The three judges conferred in whispers, their voices too low to hear. Elara forced herself to breathe evenly, to maintain the appearance of calm confidence. Inside, her heart hammered against her ribs like a caged bird.

"The court recognizes your petition," the head judge finally said. "What alternative do you propose?"

"Limited disclosure," Elara said. "I will answer any question directly related to the crimes I'm accused of. I will submit to magical verification on those specific points. But the Divine Witness exposes *everything*—every thought, every memory, every secret. That's not justice. That's a fishing expedition."

"Your past is relevant to establishing your character," the head judge countered.

"Is it? I'm not being tried for who I *was*. I'm being tried for what I *did*. The Demon Empress's crimes belong to the Demon Empress. If you want to judge me for those, fine. But don't pretend you need to know my childhood traumas or my private thoughts to determine whether I killed those villagers."

*Careful,* she warned herself. *Don't sound too defensive. Sound reasonable. Sound like someone with nothing to hide who simply values privacy.*

Chen Wei's eyes never left her face. He was studying her the way she'd once studied opposing counsel—looking for tells, for weaknesses, for the cracks in the armor.

"You're afraid," he said softly. "What are you hiding?"

"Everyone has secrets, Prosecutor Chen. Even you."

"I have nothing to fear from the Divine Witness."

"Then you won't mind if I ask you a few questions first. Under the same truth magic you want to subject me to."

The courtroom erupted again. The head judge slammed his gavel—a crystalline rod that shattered the air with a sound like breaking glass.

"Order! ORDER!"

Silence fell.

"You cannot turn the proceedings against the prosecutor," the head judge said.

"I'm not. I'm demonstrating my point. If the Divine Witness is so fair, so impartial, then surely everyone in this room should submit to it. The judges. The prosecutor. The witnesses. The gallery. We'll all bare our souls together."

"That's absurd."

"Is it? Or is it only absurd when the powerful are asked to surrender their privacy?"

The head judge's face darkened. But before he could respond, the Arbiter spoke again.

"She raises a valid philosophical point. The Divine Witness is not typically applied to the accusers."

"Because the accusers are not on trial," Chen Wei said.

"Neither am I," Elara replied. "I'm the accused. Innocent until proven guilty, remember? Or does that principle not apply in celestial courts?"

A long, tense silence.

Then the Arbiter said, "The court will recess for deliberation. We will reconvene at the next celestial hour."

The world dissolved.

---

Elara found herself in a small, spartan room. White walls. A single table. Two chairs. No windows.

She was alone.

She sat down heavily, her legs finally giving out. Her hands were shaking. She gripped the edge of the table until her knuckles turned white.

*That was too close.*

She'd bought herself time, but not much. The judges would deliberate, but ultimately, they would probably side with Chen Wei. The Celestial Sword Sect had centuries of influence. She had a law degree and a borrowed body.

*I need allies.*

But who? Everyone in the cultivation world wanted the Demon Empress dead. Even if they believed Elara was a different soul inhabiting the body, that didn't change the fact that she looked like the woman who had terrorized the realm for decades.

*Unless I can prove I'm not her.*

The thought crystallized in her mind. If she could prove—irrefutably, magically—that she was a separate soul, a separate person, then the crimes of the Demon Empress wouldn't apply to her. She'd be innocent by default.

But how?

Soul identification was rare magic. The Divine Witness could do it, but she'd just argued against using the Divine Witness. She couldn't reverse course without looking suspicious.

*Then find another way.*

She closed her eyes and reached into the Demon Empress's memories. They were still fragmented, like shards of a broken mirror, but some pieces were clearer than others. She searched for any mention of soul magic, of identity verification, of loopholes in celestial law.

And there it was. Buried deep, in a memory that wasn't even hers.

*The Hall of Ancestors.*

The Celestial Sword Sect had a hall dedicated to their founder. Within it was a relic—the Mirror of True Souls. It could reveal a person's original form, their true essence, beyond any physical disguise or possession.

If she could get to that mirror, she could prove she wasn't the Demon Empress.

But the mirror was in the heart of enemy territory. Surrounded by the most powerful cultivators in the realm. Guarded by formations and traps designed to kill even immortal beings.

*Great. No problem at all.*

The door opened.

Chen Wei stepped inside.

Elara's body tensed, but she forced herself to remain seated. "Prosecutor Chen. Come to gloat?"

"Come to talk." He closed the door behind him and sat across from her. "Off the record."

"There's no such thing in celestial court."

"Humor me."

She studied his face. He looked tired, she realized. There were shadows under his eyes, lines of strain around his mouth. The weight of prophecy, perhaps. Or the weight of hunting a monster that might not be a monster anymore.

"Why are you really here?" she asked.

"Because I'm confused."

"About?"

"About you." He leaned forward. "I've studied the Demon Empress for years. Her tactics, her patterns, her psychology. She was cruel, but she wasn't clever. Not like this. She ruled through fear and violence, not through legal maneuvering. The woman I just debated in that courtroom... she was something else entirely."

"You think I'm not the Demon Empress."

"I think the soul inhabiting that body might not be the same one that committed those crimes."

Elara's heart stopped. "You believe me?"

"I believe *evidence*. If you can prove it—prove that you're a different soul—then the charges against you are invalid. The Demon Empress is dead. You're just... someone wearing her face."

"That's remarkably reasonable for someone who's been hunting me."

"I'm not unreasonable. I'm thorough." He stood. "I'll make you a deal. If you can produce proof of your identity—verifiable, magical proof—I'll drop the charges. I'll even advocate for your release."

"And if I can't?"

"Then the Divine Witness will be used, and the truth will come out one way or another."

He walked to the door, then paused.

"One more thing. The Demon Empress killed my sister. She tortured her for three days before finally ending her life. I've spent ten years hunting her, dreaming of this moment." His voice dropped. "But I'm also a man of justice. If you're not her, then killing you would be murder. And I've killed enough people in the name of vengeance."

He left.

Elara sat in silence, her mind racing.

*The Hall of Ancestors. The Mirror of True Souls. I need to get there.*

But how? She was in celestial custody. She had no cultivation, no allies, no resources. Escaping would be impossible.

Unless...

She looked at the wall. The white, featureless wall of her holding cell.

*Unless I don't escape. Unless I make them take me there.*

A plan began to form. Risky. Dangerous. Possibly suicidal.

But it was the only plan she had.

---

The door opened again. The Arbiter stood there, her form flickering like a candle flame.

"The judges have reached a decision," she said. "They've compromised."

"What's the compromise?"

"The Divine Witness will be used—but only on the question of your identity. It will ask one question, and one question only: 'Are you the soul that originally inhabited the body of the Demon Empress?'"

Elara's throat went dry. "And if I refuse?"

"Then you're assumed guilty, and the penalty is immediate annihilation."

*No pressure.*

She took a deep breath. She thought about the Mirror of True Souls, about Chen Wei's offer, about the slim chance she had to escape this nightmare.

"Fine," she said. "I accept."

The Arbiter's form flickered with what might have been surprise. "You do?"

"If I'm going to be judged, let it be by truth. But I have one condition."

"Name it."

"The questioning must take place in the Hall of Ancestors of the Celestial Sword Sect."

The Arbiter's form went still. "Why?"
