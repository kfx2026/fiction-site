📖 Reborn as the Demon Empress by Luna Nightshade

Chapter 1: The Law of Blade and Breath

~1.8k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The cold steel against Elara’s throat was the first thing she registered. The second was the smell—iron, sweat, and something floral rotting under a noonday sun. She blinked against the glare, her vision swimming with fragments of memory that weren’t hers.

*Screaming crowds. A throne of bones. A man in white who would not stop smiling.*

Then the blade pressed deeper, and the present crashed through her like a tidal wave.

“Any last words, Demon Empress?” The executioner’s voice was gravel and contempt. He was a mountain of a man, his bare arms wrapped in leather straps, his face hidden behind a bronze mask shaped like a snarling beast. “Or shall I send you to the Nine Hells with your tongue still attached?”

Elara’s mind raced. She had been a senior partner at Chen & Associates by twenty-nine. She had negotiated hostile takeovers, defused corporate espionage cases, and once convinced a jury to award her client seven figures for a slipped-on-wet-floor lawsuit. But she had never—*never*—woken up on an execution platform.

Her hands were bound behind her back with silk cords that glowed faintly, humming with some kind of energy. A talisman, maybe. Her head was pounding. Her robes—black silk embroidered with silver phoenixes—were torn at the shoulder, and there was blood on her sleeve that she was fairly certain was not her own.

*Okay. Assess. You’re about to die. You have no cultivation, no allies, no idea where you are. Use what you have.*

The crowd below the platform was a sea of faces—some jeering, some weeping, some watching with the cold calculation of predators. Beyond them, mountains rose like jagged teeth against a bruised purple sky. Cultivation world. Definitely not Earth.

“I said,” the executioner growled, “any last words?”

Elara took a breath. She had exactly one shot.

“Under what authority?” she asked, her voice steady despite the tremor in her chest.

The executioner paused. The blade eased back a fraction of an inch. “What?”

“You heard me.” Elara lifted her chin, ignoring the sting as the edge nicked her skin. “This is an execution. By definition, it requires a legal basis. A verdict. A sentence passed by a legitimate authority.” She tilted her head, meeting his eyes through the mask’s slits. “So I ask again: under what authority are you about to kill me?”

Silence rippled through the crowd. She could feel the weight of thousands of eyes on her, confused, hostile, curious.

“You were condemned by the Celestial Tribunal,” the executioner said, but his voice had lost its certainty. “For crimes against the Heavenly Dao. For corrupting the sacred laws of cultivation. For—for being the Demon Empress.”

“Ah.” Elara nodded as if this clarified everything. “And the Celestial Tribunal—that’s the body that adjudicates matters of demonic cultivation in the Upper Realms? Comprised of seven judges, appointed by the Jade Emperor himself, with jurisdiction over all mortal and immortal realms?”

The executioner blinked. “...Yes.”

“Then you’ll have no problem showing me the writ of execution.”

A murmur ran through the crowd. Someone in the front row—a young man in blue robes, his hand resting on the hilt of a jade sword—leaned forward with sudden interest.

“The writ,” Elara repeated. “It’s standard procedure. A document signed and sealed by at least three Tribunal members, specifying the charges, the evidence, and the sentence. Without it, this execution is unlawful.” She smiled, though it did not reach her eyes. “Surely the Celestial Tribunal follows its own laws?”

The executioner turned, looking toward a pavilion draped in white silk at the edge of the square. Inside, figures sat in shadow—cultivators, judging by the faint aura of power that radiated from them. One of them rose.

He was beautiful in the way a blade is beautiful. White robes, white hair, a face carved from marble and cold intent. The man who had smiled in her borrowed memories.

“The Demon Empress,” he said, his voice carrying easily across the square, “does not get to cite procedure. She slaughtered an entire sect. She defiled the sacred springs of Mount Tai. She—"

“Allegedly,” Elara interrupted.

The white-robed man’s eyes narrowed. “Excuse me?”

“Allegedly,” she repeated. “You’re making accusations. That’s fine. But accusations are not proof. And I don’t see any proof. I don’t see a writ. I don’t see a trial transcript.” She paused. “In fact, I don’t see any evidence that this isn’t just a very elaborate assassination dressed up as justice.”

The crowd gasped. The white-robed man’s face went very still.

“You dare—"

“I dare to ask for due process.” Elara’s voice rang out, clear and sharp. “You call yourself the Celestial Tribunal. You claim to uphold the Heavenly Dao. But the Heavenly Dao is balance. Order. Law.” She looked at the crowd, at the young cultivators, the merchant families, the beggars at the edges. “If you can execute a sect leader without evidence, without a proper trial, without even a *document*—then what stops them from doing the same to you?”

She saw it happen. The shift. The doubt that flickered in a dozen pairs of eyes.

The white-robed man stepped forward, his aura flaring like a storm. “You twist words like a serpent. You are the Demon Empress. You have no right—"

“I have every right.” Elara met his gaze. “I am a defendant. And every defendant, from the lowest mortal to the highest immortal, has the right to face their accuser, to examine the evidence, and to receive a fair judgment.” She smiled again, thin and sharp. “That’s not demonic law. That’s *law* law. Even the heavens have rules.”

The executioner had lowered his blade entirely now, looking between Elara and the white-robed man with obvious confusion. Behind the pavilion’s silk curtains, the other judges were whispering.

“This is ridiculous,” the white-robed man snapped. “She’s stalling. Execute her now, before—"

“Before what?” A new voice. Old. Dry as autumn leaves.

The crowd parted to reveal an ancient woman leaning on a gnarled staff. Her robes were plain gray, her hair a wispy white cloud, and her eyes—her eyes were the color of ancient jade, sharp and knowing.

“Elder Yun,” the white-robed man said, and his voice carried a note of respect he had not shown Elara. “This is a Tribunal matter. I must ask you to—"

“You must ask me nothing, boy.” The old woman hobbled forward, her staff tapping against the stone. “I have sat on the Tribunal for three thousand years. I have presided over the trials of demon lords, rogue cultivators, and at least one screaming celestial chicken. And I have never—*never*—seen an execution proceed without a writ.”

She stopped at the base of the platform, looking up at Elara with undisguised curiosity.

“The Demon Empress was always a creature of rage and fire,” Elder Yun said. “You, girl, are something different. You speak like a bureaucrat.”

“I’ve been called worse,” Elara said.

Elder Yun chuckled, a dry rasping sound. “I’ll bet you have.” She turned to face the pavilion. “The execution is postponed until the Tribunal can produce proper documentation. In the meantime, the prisoner will be remanded to the Jade Prison.”

“That’s a mistake,” the white-robed man said, his voice soft and dangerous. “She’ll escape. She’s the Demon Empress.”

“She’s a mortal with bound hands and zero cultivation,” Elder Yun replied. “If she escapes, we have bigger problems than a missing writ.” She tapped her staff once, and the ground shuddered. “Take her away.”

The executioner hesitated, then sheathed his blade and grabbed Elara’s arm. She did not resist. She was too busy cataloging every detail—the layout of the square, the location of the exits, the faces of the Tribunal members, the position of the guards, the exact weight of the silk cords binding her wrists.

*Jade Prison. That’s my next stop. I need a plan before I get there.*

As they led her down from the platform, she caught the white-robed man’s gaze one last time. He was staring at her with an intensity that bordered on hatred.

She smiled at him. “See you in court.”

His hand twitched toward his sword, but Elder Yun’s presence held him back. For now.

The guards marched her through the city—if it could be called a city. The buildings were carved from living stone, intertwined with roots and vines that pulsed with a soft blue light. Cultivators floated on platforms of cloud, merchants haggled over glowing pills and spirit stones, and everywhere, *everywhere*, there were eyes on her.

The Demon Empress. The villainess. The monster.

Elara kept her head high and her mouth shut. She needed information. She needed time. She needed to figure out how the hell she was going to survive a world that wanted her dead.

The Jade Prison was a fortress of black stone and green light, built into the side of a mountain. The cells were not cages but formations—circles of carved runes that hummed with power, trapping prisoners in bubbles of distorted space. They shoved her into one, and the world went silent.

No sound. No smell. Just her, the runes, and the slow drip of water somewhere far away.

She sat down cross-legged on the cold stone floor.

*Okay. Think.*

She had no cultivation. No power. No allies. The body she was in was weak, starved, barely able to lift a sword. The memories she had inherited were fragmented and unreliable. And the man in white—she caught his name from the guards’ whispers: *Jian Chen*, the Sword Saint of the Celestial Tribunal, the hero who was supposed to kill her.

According to the novel she had apparently been reborn into, the Demon Empress was supposed to die in the first chapter. Executed. End of story.

But Elara had not spent fifteen years in corporate law to accept a narrative someone else had written for her.

She flexed her wrists. The silk cords were still there, glowing faintly. They were spirit bindings—they would drain any attempt to use qi. But she wasn’t a cultivator. She didn’t have qi.

She had something better.

She had a mind that had out-argued opposing counsel, out-negotiated hostile boards, and once convinced a judge to dismiss a case because the plaintiff had failed to use the correct shade of blue on their filing cover sheet.

The Celestial Tribunal ran on rules. Tradition. Precedent.

And Elara was very, very good at finding loopholes.

She closed her eyes, sorting through the fragments of memory. The Demon Empress’s past. The politics of the cultivation world. The sects, the alliances, the grudges. Somewhere in there was a weakness she could exploit. A crack she could widen into a door.

She was still searching when the wall of her cell shimmered, and a figure stepped through.

The woman was young, barely out of her teens, with sharp features and hair braided with silver bells. She wore the white robes of a Tribunal initiate, but her eyes were too hard, too knowing.
