📖 The Fox Wife's Bargain by Lan Hua

Chapter 4: The Blood on His Hands

~1.7k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The rain came three days later.

Chen Yuan sat at his desk, brush suspended over blank paper. The characters wouldn't come. They scattered like startled birds every time he tried to form them. Outside, water drummed against the temple tiles in a rhythm that matched his heartbeat.

Too fast. Too loud.

Mei had been gone since dawn.

She left every morning now, slipping out before the first light touched the mountain peaks. She never said where she went. He never asked. Some questions felt like knives—once you picked them up, you couldn't put them down without drawing blood.

The brush snapped in his grip.

He stared at the broken pieces in his palm. The ink had stained his fingers black, seeping into the lines of his palm like old rivers carved into a map. He'd had that brush since he was twelve. His mother had given it to him, along with her last words.

*Study hard, Yuan. Become someone worthy of this family's name.*

He hadn't. He was a failed scholar living in a crumbling temple, sharing his bed with a fox spirit while celestial hunters combed the mountains for her.

His mother would have wept.

The door slid open.

Mei stood in the frame, her robes soaked through, her dark hair plastered to her face. Rain dripped from her chin. She looked like a drowned ghost, beautiful and terrible in the gray light.

"You're still awake," she said.

"I haven't slept."

She stepped inside, leaving puddles on the floorboards. The fire in the brazier hissed as she passed, as if even the flames recoiled from her cold.

"Good." She knelt across from him, her movements slow, deliberate. "We need to talk."

Chen Yuan set down the broken brush. "About what?"

"The hunters. They're closer now. I can feel them."

A cold knot tightened in his chest. "How close?"

"Three days. Maybe two." She met his eyes, and for the first time since she'd appeared at his door, he saw something other than mischief or hunger in her gaze. Fear. "They have a tracker. A celestial hound. It's following my scent."

"Then leave."

The words came out before he could stop them. He didn't know why he'd said them. He didn't want her to leave. He wanted to grab her wrists, press her hands to his chest, beg her to stay until the hunters came and burned the temple down around them.

But he'd said it. And now it hung in the air between them, sharp and final.

Mei tilted her head, studying him like she was reading a book written in a language only she understood. "You want me to go?"

"No."

"Then why—"

"Because I can't protect you."

She laughed. It wasn't a kind sound. "Protect me? Scholar, I've lived three hundred years. I've outrun exorcists, outwitted heavenly officials, and bedded a dragon prince who still sends me letters begging for another night. You think I need your protection?"

"Then why are you here?"

The question cracked open the space between them. For a long moment, she said nothing. The rain filled the silence, drowning the world in gray.

"You know what they'll do if they catch me?" she finally asked.

"I've read the stories."

"No." Her voice dropped, soft and raw. "You've read children's tales. You've read scratched warnings meant to frighten farmers and scholars. You don't know what they'll do."

She pulled back her sleeve.

Chen Yuan's breath caught.

Her arm was covered in scars. Not the pale lines of old wounds, but fresh burns, black and cracked, weeping a faint golden fluid that steamed in the cold air. The marks spiraled up her wrist, disappearing beneath her robe.

"What—"

"Celestial chains." She let the sleeve fall. "They burn when the hunter gets close. They tighten. Eventually, they'll sink into my bones and hold me still while they carve out my cores."

"Cores?"

"My cultivation. My power. My lives." She smiled, but there was no warmth in it. "A nine-tailed fox has nine chances at immortality. They take them one by one. Each one pulled out is more painful than the last. By the seventh, you're begging for death. By the eighth, you forget you ever had a name."

The fire crackled. The rain kept falling.

Chen Yuan stared at the spot where her scars had been, at the fabric that now hid them again. He wanted to reach out, touch her wrist, feel her pulse beneath his fingers. He wanted to promise her safety, even though he knew promises meant nothing to creatures like her.

"What does your wish have to do with this?" she asked.

"What?"

"Your wish. The bargain. You said you had one. You haven't told me what it is."

He looked away.

"Chen Yuan." She leaned forward, her voice dropping to a whisper. "What did you wish for?"

The words wouldn't come. They were stuck somewhere deep in his chest, tangled around his ribs like roots around buried bones. He couldn't say them. If he said them, they would become real. And if they became real, she would leave.

Or worse.

She would stay.

"Not yet," he said. "The year isn't up."

"You're afraid."

"Of course I'm afraid."

"No." She reached across the table and pressed her palm against his chest. Her hand was cold, but the warmth of her touch seeped through his robes, sinking into his skin like sunlight through clouds. "Not of the hunters. Not of dying. You're afraid of what you're becoming."

He grabbed her wrist.

His grip was too tight. He knew it was too tight. But he couldn't let go. Her pulse beat against his fingers, fast and steady, a rhythm that belonged to something older than the mountains around them.

"I've always been this," he said. "I just didn't know it until you came."

Her eyes widened.

The fire guttered. The rain stopped.

And in the silence, something shifted inside Chen Yuan's chest. Something ancient. Something hungry. Something that had been sleeping beneath his ribs since the day he was born, waiting for the right moment to wake.

Mei pulled her hand back.

"What are you?" she whispered.

He opened his mouth to answer.

The temple door exploded inward.

Wood splintered. Wind howled through the gap, extinguishing the fire and plunging the room into darkness. Chen Yuan scrambled to his feet, his heart slamming against his ribs. Mei grabbed his arm, her grip iron-tight.

"Stay behind me," she said.

"No—"

"Scholar. For once in your life. Do what you're told."

A figure stepped through the shattered doorway. Tall. Wrapped in shadows that moved like living things. Two eyes burned in the darkness, gold and cold, fixed on Mei with the patience of a hunter who had been tracking his prey for a very long time.

"Mei of the Nine Tails." The voice was smooth, layered, like silk wrapped around steel. "You've led me on a fine chase."

The hunter stepped into what little light remained.

He was beautiful. That was the first thing Chen Yuan noticed. Beautiful in the way a blade is beautiful—sharp, perfect, meant to cut. His robes were white, unmarked by rain or mud. His hair fell in a silver cascade past his shoulders. And in his right hand, he held a sword made of light, its edge humming with a sound that made Chen Yuan's teeth ache.

"Who are you?" Chen Yuan demanded.

The hunter's golden eyes slid to him. For a moment, something flickered in their depths. Recognition. Surprise. And then—amusement.

"You don't know."

"Know what?"

The hunter laughed. It was a beautiful sound, like wind chimes in a storm.

"Scholar Chen Yuan," he said. "Son of Chen Wei, the Demon Vanquisher. Grandson of Chen Tian, the Star-Swallower. Heir to the bloodline that has hunted celestial spirits for a thousand years."

The world tilted.

Chen Yuan's legs gave out. He hit the floor hard, his knees cracking against the wood. Mei's hand was still on his arm, but he couldn't feel it. He couldn't feel anything except the words echoing in his skull, over and over, like a bell that refused to stop ringing.

*Heir to the bloodline that has hunted celestial spirits for a thousand years.*

"You're lying," he said.

The hunter's smile widened. "Am I?"

"Chen Yuan." Mei's voice came from far away. "Chen Yuan, look at me."

He looked.

Her face was pale. Her eyes were wide, searching his like she was trying to find the scholar she'd shared tea with, the man who stuttered when she smiled, the mortal who had offered her shelter without asking for anything in return.

"Did you know?" she asked.

"No. I swear—"

"Did you know?"

"No!"

The hunter stepped forward. The sword of light hummed louder, casting strange shadows across the walls. "He doesn't. How tragic. The last of the Chen bloodline, unaware of his own nature. Your family would be ashamed."

"Shut up," Chen Yuan snarled.

The hunter raised an eyebrow. "The cub has teeth."

"I said shut up."

He got to his feet. His legs were shaking. His hands were shaking. Everything inside him was shaking, except for that thing that had woken up when Mei touched him. That thing was still. That thing was waiting.

"Mei," he said. "Run."

"Chen Yuan—"

"Run. Now."

She grabbed his face, forcing him to meet her eyes. Her hands were warm against his cheeks. Her breath was sweet, like jasmine blooming in moonlight.

"I'm not leaving you," she said.

"You have to."

"I'm not leaving you."

The hunter sighed. "How touching. But I'm afraid I have a quota to fill."

He raised his sword.

Chen Yuan moved.

He didn't think. He didn't plan. His body simply acted, stepping in front of Mei, raising his arm to block the blow that should have cut him in half.

The sword stopped.

An inch from his throat.

The hunter's golden eyes widened.

"No," he breathed. "It can't be."

Chen Yuan looked down.

His arm was covered in light. Not the hunter's light. Something else. Something that came from inside him, pouring out of his skin in rivers of gold and silver, curling around his body like a second skin.

The scars on his palm—the ones he'd had since birth, the ones his mother said were birthmarks—were glowing.

"You," the hunter whispered, "are the Star-Swallower reborn."

Chen Yuan didn't have time to process the words.

Because behind him, Mei screamed.
