📖 The Fox Wife's Bargain by Lan Hua

Chapter 1: The Shadow at the Door

~1.9k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The mountain swallowed the last light of day.

Chen Yuan stood at the temple gate, his robes damp with sweat from the climb, and wondered if he'd made a terrible mistake. The wooden structure before him sagged like an old man's spine, its red paint flaking away in sheets. Weeds choked the stone path leading to the main hall. A broken prayer flag flapped in the evening wind, its tattered fabric snapping against the eaves.

The villagers in the town below had warned him.

"Don't go up there, young master. That temple's been empty for twenty years. Bad luck."

"Strange things happen on that mountain. Strange things."

But Chen Yuan had no choice. The inn cost three silver coins a night. The temple cost nothing. And his family had sent him here to study, not to spend their last savings on a bed he couldn't afford.

He pushed open the gate. The hinges screamed.

The main hall was intact, at least. A dusty Buddha sat cross-legged on an altar, his serene face cracked down the middle like a broken mirror. Chen Yuan bowed to it anyway—his mother had raised him to be polite to gods and spirits alike—then began setting up his meager belongings. A brush. An inkstone. A stack of books tied with string. A single blanket rolled tight.

He lit a candle. The flame was small and weak against the growing dark.

Night came fast in the mountains.

By the time he'd cleared a space to sleep, the world outside was black as ink. Wind howled through gaps in the walls. Somewhere in the rafters, something scratched and skittered. Mice, he told himself. Just mice.

He tried to read. The characters blurred before his eyes.

Days of travel had exhausted him. He set the book aside, blew out the candle, and lay down on the hard wooden floor with his blanket wrapped around him like a cocoon. The last thing he saw before sleep took him was the cracked face of the Buddha, watching him in the dark.

---

He woke to knocking.

Three sharp raps on the temple door.

Chen Yuan sat up so fast his head spun. The candle had burned down to nothing. The moon hung outside the window, pale and cold, casting long shadows across the floor.

Knock. Knock. Knock.

Three more. Insistent. Rhythmic.

"Who's there?" His voice came out rough with sleep.

No answer.

He stood, his legs stiff from the hard floor, and walked to the door. His hand paused on the wooden bolt. His mother's voice echoed in his head: *Never open a door in the middle of the night, Yuan'er. Bad things wait on the other side of doors at night.*

But the knocking came again. Softer this time. Almost pleading.

"Please," a woman's voice said. "I'm lost. I saw your light."

A lost traveler. In the middle of the night. On a mountain path no one had used in years.

Chen Yuan's hand moved before his mind caught up. The bolt slid back. The door swung open.

She stood in the moonlight.

Her robes were white, plain and unadorned, but they seemed to glow in the silver light. Her hair fell loose around her shoulders, black as a raven's wing. Her face was beautiful in a way that made his chest ache—high cheekbones, full lips, eyes so dark they seemed to swallow the night.

"I'm sorry to disturb you," she said. Her voice was soft. Musical. "I was traveling to the next town, but I lost the path. The woods... they play tricks on me."

Chen Yuan opened his mouth to speak. Then he looked down.

Her shadow fell across the threshold.

It had nine tails.

They moved like living things, swaying and curling, long and graceful and *wrong*. A fox's tails. A spirit's tails.

He looked up at her face. She was watching him with those dark, endless eyes. Waiting.

"I..." His throat closed. "I don't..."

"You see them." It wasn't a question.

He nodded.

She sighed. It was a tired sound, old and worn. "Most humans don't. Not until it's too late." She tilted her head, studying him. "But you do. That's interesting."

"What are you?" The words came out before he could stop them.

"I'm cold," she said instead of answering. "I'm tired. And I've been running for three days without rest." She looked past him into the dark temple. "Will you let me stay? Just for tonight. I'll be gone before dawn."

His mind screamed at him to say no. This was a fox spirit. A creature of trickery and deception. His grandmother had told him stories about fox spirits—how they lured men to their deaths, how they fed on human life force, how they were beautiful and deadly and never to be trusted.

But her shadow had nine tails. And she'd told him the truth when she could have lied.

"I have nothing to offer you," he said slowly. "No food. No tea. Only a hard floor and a drafty room."

A smile flickered at the corner of her lips. It was small and quick, but it transformed her face. "That's more than I've had in weeks."

She stepped inside.

Chen Yuan closed the door behind her and bolted it, though he didn't know why. It wouldn't stop her if she meant to hurt him. Nothing would.

She sat down on the floor across from his blanket, folding her legs beneath her with a fluid grace that was not quite human. Her shadow stretched across the wall behind her, nine tails swaying gently. Watching her shadow was easier than watching her face.

"You're a scholar," she said.

"Yes."

"Studying for the exams?"

"Yes."

"From the city?"

"No. A village to the east. Small place. You wouldn't know it."

"Try me."

He told her. She nodded, a slight recognition in her eyes. "I passed through there once. Many years ago. There was a river with white stones."

"It's still there."

"Good." She looked around the temple with something like approval. "This is a quiet place. The spirits here are old and sleepy. They won't bother us."

"Us?"

She looked at him. Really looked at him. Her eyes traveled from his face to his hands to the books stacked beside him, and he felt like she was reading him faster than he could read himself.

"I'm hiding," she said. "There are men hunting me. Celestial exorcists. They've killed all the others."

"Others?"

"The Nine-Tailed. My family." Her voice was flat. Empty. "I'm the last one left."

Chen Yuan's breath stopped. The Nine-Tailed Fox lineage was legend—ancient spirits of unmatched power, tricksters and guardians and bringers of fortune or ruin depending on the tale. His grandmother had told him stories about them. She'd said they were as old as the mountains themselves.

Now this woman was telling him they were all dead.

"I'm sorry," he said.

She blinked. "You're sorry?"

"I don't know what else to say." He rubbed the back of his neck. "I've never met someone whose family was... hunted before."

"You've never met a fox spirit before."

"No. That too."

She laughed. It was a small sound, barely more than a breath, but it was genuine. "You're strange, Scholar."

"Chen Yuan. My name is Chen Yuan."

"Chen Yuan." She tested the name on her tongue. "I am Mei."

"Mei." He repeated it, and the syllable felt warm in his mouth. "Just Mei?"

"Just Mei." Her shadow tails curled behind her. "For now."

They sat in silence for a long moment. The wind howled outside. Somewhere in the dark, an owl called. The candle had long since burned out, but the moonlight streaming through the window was enough to see by. Enough to see her.

"Why are you telling me this?" he asked. "I'm a stranger. A human. I could run to the village and tell everyone what I saw."

"Could you?"

He thought about it. The villagers would believe him, maybe. Or they'd think he was mad. Either way, they'd come up the mountain with torches and pitchforks, and she would be gone before they arrived. And if the exorcists were hunting her, they'd be watching for exactly that kind of disturbance.

"No," he admitted. "I don't think I could."

"Because you're kind." She said it simply, like it was an obvious fact. "I knew it the moment I saw you. You opened your door to a stranger in the middle of the night. That's not stupidity. That's kindness."

"Or foolishness."

"Sometimes they're the same thing." She leaned back on her hands, her tails flickering in the shadows. "I have a proposal for you, Chen Yuan."

His heart stuttered. He'd heard stories about fox spirit bargains. They never ended well.

"I'm listening."

"I need a place to hide. You need someone to keep you company while you study." She smiled, and this time it was sharper. More fox-like. "One year. I'll stay here with you. I'll cook for you, clean for you, keep the bad spirits away. And at the end of that year, you can ask me for one wish. Anything you want."

"Anything?"

"Within reason. I'm powerful, but I'm not a god." She tilted her head. "You could ask for riches. Fame. Success in your exams. A beautiful wife." Her eyes glittered. "I could give you all of that."

"And what do you get?"

"Safety." The word was soft. Vulnerable. "The exorcists won't think to look for me here. A mortal scholar's mountain temple. They'll be searching the cities, the forests, the places where spirits gather. They won't look for me in a dusty old temple with a cracked Buddha and a kind young man."

Chen Yuan stared at her. Her shadow tails swayed. Her eyes were dark and old and tired.

One year. One wish.

The stories said never to bargain with fox spirits. They'd twist your words, trick you, trap you with loopholes and technicalities. But Mei hadn't hidden what she was. She'd told him the truth when she could have lied.

And she looked so tired.

"What if I refuse?"

"Then I'll leave. Find somewhere else to hide." She shrugged. "It's your choice, Scholar."

He thought about his empty stomach and his thin blanket and the long, lonely months ahead. He thought about the exam that would determine his future, the crushing weight of his family's expectations, the fear that he wasn't smart enough or good enough or strong enough.

He thought about her shadow tails, beautiful and haunting, and the way she'd said his name.

"I have conditions."

She raised an eyebrow.

"No tricks. No lies. If you want my help, you deal with me honestly."

"Fair."

"And when the year is up, you leave. No extensions, no arguments."

"Of course."

"And..." He hesitated. "You tell me your real name. Before you go."

Her eyes widened. Just a fraction. Just for a moment. Then she smiled, and it was the most human expression he'd seen on her face all night.

"Agreed."

She held out her hand. Her fingers were pale and slender, her nails perfectly shaped.

He took it.

Her skin was warm. Warmer than it should have been. Warm like sunlight.

"I accept your bargain, Chen Yuan."

"I accept yours, Mei."

And somewhere in the depths of the mountain, something ancient and sleeping stirred.
