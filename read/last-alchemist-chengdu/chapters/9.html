Chapter 9: The Blood in the Mortar

~1.9k words · 2026-06-22

The chemical marker glowed violet under the microscope's light, branching through Grandma's liver tissue like frozen lightning. Liang Yue's finger trembled as she traced the pattern.

"What is this?" Her voice barely worked.

Professor Li adjusted the microscope's focus. "Mercury sulfide nanoparticles. Engineered to accumulate in specific organs over months. They call it the 'Slow Dissolve' in certain circles."

"Circles you know intimately."

The words came out sharper than she intended. Li Wei flinched, but didn't deny it. His silence was its own confession.

Liang Yue stepped back from the microscope, her pharmacy training fighting with the alchemist's intuition now burning in her chest. Mercury sulfide. Cinnabar. The most basic mineral in traditional alchemy—the Dragon's Blood that anchored spiritual energy to physical form. Someone had weaponized it.

"Grandma was making something for them," Liang Yue said slowly, the pieces clicking together like tumblers in a lock. "Something she refused to finish."

Professor Li's eyes widened. "How did you—"

"The furnace showed me. The last three days of her life, in fragments." She touched her dantian, where the brass furnace pulsed with residual heat. "She was refining a pill. A ninth-grade transformation pill. But she destroyed it before she could complete the final condensation."

"Destroyed it?" Li Wei's professional curiosity warred with his caution. "That would have required immense energy. Possibly fatal to—"

"To an old woman with mercury sulfide eating through her liver." Liang Yue's voice cracked. "Yes. She burned her remaining Qi to keep her recipes out of their hands."

The morgue's fluorescent lights hummed overhead. Outside, Chengdu's evening traffic bled through the frosted windows in streaks of red and white. Liang Yue felt the furnace pulse again, stronger this time, as if Grandma's ghost were reaching through the metal to guide her hand.

"Show me the rest," she said. "Everything you know about the Zhang family's operation."

Professor Li hesitated. Then he pulled a second folder from his briefcase, thicker than the first, held together with rubber bands that had long since lost their elasticity.

"I've been collecting this for seven years," he said, sliding it across the steel examination table. "Since your grandmother first came to me with her suspicions."

The folder landed with a heavy thud. Liang Yue opened it.

Inside were photographs. Dozens of them. Young men and women, mostly university students, their faces slack with confusion or terror. Some were chained to hospital beds. Others sat in meditation poses, wires trailing from their temples to machines that looked like a fusion of Buddhist prayer wheels and dialysis equipment.

"Test subjects," Professor Li said quietly. "Volunteers recruited from rural villages, promised money and education. Instead, they became vessels."

"Vessels for what?"

"Artificial Qi." He pointed to a photograph of a young woman whose eyes had gone completely white. "The Zhang family's researchers found a way to extract Qi from living cultivators—compressing years of spiritual refinement into injectable compounds. But the extraction process is... imprecise."

Liang Yue's stomach turned. "They're killing people."

"Worse. They're hollowing them out." He turned to another photo—a man in his thirties, his body intact but his face empty, like a doll. "The extraction takes the soul along with the Qi. These people are alive, but they're no longer present. The Zhang family calls them 'mortars'—empty vessels ready to be filled with artificial power."

The furnace in Liang Yue's dantian flared so hot she gasped. The metal seemed to scream inside her, a sound that was part rage, part grief.

"This ends tonight," she said.

"Liang Yue, you can't—"

"I can." She closed the folder, her movements precise and final. "I have the furnace. I have Grandma's recipes. And I have something they don't."

"What's that?"

"The ability to refine real Qi. Not synthetic. Not stolen." She met his eyes. "Grandma always said the Way could destroy as easily as it could heal. I just never understood what she meant."

Professor Li's face went pale. "You're talking about the Cloud Dissolving Pill. The one that disperses spiritual energy on contact."

"Among others."

"Liang Yue, that's forbidden alchemy. The ethics committee, the international cultivation treaty—"

"Grandma's dead, Professor." Her voice was flat, cold, the voice of someone who had passed through grief into something harder. "She died alone in her apartment, poisoned by people who wanted to turn alchemy into a factory. And you want to talk about ethics committees?"

The silence between them stretched like a wire about to snap.

Then the morgue door burst open.

A security guard stood in the doorway, his face flushed with panic. "Professor Li! There's a fire—the east wing—someone set the clinical research building on fire!"

Liang Yue was already moving.

---

The fire had consumed the top three floors by the time they reached the campus quad. Students and faculty stood in clusters, phones raised, faces lit by the orange glow. Sirens wailed in the distance.

But Liang Yue wasn't watching the fire.

She was watching the man standing at the edge of the crowd, perfectly still, untouched by the chaos around him. He was young—maybe thirty—with the kind of handsome face that belonged on a magazine cover. But his eyes were wrong. They were too calm. Too knowing.

And he was staring directly at her.

"Who is that?" she asked, grabbing Professor Li's arm.

Li Wei followed her gaze. His face drained of color. "That's Zhang Tianming. The Zhang family's eldest son. He runs their pharmaceutical division."

Zhang Tianming smiled. It was a predator's smile, all teeth and no warmth.

Then he raised his hand and beckoned. Once. Deliberate.

The furnace in Liang Yue's dantian lurched. Not in warning—in recognition. It knew this man. It had touched his Qi before.

"You said the Zhang family was funding artificial Qi research," Liang Yue said slowly. "You didn't say they had a genuine cultivator."

"I didn't know." Professor Li's voice was hoarse. "I swear, I didn't know."

Zhang Tianming started walking toward them. The crowd parted around him like water around a stone. No one seemed to notice him, or if they did, they chose not to see.

When he was ten feet away, he stopped.

"Liang Yue," he said. His voice was soft, musical, the voice of a man who had never been refused anything. "Your grandmother spoke of you often. I'm sorry we couldn't meet under better circumstances."

"Don't." Liang Yue's hands clenched into fists. "Don't pretend you knew her."

"I did know her." He tilted his head, studying her like a biologist examining a specimen. "I offered her a partnership. A chance to bring Internal Alchemy into the modern age. She refused."

"So you killed her."

"Her death was unfortunate." He said it the way someone might discuss bad weather. "But necessary. The old ways must make room for the new. Surely, as a pharmacy student, you understand the principle of iteration."

Liang Yue felt the furnace pulse, felt Grandma's final moments burning through her veins. The rage was there, hot and bright, but beneath it was something colder. Something that understood exactly what this man was.

"You're not a cultivator," she said.

Zhang Tianming's smile flickered.

"You've taken the artificial Qi. Multiple doses. But your body is rejecting it." She stepped closer, reading the signs—the slight tremor in his hands, the way his pupils didn't dilate properly, the faint yellow tint at the corners of his eyes. "The toxins are building up in your liver. You have maybe six months before the organ failure becomes irreversible."

For the first time, something real crossed his face. Not fear. Interest.

"Your grandmother said the same thing. The week before she died."

"Because she was right." Liang Yue pulled a small pill from her pocket—one she'd refined that morning, not knowing why. Now she understood. "This is a Draining Pill. It will pull the artificial Qi from your system. It will also destroy your ability to ever use it again."

Zhang Tianming's eyes fixed on the pill. "And what do you want in return?"

"Tell me where your father keeps the research. The original formulas. The names of everyone involved in Grandma's death."

"Or what?"

"Or I refine something else." She met his gaze, unwavering. "Something your body can't survive."

The sirens grew louder. Fire trucks screamed into the campus. Students ran past them, shouting, crying. But Liang Yue and Zhang Tianming stood in the center of the chaos, locked in a standoff that neither could afford to lose.

Finally, Zhang Tianming laughed.

It was a beautiful sound, warm and genuine. It made Liang Yue's skin crawl.

"You really are her granddaughter," he said. "Stubborn. Brilliant. Completely blind to the bigger picture." He stepped closer, close enough that she could smell the alcohol on his breath, the chemical tang of the artificial Qi leaking from his pores. "I'm not the one who's dying, Liang Yue. I'm the one who's offering you a cure."

"A cure for what?"

"For the poison already in your blood." He reached into his jacket and pulled out a syringe, filled with a liquid that glowed faintly blue. "Your grandmother's furnace. You've been refining with it for three days now. Did she never tell you about the cost?"

The furnace went cold in Liang Yue's dantian. Ice cold.

"Every pill you refine consumes a piece of your life force," Zhang Tianming continued, his voice soft, almost gentle. "The stronger the pill, the more it takes. Your grandmother was dying long before my people accelerated the process." He held out the syringe. "This will neutralize the furnace's bond to your Qi. You'll lose the ability to refine—but you'll live."

Liang Yue stared at the syringe. At the blue liquid inside. At the truth she'd been avoiding since the moment she first lit the furnace.

She had felt it. The weakness after each successful refinement. The bone-deep exhaustion that sleep couldn't touch. The way her hair had started falling out in the shower.

She had thought it was stress.

"Who else knows this?" she asked.

"Everyone who matters." Zhang Tianming smiled. "Every cultivator who's ever picked up a furnace. Every alchemist who's ever reached the ninth grade. It's the first lesson they teach—if you're lucky enough to have a teacher."

Grandma's voice echoed in her memory: *The Way exacts a price, Yue'er. Everything worth doing demands sacrifice.*

But Grandma had never told her the price. Not once. Not even at the end.

"Why?" Liang Yue whispered. "Why didn't she tell me?"

"Because she needed you to succeed her." Zhang Tianming's eyes were almost kind. "And because she loved you too much to let you refuse."

The syringe hung in the air between them. The fire raged behind them. The sirens screamed.

And Liang Yue realized she had been a fool.

Not for trusting her grandmother. Not for picking up the furnace. But for thinking she understood the game she was playing.

The rules had been hidden from her from the very beginning.

And now she had to decide: keep the furnace and die young, or surrender the legacy and live.

"Take it," Professor Li whispered behind her. "Liang Yue, take the cure. You can find another way."
