📖 The Last Alchemist of Chengdu by Midnight Quill

Chapter 12: The Blood of the Furnace

~1.8k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The morgue's fluorescent lights hummed overhead, casting everything in a sickly pallor. Liang Yue's fingers traced the stained slide in Professor Li's trembling hand, the chemical markers glowing an unnatural violet under the microscope's beam.

"Mercury," she breathed. "Cinnabar. Mixed with something else—something I don't recognize."

Professor Li's face tightened. "That's because it's not from any known compound. The Zhang family developed it specifically to target Internal Alchemists. It binds to the dantian's energy residue and catalyzes the body's own Qi into a poison."

Liang Yue's stomach turned. "They weaponized cultivation?"

"They weaponized *you*." He pulled another slide from the folder. "This is from your grandmother's bone marrow. The concentration is highest there—the toxins accumulated over months. She was being dosed slowly, carefully, in amounts that wouldn't trigger suspicion."

"Someone in her building. Someone she trusted." Liang Yue's voice cracked. "The neighbor. Auntie Chen. She brought Grandma soup every Tuesday."

Professor Li's silence was answer enough.

The furnace in Liang Yue's dantian pulsed—not with heat, but with a cold, sharp awareness. It recognized the poison. It *remembered*.

"I need to see the body."

"Liang Yue—"

"I need to see her."

The morgue was two floors below, through a labyrinth of empty hallways and locked doors. Professor Li's keycard opened them all. His silence spoke volumes.

The drawer slid open with a whisper of metal on metal. Grandma's face was peaceful, preserved by the university's best embalming techniques. She looked asleep. She looked *wrong*.

Liang Yue placed her palm on Grandma's forehead and *listened*.

The furnace flared. Her consciousness plunged through flesh and bone into the cold archives of death. She saw the poison's signature—a spiral pattern etched into every cell, like a fingerprint left by a murderer.

But there was something else.

A thread of light, thin as spider silk, running from Grandma's chest outward. *Upward*. Through the morgue ceiling, through the university, toward—

"The Zhang compound," Liang Yue whispered, withdrawing her hand. Her fingers were steaming. "They're connected to her still. They're *using* her."

Professor Li went pale. "Impossible. She's dead."

"Death doesn't sever Qi." Liang Yue's voice was flat, cold. "Not completely. They're leaching residual energy from her corpse—feeding it into their artificial cultivation process."

"That's..." He couldn't finish.

"That's why she wasn't cremated." Liang Yue turned, her eyes burning with a fire that had nothing to do with the furnace. "They needed her body intact. They're still drinking from her well."

The door at the far end of the morgue clicked open.

A figure stepped through—tall, immaculate in a charcoal suit, with the Zhang family's jade ring glinting on his finger. Zhang Weiran. The eldest son. The one who'd smiled at her in the university courtyard three days ago, offering condolences.

"Miss Liang." His voice was silk over steel. "I thought I might find you here."

Professor Li moved to block her. "Weiran. This is a restricted area."

"Nothing is restricted for the Zhang family." Weiran's smile didn't reach his eyes. "I'm here to offer Miss Liang a proposal."

"The way you offered my grandmother a cup of poison?" Liang Yue's voice was ice.

For a fraction of a second, something flickered across his face. Surprise? Respect? "You're sharper than we anticipated. Your grandmother was sharper too—she knew, at the end. She tried to destroy the furnace."

Liang Yue's blood went cold. "She gave it to me."

"She gave it to you because she couldn't break it. Eight hundred years of alchemical refinement made it indestructible." Weiran stepped closer, his shoes clicking on the tile. "But you're not indestructible. And the Zhang family has resources beyond your imagination."

"Is that a threat?"

"It's an invitation." He pulled a card from his pocket, embossed with gold characters. "The annual Alchemical Summit. Three days from now. The Zhang family would be honored to host the last Internal Alchemist as our guest."

"After you murdered my grandmother?"

"After my *father* murdered your grandmother." Weiran's expression didn't change. "I'm offering you a chance to learn the truth. To see what your grandmother was protecting you from."

Professor Li grabbed her arm. "Don't. It's a trap."

"Of course it's a trap." Liang Yue took the card. "But I'm going anyway."

Weiran's smile widened. "Excellent. A car will pick you up at dawn."

He turned and walked away, his footsteps echoing long after he'd disappeared through the door.

"You can't be serious," Professor Li hissed. "They'll kill you the moment you step inside."

"They'll try." Liang Yue looked at the card. The characters shimmered, revealing hidden layers of script—alchemical symbols, warning signs, a map of the Zhang estate. "But Grandma left me more than the furnace. She left me a key."

"A key to what?"

Liang Yue held up the card, letting the light catch the hidden ink. "To the vault where they keep their research. The research she died trying to destroy."

---

Back in her dorm room, Liang Yue spread the evidence across her desk. The poison's signature. The Zhang family's compound layout. The card's hidden map. And the furnace, sitting in her palm like a brass heartbeat.

She needed allies.

The text went out at midnight: *Emergency meeting. The old teahouse. Dawn.*

Responses came in minutes. Xiao Mei: *I'll bring the banned tomes.* Brother Chen: *The Wastrels ride at first light.* And from an unknown number: *I know where they buried the truth.*

Liang Yue stared at that last message. The number was blocked. The words were in Grandma's handwriting—sharp, elegant characters that flowed like water.

But Grandma was dead.

She replied anyway: *Who is this?*

The response came immediately: *The one who taught you to read the stars.*

Liang Yue's breath caught. Grandma had taught her to read the stars—a childhood memory, long buried. No one else knew.

*That's impossible.*

*Nothing is impossible for those who walk the Way. Come to the teahouse. Bring the furnace. Bring your courage.*

The messages deleted themselves, one by one, leaving only the empty chat. Liang Yue's hands were shaking.

She packed the furnace, the journal, the photos, and the card. She slipped out of the dorm before the sun rose, moving through Chengdu's awakening streets like a ghost.

The old teahouse stood at the edge of Jinli Ancient Street, its wooden beams weathered by centuries. Lanterns still glowed inside, though the establishment wasn't open for business.

Xiao Mei was already there, her arms full of books wrapped in oilcloth. Brother Chen stood guard by the door, his Wastrel jacket patched and worn. And at the center table, pouring tea with steady hands, sat a woman Liang Yue had never seen before.

She was young—maybe thirty—with sharp features and eyes that held too much knowledge. She wore modern clothes but moved with the grace of a martial artist.

"Liang Yue." The woman's voice was familiar. Too familiar. "Sit. We have much to discuss."

"Who are you?"

"I'm what your grandmother was, before she retired." The woman poured a cup of tea and slid it across the table. "I'm the last *other* Internal Alchemist."

The words hung in the air like a curse.

"There are more of us?"

"Not anymore." The woman's eyes darkened. "The Zhang family found me three years ago. They took everything—my furnace, my recipes, my students. They left me for dead in a ditch outside Chengdu."

"How did you survive?"

"I didn't." She pulled back her sleeve, revealing a tattoo of interlocking gears—the Zhang family brand. "They marked me. Owned me. Used me to synthesize their artificial Qi."

Xiao Mei gasped. "You're a Zhang asset?"

"I *was*." The woman's smile was bitter. "Until your grandmother found me. She helped me escape. Hid me in the mountains. Taught me how to hide my Qi signature from their sensors."

Brother Chen stepped forward. "How do we know you're not working for them now?"

"Because I know what they're planning." The woman met Liang Yue's eyes. "The Alchemical Summit isn't a negotiation. It's a harvest. They're going to drain your furnace—and your life—to power their artificial cultivation on a massive scale."

Liang Yue's hand went to her dantian, where the furnace pulsed with urgent heat. "Then why walk into their trap?"

"Because we have something they want." The woman leaned forward. "The original recipe for the Immortality Pill. Your grandmother's greatest creation. She hid it in plain sight, in her old medicine cabinet, in a jar labeled 'sugar pills.'"

Liang Yue's blood ran cold. She remembered that jar. Grandma had made her take one every morning, claiming it was for her health.

She'd been eating the Immortality Pill recipe her entire life.

"The Zhang family will do anything to get that formula," the woman continued. "And I'm going to help you give it to them."

"Give it to them?" Xiao Mei's voice rose. "That's insane!"

"It's leverage." The woman's smile was sharp. "The formula is incomplete without the furnace's fire to refine it. And the furnace is incomplete without the blood of the last Internal Alchemist to activate it."

She looked at Liang Yue with something like pity.

"Your grandmother didn't just give you the furnace. She gave you a choice. You can let them take it all—or you can burn them from the inside out."

Liang Yue's hands trembled as she reached for the tea. The liquid was cold. The room was cold. Everything was cold except the furnace in her chest, burning hotter by the second.

"Tell me everything," she said. "And tell me how to kill them."

The woman's smile widened. "I thought you'd never ask."

Outside, the sun rose over Chengdu, painting the sky in shades of blood and gold. The Alchemical Summit was three days away. And somewhere in the Zhang family compound, Grandma's body was still being drained, feeding a machine that would consume everything Liang Yue loved.

But she had the furnace. She had the recipe. And she had three days to become the weapon Grandma had trained her to be.

She drank the cold tea in one gulp and felt the poison burn through her veins.

The woman's eyes went wide. "What have you done?"

"Taken your insurance policy." Liang Yue set down the cup. "If I die, the recipe dies with me. And your only chance at freedom dies with it."

The woman laughed—a harsh, surprised sound. "You really are her granddaughter."

"I'm her *revenge*." Liang Yue stood, the poison already settling into her bones, a countdown clock only she could hear. "Now show me how to survive long enough to burn them all."

The Wastrels stirred behind her, their presence a wall of steel and silence. Xiao Mei clutched her banned tomes like weapons. Brother Chen cracked his knuckles.

And the unknown woman rose, extending her branded arm.

"Welcome to the resistance, Liang Yue. May the furnace guide your fire."

Liang Yue took her hand.

The poison sang in her blood.

Three days to live. Three days
