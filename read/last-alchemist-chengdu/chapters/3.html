Chapter 3: The Wastrel Rises from the Mud

~1.8k words · 2026-06-22

The fluorescent lights of Sichuan University’s chemistry building hummed like a swarm of trapped bees. Liang Yue pressed her palm against the cold metal of the lab bench, willing her hands to stop shaking.

Three days. Three days since Grandma died. Three days since she’d first ignited the brass pill furnace and watched a single Qi-condensing pill form from nothing but herbs and intention. Three days since she’d become, apparently, the last person on Earth who could do what she’d just done.

“Your extraction method is wrong.”

The voice came from behind her, crisp and condescending. Liang Yue didn’t need to turn around. She knew that voice—knew the precise amount of venom it could carry.

Zhang Wei. Senior student, top of the pharmacology program, heir to the Zhang family’s chain of traditional medicine clinics across Sichuan. He’d been making her life miserable since freshman year.

“I wasn’t aware you were grading my lab work, Senior Zhang.” She kept her voice flat, focused on the beaker in front of her. The ethanol extraction she’d set up as cover for her real experiment—a modified version of the furnace’s Qi-purification technique applied to synthetic compounds.

“I’m not.” He stepped closer, his white lab coat immaculate, his hair perfectly styled. “But I can smell failure from across the room. What are you even trying to do? That setup is—wait.” He stopped, frowning at the glass apparatus. “That’s not standard. What concentration are you using?”

Liang Yue’s heart hammered. The ethanol mixture was exactly 47.3%—a number she’d pulled from the alchemical texts, converted from the ancient measurement of “three parts spirit, four parts water.” There was no way he could know.

“None of your business.”

“Everything in this lab is my business.” Zhang Wei’s eyes narrowed. “Professor Li asked me to supervise the graduate students this semester. And you’re not even a graduate student, are you, Liang Yue? Just a lowly third-year undergraduate playing with equipment she doesn’t understand.”

The insult stung less than it should have. Three days ago, she would have shrunk under his gaze, muttered an apology, packed up her things. But something had changed in those three days. The furnace’s fire still burned in her dantian—the energy center just below her navel that the texts called the “elixir field.” She could feel it now, a constant warmth that pulsed with her heartbeat.

“I understand perfectly,” she said, finally turning to face him. “The question is whether you understand what you’re looking at.”

Zhang Wei’s confident smirk faltered for just a moment. Then he laughed—a short, barking sound that drew attention from the other students in the lab.

“Look at this, everyone! The wastrel thinks she’s discovered something.” He swept his arm toward her setup. “Liang Yue, who barely passed Organic Chemistry I, who needed three attempts to synthesize aspirin, is lecturing me on extraction techniques.”

Heat rose to Liang Yue’s cheeks. The words hit home because they were true. She had struggled. She’d spent her first two years of university barely scraping by, distracted by Grandma’s declining health, by the strange dreams that had plagued her since childhood, by the feeling that she didn’t belong in this world of sterile labs and precise measurements.

But the furnace had changed everything. The texts Grandma had left—hundreds of pages of handwritten notes, diagrams of meridians and energy flows, recipes for pills that could heal wounds, purify toxins, even extend life—they had unlocked something in her mind. Concepts she’d struggled with for years suddenly made sense. The alchemy wasn’t just chemistry; it was *intention* applied to matter.

“You don’t know what you’re talking about,” she said, her voice steady. “This isn’t a standard extraction. This is—“

She stopped herself. She couldn’t tell him the truth. Couldn’t explain that she was trying to replicate the furnace’s Qi-refining process using modern equipment. That the experiment, if successful, would produce a solution that could accelerate cellular regeneration by 300%.

“This is what?” Zhang Wei leaned in, his eyes gleaming with predatory interest. “Some secret family recipe? I heard your grandmother passed away. The old woman who sold fake medicines in Jinli Ancient Street?”

The furnace flared in Liang Yue’s dantian. Actual heat coursed through her veins, and for a moment, she saw something—a flicker of green energy around Zhang Wei’s chest, pulsing irregularly. A blockage?

“Don’t talk about my grandmother.”

“Why not? Everyone knows she was a fraud. A superstitious old woman peddling herbs to tourists.” Zhang Wei’s smile was cruel. “Like student, like grandmother. The wastrel and the witch.”

The furnace roared.

Liang Yue didn’t consciously decide to move. Her hand shot out, fingers closing around Zhang Wei’s wrist. He tried to pull away, but her grip was iron—strength she didn’t know she possessed flowing from the furnace’s fire.

“What are you—let go of me!”

She didn’t let go. Instead, she pushed—not with her hand, but with her Qi. The energy she’d cultivated over three days, the tiny stream of internal power she’d learned to direct through her meridians, she channeled it into Zhang Wei’s arm.

His eyes went wide. His mouth opened, but no sound came out. The green blockage she’d seen earlier—she pushed against it, feeling its texture, its location. A knot of stagnant energy in his liver meridian. Years of repressed anger, of competitive stress, of late nights and poor diet.

“What are you doing to me?” His voice was a whisper now, tinged with genuine fear.

“Nothing harmful,” Liang Yue said, surprising herself with her calm. “Just showing you what you’re missing.”

She pulled back, releasing his wrist. Zhang Wei stumbled, gasping. For a moment, color flooded his face—a healthy flush that hadn’t been there before. His eyes, usually bloodshot from too much caffeine and too little sleep, were clear.

“That’s impossible,” he breathed. “You—that was—“

“Qi,” she said softly. “The thing your family’s clinics pretend to use but don’t actually understand. The thing my grandmother spent her life mastering.”

The lab had gone silent. Every student was staring at them. Professor Li stood in the doorway, a stack of papers frozen in his hands.

“Liang Yue.” The professor’s voice was tight. “My office. Now.”

She gathered her things slowly, deliberately. The furnace’s warmth pulsed steady in her chest, grounding her. As she passed Zhang Wei, still standing stunned, she caught a whiff of something on his breath.

Alcohol. Not just alcohol—something medicinal. Something that smelled like the pills in Grandma’s forbidden recipe book. The ones marked with red ink: *Toxic. For external use only.*

“Senior Zhang,” she said, pausing. “What have you been taking?”

His face went pale. “Nothing. None of your business.”

“The Qi in your liver meridian is blocked by something artificial. Something that’s rotting from the inside.” The words came out before she could stop them, channeled by the furnace’s intuition. “If you don’t purge it within a week, your organs will start failing.”

“You’re insane.” But his voice cracked.

“Maybe.” She turned toward the professor’s office. “But I’m also right.”

---

Professor Li’s office smelled of old books and green tea. Calligraphy scrolls hung on the walls—ancient poems about mountains and rivers, about the fleeting nature of life. Liang Yue sat in the worn wooden chair across from his desk, her hands folded in her lap.

“What you did in there,” Professor Li began, his voice carefully neutral, “was that real Qi manipulation?”

She stared at him. Professor Li was a respected pharmacologist, a man of science. He shouldn’t be asking about Qi.

“Why would you call it that?”

“Because I’ve been researching traditional alchemy for thirty years.” He leaned back, his eyes tired. “And because I knew your grandmother.”

The world tilted. “You knew Grandma?”

“She was a consultant for my doctoral thesis. On the pharmacological applications of Internal Alchemy theory.” He pulled open a drawer, extracting a worn leather journal. “She gave me this. Said I might need it someday.”

He slid it across the desk. Liang Yue opened it, her hands trembling. Grandma’s handwriting—sharp, elegant characters that flowed like water.

*To Li Wei, a seeker of truth: The Way cannot be found through science alone, nor through spirit alone. But together, they open the door.*

“She knew this day would come,” Professor Li said quietly. “She told me—if anything ever happened to her, to watch for a student who could feel the furnace’s fire.”

Liang Yue’s eyes burned. “She never told me.”

“She couldn’t. The last Internal Alchemist is always a secret, even from their own blood, until the moment of succession.” He paused. “But there’s something else. Something I need to warn you about.”

“What?”

“The Zhang family. They’re not what they seem.” He lowered his voice. “They’ve been funding research into artificial Qi—pills and injections that can simulate cultivation effects without actual cultivation. They’re close to a breakthrough.”

The words hit like a physical blow. “Artificial Qi? That’s impossible. Qi comes from the furnace, from years of refinement—“

“From the soul.” He nodded. “I know. But they don’t believe in souls. They believe in chemistry, in brute force. And they’ve been using human test subjects.”

“Test subjects? That’s illegal. That’s—“

“That’s why your grandmother died.”

The room went cold. Liang Yue’s blood froze in her veins. “What?”

“She didn’t have a heart attack.” Professor Li’s voice was barely a whisper. “She was poisoned. A slow-acting toxin that mimics cardiac arrest. I found the residue on her medicine cabinet.”

“Why didn’t you tell the police?”

“Because the Zhang family owns the police. They own half of Chengdu’s government. They own this university.” His face was grim. “If they find out you have the furnace, they’ll come for you next.”

Liang Yue’s hands were shaking. The furnace pulsed in her dantian, hot and urgent. She thought of Grandma’s final days—the weakness, the confusion, the way her eyes had cleared for just a moment on the last night.

*Yue’er,* she’d whispered, pressing the furnace into Liang Yue’s hands. *Don’t let them take it. Don’t let them twist the Way.*

“I need to see the body,” Liang Yue said, her voice hard. “Grandma’s body. They said it was cremated, but—“

“It wasn’t.” Professor Li pulled out a folder, thick with documents. “I had it transferred to the university morgue. Officially, for research purposes. Unofficially, because I needed to confirm what I suspected.”

He opened the folder. Photos spilled out—medical images, toxicology reports, autopsy findings.

“Look at this.”
