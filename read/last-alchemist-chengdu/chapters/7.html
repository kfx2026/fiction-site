CHAPTER 7: Secrets in the Marrow

The chemical marker in Grandma's liver looked like a galaxy of poison stars—tiny pinpricks of darkness scattered across the stained tissue like judgment.

"What am I looking at?" Liang Yue's voice didn't sound like her own.

"Heavy metal accumulation. Specifically, a compound I've never seen documented anywhere." Professor Li tapped the image. "It's not lead. Not mercury. Not any of the standard toxins. It's something engineered—a molecule that binds to Qi pathways and slowly corrupts them from the inside."

Liang Yue's stomach turned. "How long would it take?"

"To kill someone with Grandma's cultivation level?" He paused. "Eighteen months. Maybe two years. The first symptoms would have looked like normal aging—fatigue, memory lapses, joint pain. By the time anyone suspected poison, it would be too late."

*The last year.* The year Grandma had stopped making her morning tea. The year she'd forgotten Liang Yue's birthday for the first time. The year she'd started sleeping sixteen hours a day.

It hadn't been age. It hadn't been disease.

It had been murder, administered in careful, untraceable doses.

"Who gave it to her?" Liang Yue's hands curled into fists.

"We don't know. But we know how." Professor Li pulled out another document—a pharmaceutical company's clinical trial report. "The compound was designed to look like a standard calcium supplement. Easy to disguise. Easy to administer."

"A supplement?" Liang Yue's blood went cold. Grandma had been religious about her supplements. Every morning, a row of pills lined up like soldiers. *A body is a temple, Yue'er. You have to maintain the structure.*

"She would never have suspected. None of us would have."

Liang Yue looked up from the photos. "You said the Zhang family is behind this. How do I prove it?"

"You don't. Not yet." Professor Li's voice was sharp. "The Zhang family has been in Chengdu for three hundred years. They have roots in every level of society. If you go after them directly, you'll disappear before you reach the front door of their compound."

"Then what do I do?"

"You learn. You grow stronger. You build alliances." He leaned forward. "Your grandmother left you more than just the furnace. She left you a network—people who owe her favors, who remember what she did for them. But they won't come to you. You have to find them."

"How?"

Professor Li reached into his drawer again, pulling out a business card. It was plain white, with nothing but a phone number printed in elegant black script.

"This is the first contact. Call the number. Say the words: *The lotus blooms in winter.* They'll know what it means."

Liang Yue took the card. It felt warm in her hand, like it had been held recently.

"When should I call?"

"Tonight. Before the Zhang family realizes you're onto them." He stood, walking to the window. The sun was setting over the campus, casting long shadows across the quad. "They'll be watching you now. They know you have the furnace. They know you're connected to your grandmother. The only question is how long it takes them to decide you're a threat."

"And then?"

"Then they'll come for you." He turned back, his face grim. "And they won't try to kill you quietly this time. They'll make an example of you."

---

Liang Yue walked back to her dorm room in a daze. The evening air was thick with humidity, the kind of oppressive heat that clung to skin like a second layer. Students passed her on the path, laughing, talking about exams and relationships and weekend plans.

None of them knew that a war was brewing beneath their feet.

None of them knew that their classmate was being hunted.

She climbed the stairs to her floor, her hand sliding into her pocket to touch the business card. *The lotus blooms in winter.* What kind of code was that? What kind of network had Grandma built?

As she reached her door, she froze.

The lock was broken. The door hung slightly ajar.

Her heart hammered. She pushed the door open slowly, her eyes scanning the room.

Everything was tossed. Drawers pulled out, clothes scattered, mattress flipped. Her textbooks lay in a heap on the floor, pages torn. Someone had been thorough.

But they hadn't found what they were looking for.

She crossed to her closet, moved aside the false panel at the back. The furnace was still there, wrapped in silk, pulsing with warmth. She pressed her palm against it, feeling the familiar vibration.

*Safe. Still safe.*

But for how long?

She took out her phone. The number on the card seemed to glow in the dim light. She dialed.

One ring. Two. Three.

"Hello?" A woman's voice, middle-aged, calm.

"The lotus blooms in winter," Liang Yue said, her voice steady despite her shaking hands.

A pause. Then: "Where did you get this number?"

"From Professor Li Wei. He said you could help me."

Another pause, longer this time. When the woman spoke again, her voice had changed—harder, more focused. "You're Liang Yue. The granddaughter."

"Yes."

"Then you already know too much and not enough." The woman sighed. "Listen to me carefully. There's a tea house on Jinli Street. The one with the red lanterns and the bamboo sign. Be there tomorrow at midnight. Ask for Sister Hua."

"And then?"

"And then we'll see if you're ready." The line went dead.

Liang Yue stared at her phone. The call had lasted thirty seconds. Thirty seconds to change everything.

She looked around her ruined room. The broken furniture, the scattered clothes, the violated privacy. The Zhang family's message was clear: *We can reach you anywhere.*

But they'd made a mistake. They'd let her know they were coming.

And Liang Yue had spent her entire life learning how to prepare for things that were coming.

---

The tea house was exactly where the woman had said it would be—tucked between a souvenir shop and a noodle stall, its red lanterns casting bloody light onto the cobblestones. The bamboo sign creaked in the night breeze, characters carved into it: *House of Ten Thousand Fragrances.*

Liang Yue pushed open the door.

The interior was empty. No customers, no staff. Just rows of wooden tables and the smell of jasmine tea hanging in the air like a ghost.

"Close the door behind you."

The woman's voice came from the shadows at the back. Liang Yue obeyed, the latch clicking shut.

Sister Hua stepped into the light. She was older than her voice had suggested—maybe sixty, with silver-streaked hair pulled back in a severe bun. Her face was lined, but her eyes were sharp, the eyes of someone who had seen too much and trusted too little.

"You look like her," Sister Hua said. "Your grandmother. You have her stubborn jaw."

"Everyone says that."

"Everyone's right." Sister Hua gestured to a seat. "Sit. We have much to discuss, and little time."

Liang Yue sat. The chair was old wood, worn smooth by decades of use.

"You called the number. You said the words. That means you're ready for the truth." Sister Hua poured tea from a pot that hadn't been there a moment ago. Steam rose in twisting spirals. "But you need to understand—once you hear what I have to say, there's no going back. You can't unlearn this."

"I understand."

"Do you?" Sister Hua's eyes locked onto hers. "Your grandmother was part of something larger than you can imagine. A network of alchemists that stretches back centuries. We call ourselves the Hidden Lotus—those who practice the true Way in secret, while the world believes we're extinct."

"Hidden Lotus," Liang Yue repeated. "Grandma never mentioned—"

"Of course she didn't. The first rule of the Hidden Lotus is that the Hidden Lotus doesn't exist." Sister Hua sipped her tea. "But now that you've inherited the furnace, you're part of it whether you like it or not."

"What about the Zhang family? Professor Li said—"

"The Zhang family is a symptom, not the disease." Sister Hua set down her cup with a sharp click. "They're puppets. The real threat is a group that calls themselves the New Dawn Consortium. They're the ones developing the artificial Qi. They're the ones who killed your grandmother."

Liang Yue's hands gripped the edge of the table. "Why?"

"Because your grandmother was the last person who knew how to create true Internal Alchemy pills. The Consortium wants to mass-produce a counterfeit version—pills that give the illusion of cultivation without the spiritual foundation. They want to create an army of false cultivators, loyal only to them."

"An army? For what?"

"For war." Sister Hua's voice dropped. "There's a conflict coming, Liang Yue. The supernatural world has been balanced on a knife's edge for centuries, and the Consortium wants to tip the scales in their favor. They don't care about the Way. They don't care about balance. They only care about power."

Liang Yue's mind raced. This was too big, too vast. She was just a pharmacy student. She'd only had the furnace for a month.

"Why me?" she asked. "I'm not ready for this."

"None of us are ready. But the furnace chose you, and the furnace doesn't make mistakes." Sister Hua reached into her sleeve, pulling out a small jade pendant. "This was your grandmother's. She told me to give it to you when you came."

The pendant was warm, almost alive. Liang Yue took it, and the moment her fingers touched the jade, images flooded her mind—Grandma's face, a room she'd never seen, a book written in a script she couldn't read.

And then a name: *The Silent Peak.*

"What is this?" Liang Yue gasped, pulling her hand back.

"Your inheritance." Sister Hua stood. "The Silent Peak is a place. A mountain temple where your grandmother stored her most precious knowledge. You need to go there. You need to find what she left behind."

"Where is it?"

"I can't tell you. You have to find it yourself." Sister Hua walked toward the back door. "That's part of the test."

"Wait—"

But Sister Hua was already gone, disappearing into the shadows like she'd never been there at all.

Liang Yue sat alone in the empty tea house, the jade pendant warm in her palm, the furnace pulsing in her dantian.

She had a destination she didn't know how to reach, enemies who wanted her dead, and a war she'd never asked to join.

And somewhere out there, in a mountain temple she'd never seen, her grandmother's final secrets were waiting.

She pocketed the pendant and stepped back into the night.

The red lanterns swayed in the wind, casting dancing shadows across the street.

And behind her, in the darkness of an alley, a figure watched.

A phone buzzed. A voice whispered: *She made contact. Moving to Phase Two.*

The figure disappeared.

And Liang Yue walked home alone, unaware that the trap had already closed.
