Chapter 5: The Weight of Blood and Fire

~1.8k words · 2026-06-22

The morgue's fluorescent lights hummed like trapped insects, casting everything in a sickly pallor. Liang Yue's reflection stared back at her from the stainless steel table—hollow-eyed, jaw tight, hands clenched so hard her nails bit into her palms.

Professor Li stood beside her, his face drawn. "You don't have to do this."

"Yes, I do."

The sheet was white. Clinical. Anonymous. Grandma had always hated white—said it reminded her of hospital waiting rooms and bad news. *Life is color, Yue'er. Red like cinnabar. Gold like the furnace. Never let the world wash you gray.*

Liang Yue pulled back the sheet.

Grandma's face was peaceful. Too peaceful. The kind of peace that came from careful arrangement, not natural rest. Her skin had the waxy sheen of preservation chemicals, and her lips—lips that had once whispered ancient formulas and laughed at terrible puns—were sewn shut in a thin, bloodless line.

"She doesn't look like herself," Liang Yue whispered.

"Because she wasn't allowed to." Professor Li pointed to a discolored patch on Grandma's temple. "See that? It's barely visible now, but when I first examined her, it was a deep violet. That's not typical of cardiac arrest."

"What is it?"

"Capillary rupture from extreme Qi fluctuation. In layman's terms—someone forced energy through her meridians until they burst from the inside."

The furnace in Liang Yue's dantian flared, sending a spike of heat through her chest. She gasped, grabbing the edge of the table.

"Your grandmother's body is reacting to the furnace," Professor Li said, his voice clinical but not unkind. "Internal Alchemists share a bond that transcends death. She's trying to tell you something."

"Show me," Liang Yue managed through gritted teeth. "Whatever she wants me to see."

She placed her palm on Grandma's cold forehead.

The world dissolved.

---

She was in a room she didn't recognize. Dark paneled walls. A mahogany desk cluttered with papers. And Grandma—alive, vibrant, furious—facing off against a man in a tailored suit.

"You're insane," Grandma spat. "Qi isn't a chemical compound you can synthesize. It's the breath of the universe. The essence of life itself."

The man smiled. It didn't reach his eyes. "The universe, Madam Liang, is a machine. And like any machine, it can be reverse-engineered. You've spent decades hoarding ancient knowledge that could revolutionize medicine, agriculture, energy—and for what? Superstition?"

"Balance. Harmony. The things your family forgot three generations ago when they started selling fake tonics to desperate people."

The man's smile vanished. "My family built an empire while yours hid in alleys, grinding herbs for peasants."

"Your family built an empire on lies. You know what I found in your labs, Zhang. The test subjects. The failures. The ones who didn't survive your 'treatments.'"

Zhang. Liang Yue's blood turned to ice. *The Zhang family.*

"You found nothing," Zhang said softly. "Because nothing exists. And if you try to prove otherwise, I'll make sure you disappear so completely that even your granddaughter won't remember your name."

Grandma laughed—that fierce, defiant laugh Liang Yue had heard a thousand times. "You think I'm afraid of you? I've faced warlords and revolutionaries. I've hidden the furnace through purges and famines. You're just another greedy man with too much money and not enough sense."

"I'm a man who knows when to cut his losses." Zhang stood, straightening his jacket. "This is your final warning, Madam Liang. Give me the furnace's formulas, and I'll let you retire in peace. Refuse, and—"

"And what? You'll kill me?" Grandma's eyes glittered. "Go ahead. But know this—the furnace will never serve you. It chooses its master. And it has already chosen."

Zhang's face contorted with rage. "The furnace is a piece of brass. Nothing more."

"Is it?" Grandma reached into her sleeve and pulled out a small jade pendant—one Liang Yue had never seen before. "Then explain why this—a simple protection charm I forged in the furnace—can do this."

She crushed the pendant in her fist.

The world exploded with light.

---

Liang Yue jerked back, gasping. Her palm burned, and when she looked down, a faint golden pattern was fading from her skin—the same pattern she'd seen on Grandma's jade pendant.

"What did you see?" Professor Li asked, his voice urgent.

"The Zhangs. A man in a suit. He threatened her. She crushed a pendant—a protection charm—and then—"

"Then the memory ends. Because that's when they took her." Professor Li's hands were shaking. "I found traces of that pendant's material in her lungs. She swallowed the pieces to keep them from falling into Zhang hands."

Liang Yue's stomach turned. "She ate the evidence."

"She died protecting the knowledge. Just like she said she would." Professor Li's voice cracked. "Your grandmother was the bravest woman I ever knew."

The furnace pulsed again, but this time it felt different. Hungrier. Angrier. It was feeding on Liang Yue's rage, amplifying it, turning it into something hot and sharp that demanded release.

"I need to find that man," Liang Yue said. "The one in the suit."

"Zhang Wei. Patriarch of the Zhang family. CEO of Zhang Biotech Corporation." Professor Li pulled out his phone. "I've been tracking him for months. He has offices in three cities, a private compound outside Chengdu, and—according to my sources—a secret laboratory in the basement of their headquarters."

"Then that's where I'm going."

"No. Absolutely not." Professor Li grabbed her arm. "You're a pharmacy student. You've had the furnace for three weeks. If you walk into that building, you'll be dead before you reach the elevator."

"Then what do you suggest?" Liang Yue's voice rose. "Sit here and wait for them to come for me? Grandma didn't hide. She fought."

"She fought for sixty years before she lost. You don't have sixty years. You don't have sixty days." He released her arm, his expression softening. "You have to be smart about this. Train. Learn. Grow strong enough to face them."

"Training takes years. I don't have years."

"No, you don't. But you might have weeks." Professor Li reached into his bag and pulled out a battered book—leather-bound, pages yellowed with age. "This was your grandmother's personal journal. Her alchemical notes, her experiments, her mistakes. Everything she learned in sixty years of practice."

Liang Yue took the book with trembling hands. The leather was warm beneath her fingers, as if it remembered its owner's touch.

"It's written in code," Professor Li continued. "A variant of classical Chinese mixed with alchemical symbols. It took me ten years to decipher the first three pages."

"Then how am I supposed to—"

"The furnace will help you understand. Internal Alchemy isn't taught through textbooks. It's transmitted through the bond between master and vessel. Your grandmother poured herself into that furnace. Literally. Her Qi, her memories, her techniques. You just have to learn how to access them."

Liang Yue opened the journal. The characters swam before her eyes—familiar yet foreign, like a language she'd once known but forgotten. But as she focused, the furnace pulsed in rhythm with her heartbeat, and the symbols began to shift, rearranging themselves into words she could understand.

*The Five Elements are not elements. They are states of being. Wood is growth. Fire is transformation. Earth is stability. Metal is refinement. Water is adaptability. To forge a true pill, you must embody all five—then transcend them.*

"I can read it," Liang Yue whispered. "I can actually read it."

"Good. Because you'll need every recipe in that book." Professor Li's phone buzzed. He glanced at the screen, and his face went pale. "They know."

"Who knows?"

"The Zhangs. They know you've been here. They know you have the journal." He showed her the screen—a text message from an unknown number. *Tell the girl she has forty-eight hours to surrender the furnace. After that, we'll collect it ourselves.*

Liang Yue's blood ran cold. "How did they—"

"The university is compromised. I told you. They own everything." Professor Li grabbed her arm. "You need to leave. Now. Go somewhere they won't find you."

"Where? They own everything, remember?"

"Not everything." He pressed a key into her hand. "There's a safe house in the old city. Wenshu Monastery district. Apartment 3B. Your grandmother owned it under a false name. Go there. Study the journal. Don't leave until I contact you."

"What about you?"

"I'll delay them. Create a diversion." He smiled grimly. "I've been a thorn in their side for thirty years. One more day won't make a difference."

"Professor Li—"

"Go." His voice was steel. "Your grandmother didn't die so you could waste her sacrifice on sentiment. Go. Learn. Grow. And when you're ready—make them pay."

Liang Yue clutched the journal to her chest. The furnace burned in her dantian, hot and hungry and alive. She wanted to stay. She wanted to fight. But she knew, with a cold certainty that felt nothing like courage, that Professor Li was right.

She turned and ran.

---

The night air hit her face as she burst out of the university's back gate. Chengdu's neon glow painted the sky in shades of pink and gold, and the distant hum of traffic was a familiar comfort. But everything felt different now. The shadows were deeper. The corners darker.

She ducked into an alley, checking over her shoulder every few steps. No one followed. But she could feel them—the Zhangs' eyes, their reach, their endless patience. They'd waited sixty years for the furnace. They'd wait forty-eight more hours.

And then they'd come.

The safe house was a cramped studio apartment above a tea shop. Dust covered every surface, and the air smelled of old books and dried herbs. But on the table in the center of the room sat a brass incense burner—identical to the one Grandma had used in her meditation room.

Liang Yue approached it slowly. The furnace in her dantian pulsed in recognition, and the incense burner began to glow with a faint golden light.

She touched it.

Images flooded her mind—Grandma's hands, wrinkled and steady, mixing herbs. Grandma's voice, reciting ancient poetry. Grandma's eyes, bright with laughter and wisdom, looking at her with love.

*Yue'er. You found my sanctuary.*

Tears streamed down Liang Yue's face. "Grandma. I'm so sorry. I didn't know. I didn't understand."

*You understand now. That's all that matters.*

The light faded, and the incense burner went cold. But Liang Yue felt something new stirring in her chest—not just the furnace's fire, but a thread of Grandma's Qi, woven into her own. A connection that death couldn't sever.

She opened the journal to the first page.

*To my dearest Yue'er: If you're reading this, I have passed beyond the veil. Do not mourn me. I lived a full life, and I died fighting for what I believed in. But know this—the Zhang family's artificial Qi is not their only project. They are trying to recreate the Elixir of Immortality. And they are closer than anyone knows.*
