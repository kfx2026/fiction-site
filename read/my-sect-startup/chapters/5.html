📖 My Sect is a Startup by Ren Wei

Chapter 5: The Fine Print That Kills

~1.8k words · Updated 2026-06-22

Ren held his breath as Consortium Master Huo studied him with those gentle, knowing eyes. The silence stretched, filled only by the soft hum of formation arrays embedded in the walls.

"You're bold," Huo finally said, steepling his fingers. "I appreciate boldness. Most cultivators who come to me are either desperate or delusional. You seem to be both, but at least you're aware of it."

"I prefer 'strategically optimistic,'" Ren said.

Huo laughed—a genuine sound, not the polite tittering of a man used to flattery. "I like you, Ren Wei. That's unfortunate for you. It means I'll give you a better deal than I give most. But still a deal that favors me. Business is business."

He slid a scroll across the desk. It was sealed with golden wax, the mark of the Azure Pond Consortium pressed into its surface. A koi fish swimming through a circle of coins.

"Three thousand spirit stones," Huo said. "Upfront. A fixed interest rate of fifteen percent per annum. No collateral required beyond a lien on your sect's future revenue. Standard terms for a venture of this risk level."

Ren's heart raced. Three thousand spirit stones was more than he'd hoped for. It would cover the temple renovations, basic equipment for his disciples, and leave enough for operating expenses for at least six months. The interest rate was high but not predatory.

"What's the catch?" Ren asked.

Huo's smile widened. "No catch. But there is an *option*."

He produced a second scroll. This one was smaller, the wax seal a different color—crimson rather than gold.

"If your sect achieves certain milestones within the first year, I have the right to convert my debt into equity. Twenty percent ownership of New Dao Sect."

Ren's blood went cold.

"That's—"

"Standard for high-risk investments," Huo interrupted smoothly. "Think of it as an incentive. If you succeed, I succeed with you. If you fail, I lose my investment. We're partners, Ren Wei. Partners with aligned interests."

Partners. Right. Partners where one partner could own a fifth of everything Ren had built.

"I need to discuss this with my—"

"You can discuss it with your disciples," Huo said, "but the offer is time-sensitive. I have another meeting in two hours with a promising young talisman maker. The investment pool is limited."

Ren stared at the crimson scroll. Twenty percent. It sounded reasonable on paper. In practice, it meant Huo could veto major decisions, demand access to sect secrets, and eventually push Ren out of his own organization.

But without the investment, there was no organization to protect.

"I'll need an hour," Ren said.

"Take two. The tea here is excellent."

---

Mei was pacing when Ren emerged. Li Chen had found a chair in the corner and was pretending to meditate, though his eyes tracked every movement in the room.

"Well?" Mei demanded.

"We have an offer. Three thousand spirit stones."

Her eyes lit up. "That's—"

"There's a conversion clause. Twenty percent equity if we hit milestones."

The light died. "That's *insane*. He wants to own us?"

"It's an option, not a guarantee. If we hit the milestones, we're already successful. Twenty percent of something valuable is better than a hundred percent of nothing."

Li Chen opened one eye. "What are the milestones?"

Ren pulled out the scroll he'd copied into his memory. "First: recruit fifty disciples within six months. Second: achieve at least one documented breakthrough to Foundation Establishment within the sect. Third: secure a partnership with at least one major faction."

Silence.

"That's..." Mei started.

"Impossible," Li Chen finished.

"Difficult," Ren corrected. "Not impossible. Fifty disciples is aggressive but doable if we market effectively. Foundation Establishment is harder—that requires a cultivator to reach the second major realm. None of us are close."

"You're at Qi Condensation stage four," Mei pointed out. "I'm at stage three. Li Chen is at stage two. The drunk sword master is at stage six but he's unreliable. The gambling formation expert is at stage one on a good day. And the chef cultivator is barely at stage one."

"Thank you for that thorough summary of our inadequacies."

"I'm being realistic. You asked me to be your CFO. This is what CFOs do."

Ren rubbed his temples. "The third milestone is the real problem. A partnership with a major faction means we need one of the established sects or noble houses to officially recognize us. That's... politically complicated."

"Because we're a joke," Li Chen said, not unkindly. "The startup sect with three misfits and a crumbling temple. Why would any major faction associate with us?"

"Because we offer something they don't," Ren said. "Innovation. Agility. We're not bound by tradition. We can move faster, adapt quicker, take risks they can't."

Mei crossed her arms. "You're describing a feature, not a benefit. What's the benefit?"

Ren had been thinking about this since he read the proposal. "Access to untapped talent. The major sects only recruit from established families or through their own exams. There are thousands of potential cultivators in the mortal cities who never get a chance because they don't have connections or resources. We can find them, train them, and create a pipeline that the major sects can draw from."

"That's actually... not terrible," Mei admitted.

"It's not terrible," Li Chen agreed. "It's also not proven."

"That's why it's a *startup*." Ren stood up straight. "I'm going to accept the offer. But I'm going to negotiate better terms. Twenty percent is too high for the risk they're taking. Ten percent, with a lower conversion threshold."

"You think he'll agree?"

"I think he'll counter. And then I'll counter his counter. And eventually we'll find a number that makes both of us uncomfortable enough to sign."

---

The negotiation took three hours.

Huo started at twenty percent. Ren countered with five. Huo laughed and offered fifteen. Ren said eight. Huo went to twelve, but added a clause that gave him first right of refusal on any future funding rounds.

Ren agreed, but only if the conversion option expired after eighteen months instead of twelve.

Huo considered. Then he smiled.

"You're good at this, Ren Wei. Have you considered a career in finance?"

"I considered it. But I'd rather build something than just count money."

"A philosophical difference without a practical distinction." Huo extended his hand. "Ten percent equity upon achieving two of the three milestones. Conversion option expires at eighteen months. First right of refusal on future rounds. Do we have a deal?"

Ren took his hand. The grip was firm, the skin warm. A cultivator's grip, but not aggressive.

"We have a deal."

The seals were broken. The contracts were signed. Spirit stones were transferred—three thousand of them, appearing in a storage ring that Huo provided as a "signing bonus."

Ren walked out of the Azure Pavilion feeling like he'd just sold a piece of his soul.

But at least his soul had a price.

---

The return to the temple was quiet. Mei kept glancing at Ren, her expression unreadable. Li Chen walked behind them, his hand resting on his sword hilt, alert for threats.

They reached the temple gates as the sun was setting, painting the crumbling walls in shades of gold and crimson.

"We did it," Mei said, her voice flat. "We actually did it."

"We did it," Ren agreed. "Now the real work begins."

They entered the main hall. The drunk sword master was passed out in a corner, surrounded by empty jars. The gambling formation expert was hunched over a table, muttering about hexagrams and probability matrices. The chef cultivator was in the kitchen, the smell of burning rice drifting through the temple.

"Home sweet home," Li Chen muttered.

Ren was about to respond when he felt it. A shift in the spiritual energy around them. A pressure, like the air itself was getting heavier.

Someone was coming.

Not someone. *Multiple* someones. Powerful someones.

The temple gates exploded inward.

Standing in the doorway was a woman in white robes, her hair bound in a severe bun, her eyes cold as winter ice. Behind her, a dozen cultivators in matching uniforms—the emblem of the Celestial Sword Sect embroidered on their chests.

"Ren Wei," the woman said, her voice carrying the weight of absolute authority. "You are hereby charged with violation of the Celestial Sword Sect's non-compete clause, theft of proprietary cultivation techniques, and unauthorized use of sect resources."

Ren's blood turned to ice.

"I'm sorry," he said, his voice steady despite his terror. "Non-compete clause?"

The woman's smile was razor-thin. "Did you think we wouldn't have lawyers?"

She threw a scroll at his feet. It unrolled to reveal page after page of dense legal text, all bearing the seal of the Celestial Sword Sect's legal department.

"You signed a five-year exclusivity agreement when you joined our sect," she continued. "That agreement contains a clause prohibiting you from establishing or joining any competing cultivation organization within ten thousand li of our sect's territory. You've been out of our employ for precisely three weeks."

Three weeks. Ren's mind raced. He'd been so focused on building his sect that he hadn't even thought about the paperwork.

"This is ridiculous," Mei said, stepping forward. "He was fired. You can't enforce a non-compete against someone you terminated."

"Actually," the woman said, producing another scroll, "the terms of termination specify that the non-compete remains in effect for a period of twelve months following separation, regardless of cause. It's all right here. Signed by Ren Wei himself."

Ren stared at the scroll. He'd signed so many documents when he joined the Celestial Sword Sect. Forms, waivers, agreements. He'd skimmed most of them, trusting that they were standard.

That had been a mistake.

"You have twenty-four hours," the woman said. "Disband your so-called sect. Return any property that might be considered sect assets. And report to the Celestial Sword Sect's legal department to discuss the terms of your compliance."

"And if I refuse?"

The woman's smile didn't waver. "Then we escalate. The Azure Pond Consortium might be interested to know that their new investment is built on a foundation of legal violations. I imagine they'll want their spirit stones back. With interest."

Ren's hand went to his storage ring. The three thousand spirit stones suddenly felt like a trap.

"Twenty-four hours," the woman repeated. "Don't be late."

She turned and walked away, her disciples following like shadows. The gates hung broken, the evening light streaming through the gap.

Ren stood in the middle of his temple, surrounded by his misfit disciples, holding a legal document that might destroy everything he'd built.

Mei touched his arm. "Ren?"

"We need a lawyer," he said. "And a miracle."

The chef cultivator emerged from the kitchen, holding a blackened pot. "Dinner's ruined," he said.

Ren laughed. It was a broken sound, edged with hysteria.

"Of course it is."
