CHAPTER_18: The Mortal Price

Ren watched Consortium Master Huo's face for any hint of reaction. The man's expression remained perfectly neutral, like a still lake hiding whatever lurked beneath.

"Bold words," Huo said, steepling his fingers. "You want my investment. But you're offering me something intangible — potential, vision, a dream. Dreams don't pay dividends, Ren Wei."

"Dreams build empires," Ren countered. "The Celestial Sword Sect started as a single swordsman with a broken blade. The Cloud Ascension Pavilion began as a cave with a leaking roof. Every great sect in history was once a 'minimum viable product.'"

Huo's smile widened, but it didn't reach his eyes. "You've done your research. I appreciate that. But let me ask you something more practical." He leaned forward. "Your sect has three disciples. One drunk, one gambler, one chef. Your temple is crumbling. Your total assets wouldn't buy me dinner at a decent establishment. Why should I trust you with my spirit stones?"

This was the moment. Ren had prepared for it.

"Because you're not just investing in a sect," Ren said, reaching into his robe. "You're investing in a *system*." He pulled out a scroll and unrolled it across Huo's desk. "This is our projection for the next three years. We'll start with pill distribution — we've already secured a partnership with a mortal apothecary who's been producing low-grade pills for years. No spiritual energy required, but effective enough for the early stages of cultivation."

Huo's eyes scanned the document. "Interesting. But mortal production has quality issues."

"We've solved that." Ren gestured to the next section. "My formation expert has developed a quality-control array that can be integrated into any production line. It filters impurities at the formation level, not the cultivation level. It's cheaper, faster, and more reliable than any spiritual refining method currently in use."

Huo's eyebrow rose a fraction. "And you're just showing me this?"

"I'm showing you what you get if you invest. If you don't..." Ren shrugged. "There are other consortiums."

The silence stretched. Ren could hear his own heartbeat.

Then Huo laughed. It was a warm sound, genuine, that transformed his scholarly face into something almost friendly. "You've got balls, kid. I'll give you that. Most people who walk through that door are begging. You're negotiating."

"That's because I have something worth negotiating for."

"Maybe." Huo sat back, his smile fading. "But I have concerns. Your projection assumes you can scale your production without interference from established powers. The Celestial Sword Sect alone has a vested interest in seeing you fail. They don't tolerate competition."

"We're not competing with them. We're targeting a different market — the mortal cultivators, the ones who can't afford the elite sects. That's a market they've ignored for centuries."

"And when they notice you?"

Ren met his eyes. "Then we'll have grown enough to defend ourselves. Or we'll fail trying. That's the nature of startups."

Huo was silent for a long moment. Then he reached into his desk and pulled out a contract, already written, the seal already stamped.

"I had this prepared before you arrived," he said, sliding it across the desk. "As a formality. But now I'm actually considering it."

Ren took the contract. His hands were steady, even though his heart was pounding. The terms were fair — shockingly fair. Investment in exchange for twenty percent equity. No board seats. No control over operations. Just funding and connections.

"Twenty percent?" Ren asked. "That's generous."

"It's strategic. I want to see what you can build." Huo's eyes sharpened. "But I have one condition. A personal one."

Ren's stomach tightened. There it was. There was always a catch.

"Name it."

"I want you to take on a student. My daughter."

Ren blinked. "Your... daughter?"

"She's seventeen. Gifted in formation theory, but she refuses to join any traditional sect. Says they're 'soul-crushing bureaucracies.'" Huo's lips quirked. "I don't know where she gets that from. But she's interested in what you're doing. She wants to learn."

Ren's mind raced. Taking on a student from a powerful consortium could be a blessing or a curse. A spy? A liability? Or a genuine opportunity?

"May I meet her first?" Ren asked.

"Of course. She's waiting outside."

Huo clapped his hands. The door opened, and a young woman walked in.

She was nothing like Ren expected. Instead of silk robes and elegant jewelry, she wore simple traveling clothes — practical, unadorned. Her hair was pulled back in a messy bun, and she carried a leather satchel stuffed with scrolls. Her eyes were sharp, intelligent, and judging him.

"So you're the startup cultivator," she said, her voice flat. "You look poor."

"Hu—" Ren started, then caught himself. "I prefer 'frugal.'"

"Frugal means you choose not to spend. Your robes have patches. That's not a choice." She walked around him, inspecting him like a bug. "My father says you're building something new. Prove it."

"Prove it? I don't even know your—"

"Name's Huo Ling. I'm a formation specialist. Third-level, self-taught. I've memorized sixty-seven formation theories and designed twelve original arrays. None of them work yet, but that's because I'm missing practical experience." She stopped in front of him, arms crossed. "You have practical experience. I want it. In exchange, I'll give you the resources to make your little startup work."

Ren looked at Huo. The consortium master was watching with an amused expression, clearly enjoying the show.

"She's... direct," Ren said.

"She gets it from her mother."

"I get it from myself," Huo Ling said. "So. Do we have a deal, or do I go find some other failed cultivator to mentor me?"

Ren laughed despite himself. "You know, most people try to be charming when they ask for a favor."

"Charm is manipulation. I prefer honesty." She stuck out her hand. "Deal?"

Ren took her hand. Her grip was firm, confident. "Deal."

Huo nodded, satisfied. "Excellent. The contract will be finalized by tomorrow morning. I'll transfer the initial investment to your sect's account by noon. And Ling..." He turned to his daughter. "Behave yourself."

"I always behave myself. It's other people who can't handle it."

Ren left the study with a new investor, a new disciple, and a growing sense that he had just made a deal with forces he didn't fully understand.

---

The walk back to the temple was quiet. Mei and Li Chen flanked him, both suspiciously silent.

"So," Mei finally said, "you got the investment."

"Twenty thousand spirit stones. Upfront."

"And a new disciple."

"Huo Ling. Formation specialist."

Li Chen snorted. "She looked like she could stab a man with a formation needle and not even blink."

"She's passionate."

"She's terrifying."

Ren sighed. "She's an opportunity. Her father controls the Azure Pond's southern trade routes. That's access to markets we couldn't dream of reaching on our own."

"And if she's a spy?"

"Then we'll deal with it." Ren's voice hardened. "We've survived worse. Remember, we started with nothing. A few spies won't break us."

They reached the temple gates. The crumbling walls looked even worse in the fading light, but to Ren, they looked like home.

Then he saw the figure standing by the entrance.

It was a woman in white robes, her face obscured by a veil. She held a jade token that glowed with the unmistakable mark of the Celestial Sword Sect.

"Ren Wei," she said, her voice cold. "Elder Song requests your presence. Immediately."

Ren's blood ran cold. The Celestial Sword Sect never sent messengers. They sent enforcers.

"What does Elder Song want?"

"That is between you and the elder. You will come with me. Now."

Mei stepped forward. "He's not going anywhere—"

"It's fine." Ren put a hand on her arm. "If the Celestial Sword Sect wanted me dead, they wouldn't send a messenger. They'd send a sword."

The veiled woman said nothing.

"Take care of the temple," Ren said to Mei and Li Chen. "I'll be back."

He walked toward the veiled woman, his heart pounding.

Behind him, he heard Huo Ling's voice: "Well, this is already more interesting than I expected."

Ren didn't answer. He was too busy wondering if he had just signed his own death warrant.

The veiled woman led him through the city streets, past the mortal districts, past the Jade District, to a secluded pavilion that Ren had never seen before. It was small, unassuming, surrounded by a garden of black flowers.

Elder Song sat on a stone bench, his ancient face weathered by centuries of cultivation. He looked up as Ren approached, his eyes unreadable.

"Ren Wei," he said. "Sit."

Ren sat on the bench opposite him. The veiled woman disappeared into the shadows.

"I'm surprised you came," Elder Song said.

"You sent for me. I came."

"Bold. Foolish, but bold." Elder Song picked a black flower, examining it. "Do you know why I called you here?"

"To threaten me. To warn me. To remind me of my place."

Elder Song smiled — a thin, cold thing. "All of those. And none of those." He crushed the flower in his hand. "I called you here because I have a proposal."

Ren's eyebrows rose. "A proposal?"

"You're building something. Something that threatens the established order. The Celestial Sword Sect cannot allow that to continue."

"Then why aren't you killing me?"

"Because killing you would make you a martyr. And martyrs inspire movements." Elder Song's eyes sharpened. "Instead, I'm offering you a choice. Join us. Bring your sect under the Celestial Sword Sect's banner. You'll have resources, protection, legitimacy. Everything you've been fighting for."

Ren stared at him. "You're offering me a merger."

"I'm offering you survival."

"And if I refuse?"

Elder Song's smile didn't waver. "Then I'll destroy everything you've built. Your temple. Your disciples. Your reputation. I'll make sure the mortal world forgets you ever existed."

The silence stretched between them, broken only by the rustle of black flowers in the wind.

Ren stood up.

"I appreciate the offer," he said, his voice steady. "But I'm going to have to decline."

Elder Song's eyes flickered. "You're making a mistake."

"Maybe." Ren turned to leave. "But it's my mistake to make."

He walked away, feeling the elder's gaze on his back like a sword point.

Behind him, Elder Song's voice carried through the garden: "You have three days, Ren Wei. Three days to reconsider. After that, I'll assume your answer is final."

Ren didn't look back.

He walked through the city streets, past the mortal districts, back to his crumbling temple. Mei and Li Chen were waiting at the gates, their faces pale.

Huo Ling was sitting on the wall, her expression thoughtful.

"So," she said, "what did the big bad sect want?"

Ren looked at her, then at his disciples, then at the temple that was somehow still standing.

"We have three days," he said. "Three days to prepare for war."
