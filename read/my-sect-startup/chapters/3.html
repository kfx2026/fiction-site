📖 My Sect is a Startup by Ren Wei

Chapter 3: The Investor Who Wanted Everything

~1.9k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The morning sun painted the crumbling temple walls in shades of gold and betrayal.

Ren stood in what he now called the "main hall" — a polite term for a room with half a roof and a suspicious number of bird nests — and stared at the letter in his hands. The paper was crisp, expensive, the kind that minor sect leaders used to pretend they were major sect leaders. The ink smelled of jasmine and desperation.

"Who delivered this?" he asked.

Li Chen, his first disciple and the only one who'd shown up before noon, pointed a thumb toward the entrance. "Some kid in green robes. Ran off before I could ask questions. Said it was from the 'Azure Pond Trading Consortium.'"

Ren's blood ran cold.

The Azure Pond. One of the three great trading consortiums of the cultivation world. They didn't just move spirit stones — they moved entire economies. If a sect wanted pills, weapons, or political influence, they went through Azure Pond. If Azure Pond wanted you gone, you disappeared so thoroughly that even your ancestors forgot you existed.

And they were writing to him.

He broke the seal and read.

*Ren Wei, Founder of New Dao Sect,*

*We have reviewed your preliminary business proposal with great interest. The concept of a "startup sect" operating outside traditional cultivation hierarchies presents... unique opportunities.*

*We would like to invite you to a preliminary investment meeting at the Azure Pavilion, Jade District, three days hence. Bring your financials, your projections, and your most persuasive arguments.*

*Success may change everything for you.*

*Failure will not be forgotten.*

*— Consortium Master Huo, Strategic Investments Division*

Ren read the letter three times. Then a fourth, just to make sure the words hadn't rearranged themselves into a threat.

"Boss?" Li Chen leaned over his shoulder, squinting. "Is that good or bad?"

"Both," Ren said slowly. "It's the kind of meeting that either launches a sect or buries it."

"So... good?"

"Li Chen, do you know what 'failure will not be forgotten' means in consortium-speak?"

"No."

"It means if I waste their time, they'll make sure I never cultivate again. They'll blacklist me from every pill seller, every weapon forger, every formation master within a thousand li. I'll be a mortal with a qi deficiency and a very impressive collection of debt."

Li Chen nodded thoughtfully. "So we should probably prepare."

"Prepare?" Ren laughed, the sound hollow in the empty hall. "Li Chen, we have three disciples, a roof that leaks, and exactly seventeen low-grade spirit stones to our name. Our 'financials' are written on a napkin stained with cooking oil. Our 'projections' are optimistic guesses based on the fact that I once read a book about economics."

"Seventeen spirit stones?" Li Chen's eyes widened. "That's... that's almost enough for a decent sword."

"It's almost enough for a decent meal at a roadside inn. It is not enough to impress a consortium that moves more wealth in a day than most sects see in a century."

Ren crumpled the letter, then smoothed it out again. He couldn't afford to be dramatic — the paper was too expensive to waste.

"We need help," he said finally. "Real help. The kind that comes with actual experience and maybe a few connections that don't involve drunk sword masters and gambling addicts."

"Should I fetch Senior Sister Mei?"

"Mei?" Ren blinked. "She's in the kitchen. Probably trying to figure out how to cultivate through soup again."

"No, I mean—" Li Chen pointed toward the back of the hall, where a rickety staircase led to the upper floor. "She's been up there for two hours. Said she was 'meditating on the nature of profit.'"

Ren climbed the stairs, each step creaking like a dying spirit beast. The upper floor was even worse than the main hall — three rooms, two of which had floors, and one that Mei had claimed as her "strategic planning chamber."

She was sitting cross-legged in the center of the room, surrounded by papers. Actual papers. Neatly stacked, organized by color, each one covered in precise handwriting.

"Mei?" Ren stepped inside. "What is all this?"

She looked up, and for a moment, Ren saw something he'd never seen in his first disciple's eyes before: focus. Pure, crystalline focus.

"I've been thinking," she said. "About our business model."

"Our business model is 'don't die' and 'maybe find a way to make money.'"

"No." Mei stood, brushing dust off her robes. "Our business model is *disruption*."

She grabbed a paper covered in diagrams and numbers. Ren recognized her handwriting — messy, chaotic, but somehow always arriving at the right answer.

"The old sects operate on a subscription model," she explained. "Pay for admission, pay for techniques, pay for protection, pay for advancement. Everything costs spirit stones. The bigger the sect, the more they charge, and the more they gatekeep."

"That's... how cultivation works."

"Yes. But it's inefficient." Mei's eyes gleamed. "Think about it. Most cultivators spend decades farming spirit stones just to afford the next breakthrough. They waste time on menial tasks when they could be cultivating. They're stuck in a system that extracts value instead of creating it."

Ren felt a chill run down his spine. "What are you proposing?"

"A marketplace. A platform. Instead of hoarding techniques and resources behind sect walls, we make them accessible. We charge a small fee for access, a percentage for transactions. We become the middleman — the *efficient* middleman."

"That's..."

"Insane? Yes." Mei grinned. "Also potentially revolutionary. If we can create a network where cultivators trade techniques, pills, and services freely — with New Dao Sect taking a small cut — we bypass the old power structures entirely. We become indispensable before they even realize what we're doing."

Ren stared at her. Then at the papers. Then back at her.

"Where did you learn this?"

Mei's grin faltered. "My family. Before... before I left."

She didn't elaborate, and Ren didn't push. Everyone in New Dao Sect had a past they didn't talk about. That was practically a hiring requirement.

"This is brilliant," he said slowly. "But it's also *dangerous*. The old sects won't just ignore us — they'll crush us. We're talking about the Celestial Sword Sect, the Crimson Lotus Palace, the entire established order."

"The established order is fragile," Mei said. "It's built on scarcity and control. If we offer abundance and freedom..."

"We become a target."

"Or we become the future."

Ren looked at the papers again. The numbers made sense. The logic was sound. The risk was astronomical.

"We need that investor meeting," he said finally. "But we don't go in empty-handed. We need something to show them. A proof of concept."

"What kind of proof?"

Ren thought fast. "Our first product. Something small, something we can actually deliver. A technique, a pill formula, a formation — something that proves New Dao Sect can create value."

"We have the chef's recipes," Li Chen said from the doorway. He'd followed them up without Ren noticing. "Senior Sister Fang's cooking. People already say it helps with qi circulation."

"Fang's cooking?" Ren blinked. "She's a chef. An accident cultivator. Her food is good, but it's not a technique."

"It's a *service*," Mei corrected. "And services have value. If we can prove that our methods produce consistent, measurable results — even from unconventional sources — that's proof of concept."

Ren paced the small room. Three disciples, seventeen spirit stones, and a business plan that could get them all killed. It was insane. It was impossible.

It felt right.

"Alright," he said. "Here's the plan. Mei, you're in charge of the proof of concept. I want a presentation ready in three days — something that shows Azure Pond exactly what we're building. Li Chen, you're on logistics. Find out everything you can about the Azure Pavilion, the Jade District, and Consortium Master Huo. I want to know his weaknesses, his preferences, and his deepest fears."

"And you, boss?" Li Chen asked.

Ren smiled, and it wasn't a kind smile. "I'm going to write a business plan so beautiful it makes the Celestial Sword Sect's treasury look like a beggar's bowl."

---

Three days passed like a blade being sharpened.

Ren barely slept. He wrote, rewrote, and burned more paper than he could afford. Mei worked on her presentation, muttering about "disruptive innovation" and "network effects" until even the drunk sword master started avoiding her. Li Chen gathered intelligence the only way he knew how — by drinking with information brokers and gambling with city guards.

By the morning of the meeting, Ren had a plan. A terrible, beautiful, probably-fatal plan.

They left the temple at dawn, the three of them walking through the misty streets of the mortal city below. The Azure Pavilion was in the Jade District, the richest part of the city, where spirit stones flowed like water and cultivators walked with the arrogance of gods.

The pavilion itself was a masterpiece of architectural cultivation. Jade walls that shimmered with embedded formations. A roof of golden tiles that redirected spiritual energy downward. Guards in matching armor who looked like they could kill a Qi Condensation cultivator with their pinky fingers.

Ren straightened his robes — his best set, which meant "the one with the fewest patches" — and walked through the entrance.

The interior was even more opulent. Silk carpets, crystal chandeliers, and a reception desk made from the wood of a thousand-year-old spirit tree. A woman in elegant blue robes sat behind it, her smile professional and empty.

"Ren Wei, New Dao Sect," he said, his voice steady despite his hammering heart. "I have a meeting with Consortium Master Huo."

The woman checked a jade tablet, nodded, and gestured toward a door on the left. "The consortium master will see you now. Your... associates may wait here."

Mei squeezed his arm. "Don't mess this up."

"Thanks for the vote of confidence."

"I mean it. If you mess this up, I'm going to use your corpse as fertilizer for my next business plan."

Ren walked through the door.

The room beyond was a study, lined with bookshelves that held actual tomes — not just cultivation manuals, but histories, philosophies, economics. A man sat behind a desk of polished obsidian, his face unreadable, his robes simple but clearly expensive.

Consortium Master Huo was not what Ren expected. He looked like a retired scholar, not a financial predator. Soft hands, gentle eyes, a faint smile that could mean anything.

"Ren Wei," Huo said, his voice smooth as silk. "Please, sit."

Ren sat. The chair was comfortable. That worried him.

"I've read your proposal," Huo continued. "Fascinating. A 'startup sect,' you call it. Operating outside traditional hierarchies. Using 'agile methodology' and 'minimum viable products.' Tell me, where did you learn these terms?"

"Books," Ren said honestly. "And experience. I worked for the Celestial Sword Sect for five years. I saw how they operate — the waste, the inefficiency, the way they crush innovation because it threatens their power. I thought there had to be a better way."

"And you believe you've found it?"

Ren met his eyes. "I believe I've found *a* way. Whether it's better depends on execution. That's why I'm here. New Dao Sect needs resources to scale. Azure Pond needs new investment opportunities. I think we can help each other."
