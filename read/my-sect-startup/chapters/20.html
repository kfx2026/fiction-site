CHAPTER 20: The Investor Who Already Owned Everything

Ren sat in Consortium Master Huo's study, the silence stretching between them like a blade.

Huo's smile never wavered. It was the kind of smile that made Ren want to check his pockets for missing belongings. The man leaned back in his chair, steepling his fingers, his gaze sharp despite those soft, scholarly features.

"Your proposal is ambitious," Huo said finally. "I'll give you that. But ambition without foundation is just expensive daydreaming. Tell me, Ren Wei — what makes you think your 'startup sect' can survive when the Celestial Sword Sect could crush you before breakfast?"

Ren had prepared for this question. He'd rehearsed answers in front of the mirror until his reflection looked bored.

"Scale," Ren said. "The Celestial Sword Sect is a mountain. Large, powerful, and impossible to move quickly. New Dao Sect is a river. We flow around obstacles, adapt to terrain, and carve our own path. They can't crush what they can't catch."

Huo chuckled. "Poetic. But rivers can be damned, diverted, or dried up. What's your plan when they decide to build a dam?"

"Then we become rain."

The consortium master's eyebrow twitched. It was the first genuine reaction Ren had seen from him. Good. That meant he was paying attention.

"I see you've thought about this," Huo said. "But let me ask you a more practical question. Your proposal mentions 'network effects' and 'scalable cultivation resources.' What are you actually *selling*?"

Ren reached into his robe and pulled out a jade slip, placing it on the obsidian desk. "This."

Huo picked it up, frowning. "A formation array?"

"Not just any formation. A spirit-gathering array optimized for low-quality spiritual veins. Most sects build their arrays to extract maximum qi from high-grade veins. But what about the sects that don't have high-grade veins? The minor families? The independent cultivators?"

Huo's frown deepened. "There's no profit in that market. They have no money."

"They have *potential*," Ren said. "There are thousands of minor cultivation families across the empire. Each one has at least one child with decent talent. But they can't afford the resources to train them. So those children either join major sects as outer disciples — where they're exploited — or they give up cultivation entirely."

"And your array changes this how?"

"It boosts spirit gathering efficiency by three hundred percent on low-grade veins. I've tested it. The conversion rate isn't as high as a premium array on a premium vein, but the *volume* of potential users is massive. Ten thousand mediocre cultivators are worth more than one hundred geniuses."

Huo set the jade slip down. His expression had shifted from polite skepticism to something sharper. Predatory.

"You've tested it? Where?"

"On our temple's vein. It was classified as 'negligible' by the Imperial Survey Office. After three months with my array, it produces enough qi to support five Qi Condensation cultivators simultaneously."

"Show me."

Ren blinked. "Excuse me?"

"Show me," Huo repeated. "I don't invest based on proposals. I invest based on results. Take me to your temple. Let me see this array in action. If it works as you claim, I'll fund your first expansion phase."

Ren's heart raced. This was happening faster than he'd anticipated. He'd expected negotiations, counter-offers, weeks of back-and-forth. Not an immediate field verification.

"That's... unusual for a first meeting," Ren said carefully.

"I'm an unusual investor." Huo stood, smoothing his robes. "I don't like wasting time. If you're legitimate, I want to see it. If you're a fraud, I want to know immediately so I can move on to the next opportunity."

*And if I'm a fraud, you'll probably have me killed*, Ren thought. But he didn't say that.

"Fine. Tomorrow at dawn."

"Excellent." Huo smiled that empty smile again. "I'll bring my evaluation team. Three experts in formations, cultivation, and financial auditing. If everything checks out, we'll sign the investment contract before sunset."

Ren extended his hand. "Deal."

Huo stared at his hand like it was a dead fish. "What are you doing?"

"Shaking hands. It's a mortal custom. Seals agreements."

"I prefer contracts written in blood on spirit parchment, but very well." Huo grasped Ren's hand briefly, his grip cold and firm. "I'll see you tomorrow, Sect Master Ren."

---

When Ren stepped out of the study, Mei was waiting with barely contained fury.

"What did you agree to?" she hissed, grabbing his arm and pulling him into a corner.

"Just a field verification. He wants to see the temple."

"Ren. Ren, look at me." Mei's eyes were wild. "Do you know who Consortium Master Huo actually is?"

"An investor with deep pockets and a suspicious nature?"

"No. He's the *former patriarch of the Azure Pond Consortium's rival faction*. He was overthrown in a coup ten years ago and supposedly went into hiding. And now he's running the entire operation again under a different name."

Ren's blood went cold. "How do you know this?"

"I have contacts. Li Chen's contacts, actually. He found out while drinking with some old information brokers. Huo isn't just an investor — he's a *fixer*. He takes over failing organizations, strips them for parts, and sells what's left. He's called 'The Butcher of Jade Market' because of what he did to a merchant family that refused his terms."

"And you're telling me this *now*?"

"You didn't give me time! You walked in there like a lamb to slaughter!"

Ren pressed his palms against his eyes. This changed everything. Huo wasn't looking for a partnership. He was looking for a target. The temple, the array, the startup — all of it would be evaluated not for investment, but for *acquisition*.

"I need to warn Li Chen and the others," Ren said.

"No time. We need a counter-strategy." Mei's mind was already racing, her fingers tapping against her thigh. "If Huo sees the temple as it is, he'll try to take it. But if we show him something he *wants* more than he wants to steal..."

"Like what?"

"A partnership with actual equity. Give him a percentage of future profits in exchange for protection from other predators. Make him see us as an asset to be nurtured, not harvested."

Ren shook his head. "That's just delaying the inevitable. Once he's inside our operation, he'll find every weakness and exploit it."

"Then we don't let him find weaknesses."

"How? We have a crumbling temple, three misfit disciples, and a business plan written in blood. That's basically an invitation to be exploited."

Mei grabbed his shoulders. "Then we build something he can't exploit. We show him the *vision*, not the reality. We sell him on the future, not the present."

Ren stared at her. "You want to lie to a man called 'The Butcher of Jade Market.'"

"I want to *strategically present information*," Mei said, her eyes burning with manic energy. "There's a difference."

---

They spent the rest of the day preparing. Li Chen was dispatched to gather more intelligence on Huo's past acquisitions. Xiao Bao was told to clean the temple until it sparkled — or at least until it didn't look like it was about to collapse. Old Man Zhang was given strict instructions to stay sober until the meeting was over.

"You're asking the impossible," Zhang muttered.

"I'm asking for four hours. Just four hours without alcohol."

"Fine. But I'm billing you for the headache."

By evening, the temple was as presentable as it was going to get. Ren stood on the main altar platform, staring at the spirit-gathering array he'd installed. It was crude, made from salvaged materials and cheap spirit stones, but it worked. That much was true.

The problem was everything *around* it.

The leaky roof. The cracked walls. The patches of moss growing in corners where qi had condensed into harmless but unsightly spiritual mold.

"This is a disaster," Mei said, appearing beside him. "He's going to walk in and laugh."

"Then we don't let him walk in."

"What?"

Ren turned to her, a plan forming in his mind. "We meet him halfway. There's that abandoned smithy on the main road into town. We'll set up a temporary reception there. Show him the array in a controlled environment. Keep him away from the temple entirely."

Mei's eyes widened. "That's... actually not stupid."

"Thank you."

"It's still stupid. But less stupid than the alternative."

They worked through the night, transporting a section of the array to the smithy, setting up demonstration materials, and rehearsing their pitch. By dawn, Ren was exhausted but ready.

Huo arrived with three experts as promised. The formation master was an old woman with sharp eyes and sharper criticism. The cultivation expert was a scarred man who looked like he'd rather be fighting. The financial auditor was a young woman with a ledger and a smile that promised pain.

"Impressive setup," Huo said, looking around the smithy. "Though I notice you chose a neutral location. Why not the temple?"

Ren smiled, forcing calm into his voice. "The temple is our operational headquarters. I prefer to keep some mysteries until the contract is signed."

Huo's eyes narrowed. "How many mysteries, I wonder."

"Only the pleasant ones."

The demonstration went smoothly. The array worked exactly as Ren claimed. The formation master was grudgingly impressed. The cultivation expert confirmed the qi output was three times higher than standard arrays on equivalent veins. The financial auditor took notes with the enthusiasm of a predator cataloging prey.

By noon, Huo was smiling. *Actually* smiling.

"Ren Wei," he said, "I think we can do business."

"Excellent."

"However..." Huo pulled a scroll from his robe, unrolling it on the smithy's workbench. "I've prepared a contract. I'd like you to sign it before we proceed."

Ren read the contract. His blood ran cold.

The document didn't offer investment. It offered *acquisition*. Huo's consortium would take seventy percent ownership of New Dao Sect. All intellectual property, including the array design, would transfer to Azure Pond. Ren would be retained as "operational manager" — a fancy title for a figurehead.

And buried in the fine print: if Ren refused to sign, the consortium would consider the proposal "a fraudulent misrepresentation of assets" and pursue legal action for damages.

"You planned this from the beginning," Ren said, his voice flat.

"I planned for *every* outcome," Huo replied, his smile widening. "Your array is valuable. Your 'startup' concept is novel. But you're a nobody from a broken sect with three disciples and a collapsing temple. You have no leverage. You have no protection. You have nothing I can't take."

Mei was shaking beside him. Li Chen's hand was inching toward his sword.

"Sign the contract, Ren Wei," Huo said, his voice soft as silk. "Or I'll make sure your 'startup' never gets another investor in the entire Jade Empire."

Ren looked at the contract. Then at his disciples. Then at the array he'd built with his own hands, from scraps and desperation and stubborn hope.

He picked up the brush.

And broke it in half.

"Huo, I reject your offer."

The consortium master's smile vanished. "You're making a mistake."

"No. I'm making a statement." Ren straightened his robes.
