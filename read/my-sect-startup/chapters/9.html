Chapter 9: The Soul Collateral Clause

~1.9k words · 2026-06-22

The negotiation lasted six hours.

Ren emerged from the study with trembling hands, a splitting headache, and the distinct feeling he'd just wrestled a spirit beast and lost. Consortium Master Huo had picked apart every assumption, questioned every number, and demanded proof for every claim. By the end, Ren had conceded on valuation, equity split, and timeline expectations.

But he'd gotten the investment.

Three thousand spirit stones upfront. Access to Azure Pond's distribution network. A guaranteed meeting with the Jade Market's merchant council. In exchange, Huo's consortium would own forty percent of New Dao Sect, with a right of first refusal on any future funding rounds.

Standard terms. Predatory, but standard.

"The contract is binding," Huo had said, sealing it with his blood sigil. "I look forward to seeing what you build, Ren Wei. Don't disappoint me."

Ren didn't miss the threat buried in those words.

---

Mei and Li Chen were waiting outside, surrounded by cups of tea that had long gone cold. Mei jumped to her feet the moment Ren appeared, her eyes scanning his face for answers.

"Well?"

"Three thousand spirit stones. Forty percent equity. Distribution access."

Mei's eyes went wide. "Three *thousand*? That's—that's more than the annual budget of a fourth-rank sect!"

"It's also forty percent of everything we'll ever build." Ren rubbed his temples. "He knows we're desperate. He used it."

Li Chen grunted from his chair. "At least we're not dead. I call that a win."

"Low bar, Senior Brother."

"The only bar that matters."

They left the Azure Pavilion with a storage ring containing the first installment — one thousand spirit stones, delivered immediately — and a jade slip containing the contract terms. Ren felt the weight of it in his pocket like a lead brick.

The walk back to the temple was silent.

---

The next three weeks were a blur of activity.

With actual resources, Ren could finally implement the plans he'd been sketching for months. The temple's foundation was reinforced with spirit stone dust mixed into the mortar. New buildings rose in the outer courtyard — a dormitory for future disciples, a meditation hall, a proper alchemy workshop. Mei hired a team of mortal craftsmen to repair the walls and commissioned a proper sect banner from a silk merchant who specialized in cultivation-grade materials.

Li Chen, for his part, finally had the budget to purchase real intelligence. He returned from his nightly excursions with stacks of reports: sect movements, market prices, political shifts in the mortal kingdoms below. Ren devoured them all, building a mental map of the cultivation world's intricate power structures.

The drunk sword master, Old Wen, spent his days in the meditation hall, staring at a wall. Ren wasn't sure what he was doing, but the man's spiritual pressure had increased noticeably. Either he was actually cultivating, or he'd found a way to meditate while hungover.

Probably both.

The gambling formation expert, Little Feng, had finally paid off his debts and was now designing a defensive array for the temple. He'd promised it would be "the finest formation outside the major sects." Ren had demanded regular progress reports and was actually impressed by the diagrams.

And the chef, Big Huang, had discovered that spirit stones could be ground into powder and used as seasoning. The result was a stir-fry that temporarily boosted qi circulation. Ren immediately added "qi-boosting cuisine" to the sect's service offerings.

They were growing. Slowly, but genuinely.

---

The problem came on a Tuesday.

Ren was reviewing accounts in his new office — a converted storage room with a desk, a chair, and a window that actually opened — when a messenger crane landed on his windowsill. The bird was made of folded paper, animated by a simple formation, and carried a scroll sealed with Azure Pond's insignia.

He broke the seal and read.

*Ren Wei,*

*Per Section 14, Subsection C of our agreement, I am exercising my right to appoint a liaison to oversee operations. Representative Jiang will arrive within the week to conduct an initial audit and establish reporting protocols.*

*I trust you will extend him every courtesy.*

*Huo Feng*
*Consortium Master, Azure Pond*

Ren read the letter three times, each time feeling colder.

Section 14, Subsection C. He'd skimmed that part, assuming it was standard oversight language. But "liaison" in the cultivation business world meant one thing: a spy. An enforcer. Someone who would report every decision, every expense, every whisper back to Huo.

He'd signed away not just equity, but control.

---

Representative Jiang arrived four days later.

He was a thin man with a perpetual sneer, dressed in robes that cost more than Ren's entire sect. His cultivation was at the eighth level of Qi Condensation — respectable, but not threatening. What made him dangerous was the jade token pinned to his chest: the mark of Azure Pond's internal audit division.

"Master Ren," Jiang said, not bothering to bow. "I trust you've prepared the financial records?"

Ren forced a smile. "Of course, Representative Jiang. Right this way."

The tour took two hours. Jiang examined everything — the new buildings, the formation diagrams, the spirit stone accounts. He asked pointed questions about expenditures, questioned the necessity of the qi-boosting cuisine research, and frowned at the number of spirit stones dedicated to "intelligence gathering" (Li Chen's department).

When they reached Ren's office, Jiang produced a scroll and began writing.

"Initial findings," he said, not looking up. "Operational efficiency: moderate. Resource allocation: questionable. Oversight protocols: nonexistent. I'll need weekly reports on all major decisions. Monthly budget reviews. Quarterly strategic assessments."

"That's... a lot of paperwork."

"That's the minimum required to protect Azure Pond's investment." Jiang finally looked up, his eyes cold. "I'm sure you understand. Consortium Master Huo is *very* protective of his assets."

The threat was clear. Follow the rules, or face consequences.

---

That night, Ren gathered his core team in the meditation hall.

Old Wen sat in the corner, polishing his sword with a rag that looked like it had once been a shirt. Little Feng was arranging formation flags in a pattern that might have been strategic or might have been a gambling system. Big Huang had brought samples of his new recipes, which everyone was eating because they were delicious and also because refusing a cultivator-chef's food was considered rude.

Mei and Li Chen sat on either side of Ren, their faces grim.

"We have a problem," Ren said.

"Just one?" Li Chen snorted. "I can name at least seven."

"The liaison. Jiang. He's here to control us."

Mei nodded. "I figured. What are we going to do about it?"

Ren spread his hands. "Legally? Nothing. The contract is binding. If we violate the terms, Huo can seize our assets and dissolve the sect."

"Then we work around him."

"How?"

Mei smiled — a sharp, predatory expression that Ren had learned to fear. "We give him exactly what he asks for. Reports. Audits. Data. But we control what the data says."

"You're suggesting we cook the books?"

"I'm suggesting we *frame* the narrative. There's a difference."

Li Chen leaned forward. "She's right. The mortal merchant houses do it all the time. Report everything in the most favorable light. Obscure your real margins. Make it look like you're spending more than you are on growth, then hide your true profits."

Ren considered this. It was risky. If they were caught, Huo would have every right to destroy them.

But if they weren't caught...

"Fine," he said. "We do it. But we need someone who knows the accounting tricks. Someone who's worked with Azure Pond before."

Everyone looked at Little Feng.

The formation expert blinked. "Why are you all staring at me?"

"You used to work for the Golden Coin Sect," Mei said. "They're Azure Pond's biggest competitor. You must know their methods."

"That was years ago! And I was just a junior auditor!"

"Good enough." Ren stood. "Little Feng, you're our new financial advisor. Congratulations."

"But—"

"You're also getting a raise. Five spirit stones per month."

Little Feng's protests died in his throat. "...Fine. But I'm not responsible if we get audited by the Heavenly Revenue Bureau."

"The what?"

"Never mind. You don't want to know."

---

The next morning, Jiang arrived at Ren's office with a stack of forms.

"Daily expense reports," he said, dropping them on the desk. "I need them by noon."

Ren looked at the stack. It was easily three hundred pages.

"For *one day*?"

"Consortium Master Huo is thorough." Jiang's smile was thin. "I'm sure a sect leader of your caliber can handle a little paperwork."

He left without another word.

Ren stared at the forms. His eye twitched.

"I'm going to kill him," he muttered. "I'm going to kill him and feed his body to Big Huang's experimental recipes."

"Please don't," came a voice from the doorway. Big Huang stood there, holding a steaming bowl. "I have standards."

Ren sighed. "What's that?"

"Breakfast. Soul-reviving porridge. It'll help with the headache you're about to get."

Ren took the bowl. It was surprisingly good.

"Thanks."

"Don't mention it." Big Huang hesitated. "Also, I've been meaning to tell you something."

"What?"

"The qi-boosting stir-fry? I've been testing it on the local mortals. They're showing signs of minor qi deviation."

Ren froze. "What?"

"Nothing serious. Just some dizziness and nosebleeds. But I think the spirit stone powder is too concentrated. I need to dilute it."

"How many mortals have you tested it on?"

"Twenty-three."

"Big Huang!"

"What? They volunteered! I paid them!"

Ren buried his face in his hands. This was going to be a long day.

---

Three weeks passed.

Jiang's presence became a constant, grinding irritation. He demanded reports on everything, questioned every expense, and found fault in every decision. Ren spent more time filling out forms than actually building his sect. Morale was plummeting.

But they were making progress.

Little Feng's defensive array was complete. It wasn't flashy — just a subtle formation that would alert them to intruders and slow down anyone who tried to break in. But it was a start.

Old Wen had finally emerged from his meditation, claiming to have achieved a breakthrough in his sword technique. Ren hadn't seen it yet, but the man's eyes were clearer and his hands steadier than they'd been in months.

Mei had secured three new contracts with local merchants, offering "cultivation consultation services" that were really just her showing up and looking impressive. The merchants didn't care about actual cultivation advice; they just wanted to say they'd hired a cultivator. It was a racket, but it was profitable.

And Big Huang's qi-boosting cuisine had become a minor sensation. The local mortals couldn't get enough of it, despite the occasional nosebleed. Ren had ordered him to dilute the recipe and stop testing on humans, but the damage was done.

For the first time, New Dao Sect was actually working.

Then the letter arrived.

It was delivered by a spirit crow, its eyes glowing with malevolent intelligence. The scroll was sealed with black wax, stamped with a sigil Ren recognized immediately: the Celestial Sword Sect.

He broke the seal with shaking hands.

*Ren Wei,*

*It has come to our attention that your so-called "sect" has been engaging in unauthorized cultivation activities within territory traditionally claimed by the Celestial Sword Sect. Specifically, the development of "qi-boosting cuisine" using spirit stone powder constitutes a violation
