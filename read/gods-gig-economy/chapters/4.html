Chapter 4: The Monkey King's Performance Review

~1.8k words · 2026-06-22

The noodle shop had seen better centuries.

Sun Wukong sat in the back booth, his monkey fur hidden beneath a hoodie three sizes too large. Steam rose from a bowl of beef noodles that he hadn't touched. The broth was congealing into a greasy skin.

"Two point one," he muttered, stabbing at his phone screen. "Two point fucking one."

The delivery app showed his latest rating. A one-star review from customer #4732: "Food arrived late. Monkey was rude."

He hadn't even been rude. He'd handed over the bubble tea with both hands, bowed exactly as the training video instructed, and said "Please enjoy" in perfect Mandarin. The customer had complained that he'd "stared too intensely."

"I wasn't staring," Sun Wukong said to the empty seat across from him. "I was monitoring her aura for demonic possession. That's called being thorough."

The noodle shop owner glanced over from behind the counter. A mortal woman in her sixties, she'd seen stranger things than a man talking to himself at two in the morning. This was Shenzhen, after all.

Sun Wukong's phone buzzed. A message from the platform:

*Your performance has been flagged for review. Please attend a coaching session at the Paradise Cloud Distribution Center at 9 AM tomorrow. Failure to attend will result in account suspension.*

"Account suspension," he repeated, tasting the words like poison. Five hundred years in a mountain prison, and now he was worried about account suspension.

He'd been a god. He'd fought armies. He'd eaten the Peaches of Immortality and drunk from the Jade Emperor's own cup. And now he was going to a coaching session because some mortal didn't like the way he handed over tapioca pearls.

The irony wasn't lost on him. It was just bitter.

---

The Paradise Cloud Distribution Center was a concrete building that looked like every other concrete building in the tech park. Fluorescent lights buzzed overhead. The air smelled of cardboard and desperation.

Sun Wukong stood in a line of thirty delivery drivers, all of them staring at their phones like prayer beads. No one spoke. The only sounds were the notification chimes and the occasional sigh.

A woman in a blue polo shirt walked down the line, handing out name tags. She had the dead-eyed smile of someone who had been trained to be pleasant by a manual.

"Welcome to Performance Optimization," she said. "We're going to help you reach your full potential."

"I'm already at my full potential," Sun Wukong said. "I'm a literal god."

The woman's smile didn't flicker. "That's a great attitude! Let's keep that positivity going into the session."

She handed him a name tag that read: "Delivery Associate #8492."

"I have a name," he said.

"You'll get a name after you reach Platinum Tier," she said. "Now follow me."

The coaching session was held in a room with no windows. Twenty chairs faced a whiteboard covered in motivational phrases: *Every Delivery is a Blessing. Zero Stars is Zero Excuses. Your Rating is Your Destiny.*

The coach was a man in his thirties with a shaved head and the energy of someone who had never questioned anything in his life. He stood at the front of the room, holding a laser pointer like a scepter.

"Welcome, team," he said. "Today we're going to address a common problem: negative customer interactions."

He clicked a remote. The whiteboard changed to show a diagram of a smiling face with arrows pointing to various parts.

"Studies show that customers respond best to associates who display three key traits: warmth, efficiency, and submission."

"Submission?" someone asked.

The coach nodded. "The customer is always right. Even when they're wrong. Especially when they're wrong. Your job is not to be right. Your job is to make them feel right."

Sun Wukong's tail twitched beneath his hoodie. He'd once wrecked heaven because a horse groomer looked at him wrong. This was going to be difficult.

"Let's do a role-play," the coach said. He pointed at Sun Wukong. "You. Up here."

Sun Wukong walked to the front of the room. The other drivers watched with the hollow eyes of people who had already been broken.

"I'll be the customer," the coach said. "You'll be the associate. I'm going to complain that my food arrived cold. What do you say?"

Sun Wukong considered his options. He could transform into a fly and escape through the air vent. He could summon his cloud and rain on this man's entire existence. He could simply reach out and—

"I apologize for the inconvenience," Sun Wukong said, reciting from the training video. "How can I make this right?"

The coach's face lit up. "Excellent! Now with more feeling."

"More feeling."

"Yes. Like you mean it."

"I apologize for the inconvenience," Sun Wukong repeated, and this time he imagined he was apologizing to the Buddha himself. It came out with the right amount of groveling.

"Better," the coach said. "Now let's handle a more difficult scenario. The customer says you were rude. What do you do?"

"I apologize."

"And?"

"I give them a refund code."

"And?"

"I ask them to rate me five stars."

The coach nodded approvingly. "You've been studying. But here's the secret that separates good associates from great ones." He leaned in closer. "You don't just apologize. You over-apologize. You make them feel bad for making you feel bad. It's manipulation through kindness."

Sun Wukong's ears perked up. Manipulation. That he understood.

"Show me," he said.

The coach stepped back. "I'm going to complain that you stared at me."

Sun Wukong dropped to his knees.

The room went silent.

He clasped his hands together and looked up at the coach with eyes that could have moved mountains. "I am so sorry," he said, his voice cracking. "I have failed you. I have failed my ancestors. I have failed the platform. Please, I beg you, give me another chance. My family depends on this job. My mother is sick. My father is dead. I have three siblings to feed."

He was laying it on thick. Completely unnecessary. But the coach's face had gone pale.

"That's— that's enough," the coach said.

"No, please, let me finish," Sun Wukong said, and now he was weeping actual tears. "I will do better. I will be better. I will attend extra coaching sessions. I will work double shifts. I will polish every bubble tea cup until it shines like the face of the Jade Emperor himself."

"Okay, okay, you can stop now—"

"Is it okay? Is it really okay?" Sun Wukong grabbed the coach's hand. "Because I need this job. I need this job like a fish needs water. Like a bird needs wings. Like a monkey needs—"

"Fine! Five stars! You get five stars!"

Sun Wukong's tears vanished. He stood up, brushed off his knees, and smiled. "Thank you. I think I've learned a lot today."

The other drivers stared at him with a mixture of horror and admiration.

The coach wiped his forehead. "Session adjourned. Everyone out."

---

Sun Wukong walked out of the distribution center feeling better than he had in weeks. The coaching had actually been useful. He'd learned something about manipulation that he hadn't known before. It wasn't about being strong. It was about being weak in the right way.

His phone buzzed.

A new order.

Pick up from: Nüwa's Fertility Solutions. Deliver to: The Underworld Gatekeeper.

He frowned. The Underworld Gatekeeper was a demon. A low-level one, but still. They weren't supposed to be ordering food delivery.

He checked the details. The order was for a single item: one bowl of longevity noodles, extra soft, no spicy.

Something about it felt wrong.

He called the number listed for the customer. It rang seven times before a voice answered, raspy and ancient, like bones grinding together.

"Delivery Associate #8492," Sun Wukong said. "I'm confirming your order."

"Yes," the voice said. "The noodles. For the new arrival."

"New arrival?"

"There's a soul coming through," the voice said. "A mortal. Died young. The family wants noodles waiting."

Sun Wukong's hand tightened on his phone. "Which mortal?"

"Does it matter? They all taste the same."

"Give me a name."

The voice laughed, a sound like a shovel hitting gravel. "You really want to know?"

"Tell me."

"All right, monkey. Since you asked so nicely." A pause. "Her name was Li Mei. She was your dispatcher. Killed in a car accident twenty minutes ago."

The world went quiet.

Sun Wukong remembered Li Mei. She was the one who assigned him the worst routes, the ones with impossible delivery windows and no-tip customers. She was the one who sent him to the 47th floor of a building with a broken elevator. She was the one who gave him a two-star review for "slow service" when he'd been hit by a bicycle.

He hated her.

But she was also the one who slipped him extra meal credits when the system flagged him. The one who called him "old monkey" and meant it as a joke. The one who was twenty-three years old and had a picture of her grandmother on her desk.

"Who killed her?" Sun Wukong asked.

"Does it matter?"

"It matters to me."

The voice chuckled. "Check your app, monkey. Check the ratings."

Sun Wukong opened the delivery platform. His overall rating had changed.

1.9.

He'd dropped another point in the last hour.

"The system," he whispered.

"The system," the voice agreed. "Li Mei's last action was approving a batch of bad reviews. She was trying to protect you. The algorithm didn't like that."

The call ended.

Sun Wukong stood in the parking lot of the distribution center, surrounded by delivery scooters and concrete and the buzzing of fluorescent lights. A mortal woman was dead because she tried to help him. A twenty-three-year-old girl with a grandmother's picture on her desk.

And the system was still running.

He looked up at the sky. Somewhere up there, the Jade Emperor was restructuring heaven as a tech startup. Somewhere up there, the gods were unionizing, arguing about benefits and stock options and quarterly growth.

They were all playing the same game.

And the game was eating them alive.

Sun Wukong mounted his scooter. He had a delivery to make. Longevity noodles for a dead girl's welcome party.

But first, he had a call to make.

He dialed a number he hadn't used in three thousand years.

It rang once.

"Monkey King," said a voice on the other end. "I was wondering when you'd call."

"I need a favor," Sun Wukong said.

"I know."

"I need to find out who's running the platform. The real owner. The one who's been harvesting souls through the gig economy."

A pause.

"That's going to cost you."

"I know."

"And I'm going to need something in return."

Sun Wukong gripped the phone. "What?"

The voice laughed, low and dangerous.

"Your staff. The Ruyi Jingu Bang. I want it."
