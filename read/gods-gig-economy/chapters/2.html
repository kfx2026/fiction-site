📖 Gods of the Gig Economy by Wang Cai

Chapter 2: The Monkey King's Performance Review

~1.8k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The delivery scooter smelled like peaches and regret.

Sun Wukong checked his phone for the fifteenth time in three minutes. The Meituan app glowed with notifications, each one a fresh wound to his immortal pride.

*Rating: 2.1*

*Orders completed today: 0*

*Customer complaints pending: 47*

He poked at the screen. "This mortal device has more curses than the underworld."

The scooter sputtered. Its battery indicator blinked red. The Monkey King had named it Cloud Jr., after his original cloud somersault technique, but Cloud Jr. couldn't somersault anywhere. It could barely make it up hills.

His phone buzzed. A new order.

*Delivery: One bowl of spicy beef noodles*
*From: Old Chen's Noodle House*
*To: Apartment 4B, Prosperity Tower*
*Pay: 8 yuan*
*Time limit: 28 minutes*

Eight yuan. Eight. In his prime, he'd demanded tribute of peaches from the heavenly orchard, and those were worth more than any mortal currency. Now he was expected to haul noodles across three districts for less than the cost of a bus ticket.

The Monkey King grabbed his insulated bag. "Fine. Fine. This old monkey still has dignity."

---

Old Chen's Noodle House sat wedged between a pawn shop and a massage parlor. Steam billowed from the kitchen window, carrying the smell of chili oil and star anise. A dozen delivery drivers clustered outside, all scrolling through their phones, all wearing the same fluorescent green vests.

The Monkey King pushed through them.

"Move, mortals. I have an urgent delivery."

Nobody moved.

"Nice rating," someone muttered.

Sun Wukong's ears twitched. His golden eyes scanned the crowd until they landed on a skinny teenager with acne and a smug expression. The kid's vest was clean. His scooter was brand new. His phone showed a 4.8 rating.

"Say that again, little insect."

"I said nice rating." The kid grinned. "How do you even get a 2.1? You'd have to try to be that bad."

The other drivers laughed. The Monkey King felt something hot bubble in his chest—the same fire that had once burned the heavenly peach orchard, that had shattered the pillars of the Dragon King's palace, that had made the gods themselves tremble.

He could destroy this boy. One finger. One thought. The Monkey King had once fought eighty thousand heavenly soldiers and won. This mortal was nothing.

But the mortal wasn't the problem.

The problem was the system.

He remembered the Jade Emperor's words from last week's celestial staff meeting: *"No violence, Wukong. We're trying to go public. Investor confidence is fragile."*

So the Monkey King did something harder than fighting eighty thousand soldiers.

He walked away.

---

Old Chen handed him the noodle order without meeting his eyes. "Try not to spill it this time."

"I never spill anything."

"Last week you delivered soup upside down."

"That was a creative choice."

"The customer was very creative with their complaint. Four paragraphs."

The Monkey King stuffed the noodles into his insulated bag and mounted Cloud Jr. The scooter groaned beneath him. He twisted the throttle and lurched onto the street, weaving through traffic with the kind of aggressive recklessness that had made him famous across three realms and had currently earned him seventeen near-death experiences among mortals.

The GPS screamed directions. *Turn left. Turn right. You have arrived.*

He'd arrived at a building under construction.

The Monkey King stared at the half-finished concrete skeleton. Scaffolding covered the exterior. No apartment 4B existed here. Possibly no apartment 4 existed anywhere.

He checked the order again. *Prosperity Tower.* This was definitely not Prosperity Tower.

His phone buzzed.

*Customer: Where are you?*

He typed back: *Where are YOU?*

*Customer: Prosperity Tower. Apartment 4B. Been waiting 20 minutes.*

He took a photo of the construction site and sent it.

*Customer: That's not Prosperity Tower.*

*Monkey King: Obviously.*

*Customer: Are you lost?*

*Monkey King: The map is wrong. The map is always wrong. The map is a lying piece of—*

*Customer: I'm reporting you.*

The order disappeared. His rating dropped by 0.1.

Sun Wukong sat on his scooter, holding cold noodles, watching dust blow across the construction site. Thirty-five hundred years ago, he'd declared himself equal to heaven. Now heaven had called his bluff.

---

He found the real Prosperity Tower forty minutes later, after asking six strangers and getting into an argument with a security guard who didn't believe a delivery driver would know the Monkey King's true name.

The customer didn't answer the door.

The noodles were cold anyway.

The Monkey King ate them himself, sitting on the curb, using the plastic chopsticks with the same hands that had once wielded the Ruyi Jingu Bang. The noodles were good. Old Chen knew his chili oil.

A shadow fell over him.

"You're Sun Wukong."

He didn't look up. "I'm a delivery driver."

"The Great Sage Equal to Heaven. The Monkey King. The one who fought the heavenly army and won."

"And now I eat cold noodles on a curb." He bit a mouthful of noodles. "What do you want, demon? I can smell you from here."

The shadow sat down beside him. In the dim streetlight, he took the form of a middle-aged man in a business suit—too sharp, too polished, with eyes that flickered red when he blinked.

The Monkey King recognized the aura. "White Bone Spirit. I thought we killed you. Three times, if I recall."

"You killed three of my manifestations." The demon smiled. "I've had time to regenerate. And to adapt."

"Adapt to what? Delivering noodles?"

"To the economy."

The Monkey King stopped eating. He turned to look at the demon properly. White Bone Spirit had always been cunning—that was her thing, her whole shtick. Luring travelers, stealing their life force, shapeshifting into beautiful women or helpful grandmothers. She was a classic.

But this version of her smelled different. She smelled like money.

"I don't understand," the Monkey King said.

"Of course you don't. You're still playing the old game." She pulled out her phone. "Do you know how many delivery apps there are in this city? Thirty-seven. Thirty-seven different platforms. All competing for drivers. All bleeding money. All desperate."

"Bleeding how?"

"Venture capital." The demon's eyes gleamed. "Investors pour money into these apps, hoping they'll grow big enough to buy out the competition. The apps burn through cash by subsidizing deliveries, offering discounts, giving drivers bonuses. None of them are profitable."

"Then why do it?"

"Because the goal isn't profit. The goal is market share. And while they're hemorrhaging money, they're also hemorrhaging something else." She leaned closer. "Data. Personal information. Location history. Payment details. Biometrics."

The Monkey King frowned. "And demons want this data because..."

"Because we've been doing this wrong for three thousand years. We've been hunting humans one by one. A soul here, a life force there. It's inefficient." She gestured at the city around them. "But this? This is mass harvesting. Every time someone orders food, they give us their address. Every time someone rates a driver, they give us their emotional state. Every time someone pays with their phone, they give us their financial history."

"You're using the apps."

"We own the apps." She smiled wider. "Ten of the thirty-seven delivery platforms are demon-run. We've got investors from three different hells. We're scaling."

The Monkey King set down his noodles. "And you're telling me this because..."

"I'm recruiting."

"For what?"

White Bone Spirit laughed—a sound like bones rattling in a dry wind. "For the union, of course. We're organizing the drivers. The demons. The gods. Everyone who's been ground down by this system. You think the Heavenly Court broke you? Wait until you see what the venture capitalists do."

She stood up, brushed off her suit, and handed him a business card.

The card was black. In silver letters: *Mythic Logistics Workers United.*

"Think about it, Monkey King. You've been fighting heaven for millennia. But heaven's not your enemy anymore."

"Then who is?"

"The quarterly earnings report."

She walked away, heels clicking against the pavement, and disappeared around a corner.

The Monkey King stared at the card. Then at his phone. Then at the empty noodle container.

His rating dropped another 0.1.

---

He was still sitting on the curb when his phone rang.

"Wukong." The voice was tired, regal, ancient. "We need to talk."

The Monkey King recognized it immediately. "Jade Emperor. To what do I owe the pleasure of heaven's most overworked bureaucrat?"

"Something's happened." A pause. "One of our gods is missing."

"Which one?"

"Guan Yu. He didn't show up for his security shift tonight. His apartment is empty. His phone is off."

The Monkey King felt something cold settle in his chest. "Missing how?"

"We don't know. But we found something at his place." Another pause. "A business card."

"What kind of business card?"

The Jade Emperor's voice dropped low. "From a company called SoulCycle Logistics. It's one of the delivery apps."

"I just heard about them."

"Then you know they're owned by demons."

The Monkey King looked at the black card in his hand. *Mythic Logistics Workers United.*

"I know," he said.

"Guan Yu's disappearance happened three hours after he signed up as a driver for one of their platforms. I need you to find him."

"Why me?"

"Because you're the only one who's spent enough time on the ground to understand how these apps work. And because you're the only one the demons might actually fear."

The Monkey King stood up. He tucked the black card into his pocket, next to his heart.

"I'll find him."

"Wukong." The Jade Emperor hesitated. "Be careful. The demons aren't playing the old game anymore. None of us are."

The call ended.

The Monkey King mounted Cloud Jr. The scooter's battery was dying. His rating was still terrible. Somewhere out there, Guan Yu was missing, probably trapped inside a demon-run delivery app, and the Monkey King had no idea where to start looking.

His phone buzzed one more time.

A new order.

*Delivery: One mystery box*
*From: SoulCycle Logistics*
*To: The driver who opens this*
*Pay: 1000 yuan*
*Note: Your friend says hello.*

The address for pickup was a warehouse in the industrial district. The warehouse where Guan Yu had last been seen.

The Monkey King smiled—a sharp, dangerous smile that had made heaven tremble.

"Fine," he said, and twisted the throttle. "Let's see what hell has cooked up."

Cloud Jr. sputtered to life.

Then died completely.

The Monkey King looked at the dead scooter, the empty battery, the 2.1 rating, the black business card, and the infinite abyss of a demon-run gig economy that was about to swallow him whole.

He started walking.
