📖 Silver Fang Academy by Luna Grey

Chapter 4: The Moon's Secret Library

~1.5k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The basement corridor smelled like wet stone and old blood.

Maya pressed her back against the cold wall, counting her breaths. Three floors above, the dining hall would be filling with students for the morning meal. They'd notice her empty seat. Some would smirk. Others wouldn't care.

But Maya couldn't eat. Not after what she'd found in the abandoned records room last night.

A silver locket. Tarnished and warped, hidden behind a loose stone in the foundation wall. Inside, a photograph of a woman with Maya's eyes — the same flecks of amber in the irises, the same stubborn set to her jaw.

Her mother.

The woman she'd been told died in childbirth. The woman who supposedly had no wolf, no connection to the clans.

The locket told a different story.

Maya pulled it from her pocket now, running her thumb over the engraved initials on the back. E.V.

Elena Vance.

A name that appeared in none of the academy's records. A name the librarian had claimed didn't exist when Maya had asked yesterday afternoon.

"You should be studying pack hierarchy, not genealogy," the librarian had snapped, her eyes flashing yellow. "Some histories are buried for good reason."

The way she said it — the tremor beneath the words — told Maya everything. Elena Vance was a secret. And secrets at Silver Fang Academy had teeth.

"Maya."

The voice came from the shadows to her left. Low. Male. Familiar.

Damien Blackwood stepped into the dim light, his silver eyes catching the basement's single flickering bulb. The Alpha heir. The boy who'd watched her stumble through her first transformation without offering help. The boy who'd said nothing when the purebloods mocked her wolf form.

"What do you want?" Maya's voice came out harder than she intended.

He held up his hands. "Peace."

"Peace doesn't exist at this school."

"Maybe not. But information does." He took a step closer, close enough that she caught his scent — pine and rain and something wild underneath. "I know what you found."

Maya's blood went cold. "The locket."

"The librarian reported you. Said you were asking questions about an unregistered she-wolf." Damien's jaw tightened. "The Headmaster knows. He's calling a council meeting tonight."

"About me?"

"About your mother."

The words hit like a physical blow. Maya gripped the locket so hard the edges bit into her palm.

"Why would they care about a dead woman?"

Damien looked away. For a moment, he seemed almost human — unsure, uncomfortable, fighting some internal war. When he spoke again, his voice dropped to barely a whisper.

"Because Elena Vance didn't die in childbirth. She was executed."

The corridor tilted. Maya grabbed the wall to steady herself.

"Executed for what?"

"Treason." Damien met her eyes. "She was the first hybrid — half-wolf, half-human. The council sentenced her to death when she refused to give up her human husband. Your father."

"My father died before I was born."

"That's what they told you." Damien's expression softened — almost pitying. "He's alive, Maya. And he's been looking for you for eighteen years."

The words didn't make sense. Couldn't make sense. Maya had grown up in foster homes, shuffled between families who couldn't handle a child with night terrors and too-sharp teeth. She'd learned survival young — how to hide the wolf, how to fake weakness, how to disappear.

And all that time, her father had been searching?

"How do you know this?"

"Because my mother was on the council that condemned Elena." Damien's voice cracked. "Because I read the classified records. Because I've been trying to find you since I was twelve years old."

Maya's wolf surged beneath her skin, claws threatening to break through. "You knew about me? Before I came here?"

"I knew a hybrid existed. I didn't know where until the admissions office flagged your bloodwork." He stepped closer, close enough that she could see the guilt written in every line of his face. "I'm the reason you were admitted. I pulled strings, called in favors, threatened my father's political allies. I needed you here."

"Needed me here for what?"

"To finish what your mother started."

The locket burned against Maya's palm. She wanted to run. Wanted to shift and tear through the basement walls and never stop running.

But the wolf inside her — the half that had always felt incomplete, always hungering for something she couldn't name — that part of her wanted answers.

"What did my mother start?"

Damien glanced down the corridor, checking for listeners. When he turned back, his eyes held something new. Fear.

"She was building an army. A hybrid army. Wolves who wouldn't be bound by pack law, who could move between worlds, who could challenge the council's authority." He swallowed hard. "She was three months from succeeding when they caught her."

Three months. Maya did the math in her head, the dates lining up like puzzle pieces. Three months before her birth. Three months before the execution.

"Then what?"

"Then the council destroyed everything she'd built. Killed her followers, burned her research, erased her name from every record." Damien's voice dropped to barely audible. "Except one. A hidden library, buried beneath the academy's original foundation. Your mother's library. It's been sealed for eighteen years."

Maya's heart hammered. "Where?"

"The Headmaster's office. Behind the portrait of his grandfather. There's a passage that leads down."

"And you expect me to just walk in there?"

"I expect you to be smart about it." Damien pulled a folded paper from his jacket. A map, hand-drawn, the ink fresh. "There's a council meeting tonight. The Headmaster will be occupied for at least two hours. That's your window."

Maya took the map, her fingers brushing his. A spark of heat traveled up her arm — the wolf recognizing something in him, some kinship she didn't understand.

"Why are you helping me?"

Damien's smile held no warmth. "Because my mother sat on that council. Because I've spent my whole life knowing my family helped murder an innocent woman." He stepped back, the shadows reclaiming him. "And because the moon doesn't forget debts, Maya. Neither do I."

He disappeared into the darkness before she could respond.

Maya stood alone in the basement corridor, clutching the locket in one hand and the map in the other. Above her, the academy hummed with morning activity — students laughing, doors slamming, the distant clatter of plates.

They had no idea what lurked beneath their feet.

She unfolded the map, tracing the route with her finger. Through the kitchens, past the boiler room, up a service staircase that had been bricked over and reopened. A path designed for ghosts.

For the daughter of a traitor.

The dining hall bells rang, signaling the end of breakfast. Maya folded the map and tucked it into her pocket next to the locket.

She had two hours. Two hours to find her mother's secrets, to unearth the truth that had been buried for eighteen years.

Two hours to decide if she was ready to become the enemy the council had always feared.

As she climbed the stairs toward the main floor, Maya felt something shift inside her. The wolf that had always felt like a curse, a burden, a thing to hide — it stretched and yawned and looked at the world through new eyes.

Not a curse.

A weapon.

And tonight, she would learn how to wield it.

The portrait of Headmaster Aldric's grandfather stared down at her with painted eyes that seemed to follow her every move. Maya checked her watch. 7:47 PM. The council meeting had started thirteen minutes ago.

She pressed her ear to the oak paneling beside the portrait. Silence.

The map said the trigger was hidden in the painting's frame — a small indent where the gilded wood had been carved with a crescent moon. Maya found it easily, her fingers tracing the symbol.

She pressed.

Nothing happened.

She pressed harder, pushing at different angles, desperation clawing up her throat. The portrait remained still, the wall solid.

"Think," she whispered to herself. "Think like Elena."

Her mother had been a hybrid. A creature of two worlds, bound by neither. The lock — if it could be called a lock — would require something similarly split.

Maya pulled out the locket, holding it up to the painted moon on the frame.

The silver began to glow.

A soft clicking sound came from behind the portrait, followed by the groan of ancient mechanisms waking from a long sleep. The painting swung inward, revealing a narrow staircase descending into absolute darkness.

Maya's wolf stirred, sensing something ancient below. Something that smelled like moonlight and blood and home.

She took the first step down.

The darkness swallowed her whole.

And somewhere in the depths of the academy, a door that hadn't opened in eighteen years creaked wide, inviting the daughter of Elena Vance to finally claim her inheritance.
