CHAPTER 16: The Song Before the Fall

The headmaster's study smelled of burning paper and old secrets.

Bai Su stood before the massive oak desk, her hands still damp from the frantic washing she had done in the courtyard well. The water had run clear, but she could still feel the blood beneath her nails, phantom and accusing.

"Miss Bai." Headmaster Wei did not look up from the scroll unrolled before him. His brush moved in precise, measured strokes, each character forming with the careful precision of a man who knew exactly what he was writing. "I trust your exploration of the bell tower was... enlightening."

The words hung in the air like a trap waiting to spring.

"I found a ghost," Bai Su said carefully. "A scholar. He claims he was murdered."

"Claims?" The headmaster finally raised his eyes. They were ancient, those eyes, carrying weights that no living man should bear. "You doubt him?"

"I doubt everything in this academy."

A thin smile crossed the headmaster's face. "Good. Doubt is the first lesson of scholarship. The second is knowing when to set doubt aside." He set down his brush and folded the scroll with deliberate care. "The scholar you met—did he give you his name?"

"No. He disappeared before I could ask."

"Disappeared." The headmaster's smile widened. "Or did you disappear? In the Wutong Academy, the distinction is often meaningless."

Bai Su felt a chill crawl up her spine. "What do you mean?"

"I mean that you have been walking between worlds since the moment you stepped through our gates." He rose from his desk, his robes rustling like dried leaves. "The living see the academy as it is. The dead see it as it was. But you, Miss Bai—you see both. Do you know how rare that is?"

"I see ghosts."

"You see *truth*." He walked to the window, his back to her. "The bell chamber. The footprint. The blood on your hands. Tell me—when you touched that window, did you feel the glass? Or did you feel the air on the other side?"

Bai Su's breath caught.

She remembered. Her hand reaching out. The glass parting like water. The wind from the courtyard blowing against her face, even though the window had been closed.

"I felt... both."

"Because you were both." The headmaster turned, and for just a moment, Bai Su saw something flicker in his eyes—not age, but something older. Something that had been waiting. "There is a reason you were admitted to this academy, Bai Su. It was not your scores. It was not your recommendations. It was the simple fact that you could see what others cannot."

"What do you want from me?"

"Nothing." He returned to his desk. "Everything. The truth of what happened to Chen Feng. The identity of the killer. The reason why the ghost students are growing bolder." He picked up his brush again. "Find these things, and you will have your answers."

"And if I don't?"

"Then you will become one of the ghosts you seek to understand." He dipped the brush in ink. "The academy keeps its secrets, Miss Bai. It always has. The only question is whether you will leave them, or become one of them."

---

Bai Su left the headmaster's study with more questions than answers.

The corridors of Wutong Academy stretched before her, empty and echoing. The living students were in their classrooms, the dead students in their shadows. She walked alone, her footsteps the only sound in the silence.

But she was not alone.

She felt them watching. The ghosts. Dozens of them, hundreds, pressed against the walls like wallpaper made of eyes. They did not move. They did not speak. But they saw her, and she saw them, and the weight of that mutual recognition was crushing.

"Miss Bai."

She turned. A boy stood at the end of the corridor, no older than fourteen, his face pale and his robes stained with what looked like ink. But it wasn't ink. It was too dark, too thick, too red.

"Who are you?"

"My name is Liu Wei." He bowed, formal and precise. "I was a student here. I died in the third year of my studies."

"How?"

"The same way Chen Feng died." He looked up, and Bai Su saw that his eyes were hollow, empty sockets filled with darkness. "I fell from the bell tower. They said it was an accident. They said I slipped."

"Did you?"

He smiled, and it was the saddest smile Bai Su had ever seen. "I was pushed."

The words hit her like a physical blow. "Who pushed you?"

"I don't remember." He touched his head, where the dark stain was thickest. "I hit the ground first. The memory came second. By then, it was too late."

"Too late for what?"

"To tell anyone." He stepped closer, and Bai Su forced herself not to retreat. "There are three of us, Miss Bai. Three students who fell from the bell tower in the past ten years. Chen Feng was the fourth. But we are not the only ones."

"Who else?"

"The drowned scholar in the library. The poison victim in the dining hall. The strangled girl in the east wing." He counted them off on his fingers, each name a nail in a coffin. "Seven ghosts, Miss Bai. Seven students who died before their examinations. Seven souls trapped in this academy, waiting for a justice that will never come."

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because you are the first one who can hear us." His hollow eyes met hers. "The first one who can see. The first one who can *do* something."

"What can I do? I'm just a student."

"No." He shook his head. "You are more than that. You have always been more than that." He reached out, and his hand passed through her arm, leaving a trail of cold. "The blood on your hands, Miss Bai. It was not Chen Feng's."

"Then whose was it?"

He opened his mouth to answer—

The bell rang.

Once. Twice. Three times.

Liu Wei's face contorted in pain. His form flickered, wavered, began to dissolve like smoke in wind.

"The bell," he gasped. "It's calling me. It's calling all of us."

"What's happening?"

"The killer." His voice was barely a whisper now. "They're taking another one."

"Who? Where?"

But Liu Wei was already gone, dissolving into the shadows like he had never been there at all.

Bai Su ran.

---

The courtyard was empty when she arrived.

The bell tower loomed above her, silent and watchful. The moon hung in the sky like a dead eye, casting pale light across the stones. Nothing moved. Nothing breathed.

But something had changed.

The air was different. Thicker. Heavier. It pressed against her skin like wet cloth, making it hard to breathe. The shadows seemed longer than they should be, stretching and reaching like grasping hands.

"Show yourself," she called out. "I know you're here."

Silence.

Then, a voice. Sweet and familiar, singing that old folk song she had heard in the bell chamber. It came from everywhere and nowhere, wrapping around her like a lover's embrace.

*"The moon is high, the wind is cold,*
*The scholar's tale is left untold.*
*He climbs the tower, counts the bells,*
*And in the dark, his spirit dwells."*

Bai Su's blood ran cold.

The song stopped.

A figure stepped out of the shadows, and Bai Su's heart stopped with it.

It was a girl. Young, no older than sixteen, with long black hair and a face that might have been beautiful once. But her skin was pale as paper, her eyes dark as ink, and her smile—

Her smile was the smile of someone who had already won.

"Hello, Bai Su." The girl's voice was the song, sweet and terrible. "I've been waiting for you."

"Who are you?"

"Don't you recognize me?" The girl tilted her head, and her hair fell away to reveal a scar that ran from her temple to her jaw. "We've met before. Many times. In the bell tower. In the library. In your dreams."

"Impossible."

"Is it?" The girl stepped closer, and Bai Su saw that she left no footprints in the dust. "You've been seeing me since the day you arrived. You just didn't know it."

"The shadow in the bell chamber. That was you."

"Very good." The girl clapped her hands, a mockery of applause. "I was starting to think you would never figure it out. But you did. You found the footprint. You felt the blood. You asked the right questions."

"What do you want?"

"Want?" The girl laughed, and it was the sound of breaking glass. "I want what every ghost wants, Bai Su. I want to live again. I want to feel the sun on my skin. I want to taste food, to breathe air, to *exist*."

"And you're killing people to do it."

"Borrowing." The girl's smile widened. "I'm borrowing their life qi. Just a little at first. Enough to feel alive for a few hours. But soon..." She looked at her hands, translucent and glowing. "Soon, I'll have enough to be human again."

"And then what? You'll just walk out of here?"

"No." The girl's eyes met hers, and they were ancient, hungry, desperate. "Then I'll find the person who killed me. And I'll make them pay."

"Who killed you?"

The girl's smile faltered. For just a moment, Bai Su saw something flicker in those dark eyes—pain, rage, sorrow, all mingled together like blood and water.

"I don't remember."

"Then how do you know you were killed?"

"Because I remember falling." The girl's voice dropped to a whisper. "I remember the wind. I remember the ground rushing up to meet me. I remember the sound of my own bones breaking." She touched her scar. "And I remember someone pushing me."

"Who?"

"I told you. I don't remember."

"Then why are you killing innocent people?"

"Innocent?" The girl laughed again, but this time it was bitter, hollow. "No one in this academy is innocent. Not the students. Not the teachers. Not even you, Bai Su."

"What do you mean?"

The girl stepped closer, close enough that Bai Su could see her own reflection in those dark, dead eyes.

"You've been here before, Bai Su. You just don't remember it."

"That's impossible."

"Is it?" The girl reached out and touched Bai Su's cheek. Her hand was cold, so cold it burned. "Then explain the blood on your hands. Explain the footprint that matched your shoe. Explain why you can see us, hear us, *touch* us, when no one else can."

Bai Su had no answer.

"You're one of us, Bai Su." The girl's voice was a whisper now, soft and sad. "You just haven't died yet."

The bell rang again.

Once. Twice. Three times.

The girl's form flickered. "I have to go. The bell calls me."

"Wait—"

"Find me, Bai Su." The girl began to fade, dissolving into the moonlight like morning frost. "Find me before I find you. Or I promise you—the next time we meet, you won't walk away."

And then she was gone.

Bai Su stood alone in the courtyard, her heart pounding, her mind racing.
