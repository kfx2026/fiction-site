CHAPTER 12: The Shadow in the Scroll

The headmaster's office was smaller than Bai Su expected.

She had imagined something grand—towering shelves of rare books, calligraphy scrolls worth more than a scholar's lifetime salary, perhaps a desk carved from the wood of a thousand-year-old tree. Instead, she found a cramped room in the eastern administration building, its walls lined with examination scrolls stacked so high they threatened to topple. A single oil lamp flickered on a plain wooden desk, casting long shadows that danced like living things.

Headmaster Lu sat behind the desk, his face half-hidden in darkness. He was an old man, older than Bai Su had realized—his skin papery thin, his eyes sunken but sharp, like a hawk watching from a great height. He did not rise when she entered.

"Miss Bai." His voice cracked like dry leaves. "Please, sit."

She sat. The chair was uncomfortable, deliberately so. She wondered if he had chosen it that way to keep his visitors from lingering.

"I understand you've been investigating the incident in the bell chamber," he said.

Not a question. An accusation wrapped in silk.

"I was curious about the broken window," Bai Su said carefully. "It seemed strange that no one had mentioned it."

"Strange." Headmaster Lu smiled, but there was no warmth in it. "Strange is a kind word for what happens in this academy. I prefer the word 'dangerous.'" He leaned forward, the lamp light catching his eyes. "You saw something in that tower, didn't you? Something you couldn't explain."

Bai Su's hands were still clean, but she could feel the phantom blood on her palms. She kept them flat on her knees.

"I saw a ghost," she said.

The headmaster did not flinch. "Of course you did. This academy is full of them. Half our students are dead, Miss Bai. Did you think that was a metaphor?"

"I saw the ghost of a boy who jumped from that window. He said his name was Chen Feng."

The headmaster's expression flickered—just for a moment, just enough for Bai Su to catch it. Recognition. And something else. Fear.

"Chen Feng," he repeated, the name falling from his lips like a stone. "You saw him?"

"He spoke to me. He said he was pushed."

The silence that followed was thick enough to choke on. The headmaster stared at her, his ancient eyes unreadable. Then he reached into his desk drawer and pulled out a scroll, yellowed with age, tied with a red ribbon that had faded to brown.

"I was going to burn this," he said. "I should have burned it years ago. But I couldn't." He untied the ribbon with trembling fingers. "I couldn't destroy the last proof that I failed him."

He unrolled the scroll and turned it toward Bai Su.

It was an examination paper. The ink had bled in places, smeared by what looked like water stains. But the handwriting was still legible—precise, elegant, the work of a scholar who had practiced for years.

At the top, in bold characters: Chen Feng.

And below, a single sentence repeated over and over, filling the entire scroll, line after line, until the characters blurred together:

*"I did not jump. I did not jump. I did not jump. I did not jump—"*

Bai Su's breath caught in her throat. The scroll was covered in the desperate plea of a dead boy, written in his own blood.

"When did you find this?" she asked.

"Three days after he died. In his dormitory, hidden beneath his bed. The servants were going to throw it away, but I recognized his handwriting." The headmaster's voice cracked. "I knew he hadn't jumped. I knew it the moment I saw his body. But I did nothing."

"Why?"

"Because the alternative was worse." He looked up at her, and for the first time, Bai Su saw the weight of years pressing down on him. "Chen Feng was not the first student to die in this academy. He was not the second. He was not even the tenth. Every year, a student dies—falls from a window, drowns in the pond, chokes on their own food. Accidents, we called them. But they were not accidents."

"What were they?"

"Murders." The word hung in the air like smoke. "Murders committed by something that has been feeding on this academy for longer than I have been alive. A ghost that does not just haunt this place—it owns it."

Bai Su's mind raced. The ghost scholar in the bell chamber had warned her. *They'll become something worse.* But he hadn't told her the full truth.

"Who is it?" she demanded. "Which ghost is killing the students?"

The headmaster shook his head. "I don't know. I've spent forty years trying to find out, and I have failed. Every time I got close, the trail ended in death." He reached across the desk and grabbed her wrist, his grip surprisingly strong. "You must leave this academy, Miss Bai. Tonight. Before the killer finds you."

"I can't leave. The midterms are in three weeks—"

"To hell with the midterms!" He slammed his fist on the desk, making the lamp jump. "Do you think I care about examination scores when children are dying? I have watched seventeen students die in my time as headmaster. Seventeen. And I have done nothing to stop it because I was too afraid of what I would find."

Bai Su pulled her wrist free. "Then why are you telling me this now?"

"Because you saw Chen Feng." The headmaster's voice dropped to a whisper. "No one has seen him in ten years. No one has heard his voice. But you did. That means something. That means the veil is thinning, and the killer is getting stronger." He looked at her with desperate eyes. "You are the only person in this academy who might be able to stop what is coming."

"And what is coming?"

The headmaster opened his mouth to answer—

The lamp went out.

Darkness flooded the room, absolute and complete. Bai Su heard the headmaster gasp, heard the scrape of his chair as he stood. Then she heard something else. A wet, tearing sound. A gurgle.

"Headmaster Lu?"

No answer.

She fumbled for the lamp, her fingers finding the glass, the wick. She lit it with shaking hands.

The headmaster was still standing. But his throat was open, a dark slit from ear to ear, and blood was pouring down his robes in rhythmic pulses. His eyes were wide, his mouth moving soundlessly, forming words that would never be spoken.

Behind him, a figure stood in the shadows.

Bai Su saw only glimpses—a flash of pale skin, a strip of dark cloth, a hand that held a blade still dripping red. And then the figure stepped back, melting into the darkness of the corner, and was gone.

"Headmaster!"

She caught him as he fell, his body heavy and warm against hers. Blood soaked through her robes, hot and wet, and she pressed her hands against his throat, trying to stop the flow, knowing it was useless.

His lips moved again, and this time, sound came out—a whisper so faint she had to lean in to hear it.

"The scroll," he breathed. "The names. All the names..."

"Where? Where are the names?"

But his eyes had already gone glassy, staring at something she could not see. His chest rose once, twice, and then stopped.

Bai Su laid him gently on the floor. Her hands were covered in blood—real blood this time, warm and terrifying. She looked at the scroll still lying on the desk, Chen Feng's desperate message smeared across its surface.

She grabbed it and ran.

The halls of the academy were dark, the lanterns unlit, the windows showing only the black of night. Bai Su's footsteps echoed as she sprinted, her breath ragged, her mind a storm of images: the headmaster's throat opening, the figure in the shadows, the whisper of an old folk song that followed her down the stairs.

She burst through the main doors into the courtyard.

The bell tower loomed above her, its windows dark, its rope hanging still. But the bell was moving—swaying gently, as if someone had just rung it.

She turned to run toward the dormitory—

And stopped.

The courtyard was full of students.

They stood in perfect silence, faces pale, eyes fixed on her. They were not moving. They were not breathing. They were simply *watching*, rows upon rows of them, filling the space between the buildings like statues in a garden of the dead.

Among them, Bai Su recognized faces. The servant girl from the bell chamber. The librarian who had warned her about the west wing. The ghost scholar who had told her the truth.

But their eyes were different now. Empty. Hungry.

One of them stepped forward.

It was the girl from the library, the one who had drowned. Her skin was blue-white, her hair dripping water that pooled at her feet. She opened her mouth, and water poured out, but she spoke anyway, her voice echoing as if from a great distance.

"The master has seen you," she said. "The master knows your name."

The other students began to move, shuffling toward Bai Su in a slow, inexorable tide.

"The master wants to meet you," the drowned girl said. "He has been waiting for a very long time."

Bai Su backed away, her hand clutching Chen Feng's scroll, her other hand reaching for the gate behind her.

"What master?" she demanded.

The drowned girl smiled, and her smile was full of teeth that should not have been there, rows upon rows of them, like a creature from the deep.

"The one who wrote the scroll," she said.

Then the students surged forward, and Bai Su turned and ran.

She did not look back.

She did not stop running until she reached the boundary wall, her lungs burning, her legs screaming. She scrambled over the bricks and fell into the darkness beyond, landing hard on the grass outside the academy grounds.

She lay there for a long moment, staring up at the stars, her breath coming in ragged gasps.

Then she unrolled the scroll again.

In the moonlight, she saw what she had not noticed before. At the very bottom of the scroll, beneath Chen Feng's blood-soaked plea, there was a seal.

A red stamp, faded with age.

A name.

*Wei Zhen.*

The name of the academy's founder.

The man who had built the school three hundred years ago.

The man who had died in the bell tower, under circumstances that had never been explained.

Bai Su looked up at the academy walls, at the bell tower rising above them, at the shadows moving in the windows.

And she understood.

The killer was not a student.

The killer was not even a ghost.

The killer was the academy itself.

And it had been waiting for her all along.
