CHAPTER FIVE: The Tooth of the Mountain

The rain came sideways, stinging like needles.

Su Ling pressed her back against the bark of an ancient pine, watching the Jade Dragon Sect's outer post through half-closed eyes. Three buildings. Twelve men, by her count. Maybe fourteen if the cook counted as a fighter.

The ache in her shoulder had settled into a dull throb. She'd healed enough to move, not enough to fight. Not properly.

"Weak tea and old wine," she muttered, echoing her mother's favorite complaint about dragon cultivators. "They think walls make them safe."

The outer post sat at the base of Mount Cangwu, guarding the southern approach to the dragon territories. Strategically placed. Well-manned. Su Ling had spent three days watching them rotate shifts, noting their habits, their weaknesses.

One man always drank before his watch. Two others argued about a woman every evening. The captain checked the eastern wall three times a night, the western wall once.

Patterns. Every beast had them.

She slipped through the undergrowth, her single tail pressed flat against her spine. The rain muffled her footsteps. The wind covered her scent. These were tricks any fox learned before they could walk.

But walking wasn't enough anymore.

The revelation had come to her on the second night of her watch, as she'd lain in a hollow log, shivering and hungry and furious. She couldn't fight the dragons head-on. She couldn't match their cultivation, their armies, their centuries of built power.

She needed to build something they couldn't destroy with a single strike.

An empire of shadows. A kingdom of whispers.

And she needed teeth to start.

---

The fourth building held the supply stores. Su Ling had watched them carry in crates for two days—rations, weapons, medicine. Enough to keep a small army for a month.

She slid through the crack in the back wall where the mortar had crumbled. Fox-small. Fox-quick. The guards didn't notice the shadow that moved differently than the others.

Inside, the air smelled of dried meat and iron filings.

Su Ling moved silently along the stacked crates, reading the markings. Rice. Salt. Bandages. Arrowheads.

She found what she needed in the third row: a crate of talismans, unsealed, ready for distribution. Communication talismans, mostly. A few barriers. One that smelled faintly of fire.

She pocketed the fire talisman and two of the communication ones. Then she opened the crate next to it.

Swords. Not good ones—training blades, meant for new recruits. But they'd cut just fine.

She took three.

---

The moon broke through the clouds as she crept toward the stable. The horses shifted restlessly, smelling fox before they saw her. She whispered to them in the old tongue, the language of the forests, and they quieted.

She needed one. Just one. A fast one.

The bay mare in the corner had good legs and sharp eyes. Su Ling untied her, murmuring soft assurances. The mare's ears flicked, uncertain, but she didn't bolt.

Together, they slipped out the stable's back door.

---

The first alarm bell rang when she was halfway down the mountain.

Su Ling didn't look back. She kicked the mare into a gallop, clutching the swords and talismans against her chest. Behind her, shouts rose in the night. Angry. Confused.

They'd find her tracks in the morning. They'd send riders.

But she'd be gone by then.

She rode through the night, following the old paths that the dragons never patrolled. These were fox paths, worn by generations of her kind, hidden from mortal and cultivator alike. Her mother had taught her these routes, back when she'd thought she'd have centuries to learn them.

*I'll learn them in months instead,* Su Ling thought, gritting her teeth against the jarring rhythm of the gallop. *I'll learn everything in months.*

---

Dawn found her at the edge of the Whispering Forest.

She dismounted, legs trembling, and led the mare into the treeline. The forest had a name among the fox clans—*Shuijing Lin*, the Crystal Grove. It was said the trees here grew so old they remembered the birth of the first spirits.

She needed to remember something too.

Her mother's last words had been a warning, not a blessing. "The other clans won't help you," Queen Yulong had said, blood bubbling at her lips. "They've forgotten what we were. They've made peace with chains."

Su Ling had thought that meant the clans were broken. Defeated.

Now she realized it meant something else.

They'd made peace. They'd settled. They'd built lives, small and hidden, hoping the dragons would forget about them.

She needed to remind them that dragons never forgot. They just waited.

---

The first fox clan she found was the Huang, the Yellow Foxes.

They lived in a valley cupped between three hills, hidden from the sky by a canopy of ancient oaks. Their homes were carved into the living wood, camouflaged so perfectly that Su Ling nearly passed them twice.

She found the entrance by the smell—ginger and honey, the scent of their hearth fires.

A boy spotted her first. He couldn't have been more than eight, with bright yellow fur tufting from his ears and a single tail flicking nervously. He stared at her with wide eyes.

"Who are you?" he demanded, trying to sound brave.

"Su Ling, daughter of Yulong, of the Silver Fox clan." She kept her voice steady. "I need to speak with your elder."

The boy's face went pale. He knew that name. Every fox knew that name, now. The massacre of the Silver clan had been a warning to them all.

He ran.

Su Ling waited. She could feel eyes on her from the trees, from the carved windows, from the shadows between roots. The Huang were watching. Judging.

An elder emerged twenty minutes later.

She was old—fox-old, which meant centuries—with fur gone white at the tips and eyes that had seen too much. Seven tails swayed behind her, each one a testament to her cultivation.

"The last Silver Fox," the elder said. Her voice carried no warmth. "I thought you'd be dead by now."

"Close a few times." Su Ling met her gaze. "I'm still here."

"Why?"

"Because I want to rebuild what we lost. Because I want to unite the clans." She paused. "Because I want to kill the Dragon Emperor."

The elder laughed. It was not a kind sound.

"Child, you have one tail. One. Do you know how long it takes to grow nine? Centuries. Do you know how much power it requires? Bloodlines, tribulations, cultivation resources that no longer exist." She shook her head. "You're not rebuilding anything. You're dying on your feet and you don't know it yet."

"I know I'm weak." Su Ling felt the words like stones in her throat. "I know I'm young. I know I have nothing." She reached into her pouch and pulled out the talismans, the swords. "But I have hunger. I have anger. And I have a plan."

The elder's eyes flickered to the weapons. "Stolen."

"Yes."

"From dragons."

"Yes."

A long silence. The wind moved through the oaks, carrying the scent of rain and smoke.

"You have courage," the elder said slowly. "Courage and stupidity, in equal measure. A dangerous combination."

"I'm not asking for your warriors." Su Ling's voice dropped. "I'm asking for your children. Your crafters. Your farmers. I'm building something that doesn't need cultivators to survive. I'm building a foundation."

"What do you want, exactly?"

"Your knowledge. Your skills. The things you've forgotten you possess." Su Ling gestured at the valley, at the hidden homes, at the fox families watching from the shadows. "You've survived by hiding. I want to survive by becoming something the dragons can't ignore."

The elder studied her for a long moment. Then she turned and walked back into her home.

The door closed.

Su Ling stood in the clearing, rain beginning to fall again, wondering if she'd failed before she'd truly begun.

---

She was turning to leave when the door opened again.

The elder stood there, holding a bundle wrapped in oilcloth. She tossed it to Su Ling, who caught it with fumbling hands.

"The records of our first elder," the Huang matriarch said. "She was the one who negotiated the treaty with the dragons, three hundred years ago. She was the one who thought peace was possible." The old fox's voice cracked. "She was wrong. I've known it for two hundred years. I was too afraid to admit it."

Su Ling unwrapped the bundle. Inside was a scroll, yellowed with age, covered in elegant script.

"There's a mountain called Yuelong," the elder said. "The Dragon's Peak. It's where the nine fox clans signed the original treaty, before the dragons broke it. The treaty stone still stands there." She met Su Ling's eyes. "The stone has a flaw. A channel that runs to its heart. If you can find it, you might find what you're looking for."

"What am I looking for?"

"I don't know. But my elder said it was the tooth of the mountain. The thing the dragons fear most." She stepped back into her doorway. "Come back when you have something to offer. Until then, consider this a gift. From one old fool to a young one."

The door closed again.

Su Ling stood in the rain, clutching the scroll, feeling the weight of centuries pressing down on her shoulders.

She had one tail. She had three swords and two talismans and a borrowed horse.

And she had a mountain to climb.

---

The sun was setting when she reached the base of Yuelong Peak.

The mountain rose like a jagged tooth against the red sky, its slopes bare and windswept. No trees grew here. No animals called. The dragons had stripped the life from this place, left it as a monument to their victory.

Su Ling tied the mare to a withered bush and began to climb.

The wind cut through her robes. The rocks were sharp, unstable, sliding beneath her feet. She climbed for hours, her muscles screaming, her single tail lashing for balance.

She found the treaty stone at midnight.

It stood at the summit, a slab of black granite twice her height, covered in characters she couldn't read. The old fox language, she realized. The script her mother had tried to teach her, that she'd been too impatient to learn.

She traced the characters with her fingers, wishing she understood.

Then she remembered what the elder had said: *The stone has a flaw. A channel that runs to its heart.*

Su Ling pressed her ear against the cold granite. She closed her eyes. She listened.

At first, nothing. Just the wind, the distant howl of something animal and hungry.

Then she heard it.

A whisper. So faint she almost missed it. Coming from inside the stone, like water moving through hidden passages.

She followed the sound, running her hands across the rock until she found it: a hairline crack, invisible unless you were looking for it. It ran from the base of the stone to a point near the top, where it widened into a shallow depression.

A channel.

She pressed her palm against the depression.

The stone groaned.

And then it opened.

---

A staircase spiraled down into darkness.

Su Ling stood at the edge, heart pounding, breath catching in her throat. The air that rose from below was cold and old and smelled of something she couldn't name.

"The tooth of the mountain," she whispered.

She took a step down.

The stone door began to close behind her, sealing her in darkness.
