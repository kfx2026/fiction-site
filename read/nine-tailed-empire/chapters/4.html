📖 Nine-Tailed Empire by Hu Li

Chapter 4: The Jade That Burns

~1.7k words · Updated 2026-06-22

Su Ling pressed her palm flat against the bronze mirror until the cold metal bit into her skin. Behind her, the cell stank of old blood and wet stone. Three days since they dragged her into the Dragon Palace dungeons. Three days of nothing but the drip-drip-drip of water somewhere in the dark.

The mirror showed her face. Same sharp cheekbones. Same golden eyes. Same lips that curved like a fox even when she wasn't smiling.

But the tail behind her — the single white tail she'd inherited from her mother — had turned grey at the tip.

She didn't need a cultivation manual to know what that meant. Grey meant rot. Rot meant death. The dragons had done something to the qi in this place, poisoned it so she couldn't absorb anything pure. Every breath she took was slow starvation.

"Still staring at yourself?"

Su Ling didn't turn around. She knew the voice. Captain Zhao of the Dragon Guard. He'd been the one to chain her, the one who laughed when she tried to bite his hand.

"Vanity is a fox's weakness," he said, stepping into the torchlight. His armor gleamed — black scales lacquered to look like obsidian. A dragon's favorite color. "I've heard your kind spends hours admiring your own reflections. Primping. Preening. Plotting little tricks."

"Hours?" Su Ling let the mirror drop. It clattered against the stone floor. "That's generous. I usually only need minutes to decide which part of a man to eat first."

Zhao's hand went to his sword. Good. Let him be scared. Scared men made mistakes.

"Your mother said the same thing before we hung her head from the Eastern Gate," he said. "Words. All you foxes have are words and pretty faces."

Su Ling's teeth ground together. She forced her face smooth, forced the grief down into that hollow place where she kept all the things she couldn't afford to feel. Grief was a luxury. Grief got you killed.

"She said something else too," Su Ling whispered. "Before you killed her."

Zhao's eyes narrowed. "What?"

"Did you ever wonder why the Fox Queen came to the Dragon Palace alone? Without her guard? Without her daughters?"

Silence. The torches crackled.

"She knew she was going to die," Su Ling said. "She came anyway."

Zhao stepped closer. His boots echoed. "That means nothing. She was weak. Old. Past her prime."

"She was buying time."

The words hung in the air like smoke. Su Ling watched them settle into Zhao's mind, watched him turn them over, examine them. He was smart. That was the problem. Smart guards didn't fall for obvious tricks.

But Su Ling wasn't trying to trick him. She was telling the truth.

The truth was always the best lie.

"Time for what?" Zhao asked, too slowly.

"For me to find this."

Su Ling reached into her sleeve. Her fingers closed around the jade pendant her mother had pressed into her hand three breaths before the Dragon Emperor's blade found her throat. The pendant was warm. It had been warm for three days now, growing hotter with every hour.

She held it up.

Zhao's face went white. "That's—"

"The Seal of the Nine Clans." Su Ling's voice was steady now. "The one thing your emperor wanted. The one thing my mother never carried on her person. He searched her chambers. He searched her vaults. He had his best cultivators scour every fox den from the Jade Mountains to the Crimson Wastes."

She smiled. It wasn't a nice smile.

"They didn't think to search me."

Zhao drew his sword. The blade sang as it left the sheath. "Give it to me. Now. And I'll let you die quickly."

"Let me?" Su Ling laughed. "Captain, I was dead the moment they threw me in this cell. The only question is how much damage I do before I go."

She pressed the jade against her chest. The heat intensified. It burned through her robes, through her skin, through the layers of cultivation she'd built over sixteen years of training. Her mother had taught her the activation chant when she was six years old. She'd made her memorize it in the dark, with her hand over Su Ling's mouth, whispering the words so softly that even the wind couldn't hear.

*Blood of my blood. Bone of my bone. Flame that remembers. Return to the throne.*

The jade cracked.

Light poured out. Not gentle light — violent light, the color of sunset and wildfire and the first scream of a dying star. It hit Zhao full in the chest and threw him against the wall. His armor melted. His flesh bubbled. He didn't have time to scream.

The light kept coming.

It filled the cell. It poured through the bars. It raced down the corridors of the Dragon Palace, consuming everything it touched — soldiers, servants, cultivators, all of them turned to ash before they could draw a single breath.

Su Ling didn't move. She couldn't. The jade was fusing with her, melting into her bones, rewriting the very structure of her cultivation base. It hurt. It hurt more than watching her mother die. More than losing her sisters. More than every beating and betrayal and broken promise she'd ever endured.

But she didn't scream.

Fox Queens didn't scream.

When the light faded, Su Ling stood in the center of a crater. The Dragon Palace was gone. The mountain it had been carved into was gone. For miles in every direction, there was nothing but scorched earth and the smell of ozone.

She looked down at her hands. They were covered in blood — her blood, weeping from pores that had burst under the pressure of the Seal's power. Her cultivation base was shattered. Her meridians were shredded.

But one of her tails had grown back.

Two tails. Three. They sprouted from her spine like flowers breaking through ash, each one stronger than the last. By the time the growth stopped, she had three tails. Full. White. Crackling with qi so dense it made the air around her hum.

Three tails. Her mother had ten. The legends spoke of nine. But three was enough. Three could work.

Su Ling collapsed to her knees. Her body was failing. The Seal had taken everything she had and demanded more. She could feel her organs beginning to fail, her heart stuttering, her lungs filling with fluid.

"Not yet," she whispered. "Not yet. I'm not done."

The earth beneath her hands was warm. Dead. Nothing grew here anymore. Nothing lived.

But she was alive. Somehow. Some way.

She crawled. Forward. One hand. Then the other. Then one knee. Then the other. She crawled until her arms gave out, then she dragged herself with her chin, with her teeth, with the sheer stubborn refusal to die in the shadow of the enemy's home.

She crawled for hours. Maybe days. Time meant nothing when you were dying.

Eventually, the scorched earth gave way to grass. The grass gave way to forest. The forest gave way to a road — a trade road, worn smooth by centuries of wagons and travelers.

And on that road, a cart came to a stop.

The driver was old. His face was weathered, his hands gnarled with arthritis, his eyes the pale grey of someone who'd seen too much and forgotten none of it. He looked down at the fox girl lying in the dirt, her robes burned away, her skin cracked and bleeding, three white tails spread around her like a halo.

"Girl," he said. "What happened to you?"

Su Ling tried to speak. Her voice came out as a rasp. "Dragons."

The old man's expression didn't change. "That so."

He climbed down from his cart. His joints popped. He moved slowly, deliberately, like a man who had nothing to fear from the world anymore.

When he reached her, he crouched down and studied her face. His eyes lingered on her tails, then on the faint golden light still flickering behind her pupils.

"Three tails," he said. "That's not nothing. But it's not enough to fight dragons."

"I know."

"You'll need more. A lot more. And you'll need an army. Resources. Allies who won't stab you in the back the moment you turn around."

"I know."

"Can you do that? Build something from nothing? Rule when there's nothing left to rule?"

Su Ling met his eyes. Her vision was fading at the edges, grey closing in like a tide. But she held his gaze.

"I'm Su Ling," she said. "Daughter of the Fox Queen. Last of my line. I will see the dragons burn."

The old man nodded slowly, as if he'd expected nothing less. He reached into his cart and pulled out a waterskin. He held it to her lips.

"Drink," he said. "And then we talk about what comes next."

Su Ling drank. The water was cold, clean, the most beautiful thing she'd ever tasted. When she finished, the old man helped her into the back of the cart and covered her with a torn blanket.

"Where are you taking me?" she asked.

"The only place that still has room for fox girls with too much ambition." He clicked his tongue at the horses. "A city called Nine Peaks. You'll like it. It's full of people who've lost everything and decided to get it back."

The cart began to move. Su Ling closed her eyes. Her body was broken. Her cultivation was shattered. She had three tails out of nine, no army, no allies, no home.

But she had the Seal. And she had a name.

And somewhere in the mountains ahead, a city built by the desperate and damned was waiting for a new queen.

She smiled. It hurt.

Good. Pain meant she was still alive.

The cart rumbled on, carrying her toward a future she couldn't see but could already taste — copper and ash and the first faint promise of fire.

Above her, in the branches of a gnarled pine, a crow watched with eyes that weren't quite animal.

It waited until the cart disappeared around the bend.

Then it took flight, heading east.

Toward the Dragon Emperor.
