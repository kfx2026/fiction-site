📖 Nine-Tailed Empire by Hu Li

Chapter 2: The Taste of Ash

~1.9k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The blood had dried on Su Ling's hands by the time she reached the river.

She knelt at the water's edge, watching the dark flakes peel from her fingers and drift downstream. The stream ran clear here, untouched by the fires that still smoldered in the direction she'd come from. She could see them if she turned her head—the orange glow on the horizon, like a second sunset that refused to die.

She didn't turn her head.

The water was cold. Colder than it should have been for early summer. She plunged her hands in and watched the red bloom around them, dissipating into nothing. Her reflection stared back at her—a girl with too-sharp cheekbones and eyes that had seen too much in too little time. Seventeen years old. Queen of nothing.

Behind her, the forest held its breath.

"You're bleeding again."

Su Ling didn't startle. She'd heard the crunch of leaves, the careful placement of feet that meant whoever approached wanted to be heard. Old Uncle Wei moved to stand beside her, his reflection joining hers in the water. He was ancient—nobody knew exactly how ancient—and his first tail had gone gray before she was born. The other two were still white, but the gray was spreading.

"It's not my blood," she said.

"I know." He lowered himself to the ground with a grunt that spoke of old bones and older wounds. "But you've been washing for an hour. The skin will come off if you keep scrubbing."

She looked at her hands. The knuckles were raw, pink and angry. She hadn't noticed.

"Does it matter?"

Uncle Wei didn't answer right away. He reached into his sleeve and pulled out a strip of dried meat, offering it to her. When she didn't take it, he shrugged and bit off a piece himself.

"Your mother used to do that," he said around the mouthful. "After a fight. She'd wash her hands until they bled. Said she could still feel them—the enemies. Their blood on her skin."

Su Ling's throat tightened. "I don't want to hear stories about her."

"Too bad. That's all I have left."

The words hung between them, heavy as stone. Su Ling pulled her hands from the water and dried them on her ruined robes. The silk had been white this morning. Now it was the color of rust.

"How many?" she asked.

"Seventeen dead. Four wounded. The rest scattered."

"I meant the dragons."

Uncle Wei's jaw tightened. "Three confirmed. One of them was young—couldn't have been more than a century old. The other two were veterans. They'll feel the loss."

"They'll feel it." Su Ling's voice was flat. "And then they'll come back with ten more."

"It's what they do."

"Yes." She stood, her legs protesting. The run had taken everything from her—her qi reserves were so low she could barely feel the warmth of her core. One tail. One pathetic tail, twitching uselessly behind her. "It's what they do. And it's what we do too. We run. We hide. We wait for the next massacre."

Uncle Wei's eyes narrowed. "What would you have us do, little queen? Fight? We lost three clans in the first month. The foxes that remain are scattered from here to the Eastern Sea. You can't build an army from ghosts."

"I can build something."

"Like what?"

She turned to face him fully. The old fox's ears were flat against his skull, his remaining tails bristling. He was afraid. She could smell it on him—that sharp, acrid scent that came with age and the knowledge of one's own mortality.

"An empire," she said.

Uncle Wei laughed. It was a bitter sound, scraping out of his throat like broken glass. "An empire. With what? Ten dozen foxes hiding in caves and forests? With a queen who can't even grow her second tail?"

"I'll grow it."

"When? The dragons won't wait for you to cultivate. They'll hunt us to extinction. Every fox they find, they kill. Every den they discover, they burn. You saw what they did to the palace. You saw what they did to—" He stopped. Swallowed. "You saw."

Su Ling had seen. She would see it every time she closed her eyes for the rest of her life. Her mother's head, separated from her body, held aloft on a dragon's spear. The nine tails that had once been the pride of their people, chopped off and displayed like hunting trophies.

"I saw," she said. "And I'm going to make them pay for it."

"How?"

The question was simple. The answer was not.

She thought of the Shan Hai Jing—the ancient text that her mother had made her memorize since she was old enough to speak. The book of mountains and seas, of beasts and spirits, of the old laws that bound the cultivation world together. There was power there. Knowledge that the dragons had forgotten, or chosen to ignore.

"The treaty," she said slowly. "The one that ended the War of the Five Tribes. There's a clause."

Uncle Wei's ears perked up. "I don't remember any clause."

"Because you weren't there. Mother was." Su Ling's hand went to her chest, where a small jade pendant hung from a leather cord. She'd taken it from her mother's body before the dragons could. "The dragons broke it when they attacked us. They're not supposed to exterminate a full-blood clan unless the clan has been declared rogue."

"And have we been declared rogue?"

"Not yet. But they'll do it. They'll fabricate evidence, call a council, get the other tribes to agree. Then it's legal." She spat the word. "They want it to be legal."

"Then we're doomed either way."

"No." Su Ling shook her head. "The clause. If a clan is declared rogue, they have one year to prove their innocence. During that year, they're granted sanctuary in any neutral territory. They can't be hunted. They can't be attacked."

"Neutral territory." Uncle Wei's brow furrowed. "There's no such thing. The dragons control everything from the Jade Mountains to the Silver Sea."

"Not everything."

She reached into her sleeve and pulled out a scroll—yellowed with age, sealed with wax that had cracked centuries ago. Her mother's handwriting covered the outside, elegant characters that spelled out a single word: FOR MY DAUGHTER.

Uncle Wei stared at it. "She gave you that?"

"Before the attack. She knew." Su Ling's voice cracked, and she forced it steady. "She knew they were coming, and she gave me this instead of running."

"What does it say?"

"I don't know. I haven't opened it."

"Why not?"

Because opening it meant accepting that her mother was gone. That the words inside were all that remained of the woman who had taught her to walk, to speak, to shift her form and hide her tails. Who had held her when she cried and laughed when she fell and looked at her like she was the most precious thing in the world.

Because once she read it, she would have to act.

"I was waiting," she said. "For the right moment."

"And is this the right moment?"

Su Ling looked at the scroll. Then at the smoke rising from where her home used to be. Then at the old fox who had served her family for longer than she'd been alive.

"It has to be."

She broke the seal.

The scroll unrolled with a whisper, revealing page after page of her mother's careful script. The first words made her breath catch:

*My dearest Ling,*
*If you're reading this, I am dead, and you are alone.*
*Do not grieve. There is no time for grief.*
*I have hidden something. Something the dragons want more than our lives. Something that will let you build what I could not.*
*Go to the Mountain of the Sleeping Sun. Find the Cave of Whispers. At the back, where the stone is warm, dig.*
*What you find there will change everything.*
*Trust no one. Not even the ones you love. The dragons have eyes everywhere, and gold buys more loyalty than blood ever did.*
*But when you find what I've left you, remember this:*
*A fox with one tail is prey.*
*A fox with nine tails is a god.*
*Become a god, my daughter. Become the thing they fear.*

The scroll ended there. No signature. No farewell. Just instructions and a warning that made her blood run cold.

"What is it?" Uncle Wei asked. "What did she hide?"

Su Ling rolled the scroll up slowly, her mind racing. The Mountain of the Sleeping Sun was a dead volcano in the far north, three weeks' travel through dragon-controlled territory. The Cave of Whispers was a legend, a place that might not even exist.

But her mother had never lied to her. Not once.

"She hid power," Su Ling said. "The kind of power that can challenge dragons."

Uncle Wei's eyes went wide. "The Nine-Tailed Transformation. She found it?"

"I don't know. But I'm going to find out."

"Alone?"

"Yes." She tucked the scroll into her sleeve. "You said it yourself. Trust no one."

"Ling—"

"I'm not arguing about this." Her voice hardened, and she felt something shift in her chest. A spark of heat where the cold had been. "Take the survivors to the Eastern Warrens. Stay hidden. Stay alive. If I succeed, I'll find you. If I don't..."

She trailed off. She didn't need to finish.

"If you don't," Uncle Wei said quietly, "there won't be anyone left to find."

"Then you'd better hope I succeed."

She turned and walked into the forest before he could respond. The trees swallowed her, their shadows deep and cold. She could feel him watching, feel the weight of his gaze on her back.

She didn't look back.

The mountain was three weeks away. The dragons were everywhere. She had one tail, no army, and a dead mother's treasure map.

It would have to be enough.

---

Three days later, Su Ling's hands were still raw.

She'd stopped at a stream to refill her waterskin when she heard them—heavy footsteps, the clank of armor, the distinctive hiss of dragon-tongue. She froze, her hand hovering over the waterskin's stopper.

Two of them. Maybe three. Close.

She shifted without thinking, her form shrinking and contorting until she was a small gray fox, fur blending with the rocks. She pressed herself flat against the ground, ears back, heart pounding.

They emerged from the treeline—two dragons in human form, their scales visible on their necks and hands, their eyes slitted and gold. Soldiers. Low-ranking, by the look of their armor, but armed and alert.

"—heard there was a fox sighting near the river," one was saying. "A young one. Female."

"The queen's daughter?"

"Maybe. Commander wants her alive. Wants to know what the old fox told her before she died."

The other dragon laughed. "Good luck finding her. These woods are crawling with dens. She could be anywhere."

"Then we burn the woods. Burn everything. Eventually she'll come out."

Su Ling's blood ran cold. They were already hunting her. Not just killing—hunting. And they'd burn down every tree, every den, every living thing to find her.

The first dragon stopped. Sniffed the air.

"You smell that?"

"Blood," the second said. "Fresh."

They turned in her direction.
