📖 The Demon Kitchen by Shi Fu

Chapter 3: The First Bite of Heaven

~1.7k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The kitchen clock read 1:47 AM when the qilin arrived.

Lin Hao heard it before he saw it — hooves striking cobblestone outside his alley, each step ringing like a temple bell. He set down his cleaver and wiped his hands on his apron. The meat thermometers on his station read 131 degrees for the dragon ribeye. Perfect medium-rare. It would have to hold.

The door chimed.

Most demons entered his restaurant with arrogance. They kicked the door open, or slithered through shadows, or materialized from clouds of sulfur and spite. The qilin simply walked in like a weary traveler who'd been walking for a thousand years and needed a seat.

It looked human enough. Tall, broad-shouldered, wearing a grey changshan that smelled of sandalwood and old scrolls. But its eyes burned amber, and when it breathed, tiny flames licked the air from its nostrils.

"I heard you serve the forbidden," the qilin said. Its voice crackled like embers.

Lin Hao gestured to a table by the window. "I serve what people order."

"I am not people."

"No. You're the last of your kind south of the Yangtze."

The qilin's eyes narrowed. Those flames in its nostrils grew brighter. "You know what I am and you do not run."

"Should I?"

"I could burn this building to ash with a thought."

Lin Hao picked up his cleaver again. The blade caught the low light — twenty inches of Damascus steel, folded sixty-four times by a swordsmith in Longquan who didn't know what the knife would be used for. Or maybe he did. The old man never asked questions.

"Then I'd be out of a job," Lin Hao said. "And you wouldn't get dinner. Sit. It's late. You look tired."

The qilin stared at him for a long moment. Then it sat.

"What's the special tonight?" it asked.

Lin Hao opened his cold storage. Steam rolled out, carrying the scent of aged meat and iron. He pulled out a tray covered in oiled paper. When he peeled the corner back, the flesh beneath gleamed like black pearl, marbled with veins of silver fat.

"Dragon ribeye," he said. "Yellow River dragon. Been dry-aging for six weeks. It's ready tonight."

"I've eaten dragon before."

"Not like this."

The qilin leaned forward. Its amber eyes traced the marbling, the dark crimson of aged meat, the precise trimming around the edges. A chef's cut. Nothing wasted.

"How much?"

"For you? Free."

"I don't accept charity."

"It's not charity. I've been saving this piece for someone who'd appreciate it. Most of my customers just want the power. They don't taste the food."

"And you think I will?"

"You're a qilin. You've been alive for two thousand years. You've eaten everything this world has to offer. You tell me if I'm wrong."

Lin Hao didn't wait for an answer. He laid the steak on his cutting board, felt the familiar weight of the cleaver in his hand, and began.

The first cut was pure muscle memory — two hundred thousand repetitions in kitchens from Paris to Shanghai. He portioned the steak into eight-ounce medallions, each exactly two inches thick. The fat cap he trimmed and set aside for rendering. The bone he wrapped and saved for stock.

The qilin watched in silence. Somewhere in the distance, a temple bell struck twice.

Lin Hao heated his cast iron pan until the oil shimmered. He seasoned the steak with nothing but salt — Sichuan sea salt, harvested from ancient brine pools in the mountains. When the meat hit the pan, the sound was like a thunderclap. Steam erupted. The smell filled the kitchen — not just beef, but something older. River water. Morning mist. The anger of a dying god.

He seared each side for exactly ninety seconds. The crust formed dark and crackling. He added butter, rosemary, smashed garlic cloves. Basted. Flipped. Basted again.

"Is it true?" the qilin asked. "That you cook with divine technique?"

Lin Hao didn't look up. "I cook with French technique. The divinity is in the ingredients."

"You steal power from the dead."

"I use what would otherwise be wasted. The Celestial Bureaucracy kills rogue dragons every week. They burn the bodies. Thousands of years of accumulated essence, turned to ash. That's the real crime."

"You could be struck down for this."

"I could have been struck down a hundred times already." Lin Hao slid the steak onto a heated plate. He added a dollop of bone marrow butter, a sprinkle of micro herbs, a drizzle of aged balsamic that had cost more than most people's rent. "The First Kitchen doesn't care about me. I'm too small. And the Demon Court? They're my best customers."

He set the plate in front of the qilin.

It looked down at the steak. The butter was melting, running down the crust in golden rivulets. Steam carried the scent upward — beef fat, garlic, rosemary, and beneath it all, the unmistakable smell of dragon. Not the stink of scales and swamp, but the clean scent of storm clouds and lightning.

"One bite," the qilin said, "and I could level a city block."

"Yes."

"One bite, and I could burn through the gates of the Jade Palace."

"Probably."

"One bite, and I could — "

"You could enjoy your dinner," Lin Hao interrupted. "It's getting cold."

The qilin picked up its knife and fork. European style. Interesting.

It cut a single piece. The knife slid through the meat like it was cutting butter. The interior was perfect — dark pink, warm, glistening. The dragon's blood had been aged into something almost sweet.

It put the fork in its mouth.

For a moment, nothing happened.

Then the qilin's eyes went wide. Its pupils dilated. The amber glow in its irises flickered, dimmed, flared again. Its hands gripped the table edge so hard the wood groaned.

"Master," it whispered.

Lin Hao said nothing.

"I see — I see the river. The Yellow River. Before the dams. Before the pollution. When it ran clean from the mountains to the sea." The qilin's voice cracked. "I see him. The dragon. He was young. Only three hundred years. He was swimming upstream to spawn. He never made it."

"You're tasting his memory."

"I'm tasting his life."

The qilin took another bite. This time, it didn't speak. It just chewed, slowly, deliberately, with the reverence of a monk at prayer. Tears streamed down its face, evaporating into steam before they hit its collar.

Lin Hao busied himself with cleaning his station. He'd seen this before. Every customer had the same reaction — the first bite always unlocked something. A memory. An emotion. A power they didn't know they had.

The qilin finished the entire steak. It used the last piece of bread to wipe the plate clean. Then it set down its utensils and looked at Lin Hao with those burning eyes.

"You're not just a chef," it said.

"I'm just a chef."

"No. You're something else." The qilin stood. It was taller than Lin Hao remembered. "I came here to kill you."

"Most people come for the food."

"The Demon Court sent me. They said you've been cooking celestial creatures without proper tribute. They want you stopped."

"And now?"

The qilin reached into its changshan. Lin Hao's hand moved toward the knife block. But what the qilin pulled out wasn't a weapon. It was a piece of parchment, yellowed with age, sealed with red wax that bore the mark of three intertwined dragons.

"Now I'm giving you this."

Lin Hao took the parchment. He broke the seal. The script inside was classical Chinese, written in gold ink that seemed to glow. He read it once. Then again.

"What is this?" he asked.

"An invitation," the qilin said. "To the Celestial Kitchen."

"The what?"

"There's a competition. Every three thousand years. The gods of every pantheon send their best chefs to compete. The prize is a seat at the Table of Heaven — the right to cook for the highest divine beings in existence."

Lin Hao looked at the parchment. The gold ink was warm to the touch. "Why me?"

"Because in three thousand years of competition, no mortal has ever been invited." The qilin smiled, and there was something terrible in that smile. "And because the Demon Court wants you gone. They'd rather see you die in heaven than succeed on earth."

The qilin walked toward the door. At the threshold, it paused.

"One more thing, chef."

"What?"

"Your next customer is waiting outside. She's been there for three hours. She's a fox spirit. Nine tails. Very old. Very hungry."

"She could have come in."

"She doesn't eat meat."

Lin Hao frowned. "Then what does she eat?"

The qilin laughed — a sound like burning paper.

"Hope."

It stepped through the door and vanished into the night. The alley was empty. The street was empty. Even the shadows seemed to have retreated.

Lin Hao stood alone in his kitchen, holding an invitation to cook for gods, while a nine-tailed fox waited outside to eat something he didn't know how to make.

The clock struck two.

He opened the door.

A woman stood in the alley. She wore red — a qipao that seemed to shimmer with every movement. Her hair was black silk. Her eyes were ancient.

"Chef Lin," she said. Her voice was honey and knives. "I've been waiting."

"Come in."

She stepped past him. As she moved, the air stirred, and he caught the scent of jasmine and wet earth and something else — something that made his heart beat faster.

Behind her, nine tails flickered in the darkness, each one tipped with white fire.

"Tell me," she said, settling into the qilin's chair, "have you ever cooked a memory?"

Lin Hao looked at the invitation in his hand. Then at the fox spirit in his restaurant.

"No," he said.

She smiled.

"Tonight, you will learn."
