CHAPTER 2: The Tooth Collector

The kitchen clock read 2:47 AM when Lin Hao heard the bell above his door chime.

He didn't look up from the mandolin. The blade sang through a thousand-year-old ginseng root, each translucent slice curling onto the cutting board like amber glass. The fragrance alone could cure a common cold. Or raise the dead, depending on the root's age.

"Menu's on the wall," he said. "No substitutions. No refunds. Payment upfront."

The footsteps that crossed his dining room made no sound. Not the whisper of rubber soles on tile. Not the click of heels. Just the pressure of weight shifting through air, as if the floor refused to acknowledge the presence above it.

Lin Hao set down his knife.

The woman who sat at his counter looked human enough. Late twenties. Shoulder-length black hair. A face that would be pretty if not for the hunger in her eyes. She wore a business suit, navy blue, expensive cut. Her hands were folded on the marble countertop, fingers interlaced, knuckles white.

"I need the Fire-Feather menu," she said.

Lin Hao wiped his hands on his apron. "You know what that costs?"

"I know what it does." Her voice cracked on the last word. "My mother has three days. The hospital says her marrow is already dead. The hematologist suggested I start making arrangements."

"Then you should be making arrangements."

The woman's composure shattered. Her hands slammed against the counter. The marble didn't crack, but Lin Hao felt the vibration through his soles. "I have money. I have property. I have—"

"You have a dying mother." Lin Hao reached for the kettle. "I'm sorry. Truly. But the Fire-Feather menu requires a Vermilion Bird feather. I don't have one in stock. And even if I did, I charge in memories, not currency."

"I'll give you anything."

"Anything?" He poured green tea into a porcelain cup. The leaves spiraled in the hot water, dancing their ancient dance. "Have you ever eaten a Vermilion Bird's feather, Ms...?"

"Zhao. Zhao Liling."

"Have you, Ms. Zhao?"

She shook her head.

"The first bite burns," Lin Hao said. "Not your mouth. Your soul. You'll feel every sin you've committed, every lie you've told, every kindness you failed to offer. The second bite shows you every moment you could have been better. The third bite..." He slid the tea toward her. "The third bite either rebuilds your mother's marrow or turns her to ash. The feather doesn't discriminate between disease and curse. It burns away everything impure. Including the patient, if the impurity runs too deep."

Zhao Liling's hands trembled around the teacup. "She's a good woman. She raised me alone after father left. She worked three jobs. She never—"

"Every child believes their mother is good." Lin Hao's voice was gentle, which made it worse. "The feather doesn't care about belief."

The bell chimed again.

Lin Hao's shoulders tightened. Two customers in one night. That hadn't happened since the Mid-Autumn Festival, when the Jade Rabbit stopped by for a late-night snack and brought three hungry ghosts as plus-ones.

The second customer was a man. Old. Bent-backed. He wore a gray changshan that had been expensive eighty years ago and threadbare ever since. His eyes were milk-white, filmed over with cataracts or something older. He carried a wooden cane carved with characters so ancient they predated the Shang dynasty.

He sat at the far end of the counter, three seats away from Zhao Liling.

"Master Lin," the old man said. His voice sounded like stones grinding together at the bottom of a river. "I've waited sixty-three years for a reservation."

Lin Hao studied him. The old man's aura was wrong. Not demon. Not spirit. Something in between, something that had been human once and then kept going past the point where humanity should have stopped.

"I don't take walk-ins," Lin Hao said.

"The waiting list."

"Is three centuries long."

"I know." The old man smiled. His teeth were too white, too straight, too young for his wrinkled face. "I'm patient, Master Lin. I've had time to learn patience. But my window is closing. Three days. That's all I have left."

Three days. Same as Zhao Liling's mother.

"Coincidence is a dish I don't serve," Lin Hao muttered.

"Pardon?"

"Nothing." He turned back to Zhao Liling. "Ms. Zhao, I'm afraid I must ask you to leave. I have a new customer to attend to, and—"

"I'm not leaving."

"This isn't a negotiation."

"Then give me something else." She grabbed his wrist. Her grip was iron. "Anything. A soup. A tea. Something that can buy time. I'll pay whatever you ask. I'll give you my memories of her. Every birthday party. Every bedtime story. Every time she kissed my forehead and said she loved me. Take them all. I don't care if I forget her face. I just want her to live."

Lin Hao looked down at her hand. Pale fingers wrapped around his forearm. A mortal's grip, but fueled by desperation that could move mountains.

"The Kitchen God's Mushroom Soup," he said slowly. "It won't cure her. But it might slow the decay. Buy you a week. Maybe two."

"How much?"

"Your first memory of your father."

Zhao Liling went still.

"Before he left," Lin Hao continued. "The one good memory. The one you hold onto when you tell yourself he wasn't all bad. I want that."

"You're a monster."

"I'm a chef. The ingredients don't grow on trees." He pulled his wrist free. "Do we have a deal?"

She was silent for a long moment. Then she nodded.

Lin Hao reached into his apron pocket and pulled out a small leather pouch. Inside was a dried mushroom the size of his thumbnail, black as ink, with veins of gold running through its cap. He'd found it growing on the corpse of a minor dragon in the mountains outside Chengdu. It had taken him three days to convince the dragon's spirit to let him harvest it.

He dropped the mushroom into a mortar and began to grind.

The pestle moved in circles, crushing the mushroom into powder. Golden sparks rose from the mortar with each rotation. The smell that filled the kitchen was impossible to describe—earth and sky and death and rebirth, all mixed together.

"Your father," Lin Hao said. "Think of him."

Zhao Liling closed her eyes. Her face cycled through emotions too fast to track. Anger. Sadness. Longing. And then, finally, a softness that made her look ten years younger.

"I was four," she whispered. "He came home from work early. Took me to the park. Pushed me on the swings until my legs got tired. Then he bought me ice cream. Strawberry. My favorite. He sat next to me on the bench and let me tell him about my day. Everything. Every detail. He listened like I was the most important person in the world."

Lin Hao felt the memory flow into the mortar. It tasted like strawberry ice cream and summer evenings and the safety of a father's attention.

He poured the mushroom powder into a clay pot, added water from a spring that only existed during solar eclipses, and set it over the flame. The soup would take ten minutes. In that time, he would have to deal with the old man.

"You said three days," Lin Hao said, turning to face him. "What happens in three days?"

The old man tapped his cane against the floor. Tap. Tap. Tap. Each impact sent ripples through the air, disturbing the dust motes that floated in the kitchen's dim light.

"Three days from now, the Celestial Bureaucracy will hold a review of my case. My existence has been... irregular. I was supposed to die in 1959. A heart attack. Quick. Painless. But I had unfinished business. So I made a deal with a hungry ghost. Extended my life. Extended it too far, it seems."

"You made a deal with a hungry ghost?" Lin Hao's hand stopped mid-stir. "Those creatures are bottomless. They'll eat your soul piece by piece until there's nothing left."

"I know." The old man's milky eyes seemed to focus on something far away. "I've paid pieces every year. My sense of smell. My taste buds. My ability to feel joy. My left kidney. My right lung. Every year, another payment. But now the hungry ghost wants the final piece. My soul. And when the Celestial Bureaucracy reviews my case, they'll see what I've done. They'll erase me from existence. No reincarnation. No afterlife. Just... nothing."

Lin Hao stirred the soup. The golden sparks had settled into a soft glow, pulsing like a heartbeat.

"What do you want from me?"

"Feed me something that will make me strong enough to fight."

"I don't serve weapons."

"Then feed me something that will make me fast enough to run."

"I don't serve escapes."

The old man's composure cracked. His hands, gnarled and spotted with age, gripped his cane until the wood groaned. "Then what do you serve?"

"Food." Lin Hao ladled the mushroom soup into a bowl. "I serve food. It heals or it harms depending on who eats it. It grants power or takes it away depending on what I put into it. But I don't choose the outcome. The dish chooses."

"You're a coward."

"I'm a cook." He slid the bowl toward Zhao Liling. "Drink. Slowly. Let it settle before you leave."

She lifted the bowl with both hands. The steam curled around her face, and for a moment, Lin Hao saw her mother reflected in her eyes—a woman with the same stubborn jaw, the same desperate love.

Zhao Liling drank.

The effect was immediate. Color flooded back into her cheeks. Her shoulders straightened. The exhaustion that had carved lines into her face smoothed away.

"Your mother will have more time," Lin Hao said. "Use it wisely."

She set down the bowl. Tears streamed down her face, but she wasn't crying. The soup was forcing out impurities, flushing her system clean. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me. You paid the price."

She left without another word. The door chimed behind her.

Lin Hao turned to the old man. "You should leave too."

"I can't."

"Then you'll die here."

"Better here than in a celestial courtroom." The old man reached into his sleeve and pulled out a jade pendant. It was carved in the shape of a dragon, coiled around itself, teeth bared. "I have one more payment to offer. Not to the hungry ghost. To you."

Lin Hao's breath caught.

The pendant was authentic. He could feel its power from across the counter—ancient, hungry, screaming with the trapped soul of a minor dragon.

"That's worth more than anything on my menu."

"I know." The old man placed it on the counter. "I want the Dragon's Wrath Noodles."

Lin Hao's blood went cold.

"There are only two bowls of Dragon's Wrath Noodles in existence," he said. "One was eaten by a general during the Three Kingdoms period. He conquered nine cities before the meal ended. The other is sitting in my freezer, waiting for a customer who's willing to pay the price."

"I'm willing."

"The price isn't jade."

"Then what is it?"
