📖 The Green Snake Rises by Xiao Qing

Chapter 2: The Debt of a Thousand Scales

~1.9k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The mountain path coiled like a dead serpent through the thinning mist.

Xiao Qing walked barefoot on stone worn smooth by pilgrims who had not come in fifty years. Moss grew thick between the cracks. The scent of wet earth and rotting leaves filled her lungs with something close to memory.

She stopped at the edge of a clearing.

Before her stood the bones of what had once been a temple. Broken pillars jutted from the ground like ribs. A bronze bell lay half-buried in mud, its surface green with age. The main hall had collapsed inward, roof beams snapped under the weight of centuries.

Xiao Qing tilted her head.

The qi here was wrong. Not dead—that would have been peaceful. This place hummed with something trapped, something that had not been allowed to fade.

She stepped through what had once been the temple gate.

The moment her foot crossed the threshold, the air changed. Cold crawled across her skin like fingers. The mist thickened until she could see nothing but grey. Sounds died—no birds, no wind, no rustle of leaves.

Only silence.

And then a voice, thin as spider silk:

*“You smell like her.”*

Xiao Qing did not move.

The voice came from everywhere and nowhere. It slid through the mist like a serpent through grass.

*“The white one. The one who loved. The one who lost.”*

“Show yourself,” Xiao Qing said. Her voice came out flat. Calm. She had learned calm in five hundred years of solitude. The voice wanted her afraid. She would not give it that satisfaction.

The mist parted.

A woman stood at the center of the collapsed hall. She wore robes of pale grey that seemed to blend into the fog. Her face was beautiful in the way that sharp things are beautiful—dangerous, cutting, cold. Her eyes were the color of old bone.

She was not quite solid. Xiao Qing could see the outline of the broken altar through her form.

“A remnant,” Xiao Qing said. “You should have faded centuries ago.”

The woman smiled. It did not reach her eyes. “I was bound here. By the monk. By the power that sealed your sister.”

Xiao Qing’s fingers curled into fists.

“You knew Fahai.”

“I knew him.” The remnant’s voice carried an old bitterness, worn smooth by time. “I was a cultivator. A woman of the Lan School. When your sister was captured, I spoke against him. I said the punishment was too cruel, that the White Snake had acted from love, not malice.”

She drifted closer. The air grew colder.

“Fahai did not appreciate my words. He bound me to this temple. Trapped my spirit here to watch over the seal.”

Xiao Qing’s heart beat once, hard.

“The seal?”

The remnant gestured with one translucent hand. The mist swirled, revealing a stone slab at the center of the hall. Characters were carved into its surface—Buddhist sutras, tight and precise. A faint golden light pulsed beneath them.

“Leifeng Pagoda is a cage,” the remnant said. “But the seal that binds your sister is not at the pagoda. It is here. This temple was built as the lock. The pagoda is merely the keyhole.”

Xiao Qing stared at the stone.

Five hundred years. Five hundred years of believing her sister was trapped beneath a pagoda in Hangzhou. Five hundred years of planning, of cultivating, of gathering power to break through layers of Buddhist protections.

And it was all here.

This broken, forgotten temple in the mountains.

This stone.

She could feel the seal’s power now that she knew to look for it. A deep, steady hum beneath the earth. The weight of a thousand chanting monks pressing down through the centuries.

“Why are you telling me this?” Xiao Qing asked.

The remnant’s smile turned sharp. “Because I want to see his work undone. Because I have been trapped in this place for five hundred years, watching the seasons change, watching pilgrims come and die, watching the world forget that a woman was imprisoned for the crime of loving a mortal.”

Her form flickered. For a moment, Xiao Qing saw something beneath the beautiful face—rage, old and raw and bleeding.

“I want to be free,” the remnant said. “And the only way to break the seal is to destroy the power that sustains it.”

Xiao Qing turned fully to face her. “Tell me how.”

---

The remnant’s name was Master Lian.

She had been a cultivator of the eighth rank when Fahai trapped her. A woman of considerable power, once. Now she was nothing but memory and spite, held together by the same seal that bound Xiao Qing’s sister.

“The seal draws power from three sources,” Lian said. She sat on the broken altar, her form rippling like heat haze. “The first is Fahai’s own cultivation. The second is the faith of the temple’s pilgrims—though that source has dried up, as you can see.”

She gestured at the ruins.

“The third is the most important. The seal is anchored by three artifacts. Destroy them, and the seal weakens. Destroy all three, and the seal breaks entirely.”

Xiao Qing’s breath caught. “Where are they?”

“One is here.” Lian pointed to the stone slab. “Beneath that stone, buried in the earth. The second is in the possession of Fahai’s successor. The third—”

She paused.

“The third is inside the pagoda itself. Held by the guardian of the seal.”

Xiao Qing’s mind raced. Three artifacts. Three targets. One buried here, one with a living monk, one inside a heavily guarded pagoda.

“The artifact here,” she said. “What is it?”

“A scale,” Lian said. “One of your sister’s scales. Fahai removed it from her body before sealing her. He used it as the foundation of the binding.”

Something cold settled in Xiao Qing’s chest.

Her sister’s scale. Torn from her body. Used as a lock.

She walked to the stone slab. The characters glowed beneath her feet, warm and hateful. She could feel the power pulsing through them—Buddhist energy, clean and sharp and relentless.

“If I break the stone, the seal weakens,” she said. “And my sister’s scale is freed.”

“Yes.”

Xiao Qing looked at her hands. Five centuries of cultivation had honed her body into a weapon. She could shatter this stone. She could tear this temple apart stone by stone.

But the moment she did, Fahai’s successor would know. The celestial court would know. Every monk, every cultivator, every god who had helped imprison her sister would feel the disturbance.

She would lose the element of surprise.

She would have to fight them all.

“I don’t care,” Xiao Qing said softly.

She raised her hand.

---

The blow struck the stone like thunder.

The characters flared gold, bright as the sun. A shockwave rippled outward, throwing Xiao Qing back. She landed hard, rolled, came up on her feet.

The stone was cracked.

A single line ran through the center of the slab. Golden light bled from the wound like blood.

“Again,” Lian whispered. Her form flickered violently, but her eyes were bright. “Again.”

Xiao Qing struck again. And again. Each blow sent golden light spilling across the ruins. The ground shook. The air screamed with released energy.

On the fifth strike, the stone shattered.

The characters scattered like broken teeth. Light poured upward in a pillar, pure and blinding. Xiao Qing shielded her eyes.

When the light faded, something lay in the rubble.

A scale.

It was the size of her palm, white as moonlight, iridescent as pearl. It pulsed with a faint, warm light—the last trace of her sister’s presence.

Xiao Qing picked it up.

The moment her fingers touched the scale, she felt it—a connection, thin as thread, stretching across mountains and rivers and sky. Somewhere far away, her sister was alive.

She was still there.

She was still waiting.

Xiao Qing pressed the scale to her chest. Her eyes burned, but she did not cry. She had not cried in four hundred years. She would not start now.

Behind her, the remnant of Master Lian began to fade.

“Thank you,” Lian said. Her voice was barely a whisper now. “I can feel the binding loosening. I can feel the door opening.”

“Where will you go?” Xiao Qing asked.

“Somewhere quiet. Somewhere I can finally rest.” The remnant smiled—a real smile, soft and sad. “Be careful, green snake. The world has not forgotten Fahai’s lesson. They will not welcome a demon who breaks seals and frees monsters.”

“Let them come,” Xiao Qing said.

Lian’s form dissolved into mist. Her voice lingered a moment longer, carried on the wind:

“They already know you’re here.”

---

The sky had changed.

Xiao Qing looked up. The clouds above the temple had turned strange—swirling, golden-edged, unnatural. The seal’s destruction had sent ripples across the spiritual world.

She could feel eyes turning toward her.

From the celestial court above. From the monasteries below. From every corner of the three realms that had an interest in keeping the White Snake trapped.

She was out of time.

Xiao Qing tucked the scale into her robes. She walked out of the ruined temple, past the broken bell, past the moss-eaten pillars, down the mountain path that no pilgrims had walked in fifty years.

She had one scale.

She needed two more.

And somewhere in the world, Fahai’s successor was already moving.

---

The message arrived at the Monastery of Endless Chanting as the sun set.

A monk in grey robes ran through the halls, his sandals slapping against stone. He burst into the abbot’s chamber without knocking.

“Master,” he gasped. “The western seal. It’s broken.”

The abbot sat cross-legged before a small altar. He was old—ancient, some said—with skin like parchment and eyes like chips of ice. He did not turn.

“I know,” he said.

“But Master—the seal—the White Snake’s sister—”

“I know.”

The abbot opened his eyes. They were cold. They had always been cold.

“She has awakened the scale,” he said. “She will come for the others.”

The young monk trembled. “What do we do?”

The abbot rose. His robes whispered against the floor. He walked to a wall hung with ancient scrolls, each one depicting a figure of power—gods, demons, cultivators of ages past.

He stopped before a scroll showing a woman in white, her hair flowing like water, her eyes closed in eternal sleep.

“We remind her,” the abbot said softly, “that the world is not kind to snakes.”

He touched the scroll.

The woman’s eyes opened.

---

Xiao Qing felt it as she descended the mountain—a shift in the air, a change in the spiritual pressure that pressed down on the world.

Something old had woken.

Something that had been sleeping since the day Fahai sealed her sister.

She stopped on the path. The forest around her had gone silent. No birds. No insects. No wind.

A voice came from behind her.

Soft. Feminine. Familiar in a way that made her blood run cold.

“Little green snake.”

Xiao Qing turned.

A woman stood on the path. She wore white robes that seemed to glow in the fading light. Her face was beautiful—too beautiful, inhumanly beautiful—and her eyes held the endless depth of a sky without stars.
