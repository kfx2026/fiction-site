CHAPTER 6: The Price of Remembering

The golden bowl hummed.

It sat in the center of a room that shouldn't exist—a chamber folded between breaths, tucked behind a waterfall that had dried up three thousand years ago. The bowl was old. Older than the temple that once housed it. Older than the mountain the temple had been built on.

And now it was awake.

A hand reached out and touched its surface. Old skin, thin as paper, spotted with age. The fingers traced the characters etched into the bronze—names of demons bound, spirits sealed, snakes captured.

One name at the top glowed faintly green.

"Not yet," the voice said. "But soon."

The hand withdrew. The room fell silent again.

But the bowl kept humming.

---

Xiao Qing walked through the night.

She didn't stop to rest. Her ribs ached with every step, the cracked bones grinding against each other, but she'd healed worse. Five hundred years of solitary cultivation had taught her to ignore pain. Pain was information. Pain told you what was still alive.

Her left hand stayed closed around Elder Xu's bone.

It was warm. Not the warmth of living flesh, but something else. Residual qi, yes, but also something older. Memory. The bone remembered being part of something whole. It remembered the body it had belonged to, the meridians that had carried power through it, the techniques that had shaped water into weapons and shields.

She could feel echoes of those techniques if she concentrated. Faint impressions, like footprints in wet sand.

But she didn't have time to study them now.

The Library of Forgotten Things didn't wait. It moved through the spaces between realms, drifting like a ship without anchor. If she didn't find the key soon, the library would shift again, and it might be another century before it resurfaced.

And she didn't have a century.

She had days, maybe hours, before the celestial court realized what she was looking for.

---

Dawn found her at the edge of a forest that wasn't on any map.

The trees here were wrong. Their trunks bent at angles that violated geometry, their leaves reflected light that didn't exist in the visible spectrum. The soil beneath her feet hummed with a frequency that made her teeth ache.

This was the border. The place where the mortal realm frayed into something else.

Xiao Qing stopped at the treeline and listened.

The wind carried voices—not words, but the shape of words. Conversation without meaning. The forest was alive with the echoes of things that had been said and forgotten.

"Perfect," she muttered.

She stepped forward.

The world turned inside out.

---

For a moment, she was everywhere and nowhere. Her body stretched across dimensions, her consciousness splintering into a thousand fragments, each one experiencing a different version of the same moment. She felt herself falling and rising and standing still, all at once.

Then she was solid again.

The Library of Forgotten Things rose before her.

It wasn't a building. It was a *presence*—a space that had decided to look like a building so that mortal minds could comprehend it. Walls of stone that breathed. Windows that blinked. A door that watched her approach with patient hunger.

"Welcome," the door said. Its voice was the sound of pages turning. "You are expected."

Xiao Qing stopped. "Expected?"

"Expected." The door's surface rippled. "The library knows when someone carries a question that needs answering. You have carried yours for five hundred years."

She didn't like that. She didn't like that anything knew she was coming.

"I need the original registry," she said. "The one that predates the current celestial administration."

"The registry of truths." The door's hinges creaked—a sound like old laughter. "It has not been opened in three thousand years. Do you know the price?"

"I know I need a key."

"The key is sacrifice." The door swung open, revealing darkness so complete it seemed to drink light. "But not the kind you think. Not blood. Not life. Memory."

Xiao Qing's hand tightened around Elder Xu's bone.

"What kind of memory?"

"Your most precious. The one you hold closest. The one that defines who you are." The darkness pulsed. "Give it up, and you may enter. Refuse, and you will never find what you seek."

She thought of her sister's face. The way Bai Suyin smiled when she thought no one was watching. The warmth of her hand when she'd pulled Xiao Qing from the river, all those centuries ago, when she was just a green snake drowning in her own fear.

She thought of the last time she'd seen her sister—the chains, the pagoda, Fahai's cold eyes.

"No," Xiao Qing said. "I won't give that up."

The door was silent for a long moment.

"Then you misunderstand," it said finally. "The library does not *take* memories. It *binds* them. You will still possess the memory. But you will not be able to access it. Not until you leave. And if you fail to leave..." The darkness shifted. "Then the memory remains here. Forever."

A prison for the mind.

Xiao Qing looked at the bone in her hand. Elder Xu had given his life for her. He'd burned so she could escape. She carried his last fragment with her, a promise made in ash.

But what would she be willing to sacrifice for her sister?

She already knew the answer.

"Which memory?"

"The one that makes you human."

---

She walked into the darkness.

The door closed behind her with a sound like a tomb sealing shut.

And then she was in the library.

Rows upon rows of shelves stretched in every direction, defying perspective. Books of every size and color lined them—some small enough to fit in a palm, others large enough to require ladders. The air smelled of dust and ink and something metallic. Blood, maybe. Or time.

A path opened before her, guiding her deeper.

She followed it.

The books on either side seemed to watch her as she passed. Their spines bore titles in languages she didn't recognize, scripts that shifted and changed when she tried to read them. But she caught fragments: *The Last Breath of Dying Stars. The Weight of Unspoken Words. The Geometry of Regret.*

Every book here held something forgotten.

Every book here was a tomb.

The path ended at a pedestal. On it rested a single scroll, bound with red silk that had faded to pink. Characters were embroidered into the silk—ancient script, predating the current celestial dynasty by millennia.

The original registry.

Xiao Qing's hands trembled as she reached for it.

"Touch it," a voice said, "and you accept the terms."

She turned. A figure stood in the shadows between two shelves—tall, impossibly thin, with skin the color of old paper. Its eyes were empty sockets filled with crawling text.

"The memory," the figure said. "Give it."

Xiao Qing closed her eyes.

She thought of the memory she was about to lose. The first time she'd taken human form. The rain on her skin. The terror of standing on two legs. And her sister's voice, soft and patient: *"You're beautiful, little one. Don't be afraid."*

That memory had carried her through five centuries of loneliness.

"Take it," she whispered.

Something reached into her mind. Cold. Precise. It found the memory, wrapped around it, and pulled.

She gasped. Pain exploded behind her eyes—not physical pain, but something worse. The pain of loss. The pain of a hole where something precious used to be.

And then it was gone.

She couldn't remember what she'd lost. She only knew that something was missing. Something important.

The figure bowed and vanished into the shadows.

The scroll was in her hands.

---

She opened it.

The registry was written on material that wasn't paper—something thinner and stronger, like dried serpent skin. The characters glowed faintly as she read them, names and dates and deeds stretching back to the beginning of the celestial order.

She found her sister's name near the middle.

*Bai Suyin. White Snake. Cultivator. Incarnation of the Serpent Queen's bloodline. Status: Sealed under Leifeng Pagoda by decree of the Celestial Court. Reason: Violation of the boundary between mortal and celestial realms.*

There was more. Details of the trial. Testimonies. A list of charges.

But at the bottom, written in a different hand—hastier, as if added as an afterthought—was a single line that made her blood freeze:

*Note: The sealing is incomplete. The White Snake's essence was divided. One third remains unaccounted for. Search ongoing.*

Her sister's soul.

Part of it was still out there.

Xiao Qing read the line again. And again. Her hands shook so hard the scroll rattled.

If she could find that missing piece, she could restore her sister. Not fully—not without breaking the seal entirely—but enough. Enough to speak to her. Enough to touch her. Enough to *save* her.

She looked for more information. The registry listed locations, investigation reports, the names of celestial agents assigned to the search. But the entries grew sparse toward the end, then stopped entirely.

The search had been abandoned.

Someone had decided that one third of a White Snake's soul wasn't worth finding.

Rage burned through her—cold and clean and sharp as a blade.

She rolled the scroll carefully, tucking it into her sleeve. Then she turned and walked back through the library, past the watching books, past the shifting shadows.

The door opened before she reached it.

"You found what you needed," the door said. It wasn't a question.

"I found a start." Xiao Qing stepped into the light. The forest swayed around her, the wrong trees bending in the wrong directions. "Now I need to find the rest."

"The memory will return to you once you leave this space."

It did. The moment her feet touched mortal soil, the memory flooded back—her sister's voice, the rain, the terror and joy of her first human step. She stumbled, overwhelmed, and caught herself against a tree.

The bark was warm. Alive.

She pressed her forehead against it and breathed.

*One third of her sister's soul. Lost. Forgotten. Hidden somewhere in the three realms.*

She would find it.

She would find it if she had to tear the celestial court apart stone by stone.

---

The golden bowl stopped humming.

The old hand withdrew, trembling.

"She found the registry," the voice whispered. "She knows."

Silence.

"Prepare the vessel. We have very little time."

In the distance, thunder rolled across a cloudless sky.

And in a pagoda that had stood for five hundred years, a White Snake stirred in her sleep for the first time in centuries.
