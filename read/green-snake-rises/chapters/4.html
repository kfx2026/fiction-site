Chapter 4: The Water Prison of Lost Souls

~2.0k words · 2026-06-22

The rain began three days after Xiao Qing entered the Eastern Sea.

Not ordinary rain. This rain fell upward. Droplets rose from the earth to meet the clouds, defying heaven itself as though the world had been turned inside out. The fishermen of the coastal villages knew what it meant, though none dared speak the name aloud.

Someone had awakened the Dragon King of the Eastern Sea.

Xiao Qing stood at the sea's edge, her robes soaked through, green hair plastered to her face. The waves before her did not crash. They waited. Towering walls of salt water held themselves frozen in mid-air, forming a corridor that stretched into darkness beneath the surface.

"Five hundred years," she whispered to no one. "Five hundred years, and still they build cages."

She stepped forward. The water parted.

---

The descent took longer than she expected. The Dragon King's palace sat at the deepest trench of the Eastern Sea, a place where light had never touched and pressure could crush immortals who had not trained their bodies to withstand it. Xiao Qing had spent three centuries hardening her scales, weaving metal through her skin until she could survive the crushing depths. But the cold was something else entirely.

This cold remembered her.

When she was young, barely three hundred years old, she had come here once with her sister. Bai Suyin had held her hand through the entire journey, whispering encouragement, shielding her from the worst of the pressure with her own power. Xiao Qing had been afraid then. She had hidden behind her sister's white robes and let the world see only the timid green snake who followed where the White Snake led.

No one hid behind anyone now.

The corridor opened into a vast chamber carved from black coral. Bioluminescent creatures clung to the walls, their pale blue light revealing the bones scattered across the floor. Human bones. Demon bones. Bones of creatures so ancient their marrow had turned to crystal. A path of crushed white led to a throne of living stone, and upon that throne sat a woman who had not moved for eight hundred years.

The Dragon King of the Eastern Sea was beautiful in the way that drowning was beautiful. Her skin shimmered with scales of deepest jade, and her eyes held the weight of ten thousand years of watching mortals die. She wore a crown of sea serpent teeth, and her fingers ended in claws sharp enough to tear through the veil between worlds.

"You dare come here," the Dragon King said. Her voice was the sound of waves breaking against cliffs. "You, the lesser sister. The shadow. The one who followed."

Xiao Qing did not bow.

"I've come for information, Your Majesty."

"Have you." The Dragon King's lips curved, but her eyes did not warm. "And what makes you think I would give anything to a creature who has not paid tribute to my court in five centuries? Your sister understood protocol. She came once every hundred years, brought gifts, showed proper respect. You never came at all."

"Because you would have killed me."

"Perhaps."

"Definitely." Xiao Qing stepped closer, ignoring the way the Dragon King's scales began to glow with warning. "My sister protected me from you. She knew you saw me as a threat to your bloodline, a lesser creature who might one day challenge your authority. She was right to keep me hidden. But she's not here anymore."

The Dragon King rose from her throne. The water around her began to boil.

"I know what happened to your sister, little snake. The entire realm knows. The White Snake, sealed beneath Leifeng Pagoda by a monk who should have been beneath her notice. It was pathetic. It was—"

"Fahai."

The name cut through the water like a blade. The Dragon King stopped.

"What did you say?"

"Fahai," Xiao Qing repeated. She let the name hang in the water between them. "The monk who sealed my sister. He was not beneath her notice. He was sent."

A long silence. The bioluminescent creatures dimmed their light, as if afraid to witness what might happen next.

"How do you know this?" the Dragon King asked. Her voice had changed. The amusement was gone, replaced by something colder.

"Because I found his master's tomb."

---

The tomb of Master Yuanzhi sat on a mountain that did not appear on any map. Xiao Qing had searched for it for four hundred years, following fragments of text, listening to the whispers of ghosts who had served the celestial court in ages past. She had found it by accident, chasing a demon through a valley that did not exist, and stumbled into a cave sealed with golden script that burned her scales when she tried to pass.

It took her sixty years to break that seal.

Inside, she found not a body, but a message. Carved into the walls of the cave, written in blood that still glowed with residual power, the truth of what had happened to her sister.

"Fahai was trained by the celestial court," Xiao Qing said. "Not the Buddha. Not the temples. The court itself. They chose him specifically to defeat the White Snake. They gave him the golden bowl, the incantations, the power to seal her. Your Majesty, why would the celestial court want my sister imprisoned?"

The Dragon King's claws tightened on her throne. Cracks spread through the stone.

"You speak of things that happened before my reign."

"Do not lie to me." Xiao Qing's voice hardened. "I have spent five centuries underground, eating dirt and drinking poison, turning my body into a weapon. I have crawled through the corpses of demons greater than you, seeking answers. I know you were present at the court's council when they decided to act against my sister. I know you voted to seal her."

The Dragon King's eyes flashed. "You know nothing."

"I know your son was executed by the celestial court three thousand years ago for the crime of loving a mortal woman. I know you have hated them ever since, but you are too weak to act. I know you voted to seal my sister because you thought it would keep your remaining children safe. You were wrong."

The water in the chamber began to churn. Whirlpools formed around the edges of the room, pulling at the bones scattered across the floor.

"You insolent—"

"They killed your son for love." Xiao Qing took another step forward. "They sealed my sister for helping a mortal. We are not enemies, Your Majesty. We have the same enemy."

The Dragon King's rage faltered. For just a moment, something human flickered in her ancient eyes. Grief. The kind that never healed, only scabbed over and waited to be picked open again.

"My son was gentle," she said. Her voice cracked. "He loved a fisherman's daughter. He gave up his immortality for her, knowing it would mean his death. The court did not even grant him the dignity of a quick execution. They drowned him in the River of Forgetfulness, over and over, until his soul dissolved."

"I know."

"How could you know? You were not there. You were barely a thousand years old when—"

"I asked the river." Xiao Qing reached into her robes and pulled out a jade vial filled with black water. "I walked to the edge of the River of Forgetfulness and I called your son's name. I did it a thousand times over three hundred years. On the thousandth call, a fragment of his soul rose to answer me."

The Dragon King's composure shattered. She stumbled forward, hands outstretched, tears mixing with the salt water around her face.

"You lie. You lie to manipulate me."

Xiao Qing held out the vial. "Take it. See for yourself."

The Dragon King snatched the vial from her hands. She held it up to the dim light, and within the black water, a tiny spark of gold flickered. A soul fragment. Barely enough to remember a name.

"He asked me to tell you something," Xiao Qing said. "He said—"

"Wait." The Dragon King held up her hand. Her entire body trembled. "Wait. Tell me later. I cannot—I need to—"

She turned away, clutching the vial to her chest. For a long moment, neither of them spoke. The only sound was the distant groan of the sea pressing against the palace walls.

When the Dragon King finally turned back, her face was composed, but her eyes were wet.

"Ask your questions, green snake. I will answer."

---

"Where is Fahai?"

"He retreated to a hidden temple in the Western Mountains after sealing your sister. The celestial court rewarded him with a place in their ranks, but he refused. He said his work was done."

"He was wrong."

"Yes." The Dragon King settled back onto her throne. "He was wrong. But he knew it. In the centuries since, I have heard that he meditates constantly, never sleeping, never eating. He waits for something."

"For me?"

"For anyone. He knows the seal will not hold forever. He knows someone will come."

"How do I break the seal?"

The Dragon King's expression shifted. Fear. Xiao Qing had never seen fear on the face of a dragon before.

"You cannot."

"I have five centuries of cultivation. I have turned my blood to poison and my bones to steel. I have—"

"You cannot break the seal because it was not made by Fahai alone." The Dragon King leaned forward. "The celestial court wove their power into that seal. Heaven itself enforces it. To break it, you would need to challenge the authority of the celestial court. You would need to become something greater than a demon. Greater than an immortal. You would need to become—"

The palace walls shook. A sound like thunder rolled through the deep sea, and the water around them began to drain away, rushing upward as if the sea itself was being pulled toward the surface.

"What is happening?" Xiao Qing demanded.

The Dragon King's face went pale. "He feels you. He knows you've returned."

"Who?"

Above them, the water split apart. A beam of golden light pierced through the depths, striking the palace floor and sending shockwaves through the coral. In the center of that light, a figure descended.

He wore white robes that did not touch the water. His feet hovered inches above the ground. His face was young, but his eyes held the weight of centuries.

Fahai.

"Xiao Qing," he said. His voice was calm. Kind. "I wondered when you would come out of hiding."

Xiao Qing's hands began to transform. Scales rippled across her skin. Her fangs elongated. Poison dripped from her fingers, eating through the coral beneath her feet.

"You sealed my sister."

"Yes."

"For five hundred years."

"Yes."

Fahai stepped forward, and the water around him began to freeze. Ice spread across the palace floor, crackling toward Xiao Qing's feet.

"I am not here to fight you, little snake. I am here to give you a choice."

"What choice?"

He smiled. It was the most terrible thing Xiao Qing had ever seen.

"Your sister's seal is weakening. Without my power to maintain it, she will break free within the decade. The celestial court has asked me to renew it permanently. To anchor it to something that cannot be destroyed."

"And?"

Fahai reached into his robes and pulled out a mirror. The glass was black, reflecting nothing.

"This mirror holds your sister's soul. Her memory. Her love. Everything that makes her who she is. If I shatter it, she will emerge from the pagoda as a mindless beast. A creature of pure power, with no will to control it."

Xiao Qing's blood ran cold.

"You would destroy her."
