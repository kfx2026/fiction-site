CHAPTER 10: The Price of Forgotten Things

The Library of Forgotten Things didn't want to be found.

Xiao Qing had spent three days walking south, then east, then in circles that made no logical sense. The mountains shifted around her like living things, peaks rearranging themselves when she wasn't looking. Rivers changed course. The stars above her spun in patterns she'd never seen in five centuries of watching the sky.

She was close. She could feel it.

The jade pendant pulsed against her chest, warm and urgent. Elder Xu's bone fragment rested in her sleeve, its residual qi humming like a trapped bee. She'd wrapped it in silk to keep it from attracting attention, but the thing had a will of its own. It wanted to go somewhere.

She let it guide her.

On the fourth day, she found herself standing at the edge of a cliff that shouldn't exist. The map she'd memorized showed flat plains here. The terrain she'd crossed suggested a valley. But here was a cliff, sheer and black, dropping into a fog so thick it looked solid.

The bone fragment in her sleeve vibrated violently.

"This is it," she said.

No response. The wind didn't blow. The birds didn't sing. The world held its breath.

Xiao Qing stepped off the cliff.

The fall lasted three heartbeats. Then the fog caught her—not breaking her fall, but *catching* her, cradling her like a mother's arms. She floated downward, suspended in whiteness that smelled of old paper and dried flowers.

When her feet touched ground, she was standing in a garden.

The Library of Forgotten Things wasn't a building. It was a grove of ancient trees, their trunks carved with characters so old they predated the current celestial script. Between the trees hung scrolls, thousands of them, suspended from branches by silk cords that had never frayed. Lanterns floated overhead, their flames blue and cold.

A woman sat in the center of the grove, writing on a scroll that stretched across her lap and pooled on the ground like a river of paper.

She was ancient. Not in appearance—her face was smooth, ageless, beautiful in the way that mountains are beautiful. But her eyes held depths that spoke of time beyond counting. She wore robes of grey that shifted between shadows, and her hair fell in a single braid that touched the ground and kept going, coiled in a pile beside her.

"You're early," she said without looking up.

Xiao Qing bowed. "I didn't know there was a schedule."

"There isn't." The woman dipped her brush in ink that seemed to absorb light. "But you're early anyway. The other one isn't due for another three hundred years."

"Other one?"

The woman finally looked up. Her eyes were silver, like coins worn smooth by countless hands. She studied Xiao Qing for a long moment, then smiled. It wasn't a warm smile.

"You carry death in your sleeve," she said. "And memory in your chest. And rage in your bones. You're the green snake. I've been expecting you since before you were born."

Xiao Qing's hand moved instinctively to her jade pendant. "Then you know why I'm here."

"I know what you think you need." The woman set down her brush. "The original registry. The one that predates the current administration. You want to find the truth about your sister."

"Yes."

"The truth is not what you think it is."

"Everyone keeps telling me that." Xiao Qing's voice sharpened. "I don't care. I'll find it myself."

The woman laughed, a sound like wind through dead leaves. "Bold. Stupid. But bold." She rose, and her scroll rolled up behind her without being touched. "You're right that the original registry exists. It's here, in this library. But nothing in this place is free. Everything has a price."

"I have currency." Xiao Qing reached into her sleeve.

"No." The woman's voice turned cold. "Not gold. Not jade. Not cultivation resources. The Library of Forgotten Things deals in only one currency."

She stepped closer, and Xiao Qing felt the temperature drop.

"Memories," the woman said. "You want a memory from the library. You must leave a memory of equal value."

Xiao Qing's blood ran cold. "Which memory?"

"That's not how it works." The woman circled her, robes brushing the ground. "The library chooses. I'm just the librarian. I can tell you the price, but I can't negotiate it."

"Then tell me."

The woman stopped in front of her. Those silver eyes met Xiao Qing's, and for a moment, Xiao Qing felt like she was falling again, falling through fog, falling through time.

"Your sister's face," the woman said. "The library wants the memory of your sister's face."

The world stopped.

"No," Xiao Qing whispered.

"Take a moment to consider." The woman's voice was gentle now, almost kind. "The memory of her face. That's the price. You'll remember that you had a sister. You'll remember her name, her voice, her love. But you won't be able to picture her. Her face will be a blank space in your mind. A hole where she used to be."

"Absolutely not."

"Then you'll never see the registry."

"I'll find another way."

"There is no other way." The woman's silver eyes held no mercy. "The original registry exists in only one place. This is that place. I am the only one who can retrieve it. And the price is non-negotiable."

Xiao Qing's hands were shaking. She clenched them into fists, felt her nails bite into her palms.

"Five hundred years," she said, her voice cracking. "Five hundred years I've held her face in my mind. Every detail. The curve of her smile. The way her eyes crinkled when she laughed. The way she looked at me like I was worth something, even when I was nothing. You're asking me to give that up."

"I'm not asking," the woman said. "I'm telling you the price. Whether you pay it is your choice."

Xiao Qing thought of Elder Xu, burning in those chains. Thought of the empty cell, the suppression runes, the celestial court that had rewritten her sister's history. Thought of the golden bowl in the temple that no longer existed.

She thought of her sister's face.

"I'll do it," she said.

The woman nodded, unsurprised. "Sit."

Xiao Qing sat cross-legged on the ground. The woman sat across from her, produced a silver needle from her sleeve. It was long, thin, glinting with cold light.

"This will hurt," she said.

"Everything hurts."

The needle touched Xiao Qing's temple.

The pain was indescribable. It wasn't physical—it was deeper, a tearing of something that had no name. Xiao Qing felt her mind being opened, felt hands sifting through her memories like someone rifling through papers. She tried to hold onto her sister's face, tried to grip it with every fiber of her being.

The needle pulled.

And her sister's face was gone.

Xiao Qing gasped, falling forward, catching herself on her hands. Her vision swam. She blinked, trying to remember—

She had a sister.

She knew that. She loved her sister. Her sister's name was Bai Suyin, and she was sealed under Leifeng Pagoda, and Xiao Qing had spent five centuries trying to free her.

But when she tried to picture her sister's face—

Nothing.

A blank. A void. A shape that should have been filled but wasn't.

Tears streamed down her face. She didn't know why she was crying. She just knew that something precious had been taken from her, and she would never get it back.

"The registry," she said, her voice raw.

The woman was already walking toward the largest tree in the grove. Its trunk was wider than a house, its bark covered in characters that glowed faintly. She placed her hand on the trunk, and a section of bark slid aside, revealing a hollow space.

Inside was a single scroll, bound with red silk.

"This is it," the woman said. "The original registry of demon cultivation. Compiled before the celestial court's reorganization. Before the rules were rewritten." She held it out. "Read it quickly. You have until the lanterns go dark."

Xiao Qing took the scroll with trembling hands. She unwound the silk, unrolled the paper.

The characters were ancient, written in vermillion ink that hadn't faded in millennia. She scanned them quickly, her heart pounding.

Names. Thousands of names. Demons who had cultivated in the early days, before the celestial court had established its monopoly on power. The registry listed their species, their cultivation methods, their achievements.

And then she found it.

*Bai Suyin. White serpent. Celestial-grade cultivation. Method: Jade Serpent Scripture (authenticated). Status: Ascended.*

Xiao Qing stared at the word. *Ascended.*

Not "sealed." Not "imprisoned." Not "executed for crimes against heaven."

*Ascended.*

She read the entry again, then again, her eyes tracing every character.

*Bai Suyin. White serpent. Celestial-grade cultivation. Method: Jade Serpent Scripture (authenticated). Status: Ascended.*

*Note: Subject achieved ascension through orthodox cultivation. No violations of celestial law recorded. Subject voluntarily entered sealing under Leifeng Pagoda per request of celestial court, terms to be negotiated.*

"Voluntarily," Xiao Qing breathed. "She volunteered."

"That's what it says," the woman said from behind her.

"But Fahai—the celestial court—they said she was a criminal. They said she—"

"The celestial court lies." The woman's voice was flat. "You know this. You've always known this."

Xiao Qing's hands were shaking so hard the scroll rattled. "Why would she volunteer? Why would she let them seal her?"

"Read the rest."

There was more. Smaller characters at the bottom of the entry, almost invisible against the aged paper.

*Terms of agreement: Bai Suyin agrees to enter sealing for period of one thousand years. In exchange, celestial court agrees to: (1) Grant protection to all serpent-kind for duration of sealing. (2) Grant safe passage and cultivation resources to subject's younger sister, Xiao Qing. (3) Maintain secrecy of this agreement until subject's release.*

*Witnessed and sealed by: The Celestial Emperor (previous administration).*

Xiao Qing's legs gave out. She sank to her knees, the scroll clutched to her chest.

Her sister had made a deal. A deal to protect her. A deal to protect all serpent-kind. And the celestial court had broken it. They'd rewritten history, vilified Bai Suyin, hunted serpent-kind to near extinction. They'd taken Xiao Qing's sister and made her a monster in the eyes of the three realms.

And Xiao Qing had spent five centuries thinking she was fighting to free an innocent prisoner.

She was.

But she hadn't known the full cost of that imprisonment.

"The previous Celestial Emperor," Xiao Qing said, her voice hollow. "Where is he?"

"Dead," the woman said. "Or missing. Depends on who you ask. The current administration claims he ascended to a higher realm. Others say he was assassinated."

"And the agreement?"

"Void. The current celestial court doesn't recognize any agreements made by the previous administration. They've been systematically erasing the old records, rewriting history to suit their narrative."

Xiao Qing looked down at the scroll. At her sister's name. At the proof that everything she'd believed was both true and false.

Her sister was innocent.

Her sister had sacrificed herself.

And the celestial court had betrayed her.
