📖 The Green Snake Rises by Xiao Qing

Chapter 7: The Price of Memory

~1.9k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The Library of Forgotten Things did not want to be found.

Xiao Qing had spent three days walking south, following the traces of old qi that only someone who had cultivated in complete isolation for five centuries could sense. The trail led her through forests that shifted when she wasn't looking, across rivers that flowed backward, and finally to a cliff that dropped into infinite mist.

She stood at the edge, the jade pendant warm against her chest. Elder Xu's bone fragment was still tucked in her sleeve, a constant reminder of the price others had paid for her quest.

"There's nothing here," she said aloud.

The mist did not answer.

She closed her eyes. Elder Xu had once told her that the most powerful things in the three realms were not hidden by barriers or guarded by beasts. They were hidden by *indifference*. A library that existed between realms would not respond to force. It would respond to *need*.

She thought of her sister. The way Bai Suyin had smiled when teaching her to transform. The way she'd held Xiao Qing's hand during the thunder tribulation. The way she'd looked at Fahai with something like pity in her final moments.

*I don't need the truth for myself,* she thought. *I need it for her.*

The mist parted.

A stone path materialized beneath her feet, leading downward into the cliff face. Xiao Qing did not hesitate. She stepped forward, and the world twisted around her.

---

The Library was not a building. It was a *space*—a vast, endless cavern where shelves stretched in every direction, defying gravity and logic. Books floated in clusters like constellations. Scrolls hung suspended in midair, their contents visible only to those who knew how to look.

And in the center of it all sat a figure.

She was old. Not in the way of mortals, with wrinkles and gray hair, but in the way of ancient things—her skin had the texture of parchment, her eyes the depth of inkwells. She wore robes of shifting gray, and her fingers were long and bony, like the roots of an old tree.

"A visitor," the woman said. Her voice was dry, like pages turning. "It has been... a very long time."

Xiao Qing bowed. "I seek the original registry of the celestial court. Specifically, the records from the White Snake's trial."

The woman's eyes flickered with something like amusement. "Bold. Most who come here ask for cultivation manuals or forbidden techniques. You ask for *bureaucracy*."

"The truth is often found in paperwork."

"A girl after my own heart." The woman rose, her movements fluid despite her ancient appearance. "I am the Keeper. This is my domain. And you cannot enter without a key."

"The key requires a sacrifice."

"Correct." The Keeper circled Xiao Qing, studying her. "You've already made one sacrifice, I see. A piece of bone from an old cultivator. A fragment of memory." She stopped, tilting her head. "But that was for *finding* me. Not for entering."

"What do you want?"

The Keeper smiled, and it was not a pleasant expression. "I want what you carry in your chest, little snake. Not your life. Something smaller. A memory. The most painful one you have."

Xiao Qing's blood ran cold. "Why?"

"Because pain is the currency of truth. The Library feeds on it. The older the pain, the deeper the record it can access." The Keeper leaned closer. "You have a memory from five hundred years ago. The moment you realized you couldn't save her. I can see it in your aura—a crack that never healed."

She was right. Xiao Qing could still feel it—the moment she'd arrived at Leifeng Pagoda, only to see the last stone fall into place. The moment she'd heard her sister's voice through the stone, telling her to *run*.

"Take it," Xiao Qing said. Her voice was steady, but her hands were shaking.

"Are you certain? Once given, it cannot be returned. You will remember the event, but the *pain* will be gone. The lesson will fade. You may find yourself making the same mistakes again."

"I said take it."

The Keeper reached out and pressed her palm against Xiao Qing's forehead.

The world went white.

---

She was standing at the foot of Leifeng Pagoda.

The sky was gray, heavy with rain that refused to fall. The air smelled of incense and blood. Monks chanted in a language she couldn't understand, their voices rising and falling like waves.

And there, at the top of the pagoda, was Bai Suyin.

She was beautiful even in chains. Her white robes were torn, her hair loose, her face calm. She looked down at Xiao Qing with an expression of infinite sadness.

"Little sister," Bai Suyin said. Her voice carried despite the chanting. "You shouldn't have come."

"I'm not leaving you."

"You must. Fahai has already won. This was always going to be the ending."

Xiao Qing tried to fly upward, but something held her back. Invisible chains, woven from the same suppression runes that held her sister. She struggled, screamed, clawed at the air—

"Remember what I taught you," Bai Suyin said. "When the world tries to trap you, become water. Flow around it. And when the time is right—"

"*Flood it*," Xiao Qing whispered.

Bai Suyin smiled. "Yes."

The last stone fell into place. The pagoda sealed. The chanting stopped. And Xiao Qing was left alone, her sister's voice echoing in her head.

*Run.*

She ran.

And she never stopped running.

---

The memory faded.

Xiao Qing opened her eyes. She was on her knees in the Library, gasping. The Keeper stood over her, holding something that glowed like a pearl—the extracted memory.

"Fascinating," the Keeper murmured. "Your sister knew she was going to lose. And she still chose to fight." She looked at Xiao Qing with new respect. "That kind of love is rare."

"Give me the registry."

The Keeper gestured, and a scroll floated down from the upper shelves. It was old—older than anything Xiao Qing had ever seen. The parchment was yellowed, the edges frayed. It unrolled in the air, revealing columns of names and judgments.

"Bai Suyin," the Keeper said. "Let me see."

She scanned the scroll, her bony finger tracing the characters. Then she stopped.

"Oh."

"What?" Xiao Qing demanded. "What does it say?"

The Keeper looked up, her expression unreadable. "According to this registry, your sister was never supposed to be sealed. The original judgment was *execution*. Fahai changed it."

"That's not surprising. He was always merciful."

"There's more." The Keeper's voice dropped. "The original accuser in the trial—the one who brought the initial charges against Bai Suyin—was not the celestial court. It was a mortal."

Xiao Qing's blood went cold. "A mortal? Impossible. Mortals don't have standing in celestial court."

"They do if they're a cultivator who has transcended mortality but refused ascension. Someone who chose to remain in the mortal realm." The Keeper squinted at the text. "The name is here. It's... faded. Deliberately scratched out. But I can read the remnants."

"Tell me."

"Xu Xian."

The world stopped.

Xiao Qing stared at the Keeper, her mind refusing to process the name. "That's not possible. Xu Xian loved her. He married her. He—"

"He was also terrified of her." The Keeper rolled the scroll closed. "Fear and love are not mutually exclusive, little snake. And fear often wins."

"But he *died*. He died of old age, decades before Fahai sealed her. How could he have—"

"He didn't die." The Keeper's voice was flat. "The records show that Xu Xian faked his death and continued cultivating in secret. He reached a level of power that rivaled the celestial court. And he used that power to destroy your sister."

Xiao Qing's hands clenched into fists. "Where is he now?"

"I don't know. The records end fifty years ago. But there is one thing I can tell you." The Keeper leaned forward. "The suppression runes on Leifeng Pagoda? The ones that hold your sister? They were designed by Xu Xian. He knew her weaknesses better than anyone."

The jade pendant burned against Xiao Qing's chest. The bone fragment in her sleeve seemed to pulse with anger.

Five hundred years.

Five hundred years of believing her sister's lover had been a victim. Five hundred years of blaming Fahai, blaming the celestial court, blaming everyone except the one person who had the most to gain.

Xu Xian.

"Where do I find him?" Xiao Qing's voice was barely a whisper.

"I told you. The records end fifty years ago. He vanished." The Keeper paused. "But there is a rumor. A temple in the eastern mountains. One that appears only during the new moon. They say an immortal lives there, studying the nature of *transformation*."

"Transformation?"

"Turning mortals into monsters. Turning love into hate." The Keeper's eyes glittered. "If I were you, I'd start there."

Xiao Qing stood. Her legs were unsteady, but her resolve was iron. She bowed once to the Keeper.

"I owe you a debt."

"You paid it with your pain." The Keeper held up the glowing pearl. "I'll keep this safe. Perhaps, when this is over, you'll want it back."

"I won't."

She turned and walked toward the exit. The Library's space shifted around her, guiding her back to the cliff's edge. When she stepped out, the mist closed behind her, and the path vanished.

The stars were out. The wind was cold. And somewhere in the eastern mountains, a temple was waiting.

Xiao Qing began to walk.

---

She traveled through the night, her mind racing. Xu Xian. The man her sister had loved. The man who had held her hand and watched her smile and whispered sweet words into her ear.

And then betrayed her.

Why? What could possibly drive someone to destroy the person they loved most?

She didn't have an answer. But she would find one.

By dawn, she reached the foothills of the eastern mountains. The terrain was treacherous—rockslides, hidden ravines, and the lingering qi of old battles. But she pressed on, following the Keeper's instructions.

The new moon was three days away.

She found a cave to shelter in as the sun rose. Her body was exhausted, her cultivation drained. She needed rest.

But sleep did not come easily.

Every time she closed her eyes, she saw Xu Xian's face. Not as he was—an old man, a corpse—but as he had been. Young. Handsome. Kind.

*Liar,* she thought. *You were always a liar.*

She touched the bone fragment in her sleeve. Elder Xu's last words echoed in her mind.

*Remember who you are.*

"I remember," she whispered. "I'm the one who's going to end this."

---

She woke to the sound of footsteps.

Her eyes snapped open. The cave was dark, the fire she'd built reduced to embers. But she could sense someone outside. Someone powerful.

She rose silently, her claws extending.

"Xiao Qing," a voice said. Familiar. Too familiar.

She stepped out of the cave.

The man standing there was young. Handsome. Dressed in white robes that seemed to glow in the moonlight.

Xu Xian.

Except it couldn't be.
