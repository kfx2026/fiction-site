CHAPTER 13: The Price of a Name

The Library of Forgotten Things did not want to be found.

Xiao Qing had spent three days walking south, following the pull of old blood and older magic. The jade pendant in her sleeve pulsed with each step, guiding her through forests that shifted when she blinked, across rivers that flowed uphill, past villages that appeared only in her peripheral vision and vanished when she turned to look.

She was being tested. The library only revealed itself to those who proved they deserved to find it.

On the third night, she stopped beneath a gnarled willow whose branches hung heavy with frost despite the summer heat. The pendant grew warm against her skin. She sat cross-legged at the tree's base and closed her eyes.

*You seek knowledge,* a voice said. It came from everywhere and nowhere. *But knowledge has a weight. Are you prepared to carry it?*

"I've been carrying it for five hundred years," Xiao Qing said. "Show me what you have."

The tree's roots shifted. A door opened in the earth, spiraling downward into darkness. She descended.

---

The Library of Forgotten Things was not a building. It was a wound in reality, a tear where memories went to die. Books floated in mid-air, their pages turning on their own. Scrolls hung from invisible threads. The air smelled of dust and lightning and something metallic that made her teeth ache.

A figure stood at the center of the chaos—an old woman with hair of silver and eyes of gold. She wore robes that shifted colors, never settling on one. When she smiled, her teeth were too sharp.

"Xiao Qing," the woman said. "I wondered when you'd come."

"Do I know you?"

"No. But I know you. I know everyone who comes here. I am the Librarian. I keep what the world forgets." She gestured to the floating books. "Everything that has been erased, rewritten, or buried—it all ends up here. Including your sister."

Xiao Qing's hand went to the jade pendant. "The original registry. Where is it?"

The Librarian's smile widened. "Straight to business. Good. I hate small talk." She turned and walked through the maze of floating texts. Xiao Qing followed.

They passed sections that made her skin crawl—books that whispered her name, scrolls that bled when she looked at them, a tome bound in what appeared to be human skin. The Librarian stopped before a pedestal made of black stone. On it sat a single scroll, yellowed with age, tied with a red ribbon.

"The original registry of the demon cultivation world," the Librarian said. "Dated seven hundred and twelve years ago. Before the celestial court's purge. Before your sister's name was stricken from every official record."

Xiao Qing reached for it. The Librarian's hand shot out and grabbed her wrist.

"Nothing in this library is free. You know that."

"What do you want?"

The Librarian studied her with those golden eyes. "Tell me a story I haven't heard."

"I don't have time for games."

"Neither do I. But I have eternity, and you don't." She released Xiao Qing's wrist. "Tell me a secret. One you've never told anyone. Something true. Something that hurts."

Xiao Qing stared at her. The silence stretched.

"I killed my mother," she said finally.

The Librarian's eyebrows rose. "Go on."

"She was a green snake of the Western Marshes. Weak. Cowardly. She abandoned my sister and me when we were still eggs. Left us to hatch alone in a pond that was drying up." Xiao Qing's voice was flat. "I found her three hundred years later, living in a human village, pretending to be mortal. She was married to a fisherman. Had two children. Human children."

"What did you do?"

"I waited until night. Until the children were asleep. Until the fisherman was drunk." Xiao Qing met the Librarian's eyes. "I asked her why she left us. She said she didn't remember any eggs. Said I must be mistaken. Said she'd never been a snake."

"And?"

"And I showed her what she'd forgotten." Xiao Qing's hand moved to her own throat, where a thin scar ran beneath her jaw. "She screamed. The fisherman woke up. I killed him first. Then I killed her. I burned the house down with their children inside."

The Librarian was quiet for a long moment. Then she laughed—a sharp, jagged sound. "Beautiful. The cruelty of abandoned children. I'll add it to my collection."

She stepped aside. The scroll was within reach.

Xiao Qing picked it up. The ribbon dissolved at her touch. She unrolled it, and the words glowed—names, hundreds of them, written in a script so old it predated the current celestial dynasty. She scanned down, her heart pounding.

*Lady Bai Suyin, Serpent of Mount Emei, Heir of the Western Water Dragon Lineage...*

Her sister's name. Still there. Still legible.

But beneath it, someone had written in fresh ink:

*NOTICE: This record has been superseded. Subject Bai Suyin is hereby classified as a Threat to Celestial Order. All prior achievements, titles, and honors are void. Punishment: Eternal imprisonment under Leifeng Pagoda. Terms: No visitation. No appeals. No exceptions.*

Xiao Qing's hands shook. "Who wrote this?"

"The current administration," the Librarian said. "They've been updating the old records for decades. Erasing anyone who opposed them. Your sister wasn't the first. She won't be the last."

"No visitation?"

"None permitted. The pagoda is warded against all forms of communication. Even the celestial court can't reach her."

"Then how do I free her?"

The Librarian tilted her head. "You don't. The pagoda was built by the Jade Emperor himself, reinforced by the Four Heavenly Kings, sealed with the blood of thirty-three celestial generals. It's not a prison. It's a tomb."

Xiao Qing rolled the scroll carefully. "There has to be a way."

"There is." The Librarian's voice dropped. "But you won't like it."

"Tell me."

"If you cannot reach your sister, you must bring the prison to you. Break the foundation of Leifeng Pagoda—not the structure itself, but the space it occupies. Collapse the boundary between her prison and the mortal realm."

"That would destroy everything within a thousand li."

"Yes."

Xiao Qing was silent.

"The celestial court would notice," the Librarian continued. "They would send every general, every god, every immortal under their command. You would face an army the likes of which hasn't been seen since the War of the Three Realms. And you would lose."

"Then why suggest it?"

"Because desperation makes us stupid." The Librarian smiled again, that sharp-toothed smile. "Or brave. Depends on the perspective."

Xiao Qing tucked the scroll into her sleeve. "Is there anything else I should know?"

The Librarian was quiet for a moment. "There is a boy."

"A boy?"

"Born fifteen years ago in a village on the eastern coast. He has no cultivation, no power, no name that matters. But he carries a fragment of your sister's soul."

Xiao Qing's blood went cold. "What?"

"The night she was sealed, she tore a piece of herself free. A sliver of her consciousness, wrapped in a thread of her qi. She sent it into the mortal realm, hoping it would find someone worthy. It found a newborn child instead."

"Where is he?"

"He's been in hiding his whole life. The celestial court doesn't know he exists. But they will, soon. Someone's been asking questions. Someone's been looking."

"Who?"

The Librarian's eyes flickered. "Someone who knows what he carries. Someone who wants to use him to reach your sister." She paused. "Someone who's been waiting for you to come out of hiding."

A chill ran down Xiao Qing's spine. "Show me."

The Librarian raised her hand. The floating books parted. A mirror appeared, its surface rippling like water. In it, Xiao Qing saw a village by the sea. A boy with dark hair and dark eyes, perhaps fourteen or fifteen, carrying a basket of fish. He looked up, as if sensing he was being watched.

His eyes were blue.

Bai Suyin's eyes.

"He doesn't know what he is," the Librarian said. "He thinks he's an orphan. His foster parents died three years ago in a plague. He's been surviving on his own."

"Who's looking for him?"

"A woman. Calls herself the Crimson Crane. Used to be a general in the celestial army, before she was exiled for treason. She's been building a rebellion in the shadows. She wants the boy—wants to use his connection to your sister to break the pagoda herself."

"For what purpose?"

"Power. Revenge. Who knows?" The Librarian shrugged. "She's been gathering artifacts, allies, secrets. She's patient. Smart. And she's been following your movements since you left Mount Emei."

Xiao Qing's hand went to her sword. "She's been watching me?"

"Of course. You're the only person who knows the boy exists. She needs you to lead her to him."

The mirror flickered. The image of the boy faded, replaced by a woman in crimson robes, standing on a cliff overlooking the sea. Her face was sharp, her eyes colder than winter. She was looking directly at the mirror.

At Xiao Qing.

"Found you," the woman mouthed.

The mirror shattered.

Xiao Qing stumbled back. Shards of glass fell at her feet, reflecting fragments of her face. The Librarian clapped her hands together, and the shards dissolved into mist.

"She knows you've seen her," the Librarian said. "She'll accelerate her plans now."

"How long do I have?"

"Three days. Maybe less. The boy is in a village called Tide's End, on the eastern coast. If the Crimson Crane reaches him before you do—"

"He'll be used as a weapon."

"Worse." The Librarian's voice was barely a whisper. "She'll kill him to extract the soul fragment. Your sister will die with him."

Xiao Qing turned toward the exit. The spiral staircase rose before her, leading back to the surface.

"One more thing," the Librarian called.

Xiao Qing paused.

"The boy's name. You'll need it to find him."

She waited.

The Librarian's smile was almost sad. "His name is Bai Yulong. White Dragon. Your sister gave him her name, her eyes, and a piece of her soul. She's been waiting for him to grow up."

Xiao Qing's heart clenched.

"Waiting for what?"

The Librarian's voice echoed as the library began to fade.

"For you to find him. For you to bring him home."

---

Xiao Qing emerged from the earth as the sun rose.

The forest was gone. She stood in a field of white flowers, their petals stained red at the edges. The pendant around her neck was glowing so bright it cast shadows.

She looked east, toward the sea.

Three days. A boy with her sister's eyes. A woman who wanted to use him.

Xiao Qing began to run.

Behind her, the Library of Forgotten Things folded itself back into nothing, leaving no trace that it had ever existed. The Librarian watched her go from the darkness between worlds, her golden eyes unblinking.

"Let's see if you're as dangerous as your sister," she murmured.

And in the shadows of the library, a scroll that Xiao Qing had missed unrolled itself. It was blank—except for a single line, written in blood:

*The Green Snake rises. The White Dragon waits
