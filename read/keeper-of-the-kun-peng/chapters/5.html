📖 Keeper of the Kun Peng by Yu Zhou

Chapter 5: The Weight of a God's Promise

~1.7k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The Celestial Court did not have walls.

It had clouds that moved like guards, and pillars of jade that stretched into a sky that wasn't really a sky. Hai Ping stood at the edge of a platform that floated above an abyss of stars. Below him, galaxies spun slow and lazy, like fish in a deep pond.

He tried not to look down.

"Stop fidgeting." Bi Fang's voice came from beside him, sharp as broken glass. The bird-woman had not spoken a word during their entire ascent through the clouds. Now she stood with her arms crossed, her red-feathered cloak shifting in a wind that came from nowhere.

"I'm not fidgeting," Hai Ping said. His hands were shaking. He shoved them into his pockets.

"You're thinking about falling."

"I'm thinking about how far it is to the ground."

"There is no ground." Bi Fang's eyes, golden and slit-pupiled, fixed on him. "Only the void between worlds. If you fall, you will fall forever. The gods will watch you shrink to a speck. Then nothing."

"Thanks. That helps."

She almost smiled. Almost.

A horn sounded from somewhere ahead. Deep and resonant, like a whale singing through miles of water. The clouds parted, and Hai Ping saw them.

The gods.

They sat on thrones of carved lightning and fossilized thunder. Some looked like men, but wrong—too tall, too still, their faces carved from stone that had forgotten how to feel. Others were not human at all. A serpent with twelve heads coiled around a pillar, each pair of eyes watching him from a different angle. A woman made of water sat in a floating pool, her form shifting between shapes. A man with antlers of bronze held a spear that crackled with blue fire.

And at the center, on a throne made from a single pearl the size of a fishing boat, sat the Emperor of Heaven.

He looked old. That was Hai Ping's first thought. Old in a way that had nothing to do with years. His face was smooth, his beard black as ink, his robes embroidered with dragons that seemed to move in the corner of the eye. But his eyes—those eyes had seen too much. They held the weight of ten thousand years, and they did not blink.

"Approach, Keeper of the Kun Peng."

The Emperor's voice was soft. It should not have carried across the vast hall. But it did. It pressed against Hai Ping's ears, his chest, his bones.

He walked. One step. Another. The gods watched. The serpent's twelve heads tracked his movement. The water woman rippled. The bronze-antlered man tapped his spear against the floor, a sound like a bell tolling.

Hai Ping stopped ten paces from the throne. He did not bow. He could not. His legs would not obey.

"Your name," the Emperor said.

"Hai Ping."

The Emperor smiled. It was not a kind smile. "The son of a fisherman. From a village so small it does not appear on celestial maps. And yet here you stand, bonded to the oldest creature in existence."

"I didn't ask for it."

"No. You did not." The Emperor leaned forward. The pearl throne groaned. "But the seal chose you. The Kun Peng accepted you. That is not nothing. That is everything."

Silence. The gods waited.

"You will tell me what you saw," the Emperor said. "In the depths. When you touched the creature's mind."

Hai Ping thought of the darkness. The cold that was not cold. The vastness that pressed against his consciousness like an ocean pressing against a paper boat. The voice that had spoken without words.

*Sleep. I am sleeping. Do not wake me.*

"I saw a fish," Hai Ping said. "It was sleeping."

"A fish." The Emperor's smile did not change. "You saw a fish."

"It's the truth."

"The truth." The Emperor stood. He was taller than any man Hai Ping had ever seen. His shadow swallowed the platform. "Do you know what the Kun Peng is, boy? Do you know what it means?"

Hai Ping said nothing.

"The Kun Peng is the boundary between order and chaos. It sleeps, and the world is stable. It wakes, and the world floods. For ten thousand years, it has slept. For ten thousand years, mortals have farmed and fished and died without ever knowing the thing that holds their existence together." The Emperor stepped closer. "And now you, a fisherman's son, have touched its mind. You have claimed it. You have become its Keeper."

"I didn't claim anything."

"You did. The seal does not lie. You carry its mark."

The Emperor raised his hand. Light flickered between his fingers. Hai Ping felt something pull at his chest, at the spot where the seal had burned itself into his skin. He looked down.

The mark was glowing. Blue and silver, pulsing like a heartbeat.

"Interesting," the Emperor said. "It responds to me. But it does not obey me."

"Maybe it doesn't like you."

The words came out before Hai Ping could stop them. The hall went silent. Even the twelve-headed serpent stopped moving.

Bi Fang made a sound like a choked laugh.

The Emperor stared at him. For a long moment, nothing happened. Then the Emperor laughed. It was a terrible sound, like rocks grinding together at the bottom of the sea.

"You have courage," he said. "Or stupidity. Perhaps both. It does not matter. What matters is what happens next."

He turned and walked back to his throne. The pearl groaned as he sat.

"Three days ago, a star fell from the heavens. It struck the mountains west of your village. The impact shattered the peaks and killed every living thing within a hundred li."

Hai Ping's blood went cold.

"Your village was not harmed," the Emperor said. "Not this time. But the star was not a natural occurrence. It was a message. From the Northern Wastes."

"Who sent it?"

"A god who wants the Kun Peng to wake."

The Emperor gestured. The clouds shifted, and an image formed in the air. A man—or what had once been a man. His skin was cracked like dried earth. His eyes were pits of darkness. He stood on a mountain of bones, and behind him, the sky burned green.

"His name is Gong Gong. An ancient god. A god of destruction. He was sealed away three thousand years ago for trying to break the sky. Now he has broken his seal. And he wants the Kun Peng."

"Why?"

"Because if the Kun Peng wakes, the world floods. And in the flood, Gong Gong can reshape creation. Make it in his image. A world of chaos and blood and endless storm."

Hai Ping's throat was dry. "What do you want from me?"

"Keep the Kun Peng asleep."

"Isn't that what I'm doing?"

"No." The Emperor's voice hardened. "You are keeping it calm. There is a difference. The Kun Peng is not a pet. It is not a weapon. It is a force of nature, and you are a boy who happened to touch its mind. If Gong Gong reaches it before you can truly bond with it—if he wakes it with force—the creature will thrash. The oceans will rise. Billions will die."

Hai Ping felt the weight of those words settle on his shoulders. They were heavier than the sky.

"What do I need to do?"

"Train. Learn. Grow." The Emperor waved his hand. "You will remain in the Celestial Court. You will be taught by Bi Fang. You will learn to control the bond. You will learn to speak to the Kun Peng not as a master, but as a friend."

"And if I can't?"

The Emperor's eyes went dark. "Then I will find another way."

He did not explain what that meant. He did not need to.

The audience was over. The gods began to rise. The serpent uncoiled. The water woman dissolved into mist. Bi Fang grabbed Hai Ping's arm and pulled him away.

"Come," she said. "We have work to do."

They walked through corridors of cloud. Past gardens where flowers sang. Past libraries where books flew like birds. Past warriors made of stone who turned their heads to watch him pass.

Hai Ping's mind was spinning. Gong Gong. The star. The flood. Billions dead.

"I can't do this," he said.

Bi Fang stopped. She turned to face him, her golden eyes burning.

"Then you will die. The Kun Peng will wake. And everything you have ever known will be destroyed."

"Thanks for the encouragement."

"I am not encouraging you." She leaned closer. "I am telling you the truth. The Celestial Court does not care about you. They care about the creature. If you fail, they will kill you and bond someone else. Someone stronger. Someone who will not hesitate."

"Then why haven't they done that already?"

"Because the seal chose you. Only you. When you die, the seal will break. It will take a hundred years to form again. A hundred years of chaos, of gods fighting over the power, of mortals dying in wars that are not their own."

She straightened. "So you will not die. You will learn. You will grow. You will become the Keeper the Kun Peng needs."

"I'm a fisherman's son."

"Then learn to fish for gods."

She turned and walked away. Hai Ping stood alone in the corridor of clouds, the mark on his chest still pulsing, the weight of the world pressing down on him like the deep ocean.

He thought of his father. His boat. The nets that had been mended a hundred times.

He thought of the creature sleeping at the bottom of the sea.

And he thought of Gong Gong, the god of destruction, standing on his mountain of bones, waiting for the flood.

Somewhere, deep in the Northern Ocean, the Kun Peng stirred in its sleep.

And in the Celestial Court, a boy who had never wanted to be special learned that some choices are not choices at all.

They are sentences.

Bi Fang was waiting at the end of the corridor. She did not look back. She did not need to.

She knew he would follow.
