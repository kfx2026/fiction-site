CHAPTER 7: The Price of Names

The black light from Jiang Chen's staff ripped through Lin Mei like fire through dry grass. She screamed—a sound that wasn't entirely human, layered with echoes of every voice she'd ever stolen.

He should have pulled back. Should have given her space to recover, to negotiate, to keep the deal alive.

Instead, he pushed harder.

The staff sank deeper into her spectral chest, and he felt something break. A seal. A lock. Something she'd been hiding behind for years.

Her form flickered. For a split second, he saw her true face—not the middle-aged woman she'd stolen, not the young girl she'd once been, but the moment of her death. Thirty years old. Strangled. Her body dumped in a river.

Then the mask snapped back into place.

"Stop!" She clawed at the staff, her fingers passing through the black energy. "You'll destroy me!"

"That's the idea."

"You'll lose the names forever!"

He hesitated. The staff wavered.

She saw it. Her eyes—those dead, calculating eyes—locked onto his hesitation like a wolf spotting weakness. "They're in my head. All of them. The judge who signed your death warrant. The prosecutor who fabricated evidence. The witness who lied." She gasped, her form flickering again. "Kill me, and you'll never know."

Jiang Chen's grip tightened on the staff. "You'll tell me anyway. Before I take you back."

"You think I'm afraid of Diyu?" She laughed, bloodless and bitter. "I've been running from them for seventeen years. I know every loophole, every delay, every appeal. Your precious judges can't touch me without evidence, and the only evidence is in my head." She tapped her temple. "Which I will erase the moment I feel them prying."

He'd heard of soul erasure. A technique some ghosts used to destroy their own memories before capture. It left them hollow, empty shells—but it also left their captors with nothing.

"You wouldn't."

"Watch me." Her eyes went cold. "I've lived this long by always having an exit. This is mine. You take me to Diyu, I wipe my mind. You take the names from me here, I wipe them too. The only way you get what you want is if you help me escape."

Jiang Chen's hands shook. The staff felt like lead.

"You want revenge," she said softly. "I can feel it in you. That cold, quiet rage. The kind that builds for years because you never let yourself feel it. They took your life, Jiang Chen. They took your future. They made you a murderer in the eyes of everyone who knew you."

"Shut up."

"Your mother. Does she visit your grave? Does she know the truth?"

"I said shut up."

"She probably blames herself. Thinks she raised a monster. Cries herself to sleep every night wondering where she went wrong."

He swung the staff. She dodged, barely, the black energy grazing her shoulder. She hissed in pain.

"Hit a nerve?"

"You know nothing about my mother."

"I know she's alive. I know she visits the cemetery every Sunday. I know she leaves oranges at your grave because those were your favorite as a child." Lin Mei smiled. "I saw it in your memories when I tried to possess you. You love her. You'd do anything to clear your name so she doesn't have to carry that shame."

Jiang Chen's vision blurred. He blinked rapidly, refusing to let the tears fall.

"Help me escape," she said, "and I'll give you everything. The names. The evidence. The location of every document, every witness, every piece of proof you need to overturn your case. You'll die again when your time comes, but you'll die innocent. Your mother will know the truth."

"And you?" His voice cracked. "You'll keep stealing bodies. Keep conning families. Keep killing anyone who gets in your way."

"Probably."

"Then I'd be responsible for every person you hurt from now on."

She shrugged. "That's the price of your justice."

He stared at her. This woman—this monster—had killed two soul reapers. She'd stolen a woman's body and worn it like a costume for three years. She'd taken everything from the family she'd conned, leaving them broken and bankrupt.

And now she was offering him his life back.

Not literally. He was still dead. But his name. His honor. The truth.

Was it worth the cost?

"One condition," he said slowly.

Her eyebrow raised.

"You stop possessing people. From now on, you find other ways to hide. You don't kill anyone else."

"And when the judges send another reaper after me?"

"I'll handle it."

She laughed. "You'll protect me? A reaper protecting a rogue soul?"

"I'm not protecting you. I'm making sure you don't add to your debt before I collect it."

"Collect it?"

"When I get what I need, I'm coming for you. And next time, I won't listen to a word you say."

She studied him for a long moment. Her eyes searched his face, looking for the lie, the trap, the angle. She found nothing because there was nothing. He meant every word.

"Deal," she said.

She extended her hand again. Same gesture as before. Same trap.

Jiang Chen grabbed her wrist—not her hand, but her wrist, where he could feel her pulse (ghosts didn't have pulses, but stolen bodies did) and pulled her close.

"The names. Start talking."

"Here? In the middle of a cemetery?"

"Now. Before I change my mind."

She sighed, but there was a flicker of respect in her eyes. "Fine. But not all of them. Just one. Enough to prove I'm telling the truth."

"One name."

"Judge Zhang Wei. He presides over the Third Criminal Court in Jiangzhou. He's the one who signed your death warrant."

Jiang Chen's blood ran cold. He knew that name. Everyone in Jiangzhou knew that name. Judge Zhang was famous—revered, even. He'd presided over dozens of high-profile cases, always delivering harsh sentences for corruption. He'd built his career on being incorruptible.

"Impossible."

"Impossible?" Lin Mei laughed. "He's the most corrupt judge in the province. He just hides it better than the rest."

"Prove it."

"Look at your case file. The evidence was submitted by his nephew. The witness was a paid informant who worked for his brother-in-law. The prosecutor was his golfing partner." She counted on her fingers. "Every single person involved in your conviction was connected to him. You just didn't see it because you were too busy being terrified."

Jiang Chen's mind raced. He tried to remember the trial. The faces. The names. It had all been a blur of fear and confusion, of lawyers speaking in legal jargon he couldn't understand, of a gavel falling and a sentence being read.

But she was right about one thing. He hadn't looked. He'd been too scared, too broken, too convinced of his own guilt to question anything.

"Why?" he whispered. "Why would he want me dead?"

"That's the question, isn't it?" Lin Mei's eyes gleamed. "What did a nobody accountant do to earn the attention of one of the most powerful judges in Jiangzhou?"

He didn't know. He'd worked for a small firm, handling books for local businesses. He'd taken a bribe once—fifty thousand yuan to overlook some discrepancies in a client's tax returns. It was stupid. It was wrong. But it wasn't murder.

"Think." Lin Mei's voice dropped. "Think about your clients. Think about who might have connections to Judge Zhang. Think about what you might have seen without realizing it."

His mind went blank. Then, slowly, a memory surfaced.

A client. A man named Chen Wei. He'd come to the firm six months before Jiang Chen's arrest, asking for help with his accounts. Nothing unusual. But there was something about his books that didn't add up. Discrepancies that went beyond simple tax evasion. Large sums of money moving through shell companies, then disappearing.

Jiang Chen had noticed. He'd even asked about it.

And Chen Wei had smiled, patted him on the shoulder, and said, "Don't worry about it. Just do your job."

A week later, Jiang Chen was arrested for murder.

"Chen Wei," he breathed.

Lin Mei's smile widened. "Now we're getting somewhere."

"Who is he?"

"Chen Wei is Judge Zhang's silent partner. He handles the money. Moves it around. Keeps it clean. Or tries to." She leaned closer. "When you asked about those discrepancies, you became a liability. You didn't know enough to be a threat, but you knew enough to be a risk. So they eliminated you."

"They framed me for murder."

"They framed you for a murder they committed. The victim was a woman who'd found evidence of their scheme. She was going to go to the authorities. So they killed her, then pinned it on you."

Jiang Chen felt sick. He'd been executed for a crime he didn't commit, committed by the people who'd set him up. And the man who'd sentenced him was the mastermind.

"I need proof."

"Look in Chen Wei's office. There's a safe behind his desk. Inside, you'll find ledgers. Records. Everything you need." She paused. "But you can't get there as a ghost. You need a body."

"Then I'll find one."

"No." She grabbed his arm. "You'll be clumsy. You'll make mistakes. You'll get caught." She smiled. "Let me do it."

"Absolutely not."

"I can possess Chen Wei. I can walk into his office, open the safe, and walk out with everything. No one will suspect a thing."

"And then what? You'll have all the evidence. You'll hold it over my head forever."

"I'll give it to you. I swear."

"You swore before. Then you tried to possess me."

"That was before we had a deal."

"You tried to possess me five minutes ago!"

She had the decency to look sheepish. "Old habits."

Jiang Chen ran his hands through his hair. He was dead. He was standing in a cemetery, talking to a ghost who'd killed people, considering a plan that involved letting her possess another innocent person.

This was insane.

"I'll do it myself," he said. "I'll find a way."

"How? You're dead. You can't touch anything in the living world without a body."

"I'll possess someone."

"You don't know how."

"I'll learn."

"It takes years to master possession. You don't have years. Chen Wei is already suspicious. If he moves those ledgers, you'll lose everything."

She was right. He hated that she was right.

"Fine," he said through gritted teeth. "But I'm coming with you. Every step of the way."

"Wouldn't have it any other way." She grinned. "Now, let's find you a body."

She turned and walked toward the cemetery gates, her stolen body moving with an ease that made Jiang Chen's skin crawl. He followed, his staff heavy in his hands, his mind churning with doubt.

This was a mistake. He knew it was a mistake. But it was also his only chance.

They reached the gates. Lin Mei stopped, looking back at him.

"One more thing," she said.

"What?"

"The body we're going to take. It belongs to someone who deserves it."

Jiang Chen's stomach dropped. "What does that mean?"
