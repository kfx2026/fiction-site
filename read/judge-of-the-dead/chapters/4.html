📖 Judge of the Dead: The Soul Reaper's Apprentice by Yan Luo

Chapter 4: The Truth Beneath the Skin

~1.8k words · Updated 2026-06-22

The rooftop air tasted like exhaust and rain. Jiang Chen sat cross-legged on the concrete edge, the Yan Huang Scripture open in his lap, though the words had stopped making sense an hour ago.

Below, the city hummed with its usual night traffic. Taxis. Late-night delivery bikes. A couple arguing outside a convenience store. Normal sounds. Living sounds.

He wasn't living.

The thought settled in his chest like a stone. He pressed a hand to his ribs, feeling for a heartbeat that wasn't there. Cold. Still. The bullet wound had healed into a pale scar, but the hole in his chest felt wider than any bullet could make.

Three days. Three days since Judge Zhang had pressed that seal into his hand and told him he was a Soul Reaper now. Three days since he'd hunted his first ghost — a woman named Lin Mei who'd been haunting her own funeral, confused and terrified, not knowing she was dead.

He'd guided her to the Yellow Springs. She'd cried. He'd felt nothing.

The Yan Huang Scripture said Soul Reapers were supposed to be impartial. A tool of cosmic law, nothing more. But the words on the page felt like excuses, not explanations.

His phone buzzed. A text from Old Zhao: *Got something. Warehouse District. East of Third Bridge. Come alone.*

Jiang Chen stood. The scripture folded itself into his coat pocket like it had a mind of its own. He'd learned not to question the small magics anymore.

The warehouse was a rusted skeleton against the night sky, its windows boarded, its doors chained. But the chains were new. And the lock was polished brass — expensive, out of place.

Jiang Chen circled to the back. A window on the second floor had been pried open, the wood splintered. He climbed, his body moving with a lightness that still surprised him. The dead had advantages.

Inside, the air smelled of mold and metal. Old machinery sat under tarps, a graveyard of forgotten industry. But someone had been here recently. A single light bulb hung from the ceiling, casting a yellow circle on the concrete floor.

And in that circle stood a man.

He was tall. Chinese, maybe forty, with the build of someone who'd spent years not needing to fight but knowing how. His suit was expensive — tailored, dark gray, no wrinkles. His hands were in his pockets, and he was smiling.

"You're harder to find than I expected," the man said. His voice was smooth. Confident. The voice of someone used to being in control.

Jiang Chen stayed in the shadows. "Who are you?"

"Someone who knows what you are." The man stepped closer. The light caught his face — sharp cheekbones, eyes that missed nothing. "I know you died three weeks ago. I know you came back. I know you work for the dead now."

The words hit like a physical blow. Jiang Chen felt his fingers curl into fists. "That's not possible."

"Nothing is impossible, Mr. Jiang. You should know that better than most." The man reached into his jacket. Jiang Chen tensed, ready to move, but the man only pulled out a photograph.

He tossed it. It landed at Jiang Chen's feet, face up.

The photograph showed a courtroom. Not the execution chamber — the trial. Jiang Chen sat at the defendant's table, his head bowed, his hands cuffed. The prosecutor stood at the podium, pointing at him with a finger like a blade.

The prosecutor was the man in front of him.

"You," Jiang Chen whispered. The word came out wrong. Broken.

"Me." The man smiled wider. "Prosecutor Liang Wei. I put you on death row, Mr. Jiang. I made sure the evidence was clean, the witnesses were credible, and the judge had no choice but to sentence you to execution."

"Why?"

"Because you were innocent."

The silence that followed was absolute. The warehouse held its breath. Jiang Chen felt the floor tilt beneath him, felt the world reorder itself around a truth he'd always known but never been able to prove.

"I was framed," he said. Not a question.

"You were framed beautifully. The murder weapon had your fingerprints. The security footage showed someone who looked exactly like you entering the victim's apartment. The neighbor identified you in a lineup. It was perfect." Liang Wei's smile didn't waver. "The only problem was that you were in a jail cell two miles away when the murder happened."

Jiang Chen's hands were shaking. He couldn't stop them. "Then why did they convict me?"

"Because the real killer was someone important. Someone whose name couldn't appear in the investigation." Liang Wei took another step closer. "Do you want to know who?"

The rage was a living thing inside him, clawing at his ribs, screaming to get out. But something held it back. Something cold and careful, the part of him that had learned to survive three years on death row.

"If you know all this," Jiang Chen said slowly, "why are you telling me now?"

Liang Wei's smile faded. For the first time, something real flickered in his eyes. Something that looked almost like fear.

"Because she's coming for me next."

"She?"

"The woman who ordered your execution. The woman who's been killing people for twenty years and framing innocent men for every death." Liang Wei's voice dropped. "She's a judge, Mr. Jiang. One of the Ten Judges of Diyu."

The words didn't make sense. They couldn't make sense. "The Ten Judges are dead. They're spirits. They don't—"

"They don't what? They don't walk among the living?" Liang Wei laughed, but there was no humor in it. "You're a dead man walking the earth, Mr. Jiang. You hunt ghosts. You carry a scripture that shouldn't exist. And you think a Judge can't put on a mortal face and walk through a courtroom?"

Jiang Chen's mind raced. Judge Zhang. The old man in the robe, his face weathered by centuries. He'd seemed ancient, otherworldly. But he'd also seemed tired. Bureaucratic. Like a man doing a job he'd been doing for too long.

Could a Judge be evil? Could a Judge kill?

"You're lying," Jiang Chen said. But his voice wavered.

"I wish I was." Liang Wei reached into his jacket again. This time, he pulled out a file. Thick. Dog-eared. "This is every case she's touched. Every innocent man she's sent to death row. Every family she's destroyed. I've been building this for ten years, Mr. Jiang. And three weeks ago, she found out."

The file landed at Jiang Chen's feet beside the photograph.

"She's going to kill me. Not through the courts — through her power as a Judge. She'll send something after me. Something that makes my death look like an accident, or a heart attack, or a suicide." Liang Wei's hands were shaking now. "I need protection. And you're the only person in the world who can give it to me."

"Why would I help you? You put me on death row."

"Because I'm the only person who can prove you were innocent." Liang Wei met his eyes. "Help me survive, and I'll give you the evidence. You'll have your name back. You'll have your life back."

The words hung in the air. Jiang Chen looked down at the file, at the photograph, at the truth lying at his feet.

He thought about his mother, crying in the visitor's room. He thought about the guards, the needles, the cold table. He thought about Judge Zhang, about the Yan Huang Scripture, about the seal that marked him as property of the dead.

And he thought about the woman who had ordered all of it.

"I'll help you," Jiang Chen said. "But not for my name. Not for my life."

Liang Wei frowned. "Then why?"

"Because I want to know her name. I want to know her face. And I want to be the last thing she sees before she dies."

The words came out flat. Cold. The voice of a dead man who had nothing left to lose.

Liang Wei stared at him for a long moment. Then he nodded.

"Her name is Judge Zhang," he said. "The Judge of the Fifth Court. The one who recruited you."

The world stopped.

Jiang Chen's hand went to his chest, to the seal hidden beneath his shirt. The seal that Judge Zhang had pressed into his skin. The seal that bound him to the underworld.

The seal that had been placed there by the woman who had murdered him.

"No," he whispered. "That's not possible."

"She walks among the living as a mortal woman," Liang Wei said. "Gray hair. Old face. But her soul is ancient. I've seen her true form once, in a vision — she's not what she appears to be."

The warehouse door slammed open.

Both men turned. A figure stood in the doorway, silhouetted against the streetlights. Small. Bent. An old woman in a traditional robe.

Judge Zhang.

"Jiang Chen," she said, her voice carrying the weight of centuries. "Step away from the prosecutor. He's not what he appears to be either."

Jiang Chen looked at Liang Wei. The prosecutor's face had gone pale. His hand was reaching for something inside his jacket.

"Don't trust her," Liang Wei said. "She'll twist everything. She'll make you believe—"

"She already made me believe," Jiang Chen said. "She made me believe I was dead. She made me believe I was a Soul Reaper. She made me believe I was serving justice."

He turned to face Judge Zhang. The old woman stood in the doorway, her hands clasped in front of her, her eyes unreadable.

"I want the truth," Jiang Chen said. "Both of you. Right now. Or I walk away and let the dead sort it out."

The silence stretched. The light bulb flickered.

Judge Zhang spoke first.

"The prosecutor is a ghost," she said. "He's been dead for six months. And he's been lying to you since the moment you arrived."

Liang Wei's face twisted. His hand came out of his jacket, holding a blade that glowed with pale light.

"I'm not the ghost here," he said. "She is. She's been dead for three thousand years. And she's been using you, Jiang Chen. Just like she's used everyone."

Jiang Chen looked between them. The old woman. The prosecutor. The photograph. The file. The scripture in his pocket, whispering secrets he couldn't understand.

Two liars. One truth.

And the only person who could find it was a dead man holding a blade that could cut through lies.

The light bulb went out.

In the darkness, Jiang Chen heard the sound of a blade being drawn.

And then someone screamed.
