CHAPTER 13: The Beast That Eats Shadows

The cub's shadow stretched across the cave wall, and Su Yun saw it.

He saw the thing that lived in the shadow. The thing with no shape, no form, no name. He saw its eyes—if they could be called eyes—open in the darkness, and he felt something cold slide down his spine like a finger tracing his vertebrae.

"Don't look at it," the registrar said.

Su Yun couldn't look away.

The cub purred against his leg, oblivious. Innocent. The shadow-thing watched him with hunger that predated hunger, with want that existed before wanting was invented. It didn't move. It didn't need to. It was already everywhere the light wasn't.

"Look at me, boy."

The registrar grabbed his chin and forced his head around. Her fingers were iron, her eyes harder. "You looked. That's a problem. But it's not the biggest problem."

"What is that thing?"

"Your cub's shadow." She released him. "Or rather, what lives in it. The Qiongqi is a door, Su Yun. A door to places that were sealed for good reason. The cub doesn't know it yet. But the shadow does."

The cub stopped purring. It looked up at the registrar, and for a moment—just a moment—its eyes weren't black. They were the same nothing-space as the shadow. Then they flickered back to normal, and the cub yawned, showing tiny fangs.

Su Yun picked it up. It weighed nothing. It weighed everything.

"You said every hundred thousand years, a Qiongqi bonds with a tamer," he said. "What happened to the last one?"

The registrar was silent for a long time. The lantern flickered. The shadow-thing pressed against the edges of the light, testing, waiting.

"The last tamer was called Meng Xi," she finally said. "He was the greatest beast tamer in the history of the Nine Realms. He could command beasts with a whisper. He could make Xiezhi kneel. He could make dragons weep."

"What happened to him?"

"He consumed the Eastern Sea." Her voice was flat. "He consumed it completely. Every beast, every fish, every drop of water. He consumed it and the Qiongqi consumed him, and where the Eastern Sea had been, there was nothing. Just a hole in the world. A hole that's still there."

The cub yawned again. It didn't care about history. It didn't care about holes in the world. It cared about warmth and milk and the steady beat of Su Yun's heart.

"I won't let that happen," Su Yun said.

"You won't have a choice." The registrar walked to the back of the cave, where a stone door stood half-open. "The Qiongqi will grow. It will need to eat. And when it's hungry enough, it will eat through you."

"Then I'll starve it."

"You can't starve a Qiongqi. You can only delay it." She pushed the stone door open, revealing a narrow passage that smelled of wet earth and old blood. "This tunnel leads to the tournament's third stage. If you survive it, you'll be one step closer to saving your village."

"And if I don't?"

"Then your village dies. The Qiongqi finds another tamer. And the cycle continues." She stepped aside. "Your choice, boy. The tunnel or the crater."

The crater was still filled with chaos. Through the cave entrance, Su Yun could hear screams, beast roars, the crack of breaking stone. The Xiezhi was still out there. The scarred man. Everyone who wanted his cub dead.

The tunnel was dark. But the shadow-thing was already there.

"You're sending me to die," Su Yun said.

"I'm sending you to choose." The registrar's eyes were ancient, tired, full of things she'd seen that no one should see. "The tournament isn't about capturing beasts. It's about surviving long enough to become something worth surviving for. Your cub will eat everything you love. The only question is whether you'll have anything left by the time it's done."

The cub squirmed in Su Yun's arms. It wanted down. It wanted to walk.

He let it.

The cub padded to the tunnel entrance, sniffed the air, and looked back at him. Its tail flicked. Its ears perked. It was waiting.

"Fine," Su Yun said. "But if I die, I'm taking you with me."

The registrar almost smiled. "That's the spirit."

---

The tunnel went down.

Su Yun walked for what felt like hours, the cub padding beside him, the darkness pressing in from all sides. The only light came from patches of glowing moss that grew on the walls, casting everything in sickly green.

The cub stopped.

It sniffed the air. Its ears flattened against its skull. It growled—a low, rumbling sound that shouldn't have come from something so small.

"What is it?"

The cub didn't answer. It never answered. But it moved closer to Su Yun's legs, pressing against him, and that was answer enough.

Something was ahead.

Su Yun crouched, put his hand on the cub's back. "Stay close."

They moved forward together, slow, silent. The tunnel curved, then opened into a chamber the size of a small lake. The ceiling was lost in darkness. The floor was covered in bones—thousands of them, some beast, some human, all cracked and picked clean.

In the center of the chamber, something sat.

It was vaguely human-shaped, but its proportions were wrong. Its arms were too long. Its legs were too short. Its skin was the color of wet stone, and its face had no features—no eyes, no nose, no mouth—just a smooth, blank surface that somehow looked at Su Yun.

The cub froze.

The faceless thing tilted its head. A sound came from it—not a voice, but something like rocks grinding together, like bones breaking, like the earth remembering how to scream.

"You have the beast," it said.

The words didn't come from its face. They came from everywhere, from the walls, from the bones, from the darkness above. Su Yun's ears popped. His teeth ached.

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"The beast that shouldn't be." The faceless thing took a step forward. Its feet didn't break the bones. It walked on top of them, leaving no prints. "The beast that consumes. The beast that ends."

The cub growled again. Its fur bristled. Its tiny claws scraped the stone floor.

"I'm not here to fight you," Su Yun said. "I'm here to pass through."

"There is no passing through." The faceless thing stopped a few feet away. "There is only feeding. The tournament is a lie. The Nine Realms are a cage. And you—" It pointed at Su Yun with a finger that was too long, too thin, too wrong. "—are the key that opens the door."

Su Yun's hand went to the rope at his belt. It was a stupid weapon. A useless weapon. But it was all he had.

"I don't know what you are," he said. "But I know what I am. I'm a stable boy from a village that's going to die if I don't win this tournament. I'm not a key. I'm not a door. I'm just a kid with a cub and a rope."

The faceless thing was silent for a long moment.

Then it laughed.

The sound was worse than its voice. It was wet, broken, wrong. It came from the bones. It came from the walls. It came from the cub's shadow, where the thing with no shape watched and smiled.

"You are already consumed," the faceless thing said. "You just don't know it yet."

The cub lunged.

It moved faster than Su Yun had ever seen it move—a blur of black fur and tiny claws, straight at the faceless thing's throat. The faceless thing caught it mid-air, one hand wrapped around the cub's body, and held it up.

The cub screamed.

The same scream as before. The scream that cracked the ground, that made the Xiezhi step back. The scream of chaos made flesh.

But the faceless thing didn't step back.

It held the cub in front of its blank face, and something moved behind that smooth surface. Something that wanted.

"The Qiongqi is not your weapon," it said. "You are its vessel. And vessels are meant to be emptied."

The cub's eyes changed. They went from black to nothing. The shadow on the floor grew, spread, reached for Su Yun's feet.

"Let it go," Su Yun said.

"Or what?"

Su Yun didn't answer. He grabbed the rope from his belt, looped it around his hand, and swung.

The rope caught the faceless thing's wrist. Su Yun pulled, hard, and the faceless thing stumbled—not because the rope was strong, but because it hadn't expected the attack. The cub fell, hit the ground, and scrambled back to Su Yun.

The faceless thing looked at the rope around its wrist.

"You bind me with string?"

"I bind you with faith," Su Yun said. He didn't know where the words came from. They just came. "The rope is just rope. But the faith is mine."

The faceless thing's wrist began to smoke.

It looked down. The rope was burning—not with fire, but with light. White light. The kind of light that existed before the sun, before the moon, before the stars.

"Impossible," the faceless thing said.

"Nothing's impossible." Su Yun pulled the rope again. "I'm a stable boy. I don't know cultivation. I don't know beast taming. But I know one thing: you can't eat what you can't catch."

The faceless thing screamed.

The sound was worse than the cub's. It was the sound of something old and terrible being hurt by something small and foolish. The bones on the floor rattled. The walls cracked. The light from Su Yun's rope blazed brighter, hotter, until the faceless thing's entire arm was burning.

It tore itself free.

The arm came off at the shoulder, smoking, melting, dissolving into ash. The faceless thing staggered backward, its blank face somehow expressing agony.

"This isn't over," it said. "The tournament will end. The realms will fall. And you, stable boy—you will watch."

It dissolved into the darkness, leaving nothing but the smell of burnt stone and the echo of its promise.

Su Yun fell to his knees.

The cub pressed against him, trembling. The rope in his hands was just rope again—ordinary, frayed, useless.

But it had worked.

He had fought something that shouldn't exist. And he had won.

"I don't understand," he whispered.

The cub looked up at him. Its eyes were normal again—black, wide, innocent. It licked his chin.

Behind them, in the tunnel, footsteps echoed.

Su Yun turned. A figure emerged from the darkness—tall, cloaked, carrying a lantern that burned with blue fire.

"Well, well," the figure said. "You survived the Hollow Man."

The voice was familiar.

The figure lowered its hood.

It was the scarred man from the crater. The one whose Xiezhi had nearly killed them.

"You," Su Yun said.

"Me." The scarred man smiled. It was not a kind smile. "I've been following you since the cave. I wanted to see if you'd die."

"Sorry to disappoint."

"You didn't disappoint." The scarred man walked closer, his lantern casting long shadows. "You did something no one has done in a thousand years. You hurt a Hollow Man with nothing but a rope."
