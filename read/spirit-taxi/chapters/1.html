# CHAPTER ONE: The Mirror Shows Nothing

The streetlights along Renmin South Road bled orange onto wet asphalt. Two in the morning, and Chengdu's late-summer humidity clung to everything like a wet sheet. Old Ma's taxi hummed at a red light, the only car in sight.

He'd been driving this route for thirty years. Knew which noodle shops stayed open past midnight, which alleys held the best street vendors, which corners attracted drunk college students who'd puke in his backseat and then argue about the fare. He knew the city the way old men know their own wives—not always pretty, but familiar down to the last wrinkle.

The light turned green.

He drove.

Three blocks later, a figure stepped off the curb. Not into the street—just to the edge, like they were waiting for a bus that wasn't coming. Old Ma pulled over without thinking. The figure wore a long coat, despite the heat, and moved with the careful gait of someone who hadn't slept in days.

The back door opened.

"Renmin Park," the passenger said. Male voice, young, but hollowed out. "The east gate."

Old Ma nodded and hit the meter. He pulled back into traffic, eyes automatically scanning the rearview mirror.

Nothing.

The back seat was empty.

Old Ma's hands stayed steady on the wheel. He'd been driving night shift long enough to know the difference between a trick of the light and something else entirely. Streetlights played games with shadows, sure. But they didn't erase a full-grown man from a mirror.

"I said Renmin Park," the voice repeated. Slightly annoyed now. "You deaf or just stupid?"

"Neither," Old Ma said. "Just checking."

He didn't check again. He didn't need to. The air in the taxi had changed—that was the first sign. It got heavier, like the passenger was pulling oxygen out of the space around him. The second sign was the smell. Wet earth and incense smoke, the kind that hung around old temples and freshly dug graves.

"You know," the passenger said, "most drivers would have freaked out by now."

"Most drivers don't work the night shift in Chengdu for thirty years."

A pause. Then a dry laugh that didn't carry any warmth. "Fair point."

They passed under a bridge. Graffiti covered the concrete walls, layer upon layer of tags and characters, some so old they'd faded into abstract shapes. A homeless man slept on a cardboard mat, his body curled around a half-empty bottle of baijiu.

"How much do you know?" the passenger asked.

"About what?"

"About what I am. About what this taxi is."

Old Ma took a left turn, his tires humming against the asphalt. "I know the meter works the same whether you've got a body or not. I know the fare's the same. And I know you'll get out when we get where you're going."

"That's it?"

"That's all I need."

The passenger laughed again, and this time there was something like respect in it. "You're a strange one, old man."

"People say that."

They drove in silence for a while. The streets grew narrower, older. Buildings pressed in from both sides, their balconies cluttered with laundry and potted plants and the occasional caged bird. Somewhere a dog started barking, then stopped.

Old Ma glanced at the meter. Fourteen yuan. Not much of a fare, but the night was young. And he'd learned long ago that passengers like this one didn't just appear by accident.

"You're going to ask," the passenger said. "They always ask."

"Ask what?"

"Why I'm dead. How I died. What unfinished business I've got dragging me back to the living world." The voice turned bitter. "The usual."

"I wasn't going to ask."

"Bullshit."

Old Ma shrugged. "I've been driving spirits for longer than you've been dead, kid. I've heard every story. Every tragedy. Every regret. You think you're special because you died young?"

Silence.

"I was twenty-three," the passenger finally said. "Car accident. Drunk driver. He walked away with a scratch."

"You want revenge?"

"I want—" The voice cut off. Then, softer: "I don't know what I want. That's the problem."

The taxi passed through another intersection. Street vendors had set up on the corner, selling grilled skewers and beer to the last of the night owls. The smoke from their grills curled upward, mixing with the humid air, and Old Ma caught the passenger inhaling deeply.

"You miss it," Old Ma said. "Living."

"Every second."

"Then why are you still here?"

The question hung in the air. The passenger didn't answer. Instead, he said: "Turn left up ahead."

Old Ma obeyed. They entered a residential district, the kind of place where old women still sat on stools outside their doors, fanning themselves and gossiping until the early hours. A few lights were on in the windows. Most were dark.

"There," the passenger said. "That building. The one with the red door."

Old Ma pulled over. The building was a typical Chengdu walk-up—five stories, no elevator, bamboo scaffolding covering one side where someone was doing renovations. A single light burned on the fourth floor.

"That's my mother's apartment," the passenger said. "She still leaves the light on for me. Every night. Even though she knows I'm not coming back."

Old Ma watched the glow through his windshield. "You want to go up?"

"No." The voice was barely a whisper. "I can't. I've tried. Every night for three months, I've tried to walk through that door. And every night, something stops me."

"Something?"

"I don't know what. A wall. A force. Like the building itself won't let me in."

Old Ma turned in his seat. He still couldn't see the passenger in the mirror, but he could feel his presence—a cold spot in the backseat, a weight that pressed against the upholstery.

"Three months," Old Ma said. "You've been wandering for three months?"

"I was waiting for you."

The words hit like a punch to the chest. Old Ma had suspected, of course. You didn't drive a spirit taxi for three decades without learning how the game worked. Certain passengers weren't random. Certain fares came with strings attached.

"Who sent you?" he asked.

"I don't know. A voice. In the dark. It told me to find the old taxi with the cracked windshield. Said you'd help me get home."

Old Ma's eyes drifted to the crack in his windshield. A spiderweb fracture in the lower right corner, from a stone that had kicked up on the highway five years ago. He'd never bothered to fix it. Bad luck to replace a windshield that had saved your life, his grandmother used to say.

"I'm not sure I can help you," he said.

"You're my only chance."

Old Ma sighed. He killed the engine and stepped out of the taxi. The night air hit him, still thick with humidity, but colder now. The kind of cold that settled in your bones and didn't leave.

The back door opened. Closed. The passenger was standing on the sidewalk now—a young man in a dark coat, pale as paper, with eyes that didn't quite catch the light.

"You can see me," the passenger said.

"I've always been able to see."

"Then you know."

Old Ma nodded. He knew. The dead didn't just get stuck by accident. Something was holding this boy here—something stronger than love, stronger than grief. And if he couldn't get through that red door, it meant whatever was holding him wasn't on the other side.

It was inside him.

"Tell me what happened that night," Old Ma said. "The whole truth. Not the version you've been telling yourself."

The passenger's face twisted. "I already told you. Drunk driver. Car accident. I died."

"No." Old Ma shook his head. "You said you were twenty-three. You said you died in a car accident. But you didn't say you were driving."

The passenger's eyes went wide.

"You didn't say you were the one behind the wheel."

The street went silent. Even the crickets stopped chirping. The passenger stood frozen, his mouth open, his hands shaking at his sides.

"I—" He stopped. Swallowed. "I don't remember."

"Yes you do."

"I don't—"

"Yes. You. Do."

The passenger crumpled. He sank to his knees on the wet sidewalk, his head in his hands, and for a long moment he didn't move. Then, in a voice that sounded like breaking glass:

"I was drunk. I got behind the wheel. I killed myself and I almost killed my best friend and I don't know how to face my mother because she raised me better than this and I—"

He broke off. Sobbing.

Old Ma stood over him, hands in his pockets. The night was cold and the street was empty and somewhere above them, a fourth-floor light burned for a son who would never come home.

"Your mother already knows," Old Ma said quietly. "She's known since the moment the police knocked on her door. She doesn't care how you died. She only cares that you're gone."

"But I can't—"

"You can. You just don't want to. There's a difference."

The passenger looked up. His face was streaked with tears that didn't quite look real—more like the memory of tears, the shape of grief without the substance.

"What do I do?"

Old Ma reached into his pocket and pulled out a worn brass key. Simple. Old-fashioned. The kind that unlocked nothing in the modern world.

"Go through the door," he said. "It'll open this time."

"How do you know?"

"Because I'm giving you permission. Your guilt locked you out. But guilt is just a story you tell yourself. And stories can be rewritten."

He held out the key. The passenger stared at it, then took it with trembling fingers.

"This isn't real," he whispered.

"It's as real as you need it to be."

The passenger stood. He looked at the key in his hand, then at the red door across the street. The light still burned on the fourth floor. The window was open, and Old Ma could see a silhouette moving inside—a woman, small and bent, making tea at two in the morning because she couldn't sleep.

"Thank you," the passenger said.

"Don't thank me yet. You still have to knock."

The passenger smiled. It was a thin, fragile thing, but it was real.

Then he walked across the street, pushed open the red door, and disappeared inside.

Old Ma stood alone on the sidewalk. The light on the fourth floor flickered once. Twice.

Then it went dark.

He got back in his taxi and started the engine. The meter had reset itself. The back seat was empty. The smell of incense was fading.

He drove.

Three blocks later, his phone buzzed. A text from an unknown number:

*New fare. Jinli Ancient Street. 3am. She'll be wearing red.*

Old Ma stared at the screen for a long time.

Then he put the phone away and kept driving.

The night was still young.
