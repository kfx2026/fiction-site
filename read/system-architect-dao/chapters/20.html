Chapter 20: The First Fracture

~1.8k words · 2026-06-22

Kai's finger hovered over the send button when the world lurched.

Not physically. The apartment remained solid beneath him, the water stain on the ceiling still holding its familiar shape. But something *shifted* in the qi around him, like a tectonic plate sliding deep underground. A pressure wave rippled through the spiritual fabric of reality, and every awakened person in the city must have felt it.

His phone buzzed again. Then again. Then a cascade of notifications that blurred into a single endless vibration.

He didn't need to read them. The System broadcast was still burning in his peripheral vision:

[WORLD INTEGRATION PHASE 1: COMPLETE]
[LOCAL LAWS OF PHYSICS: MODIFIED]
[SYSTEM OVERLAY: ACTIVE]
[NEW FEATURES UNLOCKED]

A second window appeared, this one personal:

[STATUS UPDATE: KAI ZHANG]
[CURRENT CLASS: Dao Architect (Unclassified)]
[CULTIVATION REALM: Qi Condensation, Stage 1 (Incomplete Foundation)]
[MERIDIANS OPENED: 3/12 (Standard Path)]
[QI PURITY: 73% — Acceptable]
[SYSTEM COMPATIBILITY: 22% — Warning: Low]
[RECOMMENDED ACTION: Seek immediate integration protocol]

Kai read the last line three times, his blood running cold. *Twenty-two percent compatibility.* The System was practically screaming that he didn't belong. That his class was an error in the code, a bug waiting to be patched out.

His phone buzzed again. He grabbed it.

The group chat had exploded.

*What the hell was that? I just gained three levels automatically!*

*Same! My mana pool doubled. This is insane.*

*Kai, you seeing this? What's your compatibility looking like?*

Before he could answer, a private message slid in from an unknown number. No caller ID. No preview. Just a single line of text:

*You felt that, didn't you? The shift. We need to talk.*

Kai's thumb hovered over the message. His qi sense flickered outward instinctively, brushing against the electronic device. And for a fraction of a second—he felt it. A faint qi signature embedded in the text itself, like a spiritual fingerprint.

Someone else had cultivation.

Someone else knew.

He typed back: *Who is this?*

The reply came instantly: *Someone like you. Someone the System doesn't understand. Meet me at the old warehouse district. Corner of 5th and Mercer. Come alone.*

Kai stared at the message. Every instinct screamed trap. But another part of him—the part that had spent six hours learning to circulate qi under Mentor's watchful eye—whispered something else. *You're not the only anomaly. And anomalies need each other.*

He grabbed his jacket and left.

---

The streets were chaos.

People stood in clusters on sidewalks, staring at their phones, shouting at each other, waving hands through the air as if testing invisible interfaces. A woman had conjured a ball of fire in her palm and was laughing hysterically while her friends backed away. Two men were arguing loudly about whether "mana regeneration" stacked with "stamina recovery." A child no older than ten was floating three feet off the ground, crying for her mother.

The System had come fully online, and no one knew the rules.

Kai kept his head down, weaving through the crowds. His qi sense pulsed with every step, mapping the spiritual landscape around him. Most people registered as faint sparks—newly awakened, their energy chaotic and untrained. But a few burned brighter. Warriors with dense, muscular qi signatures. Mages with sharp, cutting auras. Healers with soft, flowing energy.

And then there were the others.

He caught a flicker of something darker three blocks away—a qi signature that felt *wrong*. Twisted. Like a wound in the spiritual fabric. He paused, turning his head, but the sensation vanished as quickly as it had appeared.

*Focus.* He had a meeting to attend.

---

The warehouse district was abandoned. Had been for years, ever since the old manufacturing economy collapsed. The buildings loomed like hollow skeletons, their windows shattered, their walls tagged with faded graffiti. The streetlights flickered, casting long shadows that danced with each pulse of light.

Kai found the corner of 5th and Mercer. A single warehouse stood apart from the others, its rolling door partially open, a sliver of light bleeding through the gap.

He approached slowly, his qi cycling through his meridians in a defensive pattern. Not aggressive—he didn't know how to fight yet—but ready. Prepared.

"Come in," a voice called from inside. Female. Young. "I don't bite."

Kai pushed the door open and stepped through.

The warehouse interior had been converted into something between a dojo and a laboratory. Mats covered the concrete floor. A table in the center held an array of crystals, papers, and a laptop that looked wildly out of place. And standing beside the table, arms crossed, was a girl who couldn't have been older than nineteen.

She had sharp features and sharper eyes, dark hair pulled back in a practical ponytail, and a presence that made the air around her feel *dense*. Her qi signature was unlike anything Kai had sensed before—structured, efficient, almost mechanical in its precision.

"You're Kai," she said. Not a question.

"And you are?"

"Lin Mei. System Analyst, formerly of the Beijing Institute of Computational Cultivation. Currently, the only other person I've found who isn't compatible with the System."

Kai's eyes narrowed. "Formerly? What happened to the Institute?"

Lin Mei's expression flickered—a crack in her composure that she quickly sealed. "Gone. When the tutorial ended, everyone who entered received a class. Everyone except me. The Institute classified me as a 'defective integration' and began—" She stopped. Shook her head. "Let's just say I'm no longer welcome there."

"Defective," Kai repeated. "The System flagged my compatibility at twenty-two percent. Is that what you mean?"

"Twenty-two?" Lin Mei's eyes widened. "That's higher than I expected. Mine is eleven. Most of the others we've found are below five."

"Others?"

Lin Mei gestured to the table. On it, spread across the surface, were photographs. Dozens of them. Faces of people from all over the world—different ages, different backgrounds, different walks of life. But all of them had one thing in common.

Written beneath each photo, in precise handwriting, was a number.

Compatibility percentages.

Three percent. Seven percent. One percent. One woman in her forties had a compatibility of zero point four.

"These are the people the System rejected," Lin Mei said quietly. "Or rather, the people who rejected the System. There's a difference, and the System is starting to figure that out."

Kai looked up from the photographs. "What do you mean?"

Lin Mei's jaw tightened. "The broadcast you saw—World Integration Phase 1. That wasn't just an update. It was a *realignment*. The System is rewriting reality to fit its framework. Physics, biology, even the flow of qi itself. It's turning the world into a game, and everyone who can't play is being left behind."

"Left behind how?"

She picked up a photograph—the woman with zero point four percent compatibility. "This woman died yesterday. Her heart simply stopped. The autopsy showed nothing wrong. But I checked her qi signature post-mortem, and there was nothing there. The System had erased her spiritual existence as easily as deleting a corrupted file."

The words hit Kai like a physical blow. "It's killing people who can't integrate?"

"Not directly. But the world is changing, and people who can't adapt are being *pruned*. A warrior who can't level up will eventually be outclassed by monsters. A mage who can't regenerate mana will burn out. And people with compatibility too low to even form a class—they just... fade."

Kai thought of his mother. His friends. The billions of people on Earth who had received classes, who were already celebrating their new powers. How many of them were actually compatible? How many were skating on the surface of a system that would eventually reject them?

"Why are you telling me this?" he asked.

"Because you're different." Lin Mei stepped closer, her eyes burning with intensity. "Your compatibility is twenty-two percent. That's the highest I've seen among the 'defectives.' But more importantly—your class. Dao Architect. It's not a System class. It's something older. Something that exists *outside* the System's framework."

Kai's hand drifted unconsciously to his chest, where his qi pulsed in his dantian. "How do you know about my class?"

"I've been watching the data since the tutorial ended. Most classes are variations of the standard templates—Warrior, Mage, Healer, Rogue, Crafter. But yours... yours didn't fit any known pattern. It took me hours to find any reference to it, and when I did—"

She pulled a notebook from the table, flipping it open to a page covered in dense handwriting. In the center, written in red ink, were two words:

*Dao Architect.*

Below them, in smaller script: *First recorded instance. Reference: Pre-System Era texts. Cultivation-based class. Origin unknown. Function: Reality structuring through qi manipulation. Risk level: Extreme.*

"Reality structuring?" Kai read the line aloud, his voice hollow.

"The Dao isn't just a path," Lin Mei said. "It's the underlying framework of existence. The System is an artificial Dao—a human-created framework designed to govern reality. But you have the potential to *build your own*. To write your own rules. To create a system that exists parallel to the one imposed on Earth."

She paused, her voice dropping to a whisper.

"And the System knows it. That's why your compatibility is so low. It's not a bug, Kai. It's a security measure. The System is actively blocking you because you're a threat to its control."

The warehouse fell silent. Kai could hear his own heartbeat, feel the qi circulating through his meridians, sense the faint pulse of the System pressing against the edges of his consciousness like a predator testing a fence.

"What do we do?" he asked finally.

Lin Mei's smile was grim. "We build. We find others like us. We learn to cultivate outside the System's framework. And when the time comes—we break its hold on reality."

She reached into her pocket and pulled out a small crystal, glowing with a faint internal light. "This is a qi resonance crystal. I've been using it to map the spiritual structure of the city. And I found something interesting."

She placed it on the table, and the crystal began to pulse. A holographic map materialized above it—the city rendered in lines of light, with points of color scattered throughout. Most were blue, representing normal qi signatures. Some were red—anomalies.

One point, deep beneath the city, was black. A void in the spiritual landscape.

"There's something under the city," Lin Mei said. "Something the System is trying to hide. I think it's connected to the tutorial—a piece of the original framework that wasn't properly integrated. If we can reach it, we might be able to find answers."

Kai stared at the black void. It pulsed with a rhythm that matched his own heartbeat.

"When do we go?"

"Now." Lin Mei grabbed a bag from beside the table. "The System's attention will shift as more people awaken. We have a window, but it won't last long."
