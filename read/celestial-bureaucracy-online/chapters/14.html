CHAPTER 14: The Celestial Budget Committee

Zhang Wei's fingers still tingled from touching the administrative sphere. The white void around him flickered as warning notifications cascaded across his vision.

[ALERT: Unauthorized Access Detected]
[ALERT: Security Protocol 7-B Activated]
[ALERT: Your location has been logged]

He needed to move. Fast.

"Exit," Zhang commanded.

The void shattered like glass, and Zhang found himself back at his desk. The jade token sat innocuously in his palm, warm to the touch. His screen still displayed the office supply requisition form. Everything looked normal.

Everything except the new icon that had appeared in the corner of his vision: a tiny gear with an infinity symbol at its center. The Core Administrative Interface. Permanent access.

Zhang quickly minimized it before anyone could see.

"Zhang Wei!"

He nearly jumped out of his chair. Supervisor Hu stood at his cubicle entrance, face redder than a demon's backside. Three other clerks crowded behind him, clutching scrolls and tablets.

"Yes, Supervisor Hu?"

"Explain this." Hu thrust a scroll at Zhang. It was covered in glowing red stamps. REJECTED. DENIED. FLAGGED FOR REVIEW.

Zhang scanned the contents. Office supply requisitions. All of them. The forms he'd spent the last three hours processing.

"I don't understand," Zhang said carefully. "I followed standard protocol. Each requisition was verified against departmental budgets and celestial allocation guidelines."

"Exactly!" Hu's voice cracked. "You rejected twenty-three requisitions from the Department of Heavenly Feasts!"

"They requested three thousand jade chopsticks," Zhang replied. "Their budget only allows for twelve hundred. The variance requires approval from the Celestial Finance Committee, which requires a minimum fourteen-day review period."

Silence. Then someone in the back started laughing.

Hu spun around. "Who did that?"

"I did." A woman pushed through the crowd. She wore a simple white robe with silver embroidery—no rank insignia. Her hair was pulled back in a severe bun, and her glasses had chains that dangled like tiny nooses.

Every clerk in sight immediately straightened their postures.

"Director Zhao," Hu stammered. "I didn't know you were—"

"Obviously not." Director Zhao walked past him and stopped at Zhang's cubicle. She studied him with the intensity of a tax auditor examining a suspicious deduction.

"You're the new dead clerk," she said.

"That's... one way to describe it."

"I've been reading your files. Particularly the ones you accessed ten minutes ago."

Zhang's blood went cold. The Core Administrative Interface. The restricted records. She knew.

Director Zhao smiled. It was not a pleasant expression. "Relax, Junior Clerk Zhang. If I wanted you deleted, you'd already be processing reincarnation paperwork. No, I'm here because you just made my life significantly more difficult."

"I'm sorry?"

"You rejected requisitions from Heavenly Feasts. That department is overseen by Minister Pu. Minister Pu owes me seventeen favors, and now he's going to cash them all in to expedite those approvals." She tapped the scroll. "Do you know what happens when favors get cashed in?"

"Work gets done?"

"Chaos happens." Director Zhao plucked the scroll from Zhang's desk. "Protocols get bypassed. Records get lost. And people like me—people who keep this bureaucracy running—we have to clean up the mess."

She leaned closer. "But chaos also creates opportunities. Tell me, Zhang Wei, do you play chess?"

"I've been known to lose spectacularly."

"Good. Losers understand consequences." She straightened up. "You're being transferred. Special assignment. My office, effective immediately."

"But my probation—"

"Approved." Director Zhao snapped her fingers. A clerk appeared at her side, holding a document that was already stamped with official seals. "Sign here."

Zhang looked at Hu. The supervisor's face had gone from red to an unhealthy shade of purple. "Director, with all due respect, Junior Clerk Zhang has only been with us for—"

"Three days. I know." Director Zhao's smile widened. "And in those three days, he's discovered a system error that your department has been ignoring for six months, hacked into a restricted administrative interface, and angered one of the most powerful immortals in the Heavenly Court."

She turned back to Zhang. "You're either the most incompetent clerk I've ever met, or the most dangerous. Either way, I want you on my team."

Zhang signed.

---

Director Zhao's office was nothing like the cramped cubicle farm Zhang had been working in. It occupied an entire floating island, connected to the main complex by a bridge made of pure light. The walls were lined with scrolls that moved on their own, their characters shifting and rearranging like living things.

"Sit." Director Zhao gestured to a chair that looked like it was made of compressed clouds. Zhang sat. It was surprisingly comfortable.

"I'm going to tell you something that should get me demoted to janitorial duty," she said, settling behind her desk. "The celestial bureaucracy is broken."

"I noticed."

"Everyone notices. But no one's done anything about it because the people in power benefit from the chaos." She pulled up a holographic display. Zhang recognized the Core Administrative Interface—the same sphere he'd touched in the white void.

"This is the Celestial Management System. CMS for short. It was designed three thousand years ago by the Jade Emperor's chief architect. It was supposed to automate destiny processing, karma accounting, and reincarnation logistics."

"Sounds ambitious."

"It was revolutionary. But it was also flawed. The original architect was executed for treason before he could finish the final patch."

Zhang raised an eyebrow. "Executed?"

"The official story says he tried to seize power. The unofficial story—" Director Zhao paused. "The unofficial story is that he discovered something in the system's code that the Jade Emperor didn't want anyone to find."

"What did he discover?"

Director Zhao's eyes met his. "That the celestial bureaucracy is running on borrowed time. Literally. The system is consuming immortal essence faster than the Heavenly Court can produce it. In approximately six hundred years, the entire system will crash. Every soul in existence—mortal, immortal, demonic—will be recycled into raw cosmic energy."

Zhang stared at her. "That's..."

"Apocalyptic. Yes. And the Jade Emperor knows. He's been trying to fix it for centuries, but every solution requires him to relinquish power. So instead, he's buried the problem under so much bureaucracy that no one can find it."

"Except you."

"Except me." Director Zhao leaned forward. "And now, except you."

The holographic display shifted, showing a complex web of connections. At its center was a single node, pulsing with red light.

"This is the source of the error that killed you," Director Zhao said. "Your death wasn't a mistake. It was intentional."

Zhang felt his hands clench. "Who?"

"Someone who's been systematically eliminating clerks who get too close to the truth. You're the seventh one in the last year."

"Then why am I still alive?"

"Because you're useful." Director Zhao smiled again. "And because the person who ordered your death doesn't know you've accessed the Core Interface. They think you're just another incompetent clerk who'll drink himself to death within a century."

"Comforting."

"It should be. It means you have time." She stood up. "But not much. The next Celestial Budget Committee meeting is in three days. During that meeting, I'm going to propose a system-wide audit. If it passes, we'll have access to every corner of the CMS."

"And if it doesn't?"

"Then the person who killed you will know someone's investigating. And they'll come for us both."

Zhang processed this. "So I need to make sure the audit passes."

"You need to do more than that." Director Zhao pulled up another file. "The Budget Committee is controlled by three immortals: Minister Pu of Heavenly Feasts, General Feng of Heavenly Defense, and Lady Xiu of Heavenly Records. Each of them has veto power."

"Can we convince them?"

"Minister Pu is angry about the chopstick requisitions. General Feng only cares about military spending. And Lady Xiu—" Director Zhao hesitated. "Lady Xiu hasn't spoken to anyone in three hundred years."

"Why?"

"No one knows. She sealed herself in the Heavenly Records Archive and hasn't left since. Her staff say she's been reviewing something. Something that's made her... afraid."

A notification appeared in Zhang's vision.

[SYSTEM MESSAGE: New Quest Available]
[Title: The Budget Gambit]
[Objective: Secure approval for System-wide Audit]
[Time Limit: 72 Hours]
[Rewards: Director Zhao's Trust, Full CMS Access, +5000 Karma Points]
[Penalty for Failure: Soul Deletion (Irreversible)]

Zhang looked at the quest. Then at Director Zhao. Then at the pulsing red node that represented the person who had murdered him.

"I'm in," he said. "But I need something first."

"Name it."

"Access to the Heavenly Records Archive. If Lady Xiu knows something, I want to know what it is."

Director Zhao's expression flickered—surprise, then calculation. "The archive is sealed. Even I can't—"

"I can hack it."

"Excuse me?"

Zhang pulled up the Core Administrative Interface. The sphere of gears and runes materialized above his palm. "The CMS has a backdoor. The original architect built it in case he needed emergency access. It's hidden in the soul-processing algorithms."

Director Zhao's eyes widened. "How do you know that?"

"Because I accessed it when I first touched the Core. It was buried so deep even the system didn't know it was there." Zhang grinned. "Whoever designed this system was a genius. But they were also paranoid. They left themselves a way in."

"And you're going to use it to break into the archive."

"I'm going to use it to save reality." Zhang stood up. "But first, I need to find a way past the archive's security. Any suggestions?"

Director Zhao was silent for a long moment. Then she reached into her robe and pulled out a small jade key. It glowed with an inner light.

"This is a Celestial Skeleton Key," she said. "It can unlock any door in the Heavenly Court. Once."

"Why haven't you used it?"

"Because once it's used, it's gone. And I've been saving it for something that matters." She held it out to him. "Convince me this matters."

Zhang took the key. "If Lady Xiu has been hiding in that archive for three centuries, she's found something. Something that scared her enough to abandon her duties. Something that the person who killed me doesn't want anyone to find."

He closed his fingers around the key. "I'm going to find out what it is."

Director Zhao nodded slowly. "Then I expect results, Junior Clerk Zhang. Or we'll both be processing reincarnation paperwork in the Hell of Unending Data Entry."

"I'd rather not."

"Neither would I."

Zhang turned to leave, but paused at the door. "Director Zhao?"

"Yes?"

"Thank you. For trusting me."

"Don't thank me yet." Her voice was cold. "Trust is a currency in the celestial bureaucracy. And you just spent a very large portion of mine."

Zhang stepped out of the office and into the bridge of light. Below him, the Heavenly Court stretched out in all its bureaucratic glory—towers of ledgers, rivers of ink, clouds made of pending requests.

Somewhere in that mess was the Heavenly Records Archive. And somewhere in that archive was the truth about his death.

He looked at the jade key in his hand.

Three days. Seventy-two hours. And a system that was literally falling
